# Consulting Career: Thriving Inside the Firm

The skills that get you into consulting aren't the same skills that get you promoted. Case performance and analytical rigor earn you the job. Client impact, team leadership, and business development sustain and advance your career.

---

## The Career Ladder: What Each Level Actually Does

**Analyst/Associate (Years 1-3):**
The engine of the work. You run analyses, build models, conduct interviews, structure slides. Your job is to be highly reliable on structured tasks and to start generating insight, not just processing information. The transition: from doing what you're told excellently to forming independent views and pushing back constructively.

**Engagement Manager/Case Team Leader (Years 3-6):**
You own the day-to-day client relationship and the team's output. You're the bridge between the partner and the analysts. Your job is to manage the workplan, keep the team on track, develop the junior team, and be a credible face to the client. You're also expected to start contributing to the intellectual agenda — not just executing it.

**Principal/Associate Principal/Senior Manager (Years 6-10):**
You're building your own client relationships and starting to generate new business through repeat work and network referrals. You're developing expertise in an industry or function. You're managing multiple engagements simultaneously. The partner track is the explicit horizon — are you developing the skills to originate business, not just deliver it?

**Partner/Principal (Year 10+):**
Your job is business development, client relationships, and developing the next generation. Technical analytical work is largely delegated. You're selling and sponsoring. Your personal brand — as a trusted advisor in a specific domain — is your primary asset.

## Promotion Criteria: What Actually Matters

The criteria evolve with level:

**For Analyst/Associate promotion:**
- Quality and reliability of work product
- Speed of skill development (are you learning faster than average?)
- Constructiveness in team settings
- Client feedback (are you adding value directly or only through your manager?)
- Intellectual contribution (are you surfacing insights, or just processing data?)

**For Engagement Manager promotion:**
- Client satisfaction — do clients want you back?
- Team development — are your analysts learning and producing better work?
- Project economics — did engagements deliver on scope and budget?
- Independent client relationships — can you manage a client relationship without the partner in the room?

**For Partner promotion:**
- Revenue generation — have you brought in new business?
- Client ownership — do clients see you as their advisor, not just the firm's?
- Expertise reputation — are you known for something specific?
- Institutional contribution — are you building the firm's capabilities?

**The feedback trap:** Many consultants focus on doing excellent work and assume promotion follows. At senior levels, it doesn't. Partners are evaluated on business development. If you're excellent at delivery but haven't built a network and haven't had explicit conversations about where to develop your commercial skills, you'll be passed over for promotion — or counseled out — despite being technically superior.

## Building Your Reputation Inside the Firm

At MBB firms, your reputation is built through the informal network as much as the formal evaluation process.

**The "airport test" becomes real:** Would a partner want to be stuck with you for 6 hours in an airport? This is partly about being pleasant but mostly about whether you're interesting, whether you add something to the conversation, whether you're a person worth knowing.

**Intellectual generosity:** Share your frameworks, analyses, and insights with colleagues. Write up what you learned on an engagement in a way others can use. This builds your reputation as someone who contributes to the collective knowledge, not just a resource who delivers on assignments.

**Sponsor vs. mentor:** Mentors give advice. Sponsors advocate for you in rooms you're not in. Actively develop relationships with senior people who can speak to your capabilities when promotion decisions are made. Don't wait for sponsorship to happen organically — be direct: "I'd value your perspective on where I should be developing and whether you'd be willing to be a sounding board."

**The domain build:** The fastest path to differentiation inside a firm is building genuine expertise. Pick an industry and a function. Read everything. Do extra engagements in that area. Get known as "the person to call when we need [X]." Generalists have value early in their career; specialists get promoted and generate business.

## Giving and Receiving Feedback

Feedback culture is what separates top consulting firms from good ones. Most firms have it in structure; far fewer have it in practice.

**Receiving feedback:**
- Seek it proactively after every engagement: "What's one thing I should do differently?"
- Don't be defensive. The right response to critical feedback is: "Thank you. Let me make sure I understand — you're saying [restate]. Is that right?"
- Act on it visibly. "You suggested I was over-qualifying my answers. I've been deliberately working on leading with a more direct recommendation. Have you noticed any change?"
- Feedback that's ignored stops being given.

**Giving feedback:**
- Give it promptly — the further from the event, the less useful
- Be specific — "your slide titles are labels, not messages" beats "your deck needs work"
- Focus on behavior, not character — "the analysis didn't distinguish between correlation and causation" beats "you're not rigorous enough"
- Balance — the ratio should be about 3:1 positive to critical, but the critical feedback needs to be real, not sanitized
- Distinguish performance feedback (for development) from evaluation input (for promotion decisions) — conflating them makes both worse

**The upward feedback challenge:**
Giving honest feedback to people above you is one of the hardest and most valuable skills in consulting. It requires framing it as adding value rather than criticizing. "I want to share a perspective that might be useful as we think about the next phase — the client has been signaling some concerns about the implementation timeline that I don't think are fully captured in our current plan" is very different from "I think the timeline is wrong."

## Managing Your Wellbeing

Consulting has a real cost. Long hours, travel, political dynamics, constantly changing client environments. The people who thrive long-term have strategies for sustainability, not just output.

**Prioritization under pressure:** When everything is urgent, identify the 20% of work that drives 80% of the impact and do that first. Be transparent with your manager when the workload is genuinely unsustainable — overpromising and underdelivering is worse than negotiating scope upfront.

**Recovery as a professional discipline:** The ability to recover between engagements — mentally and physically — is what sustains performance over years, not just months. This means protecting time off, managing energy not just time, and building habits that restore capacity.

**The exit awareness:** Most people who enter consulting will leave within 5-7 years. Many of the best opportunities (industry roles, start-ups, PE, entrepreneurship) come through the network and relationships built during consulting. Maintain those relationships actively. The person you helped on an engagement three years ago may be the CFO of a company you want to work for.

## After Consulting: The "Alumni Effect"

The consulting alumni network is one of the most valuable assets you take with you. Former colleagues go to every sector, every function, and every geography. Maintain these relationships when you're not actively using them — people can tell when you reach out only when you need something.

The most common exits after MBB:
- Corporate strategy / strategic finance at large companies (most common)
- Private equity and growth equity (most competitive — requires top performance and timing)
- Venture capital (rarer, usually via a domain expertise thesis)
- Startups and entrepreneurship (common, especially from associate level)
- Boutique consulting / independent advisory (frequent after partnership-level)
- Government and public sector (meaningful minority, especially from social sector practices)

The skills transfer differently to each path. The analytical rigor and communication skills are universal. The business development skills are most valued in PE and independent advisory. The operational knowledge is most valued in corporate roles.
