# Workshop Facilitation Reference

*For the working session leader. Not theory—just what works.*

---

## 1. Facilitation Is Not Presenting

You're not there to inform. You're there to make a group produce something—a decision, a plan, a diagnosis of a problem.

**The mindset shift:** Your job is not to be the smartest person in the room. It's to make the room collectively smarter and to move the group toward output.

In a presentation, you control the flow and the conclusions. In a working session, you design the conditions for good thinking and let the group do the work. You step in to redirect, clarify, and synthesize—not to solve.

**What this means practically:**
- You don't walk in with "the answer."
- You prepare a structure, not a script.
- You listen more than you talk.
- You make space for disagreement—in fact, you need it.
- Success is a concrete output (a decision, a plan, an insight), not a good feeling in the room.

---

## 2. Design from the Output Backward

Start with: What does success look like at the end of this session? Be specific. Not "align on strategy"—that's too vague. "By 2pm, we've picked the top 3 market opportunities and assigned owners to validate each by month-end."

Once you have the output, work backward:

**Session structure rule of thumb:**
- 50% discussion (open dialogue, problem-solving)
- 30% structured exercises (dot voting, breakouts, issue trees)
- 20% synthesis and wrap (decisions, next steps, red flags)

**Writing an actual agenda** (not a table of contents):

```
9:00–9:15  | Purpose & norms (5 min Q&A)
9:15–9:45  | Market landscape overview (breakouts on 3 regions, 15 min each)
9:45–10:15 | Synthesis of regional insights (dot vote on 5 opportunities, 20 min)
10:15–10:30| Define evaluation criteria for final selection (structured silence, 10 min)
10:30–11:00| Evaluation & decision (15 min debate, 15 min synthesis & next steps)
```

Times matter. Ambiguity kills momentum.

**On pre-reads:** Use them when they genuinely unblock the conversation (e.g., a complex market report everyone needs to reference). Skip them when they're just confidence theater. People don't read them, you build resentment about the prep burden, and the session bogs down in explanation. If you need alignment on baseline facts, lead with a 5-minute read-aloud, not a pre-read.

---

## 3. Opening Well (First 5 Minutes)

You have about 5 minutes to shift people from passive listening mode to active thinking mode.

**What to cover:**

1. **Purpose:** "We're here to..." Be specific. Not "discuss market strategy," but "decide which customer segment we'll target first and what that means for our go-to-market plan."

2. **Stakes:** "Why does this matter?" Connect it to business outcomes or to a decision that's waiting on this. "This feeds into the Q2 business plan we're finalizing next week" or "This shapes whether we hire in this division."

3. **How we'll work:** Set norms fast. "We're going to spend 20 minutes on open discussion, then break into two groups to stress-test two different options, then come back and decide. I'll be calling out time, and I'll interrupt if we get off the rails—that's my job." This permission structure is critical. Without it, people waffle and over-explain.

Do not spend 20 minutes on agenda review. You are not running a board meeting. Get to work.

---

## 4. Core Facilitation Moves

**Parking lots.** Write them visibly (on a board, shared document, or even a sticky note on the wall labeled "Parking Lot"). When someone raises a good point that's off the session's critical path, name it: "That's a real question. Let's put it in the lot so we don't lose it, and we'll circle back if we have time." This captures the tangent, validates the person who raised it, and moves on. At the end: "We parked three things—who owns each?" Assign owners before closing.

**Structured silence.** Before you open discussion on a hard question, give people 3–5 minutes of quiet time to think individually (write on a notepad, whatever). Then go around the room. This breaks the anchor effect, surfaces introverts, and gets better first thoughts. "Let's take 5 minutes to think on this. No talking, just writing." Sounds awkward, works reliably.

**Straw proposals.** Don't ask "What should we do?" Ask "Here's what I think we should do: X. What's wrong with it?" Much faster. People react and refine instead of starting from blank paper. Use it early and often.

**Dot voting / priority stacking.** You have 7 ideas and 20 minutes. Give everyone 3 votes (dots, or just marks on a board). Vote in silence. Tally. Done. "The top 3 are clear. Let's spend time on those." This saves 30 minutes of circular debate.

**Breakouts.** Split into groups of 4–6 to work through one section of the issue tree or to stress-test an option. Assign a reporter who will synthesize in 2 minutes. Time-box ruthlessly (20 min max). Breakouts work when the task is clear and there's a defined output. They fragment the group when people feel lost or when the groups have misaligned charters.

**Synthesis reads.** Every 30 minutes, pause and say: "Here's what I'm hearing: [A], [B], [C]. Are we aligned, or am I missing something?" This consolidates ground, surfaces silent disagreement before it metastasizes, and resets momentum.

## 5. Problem-Solving Sessions

Goal: Work through a complex problem and land on a diagnosis or hypothesis set.

**Structure it as an issue tree.** Top of the tree: "Why is [problem] happening?" Sub-questions below. Each sub-question gets a breakout group or a section of discussion time.

Assign hypotheses, not questions. "We think market adoption is slow because of X. Go prove or disprove it" lands faster than "What's limiting adoption?" Hypotheses push people toward specificity.

**Bring the room back to one point of view without steamrolling:**

After breakouts report back, do a synthesis read. You're looking for: what do all groups agree on? What's the real disagreement? Not all disagreements are worth debating. Some are "If we assume X, then Y" disagreements—you can document both paths and move on. Some are "I fundamentally disagree" disagreements—those need air time.

The key: distinguish signal from noise. "It sounds like groups 1 and 2 are talking about timing, not substance. We can agree on the diagnosis and debate the timeline separately. Is that fair?"

**The test of a real insight:** You walk out of the room saying something you didn't walk in saying. If the session produced a list of things everyone already believed, you didn't do your job.

---

## 6. Decision-Making Sessions

Goal: Move from "What should we do?" to "Here's what we're doing."

**Frame options clearly.** Option A, Option B, Option C. Not strawmen (obviously bad options to make one look better). Real tradeoffs. Each option should be written down. Verbal options disappear into ambiguity.

**Make decision criteria explicit first.** Before you evaluate options, say: "We'll judge these on: speed to market, upfront investment required, and risk of competitive response. Any other criteria?" Get alignment on the scorecard before you score. Otherwise you're arguing from conclusions disguised as analysis.

**Handle the anchor effect.** When a senior person stakes out a position early, the room freezes. Heads nod. Discussion dies. Call it directly: "I heard Sarah lean toward Option B. Before we lock there, let's make sure we've heard the case for A and C. Sarah, hold that thought for a minute." Then do structured silence and dry breakouts on each option. You're creating cover for people to disagree.

**Check for real consensus.** Don't ask "Does everyone agree?" Ask "If we go with this, what might we get wrong? What would make you want to revisit?" Silence is not agreement. Ask again: "Anyone uncomfortable with this?" Make disagreement easy to surface. If someone says "I'm not sure," dig in. "What would make you confident?"

**Land the decision.** "We're going with Option B. Here's what that commits us to: [X, Y, Z]. We'll revisit if [risk] materializes by [date]." Document it. Assign owners for follow-up items.

## 7. Managing Group Dynamics

**The dominant voice.** Interrupts, fills silence, shuts down others. You need her—she's engaged. But she's crowding the room. Use structured silence before open discussion (gives others runway). Redirect: "I want to hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet on this one." Assign her a role: "Can you take on the stress-test of option A?" Keep her energy. Redirect it.

**The senior person who anchors too early.** "I think we should hire in Europe" (said at minute 2). Room locks. You now have to work around her to surface real thinking. Before the meeting: "Walk me through your thinking, and then give the room space to challenge it." In the meeting: structured silence and breakouts before open discussion. If needed: "I want to make sure we've fully tested this before we commit. Let's punch holes in the idea."

**The quiet one with the valuable perspective.** She's not talking because she's still thinking, or she's intimidated, or she disagrees with the senior person. Use structured silence (forces her to write). Call on her by name: "You've worked on this before—what are we missing?" Make it safe to disagree: "We've heard three versions of this. Let's list them and see which assumptions matter most."

**The person with a side agenda.** Wants to relitigate a past decision or steer the conversation to her priority. Acknowledge the agenda. Redirect: "That's important, and we actually need a separate session on that. For now, let's stay with the current scope." If she keeps pulling, address it directly after the meeting. In the meeting, don't make her the villain.

---

## 8. Closing Well

**Last 15 minutes.** This is not optional. The last 15 minutes determine whether you ship a decision or produce a long email later debating what was actually decided.

**Three things to do:**

1. **Synthesis of decisions (not just discussion):** "Here's what we decided: [A], [B], [C]. Here's what we're still debating: [X]." Not "We had a good conversation about market dynamics." That's not a decision.

2. **Clear next steps with owners and dates:** "Sarah owns customer validation by April 15. Raj owns competitive pricing analysis by April 10. James will present both to the leadership team on April 20."

3. **Red flags:** "We agreed on the direction but disagree on timing. We're moving fast. If we're wrong about [risk], we need to pause and reassess." Capturing explicit disagreement or open items means you're not papering over real concerns.

Write it down in the room. Don't send a "recap email" the next day—half of it will be reinterpreted by then. The owner of each item should confirm she actually owns it.

**The test:** Can someone who wasn't in the room understand exactly what was decided and what happens next? If not, you didn't close.

---

## Quick Checklist Before You Facilitate

- [ ] I can state success in one sentence.
- [ ] I have a time-locked agenda.
- [ ] I know the decision criteria (if it's a decision session).
- [ ] I know who needs to speak and why.
- [ ] I have a parking lot ready.
- [ ] I have a way to record decisions (doc, board, or notes).
- [ ] I have 15 minutes reserved for closing.

If you're missing any of these, you're not ready yet.
