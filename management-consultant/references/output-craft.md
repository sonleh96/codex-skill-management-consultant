# Output Craft

The quality of the output is a signal of the quality of the thinking. Client-ready doesn't just mean well-formatted — it means the logic is airtight, the message is clear, and the reader can act on it.

---

## The Universal Standard: Pyramid Principle

All consulting outputs — decks, memos, emails, verbal updates — follow the same underlying structure:

**Conclusion → Key Arguments → Supporting Evidence**

Lead with what you think. Then explain why. Then show the data. This is the opposite of how most people are trained to write (build to conclusion at the end). In consulting, the reader doesn't have time to get to your conclusion — if you haven't told them what you think in the first 30 seconds, you've lost them.

Every output should pass the "So What?" test: if someone reads only the first sentence/title/subject line, do they know the key message? If not, rewrite.

## 1. The Recommendation Deck

This is the main consulting deliverable — a structured presentation that walks a client from the situation to a clear recommendation with an implementation plan.

**The structure that works:**

**Situation** (1-2 slides): Where are we? Establish shared context — the business situation, what's at stake, and why this decision matters now. Don't spend more than 2 slides here. The client already knows their own situation.

**Complication** (1-2 slides): What's changed or what's the challenge? Why can't we keep doing what we're doing? This is the tension that makes the recommendation necessary.

**Key findings** (3-6 slides): What did we learn that's material to the recommendation? Each finding slide has one message — the title IS the finding, stated as a complete sentence. Supporting evidence fills the body. If the title doesn't tell the story on its own, it's a label, not a finding.

**Recommendation** (1-2 slides): What should we do? State it clearly. Give 2-3 options if relevant and explain why you're recommending this one. Don't bury the lead.

**Implementation** (2-4 slides): How do we execute it? Phasing, resource requirements, milestones, key dependencies, risks and mitigations.

**Financials / business case** (1-3 slides): What's the return? Delegate detailed modeling to finance skills, but always include the headline numbers and the key assumptions.

**The slide craft:**
- One message per slide. Not one topic — one message.
- Slide title = the "so what" of the slide, written as a complete sentence
- Body = evidence that supports the title
- If you need a "therefore" to connect the body to the title, the slide is doing too much
- Use "appendix" aggressively — everything that's interesting but not essential belongs there

**Common mistakes:**
- "Data dump" slides: tables and charts with no clear message
- Too many slides (25+ slides for a 1-hour meeting is usually too many)
- Recommendations that are actually just findings ("Revenue is declining" is not a recommendation)
- Implementation plans that are vague ("We will optimize the process" — by how much, by when, who owns it?)

## 2. Executive Summary

The one-pager that a CEO reads in 3 minutes and understands exactly what you're recommending and why.

**Structure:**

```
HEADLINE RECOMMENDATION: [One clear sentence on what to do]

CONTEXT: [2-3 sentences on the situation — why this matters now]

KEY FINDINGS:
• [Finding 1: quantified, specific, points toward the recommendation]
• [Finding 2: same]
• [Finding 3: same]

RECOMMENDATION: [More detail on what to do — 3-5 sentences]

IMPLEMENTATION OVERVIEW:
• Phase 1 (timing): [Key action]
• Phase 2 (timing): [Key action]
• Phase 3 (timing): [Key action]

EXPECTED IMPACT: [Financial or operational outcome, with confidence range]

INVESTMENT REQUIRED: [Cost, time, resources]

KEY RISKS: [1-3 material risks and mitigation]

NEXT STEPS: [Immediate actions with owners and dates]
```

**What makes it good:**
- The recommendation is specific enough that someone could act on it without reading the full deck
- Each finding directly supports the recommendation — there's no "interesting but irrelevant" information
- Numbers wherever possible — "$40M savings opportunity," "18 months to full implementation," not "significant" and "a number of months"
- The risks are real risks, not boilerplate ("execution risk" is not a risk — what specifically could go wrong?)

## 3. Business Case

Used when you need to justify investment — making the financial case for a recommendation.

**The structure:**

**Problem statement:** What's the current situation and what happens if we don't act? (The cost of inaction is often the most powerful argument.)

**Proposed solution:** What are we recommending, in plain language?

**Investment required:**
- One-time costs (implementation, technology, restructuring)
- Ongoing costs (additional headcount, licensing, maintenance)
- Timeline and phasing

**Expected benefits:**
- Revenue upside (quantified — units × price or customers × ARPU)
- Cost savings (quantified — hours × rate or headcount reduction × fully-loaded cost)
- Risk reduction (sometimes this is the primary value and it's harder to quantify — be honest about that)
- Strategic value (market position, capability, optionality — often qualitative but worth naming)

**Financial summary:**
- Payback period (how long until benefits exceed investment)
- NPV (at the relevant discount rate)
- IRR if appropriate
- Sensitivity: What if our assumptions are 20% wrong?

**Key assumptions and risks:**
Be explicit about your assumptions. If you're assuming a 30% productivity improvement, say so — and say what your evidence is. The credibility of the business case depends on the credibility of the assumptions.

**Delegate financial modeling to:** `finance:capital-budgeting` for detailed NPV/IRR, `finance:fp-and-a-forecasting` for multi-year projections.

## 4. Working Session Materials

Working sessions are where consulting gets done — they're how you build alignment and test your thinking with the client in real time.

**The purpose:** A working session is not a presentation. You're not showing conclusions — you're developing them together. The materials should create a structure for discussion, not replace discussion.

**Good working session structure:**

*Objectives and agenda (1 slide):* What are we trying to accomplish in this session? What decisions or alignment do we need to walk out with?

*Where we are (1-2 slides):* Brief context on what's been done so far — findings, hypotheses, working conclusions. Keep this tight.

*The questions to resolve (2-4 slides):* The specific issues you're working through. For each: here's what we know, here's what we're uncertain about, here's what we need from the room.

*Working documents:* Issue trees, strawman frameworks, option sketches — these are tools for discussion, deliberately incomplete, deliberately inviting input.

*Next steps (1 slide):* What did we decide? What are the open items? Who does what by when?

**The facilitation reality:**
The best working sessions feel like a conversation the consultant is guiding, not a presentation they're delivering. If you're talking more than the client in a working session, something is wrong. Your job is to structure the conversation and capture decisions — not to fill the room with your analysis.

## 5. Progress Update / Interim Communication

Regular client communication that keeps the engagement on track and maintains confidence.

**The structure (simple):**

*What we've done:* Work completed since last update. Be specific — not "we analyzed the market" but "we completed the customer segment profitability analysis and conducted 12 interviews with enterprise customers."

*What we found:* Key early findings — frame as "emerging hypotheses" if the work isn't complete. Being transparent about where you are builds trust.

*Where we've updated our thinking:* If you've changed direction or killed a hypothesis, say so. "We initially thought X but the data suggests Y" is a sign of rigorous work, not failure.

*What's coming next:* What you'll be working on before the next update. Keep expectations accurate — don't over-promise.

*Issues and decisions needed:* Anything you need from the client — data, access, decisions — be explicit and specific. Don't let the engagement get stuck because you were polite about an ask.

**The email update version:**

Subject: [Project name] — Week [X] Update

*[Opening sentence: One headline finding or progress statement]*

Completed this week:
- [Item with specific outputs]
- [Item]

Emerging findings:
- [Specific finding with evidence]
- [Specific finding]

Next steps:
- [What you're doing next week]

Needed from client:
- [Specific ask, with deadline]

## 6. The Board / Steering Committee Update

When the audience is the board or a senior steering committee, the standard shifts:

**What's different:**
- Even shorter, even more decisive
- No methodology, no process — only findings and implications
- Board members are testing strategic coherence and risk awareness, not approving the analysis
- Anticipate the questions they'll ask and answer them preemptively

**The format:**
- 5-10 slides maximum for a 30-minute slot
- Situation: 30 seconds of context (they already know the background)
- Recommendation: clear, specific, justified in 2-3 points
- Financial summary: the number that matters and the key assumption it rests on
- Key risks: the 2 things that could go wrong and what you're doing about them
- Ask: what specific approval or direction do you need from the board?

**What boards hate:**
- Long setup before the recommendation
- Equivocal language ("it seems like," "we believe potentially")
- Being presented options when you should have a recommendation
- Not knowing the financials

## What "Not Client-Ready" Looks Like

These are the flags that mean a piece of work needs more thought before it goes to the client:

- Slide titles that are labels, not messages ("Revenue Analysis" vs. "Revenue growth has stalled in core markets")
- Conclusions that aren't supported by the evidence in the deck
- Recommendations that can't be implemented in the client's context
- Numbers that haven't been sense-checked (is a 40% cost reduction realistic in this industry?)
- Language that says "we" did analysis without stating what we found
- Too much appendix in the main body (keep only what's essential to the argument)
- A recommendation the client's CFO can't defend to the board
- "TBD" in a key implementation milestone without a plan for resolving it
