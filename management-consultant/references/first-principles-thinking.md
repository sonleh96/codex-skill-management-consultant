# First-Principles Thinking

Most business thinking is analogical: "This is like what worked at Company X, so let's do that." First-principles thinking strips away analogies and asks: what do we actually know to be true, and what can we build from there?

It's not always the right tool — sometimes analogy is faster and good enough. But when the problem is genuinely novel, when conventional wisdom has failed, or when you suspect the industry is solving the wrong problem, first principles is the lens that creates breakthrough.

---

## What First-Principles Thinking Actually Is

**The Socratic definition:** Break a claim or assumption down to its most foundational truths, then reason up from there. Stop when you reach something that is true by definition or by direct evidence — not by convention, precedent, or assumption.

**The consultant's version:** Separate what we know from what we assume. What would we build if we had no legacy systems, no prior decisions, and no existing contracts? What does the evidence actually support, stripped of narrative?

**What it is not:**
- Contrarianism for its own sake
- Ignoring relevant precedent (analogical thinking has real value — don't waste it)
- An excuse for analysis paralysis ("We need to understand everything from first principles before we can decide anything")

---

## The Three-Part Protocol

**Step 1: State the assumption explicitly**

Most bad business thinking is invisible — people act on assumptions they've never articulated. The first step is making the assumption visible.

Examples of invisible assumptions:
- "Our margins are constrained by industry structure" (has anyone actually tested this?)
- "We need to hire 100 people to launch this product" (why 100? built from what calculation?)
- "We can't compete with Competitor X on price" (what is their actual cost structure?)

The question that surfaces assumptions: "Why do we believe that?"

**Step 2: Test the assumption against evidence**

For each assumption, ask:
- What would we see in the data if this assumption were true? Is that what we actually see?
- Who else has tested this assumption and what did they find?
- Is this assumption true in other industries or geographies? If so, why? If not, why not?
- What's the strongest case that this assumption is wrong?

**Step 3: Rebuild from what survives**

After stripping away unvalidated assumptions, what do you actually know? Build your analysis and recommendation from that foundation.

## Applied Examples

**Cost structure challenge:**
Assumption: "Our cost per unit is $X, which is competitive."
First-principles question: What does it actually cost to produce one unit, broken into raw materials, labor, energy, and overhead? What would each component cost if we redesigned the process from scratch? Where are we carrying costs that are legacy rather than necessary?

This often reveals that "competitive" is defined against other incumbents who've inherited the same inefficiencies — not against what's actually achievable.

**Market sizing:**
Assumption: "The market is worth $50B based on analyst reports."
First-principles question: $50B for whom, doing what, in what geography? Build the market from constituent pieces: how many potential buyers, at what transaction frequency, at what average price, for which specific applications? Often you find the analyst's number is either much larger (they included adjacent markets you can't access) or smaller (they excluded constraints you face).

**Pricing:**
Assumption: "We can't raise prices because customers will switch."
First-principles question: What do customers actually pay for alternatives? What is the switching cost — in time, integration effort, retraining, contract penalties? What share of their total cost does our product represent? Are there segments where our value delivery far exceeds our price?

The first-principles analysis often reveals that "customers will switch" is an unchallenged fear, not a tested hypothesis.

## The Five "Why?" Protocol

One of the most reliable tools for reaching first principles. When someone states a claim or problem, ask "why?" repeatedly until you reach a root cause or a foundational assumption.

**Example:**
"Our customer satisfaction scores are declining."
→ Why? "Customers are complaining about support response times."
→ Why? "Support team is understaffed for ticket volume."
→ Why? "Volume doubled after the product launch but headcount didn't scale."
→ Why? "Finance didn't approve the headcount request."
→ Why? "The business case didn't account for the support cost of new product complexity."

At level 5, you've found the actual problem: the product launch process doesn't model the ongoing support cost of complexity. Every fix upstream (hire more support staff, improve training, reduce tickets) is addressing symptoms. The root cause is structural — and the fix is in how you launch products.

The protocol breaks down when people answer "why?" with rationalizations rather than causes. Push: "What evidence do we have for that?"

## Challenging Conventional Wisdom

Industries accumulate assumptions that were once valid and no longer are. First-principles thinking is how you find them.

**The checklist:**
- "This is how it's done in our industry" → Why? Who started doing it this way? Does the original reason still apply?
- "Customers expect [X]" → How do we know? When was this last tested? Have you asked customers who chose differently?
- "It would be too expensive to [Y]" → Expensive compared to what? Have you built the cost model from scratch or inherited an estimate?
- "We tried this before and it didn't work" → What specifically failed? In what conditions? Have those conditions changed?

The highest-value application: finding the assumption that an entire industry shares that happens to be wrong. These create the biggest competitive opportunities — because everyone is optimizing around the same constraints, which creates an opening for anyone willing to challenge them.

## When to Use It (And When Not To)

**Use first-principles when:**
- Conventional approaches have consistently failed
- The problem feels intractable within existing constraints
- You suspect the industry has solved the wrong problem
- Major capital is being committed based on inherited assumptions
- You're designing something genuinely novel

**Don't use first-principles when:**
- Time is short and analogical thinking is good enough
- The conventional approach has a strong track record in highly similar situations
- You're dealing with well-understood, stable systems (accounting rules, physical laws, legal requirements)
- The risk of being wrong is very high and analogical validation is available

**The synthesis:** Start with first-principles to test the foundational assumptions. Then use analogical thinking to identify what's been proven to work. The best consulting combines both: rigor about what's actually known, combined with practical wisdom about what's been proven elsewhere.
