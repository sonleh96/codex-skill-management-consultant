## Case Interview Formats by Firm

### McKinsey
- **Format**: Interviewer-led (the interviewer drives with questions; candidate doesn't present a full structure upfront)
- **Style**: More conversational; quicker pivots; more math/data
- **PEI**: Problem Solving (PSI) + Personal Experience Interview (3 competencies: leadership, personal impact, entrepreneurship)
- **Written test**: Problem Solving Test (PST) or solve — replaced by McKinsey Digital Assessment (Imbellus game) at some offices
- **Tip**: McKinsey interviewers reward candidates who form and test hypotheses quickly; they dislike candidates who structure forever before answering

### BCG
- **Format**: Candidate-led (candidate presents their structure, then drives the case)
- **Style**: More room for creative thinking; tolerates more exploration
- **Written component**: Varies by office; some have BCG Potential Test
- **Tip**: BCG values original insight; they reward candidates who synthesize well and come to creative recommendations

### Bain
- **Format**: Candidate-led, similar to BCG
- **Style**: More collaborative tone; Bain interviewers give more hints
- **PEI**: Strong emphasis on "Bain fit" — the culture is collegial and team-oriented
- **Tip**: Bain likes candidates who are enthusiastic, collaborative, and don't over-structure

### Tier 2 (Deloitte, Oliver Wyman, Roland Berger, A.T. Kearney, L.E.K.)
- Generally follow candidate-led format
- Often have an industry or functional focus that shows in the case
- Some use written case formats (read an exhibit deck, present recommendations)

## The Candidate's Playbook

### The 7-Step Case Framework

**Step 1: Take Notes and Clarify (30-60 sec)**
- Write down everything the interviewer says
- Ask 1-2 clarifying questions before structuring (not more)
- Clarify: What's the company? What's the metric that defines success? Any constraints?

**Step 2: Pause and Structure (1-2 min)**
- Say "I'd like to take a moment to structure my thoughts"
- Build your issue tree in silence — don't think out loud at this stage
- Choose a framework OR build a custom structure

**Step 3: Present Your Structure**
- State your governing hypothesis first: "My initial hypothesis is that the profit decline is driven by cost increases rather than revenue, because the market is still growing."
- Then walk through your issue tree: "To test this, I'd want to explore three areas: first... second... third..."
- Ask for the interviewer's reaction: "Does this approach make sense, or are there areas you'd like me to focus on?"

**Step 4: Prioritize and Dive In**
- Don't try to cover everything — pick the most important branch
- State your prioritization logic: "Given [X], I'd start with the cost side because..."
- As you learn information, update your hypothesis

**Step 5: Analyze Exhibits and Data**
- When shown a chart/table: State what type of data it is → identify the key insight → connect it to the hypothesis
- Don't describe the data ("the table shows revenues by quarter") — synthesize it ("revenues declined in Q3, suggesting the issue started in September, which aligns with the competitor's product launch")

**Step 6: Do the Math**
- Be calm and structured: write it down, state your approach, then calculate
- Round numbers for speed and simplicity
- Always sanity check your answer and state the implication

**Step 7: Synthesize and Recommend**
- Lead with the answer: "Based on my analysis, I recommend X"
- 2-3 supporting reasons
- Risks and mitigations
- Next steps

### Communication Principles for Cases
- **Signpost constantly**: "There are three things I want to cover: first, second, third. Starting with first..."
- **Transition with synthesis**: Before moving to the next branch, summarize: "So what this tells us is..."
- **Think out loud during analysis, not during structuring**: Interviewers want to see your analytical process, but a chaotic structure is a red flag
- **Ask for data proactively**: "To test this hypothesis, I'd want to know X. Do you have that?"
- **React to new information**: When you get a data point, explicitly update your thinking: "That's interesting — that changes my hypothesis because..."

## Case Types and Opening Structures

### Profitability Decline
**Opening**: "To diagnose the profit decline, I'll look at two areas: revenue and costs. On the revenue side, I'll examine volume and price. On the cost side, fixed and variable. Given that the market has been growing, my initial hypothesis is this is a competitive/market share issue on the revenue side. Let me start there."

**Don't say**: "I'll use the profitability framework..." (sounds robotic)
**Do say**: Build the framework as if you're reasoning from first principles

### Market Entry
**Opening**: "To evaluate whether to enter this market, I want to look at three questions: First, is the market attractive enough to justify entry? Second, do we have the right to win — some competitive advantage over incumbents? And third, what's the best entry strategy if we decide to go? My initial lean is toward entry because [reason], but let me stress-test that."

### M&A / Acquisition
**Opening**: "I'd approach this from three angles: strategic rationale, standalone and synergy value, and integration risks. The key question is whether the synergies are real and achievable enough to justify the premium. Let me start with strategic rationale — why does this acquisition make sense for us?"

### Pricing
**Opening**: "To set the right price, I'd think about three things: the floor (our costs), the ceiling (customer willingness to pay), and the competitive reference point. Value-based pricing — anchoring to what the customer gets — usually creates the most value. Can you tell me more about what problem this product solves for the customer?"

### New Product Launch / Go-to-Market
**Opening**: "I'd structure this as: does the product have real market fit (is there a customer need and are we the best solution?), what's the go-to-market model (how do we reach customers and at what unit economics?), and what's the launch sequencing to maximize early traction?"

### Growth Strategy
**Opening**: "To identify growth opportunities, I'll use the Ansoff framework as a lens: growing the core with existing products and customers, expanding into adjacent markets or segments, and developing new products. Given you've said the core is growing at 3% but targets are 10%, the gap suggests we need at least one significant adjacent move."

## Math and Estimation in Cases

### The Consulting Math Protocol
1. **State your approach**: "I'm going to calculate X as A × B"
2. **Write it down** — never do complex math in your head without writing
3. **Round aggressively** — 52 weeks → 50 weeks; $47M → ~$50M
4. **Calculate step by step** — show your work
5. **Sanity check** — "Does this feel right?"
6. **State the implication** — the number means nothing alone

### Quick Math Techniques

**Percentage of X:**
- 10% of X: Move decimal one place (10% of 320 = 32)
- 25% of X: X/4
- 33% of X: X/3
- 15% of X: 10% + 5% (10% of 320 = 32; 5% = 16; total = 48)

**Multiplying large numbers:**
- Break into parts: 320 × 45 = 320 × 40 + 320 × 5 = 12,800 + 1,600 = 14,400
- Round first: 320 × 45 ≈ 300 × 45 = 13,500 (close enough for case math)

**CAGR quick estimates:**
- 7% annual growth doubles in ~10 years (Rule of 72: 72/7 = ~10)
- 10% annual growth doubles in ~7 years
- 15% annual growth doubles in ~5 years

**Unit conversions to memorize:**
- 1M seconds ≈ 11.5 days
- 1B dollars / 330M Americans ≈ $3 per person
- 1% of US population ≈ 3.3M people

### Market Sizing in Cases
Follow the same approach as `references/guesstimation.md`. In case interviews specifically:
- State your approach (top-down or bottom-up) and why
- Walk through each assumption explicitly
- Accept coaching gracefully: "That's a good point — let me revise that assumption to X"
- Always round to one significant figure for the final answer ("approximately $5 billion")

## Behavioral / Fit Interviews (PEI)

### McKinsey PEI: Three Competencies

**1. Personal Impact / Influence Without Authority**
They want: You changed someone's mind or drove an outcome through persuasion, not authority.
Structure: Situation → Who you needed to influence → Your approach → Result (quantified if possible)

**2. Entrepreneurial Drive / Ownership**
They want: You identified an opportunity, drove it forward through ambiguity, didn't wait for permission.
Structure: The gap you identified → What you did despite obstacles → What you built → Impact

**3. Leadership / Team Impact**
They want: You made the team better, not just yourself. Specific people you developed.
Structure: The team situation → Your role → What you specifically did for team members → Team outcome

### The STAR Structure
Every behavioral answer: **Situation → Task → Action → Result**

But MBB interviewers push beyond STAR. They want:
- **Specificity**: Which meeting? What date? What exactly did you say?
- **Your role**: What did YOU do, not "the team"?
- **The obstacles**: What almost stopped you?
- **Quantified impact**: Not "improved significantly" but "reduced time by 40%"

### Common PEI Questions

**McKinsey-style:**
- "Tell me about a time you influenced a senior stakeholder who initially disagreed with you."
- "Describe a time you started something from scratch without being asked."
- "Tell me about when you made a team member more effective."

**Bain-style:**
- "Why consulting? Why Bain specifically?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to make a tough decision under uncertainty."
- "Describe your greatest achievement — why does it make you proud?"

**BCG-style:**
- "Tell me about a time you had to solve a completely novel problem."
- "When did you have to push back on a senior person — how did you handle it?"

### Preparation Protocol
Prepare 5-8 stories that cover multiple competencies. Each story should be:
- Specific and recent (last 3-5 years ideally)
- Driven primarily by YOU, not the team
- Quantified wherever possible
- Structured (Situation → Action → Result)
- ~2 minutes when told at a natural pace

## Scoring Rubric

Use this when playing the role of interviewer or when self-evaluating:

### Structure (1-4)
- **4**: Structure is MECE, custom to the problem, hypothesis-led, clearly communicated
- **3**: Structure is solid but slightly generic or has minor gaps
- **2**: Framework is applied but doesn't fully fit the problem; no hypothesis
- **1**: Disorganized, overlapping, or missing major components

### Problem-Solving (1-4)
- **4**: Prioritizes smartly, drills in the right place, updates hypothesis based on evidence, creative insight
- **3**: Sound approach, reasonable prioritization, follows evidence
- **2**: Doesn't prioritize well, goes through motions without driving to insight
- **1**: Random or unfocused analysis; doesn't synthesize

### Quantitative Skills (1-4)
- **4**: Confident mental math, accurate, well-structured calculations, derives implications
- **3**: Mostly accurate with minor errors, corrects promptly when flagged
- **2**: Slow or needs help; doesn't connect numbers to business insight
- **1**: Significant errors, can't recover, avoids quant

### Communication (1-4)
- **4**: Clear signposting, concise, synthesis-first, executive-ready tone
- **3**: Generally clear with occasional rambling or filler language
- **2**: Hard to follow; buries the insight; too verbose
- **1**: Confusing or incoherent; no clear narrative

### Recommendation (1-4)
- **4**: Crisp governing thought, 3 supporting reasons, risks, next steps — delivered confidently
- **3**: Clear recommendation with supporting logic
- **2**: Wishy-washy; hedges; doesn't lead with the answer
- **1**: No recommendation; summarizes analysis without synthesizing

**Pass threshold**: Average ≥ 3.0 across all dimensions, no single dimension below 2.

## Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

| Mistake | What It Looks Like | Fix |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| Framework fetishism | "I'll use Porter's Five Forces..." then fits the problem to the framework | Build structure from the problem, not a textbook |
| No hypothesis | Dives into analysis without a point of view | State an initial hypothesis before structuring |
| Boiling the ocean | Tries to analyze every branch of the issue tree | Explicitly prioritize: "The highest-impact branch is X because..." |
| Summary instead of synthesis | "So revenues went down and costs went up" | "Which means the real problem is Y, and the fix is Z" |
| Math panic | Goes silent, erases repeatedly, gives up | "Let me write this out step by step" — paper always |
| Weak recommendation | "It depends... there are pros and cons..." | "My recommendation is X. I want to caveat two risks, but the base case points to X." |
| Ignoring hints | Interviewer redirects and candidate doesn't notice | Explicitly acknowledge: "That's a good point — let me adjust my focus to..." |
| Speaking before thinking | Thinks out loud through structuring, sounds chaotic | "Give me a moment to structure my thoughts" — then present, don't ramble |

## Practice Cases

### Case 1: The Falling Star (Profitability)

**Prompt**: "StarBev is a $800M specialty beverage company. Their EBITDA margin has fallen from 22% to 13% over the past 3 years. The CEO has hired your team to diagnose the issue and build a turnaround plan. Where do you start?"

**Key information to reveal progressively:**
- Revenue grew 4% (so it's not a revenue problem at the top line)
- But premium SKU mix declined from 60% to 45% (mix shift)
- COGS/unit up 18% due to packaging and raw material inflation
- Pricing only raised 6% over the same period
- Competitors raised prices 14% — StarBev under-reacted

**The answer**: Margin erosion is 70% cost-driven (input inflation + scale deleverage) and 30% mix-driven (shift to lower-margin standard products). The fix: pricing catch-up (+8-10pp more needed), premium SKU reinvestment, and procurement consolidation.

---

### Case 2: New Frontier (Market Entry)

**Prompt**: "FitWear is a $1.2B European athletic apparel brand. They're considering entering the US market. Should they?"

**Key information to reveal:**
- US athletic apparel market: ~$70B, growing 7% annually
- Dominated by Nike, Adidas, Lululemon, Under Armour
- FitWear's differentiation: premium sustainable materials, European design aesthetic
- Potential US entry cost: €40-60M over 3 years
- FitWear has an existing partnership with a US retailer (REI willing to stock them)

**The answer**: Cautious yes — enter via REI partnership first (lowest capital, validates demand), targeting premium/sustainability-conscious segment where incumbents are weakest. Go direct-to-consumer online within 18 months if REI sells through. Set kill criteria at 18-month revenue threshold.

---

### Case 3: The Big Question (Market Sizing)

**Prompt**: "A private equity firm is considering acquiring a leading pet insurance company. They've asked your team to size the addressable US pet insurance market."

**Walk through:**
- US pets: ~90M dogs, ~94M cats
- Insured rate: ~4% dogs, ~1% cats (vs. 50%+ in UK — big growth opportunity)
- Average annual premium: ~$600 dogs, ~$350 cats
- Market today: ~3.6M + 0.94M = ~4.5M insured pets
- Annual premium revenue: (3.6M × $600) + (0.94M × $350) = $2.16B + $0.33B = ~$2.5B
- Growth potential: If US reaches 25% penetration (still below UK), market = ~$16B+

**The answer**: Current market ~$2.5B, growing 20%+ annually from low base. Significant runway to $15-20B at maturity. PE thesis: ride penetration growth + brand consolidation.

## Running Cases as an Interviewer

When playing the interviewer role, follow these guidelines:

### Setup
- Present the prompt once, clearly
- After the candidate asks clarifying questions, answer them briefly
- Don't volunteer information they don't ask for (unless they're stuck)

### During the Case
- Let the candidate drive; don't interrupt their structure unless it's seriously off
- Redirect with questions, not answers: "That's interesting — but what would drive that?" not "Actually, it's because of X"
- Push back on unsupported claims: "Why do you think that's the most likely explanation?"
- Give data when asked; use the exhibits prepared in the practice case

### Math Moments
- Don't help unless they're stuck for more than 60 seconds
- A hint is fine: "You might want to check your unit conversion"
- Give a pass on small arithmetic errors if the approach is right

### The Recommendation
- Let the candidate recommend without interruption
- Then push back: "What if the CEO says we don't have the capital for that?"
- Test: "What's the one thing that could make you change your recommendation?"

### Feedback Protocol (After the Case)
Always give structured feedback:
1. What they did well (be specific — not "good job" but "your hypothesis-first approach was strong")
2. The one most impactful thing to work on
3. A drill or exercise to practice that specific thing
