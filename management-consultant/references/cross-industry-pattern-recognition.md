## 2. The Pattern Transfer Methodology

**Step 1: Abstract the Pattern**
Strip away sector-specific details. The pattern isn't "airlines use dynamic pricing based on booking curves." The pattern is "when capacity is fixed, perishable, and heterogeneous, price discrimination across customer segments and timing windows creates revenue capture." That principle exists everywhere: hotels, SaaS seat pricing, healthcare imaging slots.

**Step 2: Validate Applicability**
Check structural similarity. Industrial equipment has different constraints than SaaS (upfront CapEx, long sales cycles, installed base lock-in). But the margin structure question—where do we have pricing power?—is identical. Healthcare supply chains have just-in-time dynamics like automotive, but with regulatory constraints that aerospace invented the solutions for.

**Step 3: Adapt Implementation**
This is where most consultants fail. They say "Netflix did subscriptions" and expect your industrial equipment client to launch a subscription model unchanged. No. You're adapting the pattern to 5-year equipment lifecycles, capital budgets, and installed base service revenue. The subscription *principle* transfers. The execution gets completely rewritten.

**Step 4: Pressure-Test with Domain Experts**
Never present a cross-industry pattern to a client without testing it with someone who actually knows that industry. Call an expert network contact. Your 15 minutes of "wait, would this actually work here?" prevents a meeting where you get credibility-shattered by a client who spots the flaw you missed.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Over-analogizing: "Media went digital, so your healthcare revenue model should too." No. Healthcare has different unit economics, regulation, and customer decision cycles.
- Ignoring structural differences: Subscription works in SaaS because switching costs are low. Applying it to manufacturing equipment with high switching costs requires a different model.
- Cultural blindshots: The pattern works technically but violates how the industry actually operates. You need local credibility, not just logic.

## 3. Catalog of High-Value Cross-Industry Patterns

**Pricing Patterns**
- Dynamic pricing: Airlines perfected it in the 1990s. Hotels adopted it by 2005. SaaS seat pricing uses identical algorithms. Now healthcare is learning it can vary procedure pricing. One systematic skill; infinite applications.
- Subscription models: Music streaming → enterprise software → manufacturing (Caterpillar now charges per operating hour). Every industry with usage heterogeneity needs this.

**Supply Chain Patterns**
- Just-in-time: Invented in automotive. Retail applied it (Zara's micro-supply chain). Healthcare adopted it (and discovered its fragility during COVID). Aerospace uses ultra-precise versions for parts worth $50K each.
- Safety stock optimization: Aerospace learned to model low-probability, high-consequence disruptions. Pharma applied it post-COVID. Financial services uses it for operational liquidity.

**Operating Model Patterns**
- Platform economics: Tech's playbook (Amazon Marketplace) became fintech's (PayPal → Stripe), then agriculture's (John Deere equipment data platform). The principle: create the infrastructure others build on.
- Franchise models: QSR mastered it. Now you see it in professional services (recruiting networks), healthcare (urgent care franchises), and light industrial (inspection networks).

**Risk Management Patterns**
- Aerospace safety culture → healthcare patient safety: Checklists, near-miss reporting, blameless post-mortems. One ecosystem developed it; another ecosystem desperately needed it.
- Financial risk modeling → operational risk: Value-at-Risk calculations migrated from banking to supply chain disruption modeling. Same mathematics; different asset.

**Customer Experience Patterns**
- Hospitality personalization (Ritz-Carlton employee empowerment) → banking (wealth management) → healthcare (concierge primary care). High-touch, individual attention, discretionary decision-making.
- Retail personalization → B2B: Sephora's personalization engine became the template for enterprise software recommendation engines.

**Digital Transformation Patterns**
- Media's digitization playbook (audience, monetization, measurement) → insurance (how do you capture the insurance customer in digital, not just legacy adjudication?).
- Financial services went digital 20 years ago. Now legacy manufacturers are asking "how did they do it?" The pattern: core system modernization is the constraint, not customer interface.

**Cost Transformation Patterns**
- Lean manufacturing (Toyota) → service operations (healthcare, accounting) → back-office (finance shared services). Variation reduction, process standardization, measurement discipline.

**Talent Patterns**
- Professional sports analytics (Moneyball) → corporate talent management: How do you find undervalued talent? How do you predict performance? The statistical framework transferred.

## 4. Building Your Personal Pattern Library

**The Engagement Debrief Habit**
At the end of every project, extract 2–3 transferable patterns. Not "we fixed their supply chain," but "we discovered that their inventory turns were 40% below industry because they lacked demand signal visibility—the same constraint I saw in healthcare. The solution was different (healthcare has patient volume forecasting; this client has point-of-sale data), but the pattern was identical." Write it down. One sentence. You'll use it in 18 months.

**Categorization System**
Organize patterns by: problem type (margin compression, scale challenges, digital disruption), industry dynamic (shift from product to service, buyer consolidation), and organizational lever (pricing, supply chain, operating model, talent). Cross-index them. You'll start seeing patterns in the patterns.

**Converting Patterns to Frameworks**
When you've seen the same pattern transfer three times, turn it into a reusable framework. "Margin compression through commoditization" shows up in telecom, enterprise software, and autoparts. Turn it into a decision tree: (1) Is the commoditization driven by excess supply or customer power? (2) What's the differentiation pathway? (3) What's the time runway? That's a framework.

**Pattern Recognition vs. Lazy Analogizing**
Pattern recognition is systematic. Lazy analogizing is "Netflix did this, so you should too." Know the difference. Pressure-test it. Document the constraint mapping that makes the pattern relevant.

## 5. Deploying Patterns in Client Conversations

**The Power Phrase**
"In [industry], we encountered a similar dynamic where [problem]. Here's what happened, and here's what we learned." This positions you as bringing exterior knowledge, not claiming expertise you don't have.

Example: "In hospitality, we found that their revenue management systems captured pricing power they didn't know they had. In your case [manufacturing], the equivalent constraint isn't room pricing—it's service contract bundling. Let me show you what we're seeing in your data."

**When to Reveal the Source Industry**
If the pattern is very analogous (airline pricing → SaaS pricing), name it. If the pattern is conceptual (supply chain risk → financial risk), you don't need to. If you're not sure, don't name it. Ambiguity is better than credibility damage.

**Managing Skepticism**
Clients say "but our industry is different." Of course it is. Your job is to say "you're absolutely right. Here's why the pattern still applies to *this* problem: [constraint mapping]. And here's what's different about implementation: [adaptation specifics]."

**Benchmarking as a Conversation Starter**
"I've seen this metric in five other industries. Here's how they range: [data]. Where you sit in that range tells me something." Benchmarking from cross-industry data is credibility gold. Consultants with deep bench networks own the conversation.

## 6. The 48-Hour Industry Immersion Protocol

You're staffed on a new sector Monday. You need working fluency by Wednesday. Here's how.

**The Five Universal Business Questions** (from universal-business-analysis):
1. How does money flow through this industry? (Revenue model, margin structure, customer power)
2. What's actually constraining profitability? (Supply, demand, capital, regulation, technology)
3. Who's winning and why? (Competitive positioning, business model edges)
4. What's changing? (Technology disruption, regulatory, macro, buyer consolidation)
5. Where are the highest-ROI levers for our client? (Given their position and constraints)

Answer these five questions deeply, and you'll out-prepare 80% of the room.

**Information Sources (Priority Order)**
- 10-K filings of the top 3–5 public competitors: 2 hours for margin structure, competitive positioning, risk factors
- Analyst reports (equity research, industry databases): 1 hour for macro dynamics and disruption trends
- Trade publications (last 12 months, search for client's name): 1 hour for industry dynamics, regulatory changes, competitive positioning
- Expert network calls (schedule one pre-kickoff): 30 minutes with someone who actually works in the space

**Building Your Sector Cheat Sheet**
Create a one-page document: (1) Key metrics and how your client compares, (2) Competitive positioning of top 5 players, (3) Key regulatory/macro headwinds, (4) Most obvious high-leverage opportunities. You'll reference it constantly during the kickoff.
