# Industry Intelligence: Quick Reference for Fast Orientation

A consultant's cheat sheet for getting useful context in any industry. The patterns repeat; recognizing them saves weeks.

---

## 1. FINANCIAL SERVICES

### Economics
**Banking:** Net interest margin (NIM) + fee income = return profile. Leverage extends returns but compresses margins. Credit losses destroy profitability faster than anything else. Deposit funding is the foundation; wholesale funding is fragile.

**Insurance:** Underwriting income + investment income = total return. Expense ratio is critical (claims handling, distribution, admin). Catastrophe exposure creates earnings volatility. Policyholder behavior (lapse rates, claims inflation) drives everything.

**Asset Management:** Assets under management (AUM) × fee rate - cost base = profit. Fee compression is structural (passive eating active). Redemption risk in downturns. Talent flight when incentives misalign with performance.

### Key Metrics
- **Banking:** NIM, cost-to-income ratio, non-performing loan (NPL) ratio, return on risk-weighted assets (RORWA), loan loss provisions
- **Insurance:** Loss ratio, expense ratio, combined ratio (>100% = underwriting loss), solvency ratio, premium growth
- **Asset Mgmt:** Fee rate (bps), net flows, AUM, investment performance (alpha), employee productivity (revenue per employee)

### Structural Dynamics
Banking is capital-intensive and regulated; compliance cost is massive. Institutional vs. retail creates channel economics. Credit cycles matter more than market cycles. Insurance is about risk pooling and capital efficiency; distribution channels determine profitability. Asset management is talent-driven and hit-based (concentration in top performers).

Buyer power is extreme in all three: institutional clients negotiate fees down 30-50%, depositors can leave instantly, policyholders shop on price.

### Common Problems
- Fee compression driving margin pressure and forced cost cuts
- Technology infrastructure legacy (core banking systems 30+ years old)
- Talent retention in competitive, bonus-dependent environment
- Regulatory change (capital requirements, lending restrictions, data privacy)

### What's Different
Regulatory review is part of every analysis. Board risk committees slow decision-making. Compensation is heavily weighted toward incentives (can misalign with long-term value). Client relationships are long-duration but price-sensitive.

### Key Traps
- Assuming NIM compression is permanent (it cycles with rates)
- Underestimating regulatory constraint as an actual constraint (it is)
- Using historical volatility to predict future returns (tails are fatter than models suggest)
- Treating insurance underwriting as a commodity (brand and claims service matter more than price)

## 2. HEALTHCARE

### Economics
**Payers:** Premium revenue - claims expense - operating cost = margin. Medical loss ratio (MLR) determines profitability. Scale drives risk management efficiency. Margin is tiny (~3-5% for health plans); volume is the game.

**Providers:** Procedure revenue - labor cost - overhead - payer discounts = margin. Utilization and length of stay drive economics more than price. Capital intensity (infrastructure, equipment). Payer mix (Medicare/Medicaid pays less; commercial pays more) determines profitability.

**Pharma:** R&D is 10-15% of revenue; most drugs fail. Patent cliffs destroy revenue overnight. Pricing power exists but is politically fragile. Manufacturing at scale is profitable.

### Key Metrics
- **Payers:** Medical loss ratio, enrollment growth, premium PMPM (per member per month), pharmacy spend, admin cost ratio
- **Providers:** Case volume, average revenue per case (ARPC), length of stay, re-admission rate, cost per case, payer mix
- **Pharma:** Pipeline stage-gate success rates, peak sales forecasts, patent expiration timeline, R&D productivity, drug pricing per unit

### Structural Dynamics
Payers have buyer power over providers but are priced by self-insured employers and regulated by government. Providers have some pricing power for specialized services but are commoditized on routine care. Pharma has pricing power until patent loss, then generic competition eliminates it.

Consolidation is ongoing (fewer large payers, fewer hospital systems). Margin pressure is structural. Regulatory complexity is extreme.

### Common Problems
- Prior authorization burden (administrative cost, care delays)
- Payer-provider disputes over claims and contracting
- Pharma patent cliff and pipeline productivity failures
- Cost inflation outpacing revenue (labor, pharmacy, medical devices)

### What's Different
Regulatory approval (FDA, CMS coverage decisions) is a hard constraint. Clinical evidence matters differently than other industries. Conversation is about access, equity, and cost, not just profit. Stakeholder complexity is high (doctors, nurses, patients, employers, government).

### Key Traps
- Assuming margins can expand sustainably in insurance (regulatory pressure + competition prevent it)
- Underestimating operational complexity of hospital integration (different cultures, IT systems)
- Believing pharma pricing is purely rational (it's political; pricing power expires)
- Thinking cost reduction is mainly clinical (it's structural in payer-provider economics)

## 3. TECHNOLOGY / SaaS (B2B Focus)

### Economics
Revenue is recurring (annual contracts, multi-year). Customer acquisition cost (CAC) vs. lifetime value (LTV) determines unit economics. Gross margin is high (typically 70%+) but R&D and sales are expensive. Operating leverage comes at scale (unit economics improve as you grow).

Rule of thumb: LTV/CAC should be 3:1+. Payback period should be <12 months. Magic number (annual recurring revenue growth rate / CAC in %) should be >0.75.

### Key Metrics
- Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) and annual recurring revenue (ARR), growth rate
- Customer acquisition cost (fully loaded), lifetime value, payback period
- Net retention rate (revenue from existing customers, YoY — >100% = expansion revenue)
- Churn rate (monthly, cohort-based), customer concentration
- Rule of 40 (growth rate + profit margin > 40%)

### Structural Dynamics
Product-market fit is the gating factor (can't sell what customers don't want). Sales model determines unit economics (self-serve is lower CAC but lower ASP; enterprise is higher CAC, higher LTV). Switching costs matter; if they're low, churn is your constraint.

Market consolidation is typical (few market leaders, many small players, few middle). Competition is on product velocity and efficiency, not pricing.

### Common Problems
- Excessive customer concentration (largest 3 customers = >50% revenue)
- Churn acceleration (product-market fit was only in narrow use case)
- CAC inflation (crowded market, rising sales cost)
- Inability to land/expand (land small, expand is the playbook; if you can't expand, you can't scale)

### What's Different
Investors, not customers, set the agenda (growth at all costs → profitability flip). Board meetings are fast and growth-focused. Talent is expensive and competitive. Financial modeling is more important than the P&L (future value dominates). Technical debt accumulates fast as teams scale.

### Key Traps
- Confusing ARR growth with health (can grow revenue by losing profitable customers)
- Optimizing for new customer acquisition at expense of retention (net retention is destiny)
- Underestimating switching costs friction (takes longer than you expect for customers to adopt)
- Assuming competitive advantage is the product (often it's distribution, brand, or integration depth)

## 4. CONSUMER GOODS & RETAIL

### Economics
**CPG:** Margin = (wholesale price - COGS) × volume. Volume is sensitive to promotion and shelf space. Distribution is the constraint (getting into stores is expensive; scaling distribution is the real work). Retailer power is extreme (concentrated buyers).

**Retail:** Margin = (retail price - landed cost) × volume × store productivity. Inventory turns drive return on capital. Lease obligations are long-duration. Fixed store cost pressures margin in downturns.

**DTC:** Margin = (price - COGS - CAC - fulfillment) × volume. Unit economics are critical. Lifetime value must exceed customer acquisition cost in 12-24 months. Churn is the killer (repeat purchase rate determines profitability).

### Key Metrics
- **CPG:** Market share, distribution breadth (% of stores/geography), price per unit, promotional lift, retailer support, case velocity
- **Retail:** Comp store sales (same-store sales growth), inventory turns, traffic, average transaction value, units per transaction
- **DTC:** Customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, repeat purchase rate, gross margin post-CAC, payback period

### Structural Dynamics
Consolidation among retailers has crushed brand power. Private label has commoditized categories. Marketing spend is rising (digital, influencers) with uncertain ROI. Discounting pressure is constant. In retail, location economics determine profitability more than anything; a bad location is unfixable.

DTC is disrupting both CPG and retail by capturing the margin and owning the customer. But DTC math is brutal; not all products work (high repeat/high margin favors DTC).

### Common Problems
- Retailer concentration limiting pricing power
- Marketing spend inflation with declining efficiency
- Inventory imbalances (both obsolescence and stockout costs)
- Shift to DTC cannibalizing traditional channels without offsetting the CPG margin loss

### What's Different
Consumer sentiment and trend velocity are real constraints (you can't force demand). Board conversations are about market share and brand health, not financial engineering. Retail real estate decisions are long-duration and capital-intensive. Consumer research drives strategy more than other industries.

### Key Traps
- Assuming historical margin structure is sustainable (private label + retailer power have permanently compressed it)
- Believing marketing spend increases can drive sustainable growth (often diminishing returns kick in fast)
- Underestimating fixed cost leverage in retail (when traffic declines, profitability drops hard)
- Overestimating DTC opportunity without proving repeat purchase economics upfront

## 5. INDUSTRIAL / MANUFACTURING

### Economics
**Process Industries (chemicals, metals, energy):** Margin = (commodity price - cash cost per unit) × volume. Leverage to commodity prices is high (margin volatility is extreme). Scale drives cost (capex is massive, efficiency is path to profitability). Asset utilization is critical.

**Discrete Manufacturing (automotive, heavy equipment, components):** Margin = price realization - landed cost - overhead. Volume is the game (fixed cost spread over units). Supply chain cost is structural. Quality/warranty cost is a margin killer.

### Key Metrics
- **Process:** Capacity utilization, cash cost per unit, price realizations vs. commodity basket, EBITDA margins, capital intensity (capex/revenue)
- **Discrete:** Sell-through, inventory turns, supply chain days, warranty cost as % of sales, manufacturing cost variance
- Operating leverage (fixed cost / total cost), working capital intensity

### Structural Dynamics
Capacity cycles are brutal (oversupply drives prices down and margins to zero). Competition is often oligopolistic (few large players, high barriers to entry). Customers have power (volume commitments, payment terms). Supply chain risk is endemic (single-source suppliers, long lead times). Capex is lumpy and capital-intensive.

### Common Problems
- Capacity cycles and margin volatility (boom/bust cycle)
- Supply chain disruption (long lead times, geopolitical risk)
- Stranded assets from prior capex cycles
- Inability to pass through cost inflation to customers

### What's Different
Capital discipline matters more than revenue growth (bad capex decisions persist for 15+ years). Operating metrics matter more than financial metrics (utilization, throughput, quality). Relationships with customers are long-duration but price-based. Product complexity creates service differentiation but cost burden.

### Key Traps
- Assuming capacity will clear (sometimes it doesn't; you get stuck with stranded assets)
- Underestimating supply chain fragility and disruption risk
- Believing capex efficiency gains are achievable without compromising quality/speed
- Treating supply chain as cost to minimize rather than risk to manage

## 6. PRIVATE EQUITY PORTFOLIO COMPANIES

### Economics
The PE thesis is value creation through EBITDA growth (revenue + margin expansion) and leverage (debt at lower cost than equity return requirement). Exit multiple (EV/EBITDA) and holding period determine return. Debt covenants are real constraints (can't pursue every opportunity if it violates covenant).

Typical thesis: Buy at 6-7x EBITDA, improve operations → 8-9x EBITDA, hold 4-5 years, exit at 10-12x EBITDA if leverage is paid down. Math requires either growth or margin expansion (usually both).

### Key Metrics
- EBITDA and EBITDA margin, growth rate YoY
- Debt levels (total debt / EBITDA, interest coverage ratio)
- Working capital efficiency, capex as % of sales
- Unit-level economics (if roll-up thesis)
- Run-rate quarterly performance (to forecast exit valuation)

### Structural Dynamics
Every dollar of EBITDA improvement flows to equity return. Financial leverage amplifies returns (3-4x equity return from 2x EBITDA growth is typical). Debt covenants constrain behavior (can't invest in long-term bets if they pressure metrics near-term). Exit orientation dominates decision-making.

Portfolio company dynamics differ from corporate: autonomy is higher but scrutiny is intense. Board meetings are frequent and operational.

### Common Problems
- Overleveraging (debt burden prevents investment; covenant creep threatens returns)
- Growth ceiling without capex (acquired companies mature fast; need continuous capex/M&A to hit return targets)
- Talent flight (CEO wants founder equation, not PE portfolio terms; key employees leave if kept from upside)
- Integration failure (acquisition didn't unlock synergies; cost reduction was the only lever, now it's exhausted)

### What's Different
The 100-day plan matters more than long-term strategy (prove value creation fast or lose credibility). Financial engineering is as important as operational improvement. Board dynamics are different (PE sponsor owns majority; management is incentivized to exit). Cost is scrutinized ruthlessly; investment in anything without clear ROI doesn't happen.

### Key Traps
- Assuming margin expansion is sustainable if it comes from cost cuts only (revenue growth needs to come from somewhere)
- Underestimating talent retention risk when incentives flip from founder equity to PE equity
- Overestimating synergy from roll-ups (integration is harder than it looks; costs more)
- Believing refinancing is always available (market access dries up in downturns; covenant headroom matters)

## 7. GOVERNMENT & PUBLIC SECTOR

### Economics
No P&L. Budget is constraint and incentive is backward (underspend your budget means lower next year's allocation). ROI is measured in outcomes, not profit. Spend is politically sensitive (value for money is the public conversation). Capital projects are long-duration.

Value drivers are throughput (services delivered), efficiency (cost per unit), and quality (outcomes, citizen satisfaction). Incentives are misaligned: budget consumption is rewarded, efficiency is punished.

### Key Metrics
- Cost per unit of service (cost per school student, cost per hospital bed, cost per benefit recipient)
- Outcome metrics (graduation rate, patient health outcome, employment rate post-program)
- Process efficiency (days to issue permit, wait time for service)
- Utilization rates (bed occupancy, classroom utilization)
- Budget utilization (% of budget spent, variance vs. forecast)

### Structural Dynamics
Procurement is formal and slow (competitive bid process adds 6-18 months to any decision). Political cycle matters (4-5 year horizon, then priorities reset). Stakeholder complexity is high (citizens, unions, oversight bodies, elected officials). Change is slow (risk aversion is high; failed pilots are career-ending).

Funding is often siloed (budget line can't be moved across categories) and performance is rarely measured (hard to be held accountable if you can't measure output).

### Common Problems
- Entrenched processes that survive despite inefficiency (changing them requires political will that doesn't exist)
- Talent quality (government salaries limit recruitment; retention is better than corporate)
- Technology debt (legacy systems, lack of capex for modernization)
- Mission creep (scope expands; funding doesn't)

### What's Different
Stakeholder management is 50% of the work. The sales process is procurement (RFP-based, formal scoring). Scope change requires political approval, not just business case. "Impact" is not financial; it's real people and outcomes. Transparency is mandatory (everything is auditable, nothing is proprietary). Innovation is slower but more durable (once adopted, it persists across political cycles).

### Key Traps
- Assuming corporate best practices transfer (they don't; governance and incentives are different)
- Underestimating procurement timeline (RFP process adds 9-18 months minimum)
- Believing efficiency gains will reduce budget (they don't; savings are redirected to new priorities)
- Confusing pilot success with implementation success (pilots are often unrepresentative; scale fails)

## THE FIRST WEEK IN A NEW INDUSTRY: Rapid Orientation Sequence

### What to Read (in order)
1. **Latest 10-K and earnings call transcript (CEO comments, CFO guidance)** — 90 minutes
   - Understand the business model, P&L structure, what management is focused on
   - Identify key metrics, trends, and concerns

2. **Recent investor day presentation** — 45 minutes
   - Strategy, growth drivers, competitive positioning, financial targets

3. **Industry research report (3-5 pages)** — 60 minutes
   - Market size, growth rate, competitive dynamics, key trends
   - Sourced from Edison, Credit Suisse, McKinsey, Bain, BCG (your own firm's reports)

4. **Trade press: Recent features in key publications** (3-4 articles) — 60 minutes
   - What's actually happening vs. what management says is happening
   - Regulatory changes, M&A activity, customer/supplier shifts

5. **Regulatory filings** (if regulated) — 30 minutes
   - SEC comment letters, CMS coverage decisions, competition authority findings
   - Understand the constraints

### Who to Talk to First (in order)
1. **Your client's Chief Financial Officer or Chief Operating Officer** — 60 minutes
   - Understand their view of the business, competitive position, and key challenges
   - Ask about the P&L structure, key metrics, what keeps them awake

2. **A peer company's investor relations contact** — 30 minutes
   - Get unfiltered perspective on market dynamics from competitor's point of view
   - Ask about pricing power, margin pressure, customer shifts

3. **Industry expert (sell-side analyst, industry veteran, trade association)** — 45 minutes
   - Rapid education on industry structure, dynamics, and trends
   - Ask about what's changing and what's staying the same

4. **A customer or supplier** — 30 minutes
   - Understand the value chain from outside your client's perspective
   - Where is power, what's changing, what would shift your client's business

### Questions to Ask (Universal)

**On Economics:**
- What drives a company's profitability in this industry? (Drill on the 2-3 key levers)
- What's a "healthy" margin? What's a margin crisis?
- How does this compare to 5 years ago?

**On Competition:**
- Who are the top 5 competitors? How do they differ?
- What's changing in competitive positioning? Who's winning and losing?
- What's the barrier to entry? Why doesn't everyone do what the market leader does?

**On Customer/Supplier Power:**
- How much pricing power does a typical company have?
- Can customers easily switch? What locks them in?
- How much leverage do suppliers have? Is that changing?

**On the P&L:**
- Break down revenue: what's growing, what's shrinking, why?
- Where is cost going? Is it fixed or variable?
- What's the path to margin expansion?

**On Change:**
- What's the biggest change in the industry in the last 3 years?
- What's changing now that will matter in 3 years?
- What's everyone worried about but nobody talks about?

### How to Know When You Have Enough Context

You're ready when you can:
- Articulate the business model and the 3-4 key value drivers
- Explain why competitors differ and who's winning/losing
- Identify the 2-3 key metrics that matter most
- Spot what's changing and what's staying the same
- Know the regulatory/political constraints
- Identify the 3-4 problem archetypes that show up repeatedly
- Ask questions that surprise experienced people in the industry (not the obvious ones)

This typically takes 2-3 days of focused reading + 4-5 conversations. By day 4, you should be hypothesis-ready: able to propose 2-3 plausible root causes for your client's problem and the data needed to test them.

**Red flag:** If you're still learning basic industry definition by day 5, you've missed something important. Go back and re-read the 10-K and call transcript with more focus.

## 8. ENERGY & UTILITIES (Oil & Gas, Power, Renewables)

### Economics
**Oil & Gas:** Margin = (commodity price - cash cost per barrel) × production volume. Reserve economics determine long-term value; a barrel in the ground is worthless if it costs more to extract than current price. Leverage to commodity cycles is extreme; margin volatility makes long-term planning nearly impossible. Capex is massive and required (no capex = production decline). Stranded assets are the permanent risk (field becomes uneconomical overnight if prices drop).

**Power Generation (Thermal):** Revenue is either contract-based (long-term power purchase agreements) or merchant (spot market exposure). Fixed cost is dominant (fuel, O&M, depreciation); variable cost is fuel. Capacity factor (% of max capacity utilized) drives profitability; a 40% utilization plant vs. 60% is drastically different economics. Margin is narrow; volume is the game.

**Renewables:** Capital intensity is extreme up front; operating cost is minimal. Margins are set at project inception (contracted power price or investment subsidy). Scale of portfolio matters (one bad project can destroy returns). Government subsidy/policy exposure is structural and permanent; PTC/ITC changes overnight change returns.

**Regulated Utilities:** Return on equity is set by regulators (typically 9-11%); profit is not a variable. Revenue grows with rate base (regulated asset value); profit grows only if capex grows. Regulatory approval determines capex profile. Cost control drives returns (lower cost base → higher ROI on same rate base). Business is defined by service territory; geographic diversification is limited.

### Key Metrics
- **O&G:** Proved reserves (P1, P2, P3), reserve replacement ratio, cash cost per barrel (all-in cash cost), NPV per barrel, decline rates, production volume
- **Power Thermal:** Capacity (MW), capacity factor, heat rate (BTU/kWh), fuel cost per MWh, contract coverage %, PPA pricing
- **Renewables:** Installed capacity (MW), capacity factor (wind ~35%, solar ~25%), LCOE (levelized cost of electricity), contract pricing, subsidy dependency
- **Regulated Utilities:** Rate base, allowed return (%), cost of capital, capex program, earned return vs. allowed return, regulatory lag

### Structural Dynamics
Oil & Gas is boom/bust; most value is created in downturns (when you can acquire assets cheap). Consolidation is structural (capital needs are massive). Political/ESG pressure is rising; future of oil investments is politically constrained. Renewable economics are set by policy; subsidies disappear.

Power markets vary by geography (regulated, merchant, hybrid). Competition exists (especially in merchant markets) but is limited by infrastructure constraints (can't build new plants fast enough to add supply). Utilities are regional monopolies in many markets; consolidation is regulated but common.

### Common Problems
- Reserve replacement crises (production decline without reserve replacement = value destruction)
- Commodity price exposure creating earnings volatility and strategic confusion (can't invest through cycles)
- Stranded asset risk (wind farms become stranded if wind is poor; coal plants become stranded due to climate policy)
- Regulatory/political risk (subsidy changes, carbon policy, environmental constraints)
- Capex discipline (mega-projects overrun; $100M budget becomes $300M; lost return on capital)

### What's Different
Long-duration assets (power plants last 30-40 years; decisions made today determine returns for decades). Politics matters as much as economics (renewable policy, carbon regulation, grid access all driven by politics). Engineering and operations are central to value creation (you can't outthink bad operations). Safety and environmental compliance are non-negotiable; failure is catastrophic (accidents, spills, environmental liability).

ESG is not a CSR conversation; it's a business model conversation. Fossil fuel assets carry tail risk (stranding). Transition to renewables is structural (investors won't fund coal; countries are banning it); this is not cyclical.

### Key Traps
- Believing commodity price declines are temporary (sometimes they're structural shifts; oil at $120 may never return)
- Overestimating reserve replacement (replace P1 reserves today but they cost more to produce than original discovery)
- Assuming capex projects will deliver forecast returns (mega-projects routinely underdeliver on cost and timeline)
- Underestimating climate policy risk (stranding is accelerating; policy changes are real constraints, not noise)
- Confusing energy transition with opportunity (capital available for renewables is drying up; margins are compressing as capacity builds)

## 9. MEDIA, ENTERTAINMENT & SPORTS

### Economics
**Content Production:** Revenue from distribution (broadcast licenses, streaming fees, theatrical) minus production cost. Margin on content is hit-driven (most shows lose money; few hits carry the economics). Upfront cost is paid; revenue comes later (timing mismatch). Audience metrics (reach, engagement) determine licensing value.

**Streaming:** Subscriber revenue minus content cost minus technology/operations. Unit economics are negative for years (spend on content > revenue from subscribers). Economics work only at scale (Netflix's model requires 200M+ subscribers to be profitable). Churn is the killer (LTV depends on retention). Content spend rises with scale but subscriber growth plateaus (law of diminishing returns).

**Advertising:** CPM (cost per thousand impressions) × audience size = revenue. Audience attention is precious (declining TV viewers = declining ad revenue). Digital advertising fractured CPM (lower rates but more efficient targeting). Advertiser concentration matters (dependent on few large spenders = power imbalance).

**Sports Rights & Media:** Rights value is determined by audience (live sports is one of few things people watch live; premium CPM). Rights costs have inflated faster than ad growth (unsustainable margins). Live sports is capital intensive (production, distribution, talent); margin is compressed. Talent is the asset (star athletes drive audience; salary inflation is structural).

### Key Metrics
- **Content/Production:** Production cost per hour, audience reach (views, subscribers), engagement metrics (watch time, completion rate), revenue per subscriber (ARPU), content cost as % of revenue
- **Streaming:** Subscriber count, churn rate, ARPU, customer acquisition cost, content spend per subscriber, path to positive unit economics
- **Advertising:** CPM, audience size and demographics, impression inventory, fill rate, ad load per hour
- **Sports:** Rights value ($ per year), audience size, advertising revenue per game, talent cost as % of revenue, viewer growth/decline

### Structural Dynamics
Content is hit-driven; portfolio approach is required (hedge bets across many shows). Talent has leverage (stars can move to competitors, creating instability). Consolidation is structural (few large media companies own majority of content; gatekeeping power is enormous). Disintermediation is happening (creators go direct to audience, bypassing traditional distribution; YouTube and TikTok have disrupted the model).

Streaming disrupted traditional TV economics (linear TV is declining; cable is unraveling). Advertising is moving to digital (targeted, measurable) from traditional media (broad, unmeasurable). Audience fragmentation is permanent (attention is divided across platforms).

### Common Problems
- Content spending acceleration without corresponding revenue (Netflix, Disney+ burning cash to feed subscriber growth)
- Talent cost inflation outpacing revenue growth (sports rights and A-list actors extracting all margin)
- Subscriber growth plateau and churn acceleration (market saturation; consumer unwilling to pay for multiple services)
- Ad revenue decline in traditional media (CPM compression, audience defection, advertiser skepticism about ROI)
- Portfolio concentration risk (one hit isn't enough; need multiple hits; most shows fail)

### What's Different
Creativity is central to value creation (can't engineer a hit; you can only fund a portfolio and let probability work). Audience research is continuous but often wrong (pilots fail, franchises succeed unexpectedly). Talent negotiation is constant and high-stakes (A-list actors, star athletes have leverage). Regulatory complexity exists (FCC rules on broadcast, content restrictions, labor rules). International expansion is structurally different (content doesn't export as easily as software).

### Key Traps
- Assuming streaming margins will improve as subscriber base grows (they won't; content cost rises with demand for hits)
- Believing traditional media's decline is cyclical (it's structural; cord-cutting and audience fragmentation are permanent)
- Overestimating IP value without re-monetization strategy (owning characters is valuable only if you can make money from them repeatedly)
- Confusing audience size with pricing power (large audience doesn't guarantee ad premium; attention quality matters)
- Underestimating talent leverage (top creators/athletes can kill a franchise if they leave; salary inflation is structural)

## 10. EDUCATION (Higher Ed, K-12, EdTech)

### Economics
**Higher Education:** Revenue from tuition (largest source), grants/endowment, research funding, auxiliary (housing, food). Cost is dominated by labor (faculty, staff); capex on facilities. Margin is slim; public universities run at breakeven. Tuition can't raise much faster than cost (economic sensitivity is high; student debt limits willingness to pay). Endowment-rich schools have a different equation (can subsidize tuition, invest in outcomes).

**K-12:** Revenue is government funding (per-pupil allocation). Cost is dominated by labor (85%+ of operating budget). No margin concept; budget is constraint. Efficiency means cost per student. Capital is neglected (facilities are aging). Union contracts determine labor cost trajectory.

**EdTech:** Unit economics are brutal. Customer acquisition cost (schools are slow buyers; sales cycle is 12+ months) is high relative to revenue per student. Churn is high (schools try, abandon, try something else). Retention depends on measurable learning outcome (hard to prove). Recurring revenue model is still emerging (one-time license to per-seat subscription is the transition).

### Key Metrics
- **Higher Ed:** Tuition revenue, enrollment (headcount, FTE), retention rate, graduation rate, employment outcome (%, wage), endowment/total assets, student debt per graduate
- **K-12:** Per-pupil revenue (from government), graduation rate, test score growth, teacher quality (as % with advanced degree), facility condition
- **EdTech:** Customer acquisition cost, annual contract value per school/student, churn rate, product adoption (% of classrooms using), learning outcome metrics (score improvement)

### Structural Dynamics
Higher education is cost disease (labor cost rises faster than inflation; tuition rises to match; quality doesn't improve). Prestige/brand is durable (top schools have pricing power and can recruit talent). For-profit and non-traditional models have disrupted traditional degree economics but haven't achieved mainstream. K-12 is underfunded and union-constrained; innovation happens in charter schools and private schools (higher autonomy, lower cost structure).

EdTech faces structural headwinds: schools are conservative buyers, educational outcomes are hard to measure, teacher adoption is low (product disrupts workflows), per-seat pricing caps revenue growth.

### Common Problems
- Cost disease in higher ed (rising tuition without corresponding quality improvement; student debt crisis limiting new enrollment)
- Student outcomes not improving despite technology investment (hard to prove EdTech generates ROI; teachers are skeptical)
- Enrollment decline in traditional four-year degrees (online, bootcamp, and employer-sponsored alternatives growing)
- K-12 teacher shortage and retention crisis (burnout, low salary, political polarization)
- EdTech churn and adoption (schools adopt, don't see results, abandon; product is incumbent-hostile)

### What's Different
Accreditation is a gating constraint (can't operate a degree program without it; accreditors are slow to approve innovation). Employment outcomes are the conversation now (students buy education for wage lift; traditional liberal arts sell differently). Parents/students care about ROI (cost of degree vs. salary outcome). Government funding is political (appropriations are stable but slow; education policy is campaign fodder). Unionization in K-12 and higher ed constrains HR flexibility (can't easily adjust headcount or compensation).

### Key Traps
- Assuming universities will adopt technology at scale (they won't; institutional resistance is high; professors control curriculum)
- Believing EdTech unit economics will improve with volume (they won't without solving teacher adoption, which is hard)
- Overestimating willingness to pay for online education (quality perception is poor; employers favor traditional degrees)
- Confusing education reform with business opportunity (reform is political; business model is economics)
- Underestimating cost disease as a constraint (higher ed can't cut costs without reducing labor; labor is the product)

## 11. REAL ESTATE & CONSTRUCTION

### Economics
**Development:** Margin = sale price - (land + construction + financing cost + marketing + contingency/profit reserve). Land is often 30-40% of cost; construction is 40-50%; financing and contingency is 10-20%. Revenue timing is back-loaded (land is paid upfront; revenue comes at closeout). Profit margin is 15-25% on residential; 10-20% on commercial. Projects are 3-7 year cycles; capital is tied up for duration. Risk is asymmetric (downside is capped at loss of equity; upside is equity gain).

**Property Management/REITs:** Revenue from rents minus operating cost (maintenance, utilities, insurance, property tax, management). Cash flow is relatively stable. Return = cap rate (net operating income / property value). Most properties are valued on cap rate multiple; cap rates are set by market conditions and property type (office 5-7%, retail 6-8%, industrial 4-6%, residential 3-5%).

**Construction (as a service):** Margin = contracted price - labor - materials - overhead. Fixed-price contracts cap upside and create downside risk (cost overruns destroy margin). Time & materials contracts reduce risk but limit pricing power (clients are wary of cost overruns). Utilization of crews and assets determines profitability. Bonding and insurance are significant costs.

### Key Metrics
- **Development:** Cost per unit (residential $/sq ft), net operating income (NOI), return on equity (target 15-25%), project timeline, pre-leasing/pre-sales %
- **Property Management/REITs:** Cap rate, NOI, occupancy rate, revenue per unit, tenant quality (credit score), lease maturity profile, property value growth
- **Construction:** Gross margin (%), utilization rate (% of labor deployed), project profitability by contract type, safety metrics, schedule adherence

### Structural Dynamics
Real estate is capital intensive and cyclical. Interest rate sensitivity is structural (rising rates compress cap rates, reducing property values; lower rates expand multiples). Supply cycles are long (takes 3-5 years to bring new supply; until then pricing power exists). Location determines economics (prime locations command premium; secondary locations are commoditized). Capital is concentrated (large REITs, institutions dominate; small developers are squeezed).

Construction is fragmented and competitive (low barriers to entry; many small players). Labor shortage is structural (fewer young people entering trades). Material cost volatility is endemic. Fixed-price contracts create risk; companies push toward time-and-materials or guaranteed maximum price.

### Common Problems
- Development cycle timing mismatch (fund project at peak valuations; deliver after market cools; value destruction)
- Interest rate risk (rising rates = capital dries up; refinancing becomes expensive; projects stall)
- Construction cost overruns (labor, materials, timeline creep; contingency insufficient)
- Leasing risk in development (pre-leasing target missed; office space is especially at risk)
- Asset-liability mismatch in REITs (long-term fixed-rate debt; property values falling due to cap rate expansion)

### What's Different
Cap rates are the conversation in property investing (all property value flows from NOI and cap rate multiple). Underwriting is rigorous (debt providers set lending standards; LTV is key metric). Site control and entitlements are non-negotiable constraints (can't build without zoning and permits). Leverage is structural (80-90% of property value is often financed). Operational execution is critical (one bad property manager ruins a portfolio's value). PropTech is slowly disrupting (tenant screening, property management automation), but landlord-tenant relationship is still largely manual.

### Key Traps
- Assuming historical cap rates are sustainable (they're not; macro interest rates drive them; rising rates = multiple compression = value loss)
- Overestimating development feasibility based on land value alone (construction cost, timeline risk, leasing risk are often underestimated)
- Believing construction cost is fixed (it's not; labor shortage, material inflation, timeline creep are real)
- Confusing development opportunity with development economics (good location ≠ good project if timing is wrong or capital cost is too high)
- Underestimating refinancing risk (assume easy roll; it's not if credit spreads widen or property value declines)

## 12. LOGISTICS, SUPPLY CHAIN & TRANSPORTATION

### Economics
**Asset-Based (Trucking, Shipping, Warehousing):** Revenue from shipments × rate. Cost is dominated by assets (depreciation), labor (drivers), and fuel. Margin is thin (3-7% is typical for trucking; 10-15% for warehousing). Asset utilization is critical (empty miles = lost revenue without corresponding cost cut). Capacity cycles are brutal (oversupply drives rates down; undersupply creates pricing power).

**Asset-Light (Brokerage, Freight Forwarding):** Margin = broker spread (difference between shipper rate and carrier rate). Utilization is not a constraint (no assets = no fixed cost). Volume growth drives profitability. Churn risk is high (customers shop on price; switching is easy). Technology is the competitive advantage (matching shippers and carriers, optimizing routes).

**E-Commerce Fulfillment:** Revenue is per-unit fulfillment fee. Cost is labor + space + technology. Margin is tight (3-10% typical). Last-mile is the killer cost (delivering individual packages is expensive vs. bulk freight). Returns logistics is complex and unprofitable.

### Key Metrics
- **Asset-Based:** Revenue per mile (or per unit), cost per mile (labor + fuel + asset + maintenance), asset utilization, fleet age, driver retention, fuel efficiency
- **Asset-Light:** Revenue per shipment, gross margin %, customer acquisition cost, customer concentration, scale of network, technology investment
- **E-Commerce:** Cost per unit fulfilled (labor, space, pick/pack), labor productivity (units per hour), space utilization, last-mile cost, return rate

### Structural Dynamics
Trucking is fragmented (many small carriers; consolidation is slow). Labor is tight (driver shortage is structural). Fuel price volatility is endemic. Regulation is heavy (safety, hours of service, emissions). Asset cycles are lumpy (trucks cost $100K+; replacement happens in cohorts).

E-commerce has fundamentally changed network economics (dense last-mile networks required for next-day delivery; economics work only at massive scale). Returns have become a separate cost center (not profitable).

Consolidation is structural (scale enables network optimization; small players can't survive). Technology is increasingly important (routing, demand forecasting, autonomous vehicles in long-haul).

### Common Problems
- Capacity utilization swings (boom times = high utilization, tight margins, pricing power; slowdown = excess capacity, rate competition, margin collapse)
- Driver/labor retention crisis (burnout, low pay relative to cost of living, lifestyle demands)
- Fuel price volatility (pass-through to customers is partial; squeeze margin)
- Last-mile cost structure (e-commerce returns are unprofitable; customer expectations exceed economics)
- Asset obsolescence risk (emissions regulations forcing truck replacement; autonomous threat to long-haul trucking)

### What's Different
Operational execution is central (network optimization, asset allocation, labor scheduling). Real-time visibility is increasingly competitive advantage (customers demand tracking; technology enables it). Sustainability is becoming a cost center (zero-emission vehicles are more expensive; government mandates are coming). Customer concentration is high in some segments (losing Walmart or Amazon is catastrophic). Regulatory compliance is non-negotiable (safety metrics, emissions, hours of service).

### Key Traps
- Assuming capacity cycles will normalize (they won't; structural e-commerce growth creates permanent demand volatility)
- Believing asset-light models have no capital risk (technology infrastructure requires continuous investment; disruption risk is high)
- Underestimating last-mile cost structure (margins are getting worse, not better, as consumer expectations rise)
- Overestimating autonomous vehicle disruption timeline (full autonomy is 10+ years away; adoption will be gradual)
- Confusing network scale with profitability (size helps but doesn't guarantee margins if rate compression is structural)

## 13. PROFESSIONAL SERVICES (Law, Accounting, Consulting, Architecture/Engineering)

### Economics
**Professional Partnerships:** Revenue = billable hours × billing rate. Profit = revenue - cost (partner compensation + staff wages + overhead). Leverage is the value driver: junior staff (lower wage) do work, seniors bill high rates. Partner economics: origination (bring in client) → delivery (earn from client engagement) → extraction (take profit as partner compensation).

**Big 4 Model:** High leverage model (1 partner : 10 staff ratio typical). Revenue per employee increases with firm size. Operating margin is 25-35% (high leverage). Partner ownership determines profit distribution (origination partners often earn 2-5x salary partners).

**Specialized Boutiques:** Lower leverage (more balance between senior/junior staff). Higher billing rates (specialized expertise). Higher partner compensation (must attract top talent). Lower overall profitability (less leverage).

### Key Metrics
- **All Models:** Billable hours per employee, billing realization (% of standard rate achieved), partner productivity (revenue per partner), utilization rate (% of time billable), client concentration, client profitability by engagement
- **Partnership:** Partner compensation (fixed + bonus), promotion timeline (% of staff promoted to partner), leverage ratio (staff per partner), partner origination (% generating their own work)

### Structural Dynamics
Client relationships are the core asset (client stickiness is high; switching cost is real). Talent is the constraint (best people attract best work; can't scale without talent). Commoditization is structural (routine legal work, audit, tax work faces price compression from Big 4 and legal services commoditization). Specialization commands premium (complex litigation, mergers, specialized tax work has pricing power).

Partner lockstep (equal pay regardless of contribution) vs. "eat what you kill" (compensation tied to origination) are different models with different trade-offs. Consolidation is slow but structural (smaller firms are squeezed; merger to scale is common).

### Common Problems
- Talent retention crisis (equity lockstep doesn't motivate top performers; partner track is uncertain; better opportunities elsewhere)
- Client concentration (largest client = 15-20% revenue; loss would be catastrophic)
- Commoditization of core services (hourly rate is benchmark-able; price wars are common)
- Origination imbalance (partners who bring business hoard clients; prevent younger partners from building books)
- Promotion/partnership bottleneck (few junior staff become partners; creates retention problem as people leave for in-house roles)

### What's Different
Utilization is a real constraint (have to bill hours; no "bench" time; project cycles matter). Realization is critical (standard rate vs. actual bill rate depends on negotiating power and client relationship quality). Profit pools are often opaque (partners don't know how much colleagues earn; breeds resentment). Ethics are non-negotiable (professional standards, client conflicts, regulatory compliance are serious). In-house functions are increasingly stealing work (large companies hire in-house counsel, in-house engineers, in-house accountants; reduces addressable market).

### Key Traps
- Assuming partner leverage is sustainable (best people leave if lockstep undercompensates; talent exodus destroys firm)
- Believing specialization is defensible indefinitely (expertise commoditizes; must keep moving to new specialized areas)
- Overestimating partner origination value (client relationship is personal; partner departure often loses the client too)
- Confusing utilization with profitability (100% utilization with low realization (low billing rates) kills profit)
- Underestimating in-house displacement risk (large clients are systematically bringing work in-house; addressable market is shrinking)

## 14. TELECOMMUNICATIONS

### Economics
**Mobile:** Revenue from ARPU (average revenue per user) × customer base. Cost is spectrum (licensed, high capital requirement), network infrastructure (towers, base stations, fiber backbone), and direct costs (customer service, billing, roaming). Churn is critical (acquiring customer costs $300-500; lifetime value must justify CAC). Margin is 35-45% on EBITDA basis (high fixed cost structure).

**Fixed (Broadband, Landline):** Revenue from broadband ARPU + declining voice/video. Cost is fiber/copper infrastructure (capex intense), subscriber acquisition (competitive). Margin is 40-50% (high fixed cost leverage). Churn is rising (fiber overbuilding and wireless cannibalization).

**Enterprise/B2B:** Revenue from managed services, connectivity, cloud services. Higher margin (50-60%) but requires sales force. Growth is offset by commoditization (connectivity is commodity; differentiation requires added services).

### Key Metrics
- **All:** Customer count and net adds (additions minus churn), ARPU, churn rate (monthly, valuable to understand cohort churn), capital expenditure as % of revenue, EBITDA margin
- **Mobile:** Market share, contract vs. prepaid mix, customer lifetime value, customer acquisition cost, spectrum portfolio and renewal dates
- **Fixed:** Broadband penetration (% of passable households), fiber/copper mix, speed/tier mix (impact on ARPU), bundling (bundled > standalone)
- **All:** Network quality metrics (call quality, data speed), capital intensity (capex/revenue), capital efficiency (ROIC, return on deployed capital)

### Structural Dynamics
Telecom is high fixed cost, low marginal cost (once network is built, incremental user is cheap). Spectrum is scarce and regulated; licensing determines competitive landscape. Consolidation has created oligopolies (3-4 national carriers in most markets). Network effects are moderate (switching cost exists but is not extreme). Investor structure matters (private equity consolidation in some segments; public utilities in others).

OTT (Over-the-top) substitution is structural threat (WhatsApp replaces SMS; Skype replaces international voice). Converged offerings (fixed + mobile + content) are strategic play (bundling increases switching cost and ARPU). 5G capex is massive and required (competitive necessity; not a value-creating investment in itself).

### Common Problems
- ARPU decline and mix shift (voice/SMS declining; data becoming commodity; unable to price for value)
- Churn acceleration and competitive intensity (market saturation; price competition; customer easily switches)
- Capex trap (5G requires massive investment; ROI is unclear; balance sheet strain)
- Regulatory risk (spectrum auctions, net neutrality rules, international regulatory changes)
- Technology obsolescence (fiber capex required to compete with wireless; legacy copper is dying; stranded asset risk)

### What's Different
Spectrum ownership and regulatory approval are non-negotiable assets (can't operate mobile network without licensed spectrum). Network quality is a real competitive dimension (coverage, speed, reliability matter). Infrastructure sharing is common (tower sharing, roaming agreements) and creates complex economics. International operations are fragmented (different regulatory regimes, different competitors, different capex models). Technology transitions are structural (3G → 4G → 5G; each requires capex; timing matters).

Subscriber data is strategic asset (consumer behavior, location data). Privacy regulations are tightening (limit monetization). Data analytics and targeting are next battleground.

### Key Traps
- Assuming ARPU will stabilize (it won't; data is commodity; pricing power is declining)
- Overestimating 5G ROI (capex is massive; revenue uplift is unclear; investment is required for competitive parity, not value creation)
- Believing unlimited data plans can't scale (they can; network is built for peak; off-peak capacity is abundant)
- Confusing market consolidation with margin improvement (consolidation can reduce competition but doesn't create pricing power; capex requirements remain)
- Underestimating OTT risk (voice is gone; messaging is gone; video is threatened; connectivity is core asset but insufficient alone)

## THE FIRST WEEK IN A NEW INDUSTRY: Rapid Orientation Sequence (Updated)

### What to Read (in order)
1. **Latest 10-K and earnings call transcript (CEO comments, CFO guidance)** — 90 minutes
   - Understand the business model, P&L structure, what management is focused on
   - Identify key metrics, trends, and concerns

2. **Recent investor day presentation** — 45 minutes
   - Strategy, growth drivers, competitive positioning, financial targets

3. **Industry research report (3-5 pages)** — 60 minutes
   - Market size, growth rate, competitive dynamics, key trends
   - Sourced from Edison, Credit Suisse, McKinsey, Bain, BCG (your own firm's reports)

4. **Trade press: Recent features in key publications** (3-4 articles) — 60 minutes
   - What's actually happening vs. what management says is happening
   - Regulatory changes, M&A activity, customer/supplier shifts

5. **Regulatory filings** (if regulated) — 30 minutes
   - SEC comment letters, CMS coverage decisions, competition authority findings
   - Understand the constraints

### Who to Talk to First (in order)
1. **Your client's Chief Financial Officer or Chief Operating Officer** — 60 minutes
   - Understand their view of the business, competitive position, and key challenges
   - Ask about the P&L structure, key metrics, what keeps them awake

2. **A peer company's investor relations contact** — 30 minutes
   - Get unfiltered perspective on market dynamics from competitor's point of view
   - Ask about pricing power, margin pressure, customer shifts

3. **Industry expert (sell-side analyst, industry veteran, trade association)** — 45 minutes
   - Rapid education on industry structure, dynamics, and trends
   - Ask about what's changing and what's staying the same

4. **A customer or supplier** — 30 minutes
   - Understand the value chain from outside your client's perspective
   - Where is power, what's changing, what would shift your client's business

### Questions to Ask (Universal)

**On Economics:**
- What drives a company's profitability in this industry? (Drill on the 2-3 key levers)
- What's a "healthy" margin? What's a margin crisis?
- How does this compare to 5 years ago?

**On Competition:**
- Who are the top 5 competitors? How do they differ?
- What's changing in competitive positioning? Who's winning and losing?
- What's the barrier to entry? Why doesn't everyone do what the market leader does?

**On Customer/Supplier Power:**
- How much pricing power does a typical company have?
- Can customers easily switch? What locks them in?
- How much leverage do suppliers have? Is that changing?

**On the P&L:**
- Break down revenue: what's growing, what's shrinking, why?
- Where is cost going? Is it fixed or variable?
- What's the path to margin expansion?

**On Change:**
- What's the biggest change in the industry in the last 3 years?
- What's changing now that will matter in 3 years?
- What's everyone worried about but nobody talks about?

### How to Know When You Have Enough Context

You're ready when you can:
- Articulate the business model and the 3-4 key value drivers
- Explain why competitors differ and who's winning/losing
- Identify the 2-3 key metrics that matter most
- Spot what's changing and what's staying the same
- Know the regulatory/political constraints
- Identify the 3-4 problem archetypes that show up repeatedly
- Ask questions that surprise experienced people in the industry (not the obvious ones)

This framework works across all 14 industries covered in this document. Whether you're diving into Energy, Education, Professional Services, or Telecommunications, the orientation sequence remains the same: read the financials, understand the structure, talk to practitioners, and identify the recurring problem patterns.

The specifics change (energy reserves vs. software cohorts; ARPU vs. cap rates), but the underlying logic is identical. Every industry has economics (value creation mechanism), constraints (regulation, capital intensity, customer power), dynamics that are shifting, and problems that repeat. Your first week is about learning to see all four.

This typically takes 2-3 days of focused reading + 4-5 conversations. By day 4, you should be hypothesis-ready: able to propose 2-3 plausible root causes for your client's problem and the data needed to test them.

**Red flag:** If you're still learning basic industry definition by day 5, you've missed something important. Go back and re-read the 10-K and call transcript with more focus. The framework is universal; execution is specific to the industry context.
