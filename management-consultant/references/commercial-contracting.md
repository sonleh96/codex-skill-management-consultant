## Statement of Work (SOW) Architecture

The SOW is your contract with risk. A bad SOW is a mortgage on margin.

### Core Components of an Airtight SOW

**Objectives & Success Criteria** (Not "improve strategy"—*specific, measurable outcomes*)
Example: "Develop a three-year cost rationalization roadmap identifying $45M in annual run-rate savings with implementation phasing and organizational design, validated through detailed P&L impact modeling."

**Scope Boundaries** (Explicitly state what you will NOT do)
- Execution support or change management change
- Integration with existing systems unless specified
- Regulatory approval navigation
- Change of scope triggers re-engagement and re-pricing

**Deliverables** (Describe each, not "deliverables as agreed")
- Diagnostic report with findings and hypotheses (50 slides)
- Options analysis with implementation roadmaps (3 scenarios)
- Final recommendation brief (20 slides)
- Month-1 implementation playbook
*Specify format, length, and review cycles to prevent "one more revision" death spirals.*

**Milestones & Payment Triggers** (Gate every payment)
- Kickoff: 25% on contract execution
- Interim findings review: 35% upon steering committee approval
- Recommendations delivered: 30% upon presentation
- Final deliverables: 10% upon signoff
*Payments tied to deliverable acceptance prevent holdback disputes.*

**Governance & Decision Rights** (Who approves what, by when)
- Steering committee: CFO, Chief of Staff, COO
- Weekly working sessions with sponsor's team
- Escalation path if scope disputes arise
- Sponsor accountability for access and data provision

**Assumptions & Dependencies** (These protect you)
- Client provides data within 5 business days of request
- Core team of 4 identified resources available 80% of time
- No major organizational changes or leadership transitions
- Budget scenarios use baseline historical performance
- External factors (interest rates, commodity prices) held constant at [date] levels

**Exclusions** (Explicit list—critical)
- Does not include execution or implementation
- Does not cover more than 2 full-day workshops
- Does not include benchmarking data procurement
- Does not address tax or legal implications
- Does not cover communications or change management campaign

### Common SOW Failures That Erode Margin

**The "strategic fit" trap:** Objectives phrased at 10,000 feet ("transform the business") with no operational definition. You can never declare victory.
*Fix:* Attach metrics and a quantified baseline.

**Vague deliverables:** "Final presentation" vs. "Executive brief (15 slides max, 3 strategic options with 5-year NPV, 2 review cycles included)."
*Fix:* Deliverables checklist with acceptance criteria pre-approved by sponsor.

**Missing governance:** No meeting cadence or escalation path defined. Sponsors disappear mid-engagement.
*Fix:* Embed steering committee into SOW; make sponsor's participation a contractual obligation.

**Infinite iteration:** "We'll work with you until you're satisfied" leads to death by 1,000 cuts.
*Fix:* Cap review cycles in SOW (e.g., 2 rounds of feedback on final deliverable; subsequent changes require change order).

**No assumptions section:** Client later claims "you didn't account for the integration project we're running simultaneously."
*Fix:* Document baseline conditions, external constraints, and resource availability assumptions.

### How to Define "Done" Precisely

"Done" is when the sponsor signs off on a specific list of deliverables according to pre-defined acceptance criteria.

Create a "Deliverable Acceptance Template" with:
- Deliverable name and format
- Specific content requirements
- Quality standards (e.g., analysis based on [data sources], assumptions documented)
- Sign-off authority (sponsor, steering committee)
- Revision allowance (e.g., 2 rounds of material feedback; editorial changes unlimited)

Example: "Final Strategic Roadmap (PowerPoint): 25-35 slides, 3-5 year horizon, includes phased implementation with quarterly milestones, resource requirements by function, ROI modeling with sensitivity analysis. Sponsor approves by [date]. Up to 2 rounds of material revisions included; additional revisions billed at $[rate]."

### Managing the Gray Zone: In-Scope vs. Out-of-Scope

The margin killer is the "good client problem"—they ask reasonable-sounding questions that fall into the gray.

*Gray zone examples:*
- "While you're analyzing our supply chain, could you also look at logistics partners?" (2-week detour)
- "We'd like you to present to our board" (not originally planned)
- "Can you validate the operational assumptions with our plant managers?" (adds field work)

*Protocol for the gray zone:*
1. **Recognize it immediately** (within 48 hours of the request)
2. **Frame it explicitly:** "That's a great question, and here's how we think about it: the SOW covers [X], this request falls into [new area]. Let's categorize it."
3. **Quantify the effort** (not in front of the client—internally first): "This adds 80 hours of field work, 4 weeks of timeline."
4. **Present options:** "Option A: Include it in scope (delays by 4 weeks, fee increases by $X). Option B: Descope it from core engagement, deliver follow-on proposal. Option C: Fast-track it with reduced depth (additional $X fee, 2-week timeline)."
5. **Get sponsor alignment:** Never accept gray work unpaid. Use it as upsell currency.

**Decision Framework:**
- Strategic account (long-term relationship value): absorb small gray items (< 40 hours) to build goodwill
- One-off transaction: charge everything outside original SOW scope
- Recovery engagement (fixing predecessor's work): be generous on scope, stringent on timeline

## Pricing Models

Your pricing model determines your margin structure before you bill a single hour.

### Fixed Fee

**When to use:** Defined scope, predictable methodology, high utilization leverage, confidence in estimation.

**Risk profile:** You own execution risk. Underestimation destroys margins. Overestimation loses deals.

**How MBB structures it:**
- Estimate blended rate (Partner hours at $400/hr, Manager at $200/hr, Analyst at $100/hr = blended $180/hr)
- Estimate hours with 20% buffer for unknowns
- Apply leverage multiplier (1.8x to 2.2x blended rate) = $180 × 500 hours × 2.0 = $180,000
- MBB typically 50-55% margin on fixed fee after delivery costs

**Margin protection tactics:**
- **Toolkit leverage:** Use templates, prior analyses, frameworks. First engagement on supply chain optimization: 600 hours. Third engagement: 380 hours (same fee). 36% margin improvement through reuse.
- **Utilization efficiency:** Don't over-staff. A 15-person team for 4 months kills margins. Right-size to 6-8 people, use part-time specialists, run lean.
- **Change order discipline:** Every scope change adds a change order (even if it's "we'll absorb it this time"). Creates paper trail proving original fee was reasonable.
- **Phase gating:** Break into phases; deliver early wins. Phase 1 (diagnostic): fixed fee, low risk. Phase 2 (design): review after phase 1 to re-estimate. Prevents being locked into a bad price for 6 months of work.

**Risks to price for:**
- Longer access cycles than estimated ("You need CFO approval for every data request")
- Stakeholder churn mid-engagement (new sponsor wants to restart analysis)
- Client-side delays (data takes 6 weeks to assemble, not 2)
- Scope creep (most dangerous in first month when you're establishing rapport)

### Milestone/Deliverable-Based

**When to use:** Multi-phase engagements where phases depend on prior phase outcomes; client wants payment linked to milestones, not hours.

**Structure:**
- Phase 1 (Diagnostic): $150K, due [date], payment on steering committee sign-off
- Phase 2 (Design): $200K, due [date], payment on recommendations approved
- Phase 3 (Roadmap): $100K, due [date], payment on final deliverable accepted

**How to protect margin:**
- Each phase includes defined deliverables and acceptance criteria
- Payment only flows when deliverable is accepted (not when work is "done")
- If client stalls approval, phase timeline extends but engagement holds
- Prevents client from extending engagement indefinitely by deferring approval

**Common mistake:** Treating milestone fees as hours, then doing scope creep unpaid. Don't.
*Fix:* Treat each milestone as a fixed-fee mini-engagement. If scope changes, add a change order with a new fee.

### Upside-Based (Value-Based) Pricing

**When to use:** Cost savings, revenue uplift, or efficiency gains are quantifiable and material. Client is sophisticated and values the outcome over the effort. You have conviction in baselining methodology.

**Structure:**
Baseline fee (e.g., $150K) + 20% of value created above a pre-agreed target.

*Example:* "Supply chain optimization: $150K baseline + 20% of annual savings above $40M run-rate savings."
- If you deliver $50M savings: $150K + (0.20 × $10M) = $350K
- If you deliver $35M savings: $150K (no upside, but you've still earned baseline)

**Pre-contract baselining methodology (critical):**
1. **Audit the baseline:** You jointly establish "as-is" state before engagement starts
   - Current spend by category
   - Current process flow and headcount
   - Current system capabilities
   - Current contracts and terms
   *Do this in writing, signed by CFO, before project starts.*

2. **Define the value boundary:** What counts as "value"?
   - Gross savings or net of reinvestment?
   - One-time or run-rate?
   - Does it include supplier rebates or only direct cost reduction?
   - What's the measurement period (year 1, 3-year, perpetual)?

3. **Lock the methodology:** How will you measure it post-engagement?
   - We audit actual performance against baseline
   - Client accounting team confirms numbers
   - Independent validation if target is >$50M

**Procurement navigation:** Value-based pricing is a procurement red flag. Procurement hates revenue-based fees (they're "not costs"). Reframe as "outcome-based partnership pricing." Position baseline fee as your confidence deposit. Upside is shared risk.

**Trust requirements:** Value-based pricing only works with sponsors who:
- Have executive authority over their budget
- Won't move the goalposts after launch
- Will provide transparent data post-engagement
- Understand that margins must be earned through delivery, not negotiated down

**When to walk:** If procurement insists on 25% upside caps on $300M+ transformations, the risk/reward is broken. Walk.

### Time & Materials (T&M)

**When to use:** Scope is unclear, engagement is exploratory, or client wants maximum flexibility.

**Rate cards (MBB benchmark):**
- Partner/Principal: $350-450/hour
- Director: $250-350/hour
- Manager: $150-225/hour
- Senior Analyst: $100-150/hour
- Analyst: $75-100/hour
*(These are 2024-2025 market rates; adjust for geography and firm tier)*

**Blended rates:** Instead of specifying individual role rates, offer blended rate. Example: "Supply chain optimization: $185/hour blended (all-in), billed monthly, 2-hour minimum increment."

**Cap structures (protect against scope creep):**
- Hard cap: "Not to exceed $250K without written change order"
- Soft cap: "Estimated $250K based on 1,200 hours; hours above this trigger monthly review"
- Trigger cap: "If project extends beyond 6 months, fee model shifts to fixed-fee roadmap"

**Risk:** Clients view T&M as "blank check." You deliver an inch, they ask for a mile at "hourly rate." Margin collapses.
*Fix:* Combine with sprint contracts. "8 weeks at estimated 1,200 hours, then we evaluate and reset terms."

### Hybrid Models

MBB typically uses hybrids:

**Fixed + T&M upside:**
Base fee $200K (diagnostic + initial options), then T&M for design phase at $180/blended, not to exceed $150K.
- Predictability: Base fee is locked
- Flexibility: If client needs extra work, T&M enables it without scope dispute
- Client satisfaction: No hard cap, but transparent hourly basis

**Phase 1 fixed + Phase 2 variable:**
Phase 1 (diagnostic): $120K fixed, includes preliminary recommendations.
Phase 2 (design, if approved): Milestone-based, tied to steering committee gates.

**Retainer + project fees:**
Strategic advisory account gets $30K/month retainer (10 hours per week, senior partner). Project work (transformations) billed separately at fixed fee. Retainer creates revenue predictability; projects are margin plays.

### How MBB Prices Differently from Big 4

**MBB margin structure (target 55-60% delivery margin on client work):**
- Fixed fees run 2.0x-2.3x blended rate
- Blended rates are 20-25% higher than Big 4
- Leverage expectation is 1.8:1 (1 partner to 2.8 staff) = higher partner billing, higher margins
- Value-based upside is more common (40% of engagements)

**Big 4 margin structure (target 45-50% delivery margin):**
- Fixed fees run 1.7x-1.9x blended rate
- Blended rates are lower, but volume is higher
- Leverage expectation is 1:3 (1 partner to 3+ staff) = broader team, commodity pricing
- T&M and cap structures more common (pricing perceived as "more flexible," less friction in procurement)

**Why the gap?**
MBB differentiates on outcome and insight; Big 4 competes on breadth and access. MBB charges for ideas; Big 4 charges for bodies. Know which game you're playing.

## Scope Management and Creep Prevention

Scope creep is not accidental. It's a failure of governance.

### The Scope Creep Spiral

**How it starts (Week 2-3):**
Client sponsor asks: "While you're modeling supply chain, could you also look at our procurement organization? Just as context."
Your team thinks: "That's reasonable. We're already talking to procurement."
You absorb it: 40 hours of work, no change order, no fee adjustment.

**How it accelerates (Week 6-8):**
Client says: "The CFO is now interested in the treasury implications. Can you model that?"
Sponsor's politics: CFO is new, sponsor needs to show value to CFO, sponsor volunteers you for free.
You absorb it: 60 hours of work, different data requirements, different model.
*Sunk cost fallacy kicks in:* "We're already at week 8, might as well finish..."

**How it kills margin (Week 12-14):**
Client's steering committee expands. 8 new stakeholders with 8 new questions.
You're now 140% over original scope, your team is exhausted, quality is declining, you're re-estimating timelines.
Margin has eroded from 50% to 15%.

### Early Warning Signals

**Watch for these (flag within 48 hours):**
1. **"Just one more thing"** requests increasing in frequency
2. **New stakeholders** joining steering committee mid-project
3. **Scope questions** in standup meetings ("Should we also look at...?")
4. **Data requests** expanding (new systems, new geographies)
5. **Timeline slippages** triggered by client delays, not your team's work
6. **Working sessions** extending beyond scheduled hours ("We have 30 minutes left, can we talk about...")

**Protocol on day 1:**
"We're excited to deliver value on [X]. What we're NOT doing is [exclusions list]. If new questions come up that fall outside scope, we'll be transparent about it: we'll size the effort, we'll price it, and we'll get alignment before we dive in. This protects both of us."

### Change Request Protocol

Every scope addition = change request. Formalize it.

**Change Request Template:**
```
PROJECT: [Name]
REQUEST DATE: [Date]
REQUESTER: [Name, Title]

DESCRIPTION OF ADDITIONAL WORK:
[What the client is asking for]

SCOPE BOUNDARY:
[What this includes/excludes]

EFFORT ESTIMATE:
- Estimated hours: [X]
- Calendar time: [X weeks]
- Team composition: [roles]

FEE IMPACT:
- Additional fee: $[X]
- OR hours added to T&M cap: [Y hours]

TIMELINE IMPACT:
- Original delivery date: [Date]
- Revised delivery date: [Date if applicable]
- Impacts on other phases: [Yes/No, describe]

SPONSOR DECISION:
- [ ] Approved—add to engagement, fee = $[X]
- [ ] Deferred—capture for future engagement
- [ ] Rejected—document rationale
```

**Who approves:** Engagement leader + client sponsor. Both sign. This is now part of the contract.

**Pricing the change order:**
- Small addition (< 40 hours): Blended rate × hours × 2.0 multiplier
- Medium addition (40-120 hours): Phase gate into mini-engagement, fixed fee
- Large addition (> 120 hours): This is a new Phase II. Repricing and re-scoping conversation

**The discipline:** Document every change. Even ones you "absorb." At project close, you should have 5-7 change orders on file. If you have zero, you've likely underpriced the engagement.

### The "Additional Opportunities" Reframe

Don't fight scope creep—monetize it.

When a scope question surfaces:
1. **Don't say:** "That's out of scope" (defensive, kills relationship)
2. **Do say:** "That's a great question. It's outside the original scope, but we see the value. Let's scope it as a follow-on engagement."

*Example:*
Client: "Could you recommend our top 3 vendor consolidation targets?"
You: "Our current engagement analyzes your current vendor footprint. Recommending consolidation targets would require detailed negotiation simulation and contract optimization analysis—that's a 6-week Phase II. We could propose that as [Fee]. Want to chat about it?"

**Why this works:**
- You're framing it as opportunity, not burden
- Client sees you understand their business (they WILL eventually want this)
- You're protecting your original engagement margin
- You're building the case for Phase II

**Portfolio strategy:** On strategic accounts, you can be generous with Phase I scope to win Phase II pricing. On transactional accounts, you price it all upfront.

### Managing Tension: Client Expectations vs. Firm Profitability

There is no magic solution. This is a trade-off.

**Matrix for decisions:**

| Account Type | Priority | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic (5-year relationship)** | Maintain relationship | Absorb up to 50 hours of scope creep in Phase I; use Phase II to recover margin |
| **High-value account** | Profitability + growth | Absorb 20 hours; change-order everything else; position as "investment in partnership" |
| **Transactional** | Profitability | Charge for everything over scope; scope management is the sales tool (prevents loss of margin) |
| **Distressed/rescue** | Getting unstuck | Absorb 100+ hours if it derails the engagement; next phase is paid at premium rates |

**Red line:** Never absorb > 20% of original scope. At that threshold, engagement margin is compromised.

## Commercial Risk Management

Bad contracts are silent profit killers. Protect yourself.

### Exclusion Clauses and Limitation of Liability

**What every SOW must include:**

**Exclusion of Consequential Damages:**
"Neither party shall be liable for indirect, incidental, consequential, or punitive damages, including loss of revenue, profits, or business opportunity, even if advised of the possibility of such damages."

*Why:* Client loses $50M deal due to flawed recommendation. Without this clause, they sue for $50M. With it, you're capped at your fee.

**Limitation of Liability Cap:**
"Total liability of [Firm] shall not exceed the fees paid under this Agreement in the 12 months preceding the claim."

*Standard benchmark:* Liability cap = 1-1.5x annual fees on the engagement.

**Professional Negligence / Errors & Omissions (E&O) Coverage:**
"[Firm] maintains professional liability insurance in the amount of $[X]M. Client acknowledges this represents the firm's total liability limit."

*What this means:* You're capped at insurance limits, not unlimited exposure.

**Exclusion of Specific Risks:**
Document what you're NOT warranting:
- "We do not warrant the accuracy of third-party data provided by Client"
- "We do not warrant legal compliance of recommendations; Client shall engage legal counsel"
- "We do not warrant tax implications; Client shall consult tax advisors"

### Post-Delivery Risks and Indemnification

**Indemnification (mutual, not one-way):**

Standard clause:
"Each party indemnifies the other against third-party claims arising from: (a) breach of this Agreement, (b) IP infringement claims, (c) claims arising from use of Deliverables contrary to recommendations."

*What this protects:*
- Client claims you recommended something unethical? You're not indemnifying.
- Third party claims your IP is a knockoff? You're indemnifying (your E&O insurance covers it).
- Client uses your recommendation in a way that causes harm? Client indemnifies you if it's misuse.

**Audit and Support Period:**
Many engagements include 30-60 days of post-delivery support (included in fee).
Beyond that: "Support provided after [date] billed at $[rate]."

*Why this matters:* Client calls 4 months post-delivery: "Your recommendation didn't work. We need you to fix it for free."
You: "That's outside the support period. This becomes a follow-on engagement [Fee: $X]."

### IP Ownership

**Work Product:**
"All work product created specifically for Client (reports, analyses, models, recommendations) are owned by Client upon final payment."

*This is standard. MBB gives all work product to clients.*

**Pre-Existing IP and Firm Toolkit:**
"Firm retains ownership of: (a) methodologies, frameworks, and processes developed prior to engagement, (b) templates, tools, and analysis frameworks, (c) any materials marked as 'Pre-Existing IP' in the SOW."

*Why this matters:* Your supply chain diagnostic framework—you've used it 200 times. You can't give that away; you need to reuse it.

**Confidentiality of Pre-Existing IP:**
"Client acknowledges that deliverables incorporate Firm's pre-existing IP and methodologies. Client may not reverse-engineer, extract, or independently use Firm IP without license."

*This prevents:* Client extracts your analytical framework, hires juniors in-house to do it themselves for $50K instead of paying you $200K next time.

**Licensed Components:**
If you embed third-party software or databases (market data, benchmarks, etc.), clearly state:
"Deliverables include licensed content from [vendor]. Client's right to use this content is limited to internal use; Client may not sub-license or redistribute."

### Regulatory Considerations

Different industries have specific risks.

**Financial Services:**
- Recommendations may impact regulatory compliance (Basel III capital, CCAR, etc.)
- Include explicit disclaimer: "This engagement does not constitute regulatory advice. Client shall consult with [regulator] and external counsel on regulatory implications."
- Indemnification clause: Client indemnifies Firm against regulatory penalties arising from Client's implementation choices

**Healthcare:**
- HIPAA compliance required if you're accessing PHI
- Data security and breach notification protocols must be pre-defined
- Liability caps may be higher due to health/safety sensitivity

**Energy & Utilities:**
- Environmental and regulatory approval risks are Client's
- "Recommendations assume continued regulatory environment as of [Date]. Material regulatory changes may require re-evaluation."

**Get the CFO and General Counsel to review the SOW before signing.** Non-negotiable.

### Managing Procurement Departments

Procurement is not your friend. They exist to minimize spend and maximize control.

**Their priorities (in order):**
1. **Minimize cost** (beat down your fee)
2. **Protect the company** (broad indemnification, insurance requirements)
3. **Control scope** (everything in writing, minimize gray zones)
4. **Maintain leverage** (retain right to withhold payment)

**Their language vs. your language:**

| Your language | Procurement language | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| "We estimate 600 hours" | "This is an estimate, not a commitment" | Add cap: "Not to exceed $[X] without change order" |
| "Deliverables subject to Client approval" | "We retain unilateral right to reject deliverables" | Define "approval": "Sponsor approval within 5 business days" |
| "Engagement timeline: 16 weeks" | "Timeline is aggressive; Firm is responsible for delays" | Add: "Timeline assumes Client resources 80% available; delays caused by Client extend engagement" |
| "Payment 30 days after invoice" | "Payment 60 days after invoice, net 90" | Agree on terms upfront; apply early-payment discount if they'll do 15 days |

**How to navigate procurement (without losing deals):**

1. **Don't negotiate with Procurement first.** Get sponsor buy-in on SOW, fee, and timeline. Then Procurement is negotiating terms, not value.

2. **Use Procurement's language.** They love contractual specificity. Give them exactly that.
   - Instead of "deliverables of high quality," write "deliverables subject to acceptance criteria: [list]"
   - Instead of "fair market fees," write "fees of $[X] based on [methodology]"

3. **Escalate smartly.** If Procurement wants to add "Client may terminate for convenience with 10 days notice," escalate to sponsor: "Here's the commercial risk this creates [explain]. Recommend we counter with 30 days notice."

4. **Insurance and indemnification are non-negotiable.** Don't fight them. Say yes fast. They're your risk cover, not overhead.

5. **Know your walk-away point.** If Procurement insists on:
   - Liability cap of 5x fees (yours is 1x)
   - Indemnification for Client's use of recommendations (Client should indemnify you)
   - Performance guarantees (you can't guarantee client execution)
   - Payment terms beyond 45 days (cash flow killer)

   ...then walk. The deal is broken.

## Negotiation of Commercial Terms

You're negotiating not just a fee, but the probability of profitability.

### Fee Negotiation Tactics

**Anchoring:**
You propose first. Your anchor sets the negotiation ceiling.
- Prepare your anchor based on: hours estimate × blended rate × leverage multiplier × risk premium
- Client's counter-offer will be 20-30% below your anchor (this is expected)
- Your walk-away is 10-15% below your anchor

*Example:*
You: "$300K for 4-month diagnostic + recommendations"
Client (procurement): "$240K" (20% reduction)
You: "$285K, and we descope 1 iteration on final deliverable"
Client: "Done"

Your real anchor was $300K. You're at $285K (5% reduction, not 20%). You "gave" a deliverable concession that costs you 2%. Outcome: You won.

**Concession Sequencing:**
Always give concessions in this order:
1. **Scope reductions** (save the most hours)
2. **Deliverable concessions** (reduce iterations, frequency)
3. **Fee discounts** (never give pure discount)
4. **Payment terms** (extend payables if you must)

Never do: Fee discount → then scope gets added back. You've just taken a haircut with no scope reduction.

**Walk-Away Points:**
Define these before negotiating:
- **Fee floor:** 10% below your anchor (e.g., $270K on $300K anchor)
- **Scope floor:** Must include [core deliverables]. Can't descope below X
- **Timeline floor:** Can't compress below Y weeks without additional fee
- **Relationship floor:** If this is strategic account, you can be generous. If transactional, walk at your floor.

*If Client says:* "We need $200K for this." (33% below anchor)
*You say:* "I appreciate you sharing your budget. Here's our fee at $285K based on [scope]. If you need $200K, we'd need to reshape the engagement: descope to [X], extend timeline to [Y], or phase into [phase 1 + phase 2]. Which works?"

Most clients will choose phasing. You've turned a "no-deal" into two deals.

### The RFP Trap

RFPs are designed to commoditize consulting. Resist it.

**How the RFP trap works:**
1. Client issues RFP with detailed scope
2. Procurement invites 3-4 firms to "compete"
3. Each firm submits sealed bid
4. Procurement awards to lowest bidder
5. You lose to Big 4 on price ($250K vs. your $300K)

**Why it's a trap:**
- You're competing on price, not value
- RFP scope is usually written by client's previous consultant (built-in bias)
- You don't know Client's true objectives (RFP is defensive, not strategic)
- Winning this deal is losing (you're at 35% margin instead of 50%)

**How to avoid it:**

1. **Pre-empt the RFP.** Before procurement gets involved:
   - Meet with sponsor
   - Do a 3-day diagnostic (proposal only, not a project)
   - Present findings: "Here's what we see, here's how we'd tackle it, here's our investment"
   - Get sponsor to buy-in
   - When RFP goes out, your SOW is pre-agreed (other firms' bids are based on incomplete information)

2. **If you're already in an RFP:**
   - Request a pre-proposal meeting with Client sponsor (not Procurement)
   - Ask: "What's the actual objective you're trying to achieve?"
   - Listen for the gap between RFP scope and real problem
   - Propose tailored approach addressing real problem, not RFP requirements
   - Memo to sponsor: "The RFP asks for [X]. We'd actually recommend [Y] because [reason]. Here's our proposal."
   - Client can accept your proposal or insist on RFP; you've positioned yourself as strategic

3. **Respond to RFP with options:**
   - Option A (RFP scope): $250K, 16 weeks
   - Option B (recommended scope): $300K, 20 weeks, includes [higher-value component]
   - Option C (phased): Phase I at $120K, Phase II at $180K based on Phase I findings

   This signals you're thinking strategically, not just bidding.

### Structuring Retainers and Ongoing Advisory

Retainers create revenue stability. Here's how to structure them.

**Monthly Retainer Model:**
"$25K/month, includes up to 10 hours per month of partner/director-level availability for strategy questions, ad-hoc analysis, and market intelligence."

*Why this works:*
- Client has ongoing access (relationship stickiness)
- You get predictable monthly revenue
- Ad-hoc requests don't turn into unpaid scope creep
- Over 12 months, it's $300K with minimal hours (100 hours = $3K/hour, vs. $180/hour blended rate—very profitable)

**Retainer + Project Model:**
Strategic advisory account: $30K/month retainer + project work billed separately
- Retainer covers: weekly 1-hour steering sessions, ad-hoc questions, 2 days/month availability
- Projects (transformation work, M&A assessment): fixed-fee or milestone-based, separate from retainer

**Retainer Cap Structures:**
"Retainer includes up to [X] hours per month. Hours beyond [X] billed at $[rate]. Any projects exceeding [Y] hours require separate engagement."

This prevents: Client calls for a "quick 40-hour market study." With retainer, they expect it free. With cap, you're clear on the boundary.

### Multi-Year Deal Structures

Strategic accounts often move to 2-3 year engagements.

**Tiered Model:**
- Year 1: Core transformation ($400K fixed fee)
- Year 2: Optimization and execution support ($250K fixed fee)
- Year 3: Sustainment and strategic advisory (retainer + project)

**Why this works:**
- Client gets pricing certainty
- You get revenue visibility (3-year pipeline)
- Natural exit ramps (if not renewing, you know at year-end planning)

**Annual Price Escalation:**
"Fees in Year 2 and Year 3 escalate at 3% annually, or CPI + 1%, whichever is lower."
*Never agree to zero escalation. It signals doubt in value.*

**Kill-Switch Clauses (mutual):**
"If project doesn't meet milestones [list], either party may exit with 60 days notice and 50% pro-rata refund."

*This protects both parties:*
- If you're under-delivering, Client can exit without sunk cost
- If Client isn't committed, you can exit before sinking months into a failing project

### When to Give Discounts (Strategic Pricing)

Never give pure discounts. But strategic pricing is fair game.

**When to discount:**
1. **Portfolio account** (long-term relationship value > this engagement margin): 10-15% discount on Year 1, recover in Year 2+
2. **Proof-of-concept** (client doesn't know you yet): 15% discount for first engagement, full pricing on follow-on
3. **Loss-leader** (breakthrough into new sector): Accept 40% margin on first deal to build case study and reputation
4. **Bundled projects** (Client commits to 2+ simultaneous engagements): 5-10% blended discount on total, higher margin overall volume

**How to present it:**
Don't say "We'll give you a discount."
Say: "We see long-term opportunity here. We're pricing this at $250K (vs. our standard $300K) in exchange for: (a) commitment to 3-phase engagement [Phase II, Phase III defined], (b) case study rights for our website, (c) client reference."

This frames the discount as transactional (you got something in return), not desperation.

**When NOT to discount:**
- One-off transaction deal (you'll never see them again)
- Already-committed Client (they're not negotiating; they need you)
- Competitive bidding situation (discounting signals weakness; position on value)
- Desperate engagement (your team has capacity; don't discount—just walk)

## Contract Lifecycle Management

From kickoff to close, manage the engagement like a business (because it is).

### From Proposal to Signed SOW: The 6-8 Week Cycle

**Week 1-2: Proposal submitted**
- SOW includes objectives, scope, deliverables, milestones, pricing, assumptions, exclusions, legal terms
- Included with SOW: Q&A document, detailed engagement team resumes, 2-3 case studies relevant to Client
- Client review with sponsor + Procurement begins

**Week 2-3: Initial feedback**
- Sponsor feedback: "This is good, a few clarifications on deliverables"
- Procurement feedback: "We need adjusted indemnification clause, insurance certificate of currency"
- Your response: Change orders to SOW within 48 hours; insurance docs within 24 hours

**Week 3-4: Negotiation round 1**
- Fee negotiation: Client counter at 20% below your anchor
- Scope negotiation: Client wants to add 2 items, you suggest phasing
- Legal negotiation: Procurement wants 2x liability cap, you counter with 1.25x
- Timeline: 10 business days for your responses to their requests

**Week 4-5: Your redline**
- Updated SOW reflecting agreed scope, pricing, milestones
- Revised legal terms (marked with change-track)
- Email from your General Counsel (if > $500K deal): "Cleared for execution from legal perspective"
- Sponsor signs; Procurement reviews

**Week 5-6: Final legal review**
- Procurement Legal reviews final terms
- E-signature process (usually Docusign, sometimes wet signature)
- Multiple signatories: Client CFO, Sponsor, Procurement director, your Engagement Director, your Regional Managing Director (for governance)

**Week 6-8: Kickoff**
- Signed SOW in place
- Kickoff meeting scheduled: Client sponsor, steering committee, your team, engagement leader
- First deliverable calendar dates locked
- Data intake begins

**Common delays:**
- Sponsor disappears (vacations, emergencies): Add 2-3 weeks
- Procurement Legal wants to "review with outside counsel": Add 1-2 weeks
- Multiple Client sign-offs required (CEO, General Counsel, etc.): Add 1+ week per additional signature

**How to accelerate:**
- Get sponsor to personally call Procurement director: "This is a priority for me, please expedite"
- Have your GC call their GC to negotiate legal terms (peer-level, faster)
- Propose 2-week extensions: "Rather than delay, sign now with 2-week extension if needed"

### Extension and Renewal Strategies

Projects end. Design the next one.

**At 80% completion of engagement (e.g., Week 12 of 16-week engagement):**

1. **Identify Phase II opportunity**
   - What did you uncover during Phase I that warrants deeper work?
   - "The diagnostic identified 5 value levers. Phase II would detail design and roadmap for 2-3 highest-priority levers. Timeline: 12 weeks, investment: $225K."

2. **Propose extension to current engagement**
   - "We've identified 3 additional opportunities that fit naturally into this work stream without derailing our current timeline. We can extend by 4 weeks and $120K, or phase Phase 2 separately."

3. **Build the case for follow-on**
   - "We've uncovered organizational design implications. Your Head of Organization Development should see this before Phase I closes. Propose: 2-day session + 40-hour follow-on org design phase ($45K)."

**Renewal conversations (for retainers):**
- 60 days before retainer ends: "Let's review the past 12 months of work and discuss Year 2 structure."
- Position as natural: "We can expand the retainer from 10 to 15 hours/month, add a second steering session, or stay flat but shift focus to [new priority]."
- Pricing: CPI + 1-2% escalation (never flat, signals staleness)

### Post-Project Evaluation and ROI Demonstration

This is where you win the next deal.

**30 days post-final deliverable:**

**Client Self-Evaluation:** Ask them to complete a brief survey:
- Did we deliver on the original objectives? (Yes/No)
- What was the quality of recommendations? (1-5 scale)
- How did we compare to previous consultants? (Better/Same/Worse)
- What would you change about our engagement approach?
- How likely are you to engage us again? (1-10 scale)

*Why:* This data is your sales tool. "Our last 12 clients rated us 4.7/5 on quality and 9.1/10 on likelihood to re-engage."

**Your Evaluation:** Complete internally:
- Engagement margin: Actual hours vs. estimate, actual fee vs. budget
- Team satisfaction: How efficient was the delivery? Were there team challenges?
- Client satisfaction: Sponsor's sentiment, any complaints, unresolved issues?
- Quality: Do we stand behind the recommendations? Any caveats?
- Learning: What would we do differently next time?

**ROI Demonstration (3-6 months post-engagement):**
Schedule a "Lessons Learned and Impact Review" with sponsor.
- Walk through initial objectives: "We set out to [X]. Here's the progress to date."
- Quantify the impact: "Of the 5 recommendations, 3 are in implementation. Conservative estimate of Year 1 value: $[X]M."
- Get sponsor's voice: "CFO told me yesterday that the supply chain redesign is tracking to save $8M this year."
- Document it: "Based on our conversation, we're estimating a Year 1 impact of $8-12M, with full value of $25M by Year 3. Can we document this internally?"

*Why:* This becomes your case study. It's proof that your recommendations work. It's the best sales tool you have.

### Building the Case for Follow-On Engagements

Every engagement ends with a question: What's next?

**Design the next engagement before the current one closes.**

At Week 10 of a 16-week engagement:
- "We've identified 3 follow-on opportunities:
  - Option A: Supply chain execution support (12 weeks, $250K)
  - Option B: Organizational redesign detailed planning (8 weeks, $180K)
  - Option C: Procurement optimization (quick strike, 4 weeks, $75K)
  Which of these aligns with your priorities?"

**Don't ask:** "Would you like another engagement?"
**Do ask:** "Which of these opportunities is highest priority for your next phase?"

**Position follow-on as continuity, not hard sell:**
"We've built deep context on your business over the past 4 months. The natural next step is [Phase II]. We'd be most efficient team to execute this because [reasons]. Want to discuss timing?"

**Pricing follow-on strategically:**
- If Phase I margin was strong (50%+): Phase II pricing is full price (possibly premium if it's higher-value work)
- If Phase I margin was tight (35-40%): Phase II can be discounted 10-15% if it's bundled and extends relationship
- If Phase I margin was thin (< 35%): Phase II must be full margin or you walk

**Document the handoff:** At engagement close, create a "Phase II Scope Outline" document. Even if Client isn't committing immediately, you've framed the next steps in writing. When they call in 2 months and say "We want to move forward with [that org redesign]," you have a scope document ready to convert to an SOW.

## Key Decision Frameworks

### When to Use Each Pricing Model

```
Is scope well-defined and stable?
├─ YES → Can you predict hours accurately?
│   ├─ YES → Fixed Fee (50-55% margin target)
│   └─ NO → Phase 1 Fixed + Phase 2 Variable (hedge risk)
└─ NO → Is the value outcome quantifiable and measurable?
    ├─ YES + High trust → Value-Based (baseline + % upside)
    └─ NO + Exploratory → Time & Materials + Cap (protect downside)
```

### When to Absorb Scope Creep

```
Scope addition requested (estimate hours)
├─ < 20 hours
│   ├─ Strategic account? → Absorb, build relationship
│   └─ Transactional account? → Change order, charge it
├─ 20-60 hours
│   ├─ Directly related to core scope? → Phase into Phase II
│   └─ Tangential? → Change order, separate engagement
└─ > 60 hours → Always new engagement, new SOW, new pricing
```

### Fee Negotiation Walk-Away Analysis

```
Client's offer: $[Y]
Your anchor: $[X]
Reduction: (X-Y)/X = ___%

If reduction is:
├─ < 10% → Take it, you've won
├─ 10-20% → Negotiate concessions (scope, timeline, deliverables)
├─ 20-35% → Offer phasing (Phase 1 at discount, Phase 2 at full price)
└─ > 35% → Walk, deal doesn't make economic sense
```

## Closing Checklist

Before you sign any SOW:

- [ ] Objectives are measurable and quantified (not "improve strategy")
- [ ] Scope has explicit exclusions (what you are NOT doing)
- [ ] Deliverables are itemized with acceptance criteria
- [ ] Milestones tied to payment triggers (no work without payment gate)
- [ ] Governance and escalation path defined
- [ ] Assumptions documented (data availability, resource commitment, baseline conditions)
- [ ] Pricing model aligned with risk (fixed fee = your risk; T&M = client risk)
- [ ] Fee is at or above your walk-away (check your anchor logic)
- [ ] Legal terms reviewed by your GC (indemnification, IP, liability caps)
- [ ] Procurement concerns addressed (insurance, indemnification, payment terms)
- [ ] Partner/Director sign-off (second pair of eyes on commercial terms)
- [ ] Engagement team capacity confirmed (you can staff it at planned utilization)
- [ ] Change order protocol agreed with sponsor (in writing)
- [ ] Phase II or follow-on opportunity identified (before project kicks off)

**If any of these are incomplete, don't sign. The SOW is your control document.**
