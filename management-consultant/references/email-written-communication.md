# Email & Written Communication for Consultants

The average senior executive receives 150+ emails a day. Your email will be skimmed in 8 seconds or less. If your point isn't in the first two lines, it may never be read.

---

## The Consulting Email: Core Principles

**Subject line = the message, not the topic**

Wrong: "Update on pricing analysis"
Right: "Pricing analysis complete — recommend launching new tier in Q3"

The subject line is read in isolation. Make it contain the decision or finding, not just the topic.

**First line = the bottom line**

Lead with what you need the reader to know or do. Context, background, methodology — all of that comes after, if it's needed at all.

Wrong: "I wanted to follow up on our conversation last Tuesday about the procurement study. We've been working through the analysis and have reviewed benchmarks from 12 peer companies and also spoken to the procurement team. Based on all of that, we have some findings to share."

Right: "We've completed the procurement benchmarking. Your costs are 18% above peer median, with the biggest gap in logistics and professional services. Details below."

**Structure by what the reader needs to do with it:**

- **Decision requested:** State the decision first, give the options and recommendation, provide supporting context
- **Information only:** Lead with the key finding, follow with supporting detail
- **Action items from meeting:** List numbered actions with owners and deadlines — nothing else

**No "FYI" emails that require action.** If someone needs to do something, say so explicitly: "Please review and confirm by Thursday" — not "Thought you'd find this useful."

## The Formats That Work

**The "For Your Review" email (sending materials):**

```
Subject: Q3 Profitability Analysis — requesting your input before partner review Friday

[Name],

Attached is the draft profitability analysis for your review. Key findings:
• APAC margins declined 4 points — driven by product mix, not pricing
• North America is at risk of following the same pattern in Q4
• Two options for intervention with materially different cost implications

One question we'd welcome your perspective on: should we recommend the accelerated option despite the implementation risk given Q4 pressures?

I'd appreciate your thoughts by EOD Thursday so we can refine before the partner review.

[Signature]
```

**The status update email:**

```
Subject: [Project Name] Week 3 Update — on track, one data access issue

Progress this week:
• Customer interview program complete (12/12 interviews done; synthesis underway)
• Financial model v1 built — pending revenue data from finance team

On track: recommendation readout still scheduled for March 15

One issue: Finance has not yet provided the segment-level P&L we requested on Feb 28. Without this by March 5, we'll need to use estimated data in the model. Confirming you can escalate the request?

Next week:
• Customer interview synthesis complete
• Financial model finalized (pending data)
• Interim client check-in Tuesday

[Signature]
```

**The decision-request email:**

```
Subject: Decision needed by Friday: Launch timing for pilot program

We need a call this week to align on one decision: whether to launch the pilot in Q2 or Q3.

The case for Q2: captures the seasonal opportunity and validates before the full budget decision
The case for Q3: allows proper team build-out and reduces execution risk

My recommendation: Q2, accepting some execution risk given the strategic value of early validation.

Can you do 30 minutes Wednesday or Thursday?

[Signature]
```

## Writing for Senior Audiences

**Executive memos:**

When a situation requires more than an email but less than a full deck, the consulting memo is the right format. Typically 1-3 pages.

Structure:
```
MEMO

To: [Name, Title]
From: [Name]
Date: [Date]
Re: [Decision or Issue — stated as a complete sentence]

RECOMMENDATION
[One paragraph. What should be done, stated directly.]

CONTEXT
[2-3 paragraphs of background — only what's necessary for the recommendation to make sense.]

KEY FINDINGS
[Numbered list. 3-5 findings that support the recommendation. Each as a complete sentence stating the finding, not a topic.]

IMPLEMENTATION IMPLICATIONS
[What needs to happen next if the recommendation is adopted.]

KEY RISKS
[1-3 specific risks and mitigations.]

OPEN QUESTIONS
[Any issues requiring additional input or decisions from the reader.]
```

**Writing principles for senior audiences:**
- Active voice always ("We recommend" not "It is recommended")
- Short sentences — 15-20 words maximum for most sentences
- No acronyms that aren't defined on first use
- No weasel words ("it appears," "it may be the case," "there are indications")
- Numbers are specific ("$47M" not "significant savings")
- Conclusions are stated, not suggested ("This suggests we may want to consider" → "We should")

## Common Email Failures

**The wall of text.** Paragraphs with no breaks, no structure. The reader's brain rejects it. Fix: Use bullet points for lists. Use white space. Break any paragraph longer than 4 lines into two.

**No clear ask.** "Thought you'd find this interesting" emails clog inboxes and train people to ignore your messages. Every email either needs an action or an explicit "no response needed." If it's just informational and truly has no action, ask yourself whether it needs to be sent at all.

**Reply-all by default.** When you reply-all to a long thread, everyone gets your response whether they need it or not. Before hitting reply-all, ask: who actually needs to see this? Then reply only to those people.

**The apology email.** "Sorry for the delay in responding, I've been swamped..." is information no one needs and takes time they don't have. Skip the apology. Get to the point.

**Email for complex or sensitive topics.** Email is for information and decisions that can be communicated clearly in writing. For anything nuanced, political, or requiring real dialogue — pick up the phone or get in a room. Long email threads resolving complex disagreements rarely resolve them; they usually entrench them.

**The passive-aggressive paper trail.** Sending an email to document a conversation that didn't go your way, CC'ing the senior partner. This is universally recognized and universally resented. If you need to document something, do it through proper channels, not email theater.

## Meeting Follow-Up Notes

Every client meeting should produce a brief follow-up note within 24 hours. Not a transcript — a decision record.

Format:
```
Subject: Notes from March 7 Strategy Discussion

Key decisions:
1. Confirmed that the market entry analysis will focus on Southeast Asia, not India — given existing partnerships in the region
2. Approved proceeding with customer interviews (15 enterprise customers; [Name] will provide the contact list by March 10)

Open items:
• Finance team to provide segment P&L by March 12 (Owner: [Name])
• Consultant team to provide interview guide for review by March 9

Next meeting: March 14 at 2pm — interim findings readout

Please flag any corrections or additions by March 8.
```

This serves three purposes: confirms alignment, creates accountability, and documents the engagement for reference. Keep it short. If the meeting was two hours long, the follow-up should still be one page.
