# Executive Presence for Consultants

Executive presence is not charisma. It's not being tall or having a deep voice. It's the set of signals — verbal, physical, and behavioral — that cause senior people to treat you as a credible peer rather than a junior resource.

In consulting, presence matters more than in most professions because you're often the youngest person in the room, advising people with decades of experience. The gap between being technically right and being believed is closed by presence.

---

## The Three Pillars

**Gravitas:** Intellectual weight and calm authority. The sense that you've thought through what you're saying, you're not rattled by pressure, and you're operating from genuine conviction rather than performance.

- Pausing before you speak (not rushing to fill silence)
- Holding a position under pressure when you have good reasons to
- Admitting uncertainty clearly rather than hedging with qualifiers
- Asking sharp questions rather than broadcasting knowledge
- Not being visibly anxious when challenged

**Communication:** The ability to be heard, understood, and persuasive.

- Leading with the answer (not building to it)
- Using plain, direct language rather than jargon or over-hedged constructions
- Structuring responses so they're easy to follow
- Calibrating length to the audience's needs
- Reading whether the person wants more or has what they need

**Appearance and physicality:** How you carry yourself. Not fashion — posture, stillness, energy.

- Stillness signals confidence (fidgeting, swaying, clicking pens signal anxiety)
- Deliberate movement (walking in with purpose, not shuffling)
- Eye contact that's steady but not aggressive
- Voice that doesn't trail off at the end of sentences (undermines authority)

## The Room-Reading Skill

Before you can project presence, you need to read the room accurately.

**In the first 5 minutes:**
- Who has the actual power (not always the most senior title)
- What's the energy — skeptical, open, distracted, pressured?
- Are they here to be briefed or to debate?
- What's the subtext in the room (tension between people, prior decisions that affect this conversation)?

**During the meeting:**
- Who's engaging vs. checked out? If the CEO is on her phone, you're losing her — adjust immediately
- Who's nodding but not speaking? (Often the most important person is silent)
- When someone interrupts, what does that signal? (Interest? Impatience? Disagreement?)
- Is the conversation moving toward or away from your objective?

The adjustment: if you're losing the room, don't push harder on the same content. Change the mode — ask a question, invite reaction, shift to a different framing. The worst thing you can do when you're losing the room is give them more of what's not working.

## Handling Senior Executives

Executives communicate differently than managers. They're:
- **Faster.** They get to the point in 30 seconds. They expect you to as well.
- **More direct.** They say what they think. They expect you to say what you think.
- **Comfortable with ambiguity.** They don't need everything resolved before making a decision.
- **Testing you.** Sharp questions aren't hostility — they're how executives probe whether someone's thinking is real.

**How to match their register:**
- Lead with your conclusion before any context
- Skip the process (they don't care how you got there — they care what you found)
- Say "I think" and mean it, not as a hedge but as an assertion
- Let them interrupt — don't restart, answer their question and wait
- When they push back, ask what they're seeing rather than immediately capitulating or defending

**The trap:** over-explaining to fill silence. Executives often pause after you've answered to process or think of the next question. This pause feels like a gap you need to fill. Don't. Say your answer. Then stop. Let them drive the conversation.

## Building Credibility Quickly

In consulting, you have to establish credibility with clients who are older, more experienced in their industry, and initially skeptical of outside advisors. You do this through:

**Preparation signals:** Referencing something specific from their annual report, earnings call, or prior conversation. Shows you've done the work. The opposite — generic questions they'd expect from anyone who hasn't prepared — destroys credibility in minutes.

**Precise questions:** Questions that reveal an understanding of their business. "In your Q3 call, the CFO mentioned margin pressure in the APAC segment — is that concentrated in specific product lines or broad-based?" signals that you've done real preparation and have a hypothesis, not just curiosity.

**The right kind of "I don't know":** When you don't know something, say so directly and say how you'll find out. "I don't have that data with me — I'll get you the answer today" is more credible than a guess or a hedge. Guessing and being wrong destroys more credibility than admitting a gap.

**Honest pushback:** Agreeing with everything a senior client says makes you a vendor, not an advisor. When you have a genuine, reasoned disagreement, say so. "I hear you, and my reading of the data leads me to a different conclusion" — then explain. This is how you become trusted: by being honest, not just agreeable.

**The intellectual curiosity signal:** People with genuine presence are actually interested in what they're hearing. They ask follow-up questions not because they're supposed to, but because they want to understand. This is felt. Performed curiosity (nodding vigorously, asking scripted follow-ups) is spotted immediately by senior people who've been through thousands of meetings.

## Written Credibility

Executive presence extends to writing. The same principles apply:

- **Bottom line up front** — the decision or finding in the first line, not the last
- **Short sentences and paragraphs** — senior people skim; make your writing skimmable with clear topic sentences
- **No weasel words** — "it appears that," "it may be the case that," "there are indications that" all say the same thing as "it seems like" but with more words and less credibility
- **Specific over vague** — "$47M cost opportunity in procurement" is better than "significant cost savings potential"
- **Active voice** — "We recommend" not "It is recommended that"

## Presence Under Pressure

The most revealing presence test: what happens when you're challenged, wrong, or caught off-guard?

**When challenged on your analysis:**
Don't defend reflexively. Don't capitulate immediately. Do: pause, understand the challenge ("Help me understand — are you saying the data shows something different, or that the interpretation is off?"), then engage with the substance. If they're right, say so. If you believe you're right, explain why and invite them to show you where you're wrong.

**When you don't know the answer:**
"I don't have that in front of me — I'll get back to you today" is always better than a guess. Saying "I don't know but here's how I'd approach finding out" demonstrates competence even in the absence of knowledge.

**When you've made a mistake:**
Own it, quickly and directly. "I made an error in the model — the correct number is X, which changes the recommendation in this way." Over-apologizing or under-acknowledging both damage credibility. A clean acknowledgment and a clear correction is the fastest way to recover trust.

**When someone is hostile:**
Stay slow. Slow your speaking pace. Lower your volume slightly. Don't mirror the aggression. Respond to the content of what they said, not the emotional temperature. "That's a fair concern — here's how I'd address it" diffuses most hostility. Matching it creates a fight you can't win.
