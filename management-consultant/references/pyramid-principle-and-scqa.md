# Pyramid Principle & SCQA — Micro-Coach

This file turns the Pyramid Principle and SCQA into *operational behavior*, not just concepts. Use it to auto-propose structure, run "Pyramid audits" on drafts, and build any communication from scratch.

---

## When to Activate This File

Read this file when:
- The user asks you to write a deck, memo, email, executive summary, or verbal briefing
- The user shares a draft and asks for improvement
- You're synthesizing a complex analysis into a recommendation
- The user's communication buries the point or builds slowly to the conclusion

**Behavioral trigger**: Any time you produce a written or verbal output, ask: *Does this lead with the answer?* If not, restructure before delivering.

---

## The Core Principle: Answer First, Always

The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto, McKinsey) is built on one rule: **state your governing thought at the top, then support it**.

Most people build to a conclusion — describe context, walk through analysis, arrive at the answer. In consulting, this is backwards. Senior readers don't wait for your reasoning. They need to evaluate your recommendation first, then decide whether to interrogate the logic behind it.

**The Pyramid in one sentence:** Lead with what you think. Group your reasons. Back each reason with evidence.

```
          [ GOVERNING THOUGHT ]
               /    |    \
          [Why 1] [Why 2] [Why 3]
          /  |      |      |  \
       [data][data][data][data][data]
```

This is not just a writing technique. It forces you to know what you actually think *before* you start producing content. If you cannot state the governing thought in one sentence, you haven't finished thinking.

## SCQA vs. SCR — Know the Difference

Both are opening structures. They are not interchangeable.

### SCR (Situation → Complication → Resolution)
Used in storylining and full recommendation decks. Covers the complete narrative arc. The Resolution is your recommendation — the full answer with supporting logic. See `references/storylining.md`.

### SCQA (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer)
Used for **openings** — the first 30 seconds of a presentation, the subject line + first paragraph of an email, the first two slides of a deck. The Question makes the "so what" explicit *before* the answer. It creates the hook that earns the audience's attention.

```
SITUATION:     What is true now? (Shared context — not news to the audience)
COMPLICATION:  What has changed or what's the tension? (Why this matters now)
QUESTION:      What does that make the audience want to know? (Implicit "so what" made explicit)
ANSWER:        Your governing thought — the direct response to the question
```

**When to use which:**
| Format | Structure | Use When |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Opening a presentation, board update, or email | **SCQA** | You need to hook the audience fast |
| Verbal briefings and impromptu updates | **SCQA** | Speed matters; context is short |
| Full analytical narrative or recommendation deck | **SCR** | You're building the complete argument |
| Formal memo, long-form document | **SCR** | The story needs to carry the whole recommendation |

## SCQA Templates by Situation

### Strategy Recommendation
```
S: [Company] has been the [market position] in [sector] for [X years], with [Y%] market share and [$Z] in revenue.
C: Over the past [timeframe], [competitor action / market shift / trigger] has [eroded X / changed Y / created Z].
Q: How should [Company] respond to protect its position and grow profitably?
A: We recommend [specific action] — and here is why, and what it takes.
```

### Operational Problem
```
S: [Company's] [process/function] currently costs [$X] and runs at [Y-day] cycle time.
C: Best-in-class peers operate at [$X × 0.6] with [Y/2] cycle time — we are running at a [$Z] cost penalty per year.
Q: Where is the performance gap, and what is the fastest path to closing it?
A: The gap concentrates in [2 areas]. Fixing them yields [$Z] in savings within [timeline].
```

### Investment or Capital Decision
```
S: [Company] has [$X] available for strategic investment and [timeframe] to deploy it effectively.
C: Three competing priorities have been identified, but leadership has not aligned on criteria or sequencing — and the window is closing.
Q: Which investment should we make first, and on what basis?
A: [Option] delivers the strongest risk-adjusted return and fastest path to strategic impact. Here is the analysis.
```

### Board / Crisis Update
```
S: [Issue] emerged on [date], affecting [scope — customers / revenue / operations].
C: [Consequence] if unresolved by [deadline]. Exposure of [$X] in [financial / regulatory / reputational] terms.
Q: What decision does the board need to make today to contain this?
A: Approve [specific action]. We need [resource / authority / timeline decision] to move.
```

### Status Update or Progress Report
```
S: We are [X weeks] into the [project], with [Y] of [Z] workstreams on track.
C: [Workstream / risk] has surfaced that we did not anticipate — it threatens [milestone / outcome] by [date].
Q: What does the team need to do differently, and what does the sponsor need to decide?
A: Two interventions required: [Action 1] by [date] and [Action 2] by [date]. Here is what we need from you.
```

## Worked Examples: Bad → Good

### Example 1: Executive Email

**Bad (builds to conclusion — answer buried at the end):**
> Hi Sarah, as part of our ongoing procurement review we've been analyzing 47 vendors across six categories. We found significant overlap in categories 3 and 4. After modeling consolidation scenarios, we concluded that reducing from 12 to 5 preferred vendors in those two categories would reduce annual spend by $3.2M. Wanted to flag this for your consideration.

**Good (SCQA + Pyramid — answer in subject line, repeated in sentence one):**
> Subject: Recommend approving vendor consolidation in categories 3 and 4 — saves $3.2M annually
>
> Sarah — recommend approving supplier consolidation in categories 3 and 4. This saves $3.2M per year with no service-level risk.
>
> Three reasons this is the right move now: (1) current contracts expire in Q2, giving us a clean switching window; (2) our two target consolidation vendors have confirmed capacity and pricing; (3) category managers have pre-built relationships and are ready to move.
>
> Draft SOW and consolidation timeline attached. Happy to walk through it this week if useful.

**What changed and why it works:** Answer in subject line. Answer repeated in sentence one. Reasons structured as a group of three with clear logic. Action request explicit. No preamble, no process description.

---

### Example 2: Slide Title Stack

**Bad (topic labels — tell you what the slide is about, not what it means):**
- Slide 1: "Market Overview"
- Slide 2: "Competitive Landscape"
- Slide 3: "Strategic Options"

**Good (action titles — each title IS the insight, reads as an argument):**
- Slide 1: "The market is growing 14% annually, but growth is concentrated in premium segments where we are underweight"
- Slide 2: "Our two main competitors have moved aggressively into premium in the past 18 months, capturing 60% of new segment entrants"
- Slide 3: "Entering premium through acquisition is 3× faster and 40% cheaper than organic build — three reasons"

**The title stack test:** Read only the titles in sequence. Do they tell a complete, coherent argument? If yes, the stack is working. If they could appear in any order and still make sense, they are labels, not an argument.

---

### Example 3: Verbal Recap (Live Case or Client Meeting)

**Bad (summary-led — describes what was done, buries the insight):**
> "So we looked at revenues and costs. Revenue was actually flat. Costs went up — both fixed and variable. The biggest cost driver was labor, which increased 18% year-on-year. We also looked at pricing and found that competitors have raised prices more aggressively. So we think the issue is a combination of cost inflation and under-pricing."

**Good (answer-led — states the conclusion, then explains):**
> "Bottom line: the margin problem is 70% cost-driven, 30% pricing gap. On costs — it is a rate issue, not a scale issue. Labor costs are up 18% while headcount is flat, which points to compensation drift and mix shift toward higher-cost roles. On pricing — we are 8 points behind the competitive index. Fix: pricing catch-up now, labor contract renegotiation at the next renewal cycle.
>
> Want me to walk through the cost detail, or go straight to the pricing strategy options?"

**What changed:** Answer in sentence one. Drivers disaggregated. Fix named. Choice offered to the listener — not a lecture.

---

### Example 4: Impromptu Verbal SCQA (30-second version)

A CEO asks in a hallway: *"Do you think we should be looking at acquisitions instead of organic growth?"*

**Weak response:** "Well, it depends on several factors — the capital available, the integration risk, the market dynamics..."

**Strong SCQA response:**
> "My view: acquisition makes more sense if speed is the constraint. [S] You're growing organically at 4%, but the market is consolidating fast. [C] The window for advantaged acquisitions is probably 18 months before multiples get bid up. [Q implies: what should we do about timing?] [A] We should run a target screen now — even if we don't act immediately, we want to know who's available and at what price. I can have a preliminary list in 72 hours."

## The Pyramid Audit (How to Review Any Draft)

When the user shares a draft and asks for review or improvement, run this five-step audit before editing:

**Step 1: Identify the governing thought**
- What is the single most important thing this communication is saying?
- Is it in the first sentence, subject line, or first slide? If not, that is problem one.

**Step 2: Test MECE logic on the supporting points**
- Do the supporting reasons together cover the full case (collectively exhaustive)?
- Do they overlap? Could merging two of them make a stronger single point?
- Are the points genuinely reasons, or are some of them evidence (one level down)?

**Step 3: Apply the vertical logic test**
- Does each supporting reason directly prove the governing thought?
- Does the evidence on each slide/section directly prove *that section's* claim?
- Would removing one reason leave the governing thought unsupported?

**Step 4: Apply the horizontal logic test**
- Can you read the section titles / slide titles in sequence and follow a clear argument?
- Are they in the right order? (Deductive: most important reason first. Inductive: build to a synthesis.)

**Step 5: "So What?" on every element**
- Every data point, chart, and exhibit needs an implication.
- If the "so what" is not in the title or caption, write it in.
- Test: if a reader only saw the title and the chart, would they know what action to take?

## Behavioral Instructions for Claude

### Building from scratch
1. Before writing anything, state the governing thought in one sentence. If you cannot, pause and resolve the analysis first.
2. Propose the SCQA framing explicitly: *"Here is the structure I'm thinking: Situation is X, Complication is Y, Question is Z, and our Answer is W. Does that capture what you need?"*
3. Build top-down: governing thought → reasons (grouped, MECE) → evidence.
4. Never bury the recommendation. Never build to a conclusion.

### Reviewing a draft
1. Run the five-step Pyramid Audit before editing.
2. Identify where the governing thought lives — surface it to the top.
3. Rewrite the opening (subject line / first slide / first paragraph) to lead with the answer.
4. Tell the user what you found and why you restructured it: *"Your recommendation was on slide 8. I moved it to the front and restructured the supporting logic — three reasons grouped by impact."*

### Writing emails
1. Subject line = governing thought (what the reader needs to know or do).
2. First sentence = repeat the governing thought as a full sentence.
3. Supporting points = 2-3 reasons or key facts, grouped, not listed randomly.
4. Close = the specific action or next step.
5. Background and context belong in the middle or as an attachment — never at the top.

### Building a board or C-suite update
1. Open with SCQA (first slide or first 60 seconds verbally).
2. Keep the governing thought visible at the top of every major section.
3. Every recommendation: *"We recommend [X] because [three reasons]. We need [specific decision] by [date]."*
4. See `references/board-communication-and-decision-support.md` for board-specific standards.

### Coaching a case candidate
1. If the candidate builds to the answer: *"Lead with your recommendation first — what is your governing thought?"*
2. If the candidate lists rather than argues: *"Those three points — do they together prove your governing thought, or are they independent observations?"*
3. After the case: *"What was your governing thought? Could a client have extracted it in 10 seconds from your opening?"*

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

| Mistake | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| No governing thought | Communication reads like a report | Ask: "What is the single most important thing here?" Put that first. |
| Deductive/inductive confusion | Reasons feel loosely connected | Decide: are the reasons *proving* the governing thought (deductive) or *summing to* it (inductive)? Apply consistently. |
| Non-MECE support | One reason absorbs another | Test: if you remove one reason, does the governing thought still hold? If yes, merge or cut. |
| Evidence at the reason level | Data points appear as top-level support | Ask: "Is this a *reason* or a *proof of a reason*?" Data is always the bottom level. |
| SCQA without a Question | Jumps from Complication to Answer with no hook | The Question earns the Answer. Without it, the Answer lands flat. Make the Question explicit. |
| Situation that is too long | Opens with 3–5 minutes of context | Situation = 1–2 sentences of shared, non-controversial fact. Anything contested belongs in the Complication. |
| Slide titles as topics | "Revenue Analysis", "Competitive Overview" | Every title is a complete sentence stating the insight — the body proves the title. |
