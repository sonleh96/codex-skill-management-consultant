# Logic and Synthesis: Building Arguments That Hold

You'll spend three years learning two things that look different but are actually the same: how to build logically sound arguments, and how to see the pattern across findings that isn't visible in any single piece of analysis. These separate a good analyst from a consultant.

## Logic vs. Evidence — Knowing the Difference

An argument has two components that operate independently: logic (structure — if A then B) and evidence (support for premises — A is actually true). Weak consulting work has one without the other.

**Good logic, weak evidence:** A beautifully structured argument built on assumptions no one has validated. You'll see this in elegant slides that collapse the moment a client asks "how do you know that's true?" The reasoning is sound, but the foundation is air.

**Good evidence, weak logic:** A pile of well-sourced facts that don't actually support the conclusion. You have interviews, benchmarks, financial data — the evidence is real. But the logical path from "here's what we found" to "therefore you should do this" is missing or broken.

Both fail. You need both. When reviewing your own work or someone else's, ask two separate questions: Is the logical structure sound? AND is each premise actually supported by evidence? If either is no, the argument fails.

## Deductive vs. Inductive Reasoning

Two modes of reasoning, both legitimate, with different strengths.

**Deductive:** You start with a general principle and apply it to a specific case. "Best-in-class SG&A ratios for this industry are 15-18%. This company's SG&A is 27%. Therefore, there is a structural cost opportunity in overhead." Deductive arguments are as strong as their weakest premise. If 15-18% is truly industry standard and your company actually operates in that industry, the conclusion is solid.

**Inductive:** You observe specific instances and generalize to a pattern. "In 18 of 23 customer interviews, respondents mentioned implementation took longer than expected and exceeded allocated budget. This suggests a systemic issue with how implementation is scoped and managed." Inductive arguments are probabilistic, not certain — but they're how most consulting insights are actually generated. You're not proving something; you're establishing probability.

Know which mode you're using. Don't present an inductive argument ("most customers report this problem") as if it were deductive certainty ("this is definitively the root cause"). The reverse trap: don't dismiss an inductive argument because it's not mathematically certain. Probability is the medium of consulting work.

## Necessary vs. Sufficient Conditions

A logical error you'll see constantly, often in your own thinking before you catch it: treating a necessary condition as if it were sufficient.

A **necessary condition** must be true for X to happen, but doesn't guarantee X. You need a product customers actually want to build a successful business — true, and necessary. But not sufficient. You also need competitive differentiation, a viable business model, execution capability.

A **sufficient condition** is rarer and more powerful. If it's true, X happens. Sufficient conditions are the real drivers; necessary conditions are the baseline.

The consulting trap: "Market research shows strong customer interest — therefore we should enter this market." Customer interest is necessary. Not sufficient. You're building a recommendation on a necessary condition and ignoring everything else the client needs to succeed. Your recommendation needs to address all the critical necessary conditions, not just the most compelling one.

## Common Logical Fallacies

These show up in client work, in your own presentations, and in the thinking of smart people. Learn to spot them fast.

**Post hoc ergo propter hoc:** "We launched the new product in Q2 and revenue increased in Q3 — the new product is driving growth." Maybe. Or Q3 is seasonally strong. Or a major competitor exited. Or your sales team finally recovered from onboarding. Correlation in time doesn't establish causation. Separate the signal from the noise.

**False dichotomy:** "We either invest $50M now in the new market or we lose share." Usually there's a $20M targeted investment that captures 80% of the benefit with half the risk. The two options presented aren't the only options. Ask: what else is possible here?

**Straw man:** Misrepresenting an opposing view to make it easier to dismiss. "Some people say we should do nothing — clearly that's not viable." No serious person was advocating doing nothing. Engage with the strongest version of the opposing argument, not the weakest. This is how you lose client trust.

**Appeal to authority:** "McKinsey's research shows..." — McKinsey's research is worth reading, but it's not a substitute for applying the framework to your specific situation with your specific data. Use research as a starting point, not as an answer.

**Hasty generalization:** "Three customers told us the product is too complex — we need to simplify." Three customers is not a representative sample. You need to know: Are these customers representative? Do the non-complaining customers have a different view? What's the distribution?

**Survivorship bias:** You analyze successful deals and miss what failed deals have in common. "All our best customers have technical expertise in their team." What about customers with technical expertise who churned? The pattern you're seeing might be invisible without understanding the failures.

## Building a Bulletproof Argument

The structure of a consulting argument that actually holds:

1. **Claim:** Your conclusion — clear, specific, falsifiable. Not "costs are high" but "the company's SG&A ratio is 27%, representing a 12-point gap versus best-in-class."

2. **Grounds:** The evidence directly supporting the claim. Benchmarks, data, research, interviews — the raw support.

3. **Warrant:** The logical connection between grounds and claim. This is where arguments are won or lost. "Revenue is declining [grounds] therefore we should cut prices [claim]" skips the warrant entirely. The warrant would be "price is the primary driver of revenue decline" — which itself needs evidence.

4. **Backing:** Support for the warrant. Why is the logical connection valid? What analysis, research, or expert judgment confirms that this logical step is sound?

5. **Qualifier:** The conditions under which the claim holds, and the degree of certainty. "Likely," "in this market segment," "assuming no competitive response." Precision matters.

6. **Rebuttal:** What would make the claim false, and why that hasn't happened. This is the move that separates consulting from advocacy. Ask yourself: What's the strongest counterargument? Why don't I believe it?

Most consulting arguments stop at grounds. You present data and hope the logic is obvious. It rarely is. The warrant is where the real work happens.

## Synthesis — Seeing the Forest

Summary is easy: "Here are the things we found." Synthesis is hard: "Here is what these findings mean together."

A summary presents findings side by side. Synthesis identifies the pattern across findings, the tension between findings, or the implication that only becomes visible when you look at multiple pieces together.

**Example 1:** Three separate analyses show three separate problems. Cost structure analysis shows overhead is high. Pricing analysis shows you're losing deals on price. Customer research shows satisfaction is declining. The synthesis: "You're caught in a value trap. Pricing pressure compresses margins, forcing cost cuts, which degrades quality, which accelerates churn. Three symptoms, one problem."

**Example 2:** Customer research shows high satisfaction. Financial analysis shows declining revenue. Competitive analysis shows share loss. The synthesis: "Customers who stay love the product. The problem isn't satisfaction — it's consideration. New customers aren't choosing you. There's an awareness or positioning problem, not a product problem."

The difference is massive. The first example changes what you recommend (don't just cut costs; solve the pricing problem at the root). The second example flips the entire strategy.

**How to force synthesis:**

- Put all your major findings on one page. Actually do this. Whiteboard or slide — doesn't matter.
- Ask: What's the "so what" that only becomes visible when you have all of these pieces?
- Ask: Where's the tension? Two findings that seem to point in different directions often reveal the most interesting insight.
- Ask: If I reduced all of this to one sentence, what would it be?

That sentence is your synthesis.

## Framework Creation — When to Build vs. Use

**When to use an existing framework:** The problem fits well, the framework is understood by the client, and the key insight isn't "here's how to use this framework" but "here's what we found inside it."

**When to build your own:** No existing framework captures the specific trade-off at the center of this problem. Using an existing framework would force the problem into a shape that doesn't fit. You need a framework that makes your specific decision clearer.

How to create a useful framework:

1. **Start with the decision, not the framework.** What does the client need to decide? What are the two or three variables that actually drive the decision?

2. **Find the natural tension.** Most useful frameworks capture a trade-off between two important things. A 2x2 is powerful because it forces a choice about the two dimensions that actually matter.

3. **Test it on real data.** Does your framework create meaningfully different categories? Do real options fall in different quadrants? If everything clusters in one corner, the framework isn't illuminating — it's just pretty.

4. **Name it clearly.** The name should make the tension or structure immediately apparent. "Transformation Speed vs. Risk Profile." Not "The Strategic Options Matrix."

The trap: Building a framework that's visually elegant but doesn't help you make the decision. A good framework creates clarity. A bad framework creates the appearance of clarity.

## From Analysis to Insight

The analysis is done. Now what?

**The analytical finding:** "Our SG&A ratio is 27%, vs. best-in-class of 15%."

**The insight:** "The 12-point SG&A gap represents $84M in annual overhead. It's driven primarily by two factors: management layers in central functions (60% of the gap) and duplicated IT support across business units (30%). This is addressable without revenue impact — the overhead isn't supporting value creation."

The move from finding to insight requires four things:

1. **Magnitude:** Not just "we're above average" but "by $84M."

2. **Drivers:** Not just "overhead is high" but "management layers and duplicated IT are the specific levers."

3. **Actionability:** What does this mean we should do? If you can't answer this, it's not an insight yet.

4. **Robustness:** Is there a reason this overhead is actually needed that we're missing? What would make this insight wrong?

An insight changes what someone does. Everything else is just analysis.

---

**The core:** Your job isn't to be smart about frameworks or to sound clever about logic. Your job is to help the client see something true they couldn't see before, and to make sure that what you're telling them is actually sound. That requires both clear thinking and honest evidence. Both matter. Master both.
