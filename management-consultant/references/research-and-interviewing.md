# Research and Interviewing: A Practitioner's Guide

## Expert Interviews

The biggest mistake junior consultants make is turning interviews into interrogations. You're not a journalist. You're not verifying facts. You're trying to understand someone's mental model.

A discussion guide is not a questionnaire. It's a map of the terrain you want to explore, but the route changes based on what you learn. Write three to five core topics you need to understand. Under each, write one opening question and maybe two follow-ups. That's it. If you write ten questions and read them in order, you've failed the interview before it starts.

The opening matters. First five minutes, you establish whether this person will tell you the truth. You do that by listening more than talking, by showing you've done your homework (not so much that you seem to know everything already—that kills candor), and by being genuinely curious rather than defensive. "Walk me through your role" beats "Tell me about your responsibilities." One feels like a conversation. One feels like a deposition.

Getting past rehearsed answers is hard because most of your sources have given some version of this interview before. You fight this by asking adjacent questions, not direct ones. If someone gives you the corporate line on customer satisfaction, don't argue. Ask "When was the last time a customer did something unexpected?" or "What's the biggest complaint you hear that you can't do anything about?" The rehearsed answer shatters because those are real questions.

Learn to love the silence. After someone answers, count to five in your head before you speak. Half the time they'll keep talking and say something true. The other half, you've given yourself time to actually think instead of nodding along.

Probing without leading is a real skill. Compare these: "So you're saying the main problem is cost?" versus "What would need to change for you to recommend this to a colleague?" The first plants a flag. The second lets them decide what matters. Use "What else?" and "Help me understand why" far more than "Is it because...?" When you feel the urge to suggest an answer, that's your sign you're leading.

A source who won't open up usually isn't hostile—they're scared. Sometimes scared of their boss finding out what they said. Sometimes scared of saying something that makes them look bad. Sometimes they just don't trust you yet. The fix isn't to push harder. It's to go smaller. "What's one thing you'd change if you could?" is less threatening than "What are the major problems in your department?" And if someone stays closed off, that's data too. It tells you this topic is politically hot or they think you're the wrong audience.

A source who gives you noise is the opposite problem. They're enthusiastic but disorganized. You can't shut them up, and you're leaving with a transcript full of war stories and nothing actionable. Manage this by being very specific: "Help me understand the economics here—what are the three biggest line items?" Forces them to think instead of ramble. And use your notes to guide them back: "You mentioned X earlier. How does that connect to the decision-making process?"

Document in real time, but not verbatim. You're capturing the insight, not transcribing a court proceeding. One sentence per key point. Within an hour of the call, write a one-page memo of what you learned and what questions it raises. Do this consistently and you'll start to see patterns before you finish the interview program. That's when you know you're ready to synthesize.

## Rapid Secondary Research

You have two days to get smart about an industry you've never worked in. You don't have time to read everything. You need a reading sequence that tells you the business model, the competitive dynamics, and what's actually changing right now.

Start with the three largest players' most recent annual reports. Read the CEO letter and the "Business Overview" section. Thirty minutes each. You're extracting: What do they say they do? What are the five to seven business segments? Where is growth? Where are they losing money? This gives you the structure.

Then read an earnings call transcript—the CEO and CFO talking to investors—from each of those three companies. Read the opening remarks and skip to the Q&A. Forty-five minutes total. You're listening for what investors are worried about and where management is defensive. Defensive answers are goldmines. "We're seeing strong momentum in the channel" when asked three times about the channel means the channel is actually dying.

Next, read one industry association report or two analyst reports (Gartner, IDC, whatever covers your sector). One hour. Analyst reports are useful because they're structured and they flag the main narrative everyone is telling about the industry. They're useless because everyone is telling the same narrative, so you'll miss the heterodox stuff.

Now read one to two articles from the business press from the last six months. Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, Financial Times. Not news bites—actual reporting. Thirty minutes. This tells you what actually happened recently, not what companies wish happened.

At this point, you know the industry structure. You've invested three to four hours and you're oriented. You can talk to a source without embarrassing yourself.

The trap is going down rabbit holes. You'll find an interesting company or a weird business model and want to understand it completely. Resist. You're building a map, not a picture of every tree. If something seems important but you don't understand it, flag it for an interview. That's better use of your time.

Document as you go. Keep a one-page summary: market size, growth rate, top five players, core business model, one to two major trends. Update it as you learn. By the time you're done, you'll have a reference you can share with junior team members.

## Voice of Customer

The difference between a customer interview and a survey is that in an interview, you can follow reality instead of your hypothesis. This matters more than you think.

Design a discovery session in three acts. First, establish context and build rapport. "What's your role? How long have you been there? What does a typical week look like?" Five minutes. You're learning their world.

Second, explore their current state and decision-making. "Walk me through how you currently solve [the problem]. What's the process? Who's involved? What's frustrating about it?" Ten minutes. You're not trying to hear that your solution is good. You're trying to understand the current workaround and why it exists. The workaround is often better designed than you'd expect. That tells you something about the constraints they're optimizing for.

Third, surface unmet needs—carefully. "If you could wave a magic wand, what would change?" is too vague and gets you dreams. Better: "What do you spend the most time on that doesn't feel essential?" or "When was the last time this process broke, and what did you do?" These questions reveal actual pain, not aspirational pain.

The most important distinction: purchase behavior versus stated preference. People will tell you they want Cadillac service if it costs nothing. They'll also tell you they'd never pay for it. What they actually do—the choice they make when money is limited and time is real—that's the signal. Watch what they choose, not what they say they want.

Avoid confirmation bias by interviewing people who don't use you and actively dislike what you're doing. This sucks but it's necessary. A customer who chose a competitor has a lot to teach you about what your solution got wrong. You're not going to change their mind. You're going to extract their reasoning. "Help me understand why you went with [competitor]. What were we missing?" They'll usually answer honestly because you're not trying to sell them.

Triangulate across different customer types. A VP of sales has a different lens than a product manager and a frontline user. They'll disagree on what matters. That disagreement is data. The VP cares about adoption. The user cares about ease. The product manager cares about retention. All three are right and all three are incomplete.

## Survey Design Basics

A survey is the right tool when you need to quantify something across a population too large to interview. It's the wrong tool when you're trying to understand why something happens or when your population is small enough to interview (generally, if N < 50, interview instead).

The cardinal rule: every question must have exactly one right answer. If a question can be interpreted two ways, you'll get noise. "How important is customer service to your business?" will generate useless variation because customer service means something different to everyone. Better: "In the last 12 months, how many customer service complaints did you receive per 1,000 customers?" Forces precision.

Avoid leading language. "Do you agree that improved efficiency would benefit your company?" is a softball. Everyone agrees. "What is the single biggest operational challenge in your function?" forces a real choice. Leading questions make you feel good because you'll get high agreement. They make your findings useless.

Keep scales consistent and reasonable. If you use a 1-10 scale for one question and a 1-5 scale for another, you've introduced noise. Pick one. And don't use scales longer than 5-7 points—people stop differentiating and start guessing after that.

Open-ended questions are powerful but expensive. "What is the biggest barrier to adoption?" generates rich data but requires human coding to extract meaning. Use them sparingly and only when you'll actually code them.

What to do with survey data: use it to quantify uncertainty bands around your interview insights. "We heard from five customers that price is the constraint. We surveyed 200 and 47 percent cited price as a primary factor." That's meaningful. What not to do: don't use survey data to find insights you didn't already suspect. Surveys are terrible at discovery. If you're running a survey hoping to stumble on something interesting, you'll stumble on noise instead.

## Triangulating Conflicting Data

You will have contradictions. A senior executive tells you the market is consolidating. An analyst report says it's fragmenting. A customer tells you that competitor gained share because of price. The competitor's earnings call says they gained share on features. This is normal. This is the job.

Start by asking: who is incentivized to tell me this version of the truth? The executive at a large player might say the market is consolidating because that's good news for large players—it justifies their size. The analyst might say it's fragmenting because fragmentation is a story that generates consulting work. Neither is lying. Both are choosing which facts to emphasize.

Assess source credibility on three dimensions. First, do they have direct observation or are they reporting hearsay? A salesperson who calls on five customers a week has direct signal. An analyst who reads earnings calls has filtered signal. Second, do they have skin in the game? A customer paying for a solution is optimizing for something real. A pundit with no customer relationship can optimize for being interesting. Third, how often have they been right in the past? This is impossible to know in a project, but you can infer it from track record and from whether they admit uncertainty.

When data conflicts, go after the mechanism. "For that to be true, what would have to change?" If the market is really consolidating, you should see evidence in bankruptcy rates, M&A activity, market share concentration. If you don't see that evidence, then either the consolidation is slower than claimed or the hypothesis is wrong. Same with the other side—if fragmentation is real, you should see new entrants, price dispersion, localized market leaders. Mechanism is harder to fake than conclusions.

Sometimes you need to explicitly build multiple scenarios. "Scenario A: the market is consolidating. That means we'd expect to see [X, Y, Z]. Scenario B: the market is fragmenting. We'd expect [A, B, C]. Here's what we actually observed: [data]. Which scenario does that support?" You might not converge on one. That's okay. Present uncertainty honestly. A client would rather have "We're 70 percent confident the market consolidates, 30 percent confident it fragments, and here's what changes the odds" than "The market is definitely consolidating."

## Knowing When You Have Enough

This is the hardest research skill to develop because it's about judgment, not method. And judgment only comes from doing it wrong multiple times.

You've hit diminishing returns when the tenth interview is telling you the same three insights as the first three interviews, phrased differently. After you've talked to seven to twelve expert sources on the same topic, you're generally not learning new things—you're building confidence in things you already know. That's useful, but it's not discovery. Know the difference.

The trap of "just one more interview" is real because talking to one more person is always easier than writing down what you've learned and making a recommendation. And you'll always find someone willing to talk. Resist. The person you haven't interviewed yet probably has the same insights as the people you have. You're burning time you should be spending on synthesis and analysis.

The skill is recognizing when your sources are explaining variation (person A did it this way, person B did it that way, but the underlying logic is the same) versus telling you that your hypothesis is wrong (you thought the constraint was cost but four independent sources said it's actually complexity). The first is data collection going normal. The second is a red flag that you need to reframe your research before you keep going.

Make a defensible recommendation from imperfect information by being explicit about what you know and don't know. "Based on interviews with six customers and secondary research on three competitors, we believe customer acquisition cost is the primary constraint on growth. We didn't interview the fourth-largest competitor because they were unresponsive, so there's some uncertainty there. The data would be stronger if we had surveyed frontline users, but time constraints prevented that." That transparency is more credible than false certainty. It's also more useful to a client because they can decide whether the gaps actually matter.

And sometimes you just have to make a call. You're never going to have perfect information. You're never going to feel entirely confident. That's not a sign you need more research. That's a sign you're ready to write.
