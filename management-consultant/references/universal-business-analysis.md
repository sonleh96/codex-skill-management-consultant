# Universal Business Analysis: The Decoder

This is your foundation. Every business in the world—regardless of industry, geography, or size—can be decoded through the same first-principles questions. If you can answer these questions, you can start doing useful consulting work in a completely unfamiliar industry within 2-3 days.

## The Five Questions That Decode Any Business

Before any framework, before any industry knowledge, five questions give you the skeleton of any business.

### Q1: What value does this business create, and for whom?

The "job to be done"—what problem does the customer have that this business solves? Be specific. Not "we provide financial services" but "we help small business owners manage cash flow when receivables are delayed." The more specific the answer, the better your grasp of the business.

**Examples:**
- **B2B SaaS:** We help manufacturing companies reduce machine downtime by 15% through predictive maintenance powered by IoT data.
- **Hospital:** We provide emergency care to patients with acute medical events within 60 minutes of arrival.
- **Chemical manufacturer:** We supply high-purity specialty chemicals that meet strict pharmaceutical manufacturing tolerances.

### Q2: How is that value delivered?

The operating model—what activities, assets, and people does it take to produce and deliver the value? Where are the key dependencies? What's the constraint (the thing that limits how much value can be delivered)?

**Examples:**
- **B2B SaaS:** Engineers build algorithms and cloud infrastructure; sales engineers integrate with customers' systems; customer success keeps customers using the product.
- **Hospital:** Emergency physicians and nurses work in shifts; diagnostic equipment (imaging, labs) operates 24/7; supply chain ensures surgical equipment availability.
- **Chemical manufacturer:** Production facilities operate continuously; raw material sourcing is global; quality control happens at multiple checkpoints; logistics moves product to customers.

### Q3: How does the business capture value (the revenue model)?

Who pays, when, how much, and on what basis? Subscription, transaction, usage, licensing, service, asset sale? What drives the price—cost-plus, value-based, competitive anchoring? What's the unit of revenue and what determines it?

**Examples:**
- **B2B SaaS:** Monthly subscription per customer, priced by number of machines monitored, negotiated enterprise contracts at 20-30% discount, payment in advance.
- **Hospital:** Per-procedure billing (DRG-based reimbursement), insurance plans pay 80%, patients pay deductibles/copays, government (Medicare/Medicaid) pays fixed rates.
- **Chemical manufacturer:** Per-ton pricing with volume discounts, multi-year contracts, prices tied to underlying commodity costs, quarterly price adjustments.

### Q4: What does it cost to create and deliver the value?

Fixed vs. variable. What are the major cost pools? What drives cost growth—volume, complexity, headcount? Where is the operating leverage (costs that don't scale with revenue)?

**Examples:**
- **B2B SaaS:** Fixed: R&D ($5M/year), customer success team ($2M/year), infrastructure ($1M/year). Variable: sales commissions (12% of revenue). Leverage: each new customer adds minimal infrastructure cost.
- **Hospital:** Fixed: facility lease/depreciation ($50M/year), base physician staffing, IT systems. Semi-variable: nursing staff (scales with patient volume in steps). Variable: medical supplies, drugs. No leverage: costs roughly scale with patient volume.
- **Chemical manufacturer:** Fixed: depreciation on $100M facility, base operators. Variable: raw materials (60% of COGS), energy (20% of COGS), logistics (10% of COGS). Leverage: each additional ton of production uses existing fixed assets.

### Q5: Why does the customer choose this over alternatives?

The competitive advantage. Is it price? Quality? Speed? Relationships? Switching costs? Network effects? Geography? IP? Each type of advantage has different durability and different attack surfaces.

**Examples:**
- **B2B SaaS:** IP advantage (proprietary algorithm), switching costs (integration depth), quality advantage (more accurate predictions than competitors).
- **Hospital:** Geographic advantage (only hospital within 20 miles), regulatory advantage (license requirements), relationships with physicians.
- **Chemical manufacturer:** Switching costs (long qualification process for new suppliers), quality reputation, cost advantage through manufacturing efficiency.

## The Business Model Decoder

Seven generic archetypes that cover most of what you'll encounter. For any new business, identify which applies—it immediately tells you what metrics matter and what levers the business has.

**Asset-based:** Value from owning and deploying physical assets (real estate, infrastructure, equipment). Revenue from utilization or yield. Key driver: asset efficiency and capital discipline. *Examples: railroads, utilities, office REITs.*

**Service/labor-based:** Value from deploying skilled people. Revenue from time, project, or retainer fees. Key driver: utilization rate, pricing power, talent leverage. *Examples: management consulting, law firms, staffing agencies.*

**Product (discrete):** Value from creating and selling products. Revenue from unit sales. Key drivers: volume, margin per unit, customer acquisition cost. *Examples: automotive, consumer goods, semiconductors.*

**Subscription/recurring:** Value from ongoing relationship and continuous delivery. Revenue from periodic fees. Key drivers: retention, lifetime value vs. CAC, expansion revenue. *Examples: SaaS, insurance, utilities.*

**Platform/marketplace:** Value from connecting multiple sides of a market. Revenue from transaction fees, subscriptions, or advertising. Key drivers: liquidity (enough buyers and sellers), network effects, take rate. *Examples: Uber, Amazon, eBay.*

**IP/licensing:** Value from intellectual property created once and monetized repeatedly. Revenue from licensing fees or royalties. Key drivers: IP durability, breadth of application, enforcement. *Examples: pharmaceutical patents, software licensing, music streaming.*

**Integrated:** Combines multiple models. Asset-based plus service (hospital owns facilities, employs clinicians, manages insurance relationships). Asset-based plus product (automaker manufactures and sells vehicles, provides financing, runs service centers).

## The Universal Value Chain

Every business has six core activities—in different proportions with different importance:

1. **Input sourcing:** Acquiring raw materials, talent, technology, capital needed
2. **Operations/production:** Creating the product or service
3. **Quality and compliance:** Ensuring output meets standards and requirements
4. **Sales and marketing:** Generating demand and converting it to revenue
5. **Delivery and service:** Getting value to customers and supporting them
6. **Support functions:** Finance, HR, IT, legal—enabling the core activities

For any business, ask: Where is the most value added? Where is the most cost concentrated? Where is competitive advantage created? The answers vary enormously—a pharmaceutical company creates most value in R&D and regulatory approval; a logistics company creates most value in delivery efficiency.

## Revenue and Cost Driver Trees

Regardless of business model, these decompositions work everywhere:

**Revenue = Volume × Price × Mix**
- Volume: units sold, customers, transactions, subscriptions, procedures
- Price: average revenue per unit, ARPU, deal size, rate, reimbursement
- Mix: composition of what's sold (higher vs. lower margin products, segments, channels)

**Costs = Fixed Costs + (Variable Costs × Volume)**
- Fixed: depreciation, base headcount, leases, system costs
- Variable: materials, transaction costs, volume-driven labor
- Semi-variable: costs that jump at volume thresholds

Understanding the fixed/variable split tells you about operating leverage and how margins change with volume.

## The Universal Diagnostic Protocol: Your First 48 Hours

When you land on an engagement in an unfamiliar industry, use this exact sequence:

**Hours 1-4: Read the basics**
- Company 10-K or annual report (read business description, risk factors, MD&A)
- Last 2-3 earnings call transcripts (management's narrative of the business)
- 1-2 industry analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IBISWorld)

**Hours 4-8: Answer the five questions**
Draft rough answers to all five fundamental questions. You're building a working model to test and refine, not getting it perfect.

**Hours 8-16: Talk to someone who knows the industry**
Ask an internal expert or industry contact: "What don't outsiders understand about this industry?" and "What metrics actually matter here?" These two questions accelerate orientation more than any reading.

**Hours 16-48: Fill the gaps**
Identify your biggest uncertainties. Prioritize reading or conversations that address highest-impact unknowns.

**By 48 hours:** You can explain the business model, identify key value drivers, name major competitive dynamics, and state your initial hypothesis. You're not an expert—but you're oriented enough to do consulting work.

## Cross-Industry Transfer: Borrow What You Know

Experienced consultants accelerate by recognizing patterns from industries they know and applying them to unfamiliar ones:

- A **logistics company's route optimization** problem is structurally similar to a **field service company's technician dispatching** problem—both are assignment/routing problems with capacity constraints.
- A **hospital's specialist utilization and patient flow** challenge is structurally similar to an **airline's seat utilization and passenger connections** challenge—both are yield management problems.
- A **professional services firm's scaling quality as headcount grows** is identical to a **franchise operator's maintaining brand consistency as locations multiply**.

When borrowing across industries, be explicit about the analogy and its limits: "This looks structurally like a yield management problem—similar to what we've seen in hospitality and airlines. The key difference here is [X]."

## What Changes Across Industries (And What Doesn't)

**What stays constant across all industries:**
- The logic of value creation, delivery, and capture
- The revenue = volume × price × mix decomposition
- Understanding the customer's job to be done
- Understanding cost structure and operating leverage
- The fundamental competitive dynamics (differentiation vs. cost leadership)
- Human behavior: incentives, organizational politics, change resistance

**What varies significantly:**
- Relevant metrics (NRR in SaaS, EBITDA margin in industrials, combined ratio in insurance, HCAHPS scores in healthcare)
- Regulatory environment and compliance constraints
- Capital intensity and asset base
- Speed of competitive response
- Role of relationships vs. product in winning customers
- Cultural and organizational dynamics

When entering a new industry, your job is to quickly learn the "what varies"—because you already know the "what stays constant."

## The Quick Orientation Test

You're ready to start consulting work when you can answer these without looking anything up:

1. Who is the customer and what problem do they have?
2. What is the operating model—what activities/assets/people create the value?
3. How does money flow (who pays, when, how much, what's the unit)?
4. What are the top 3-4 cost pools and what drives them?
5. Why do customers choose this business over alternatives?
6. Which business model archetype applies?
7. Where in the value chain is most value created? Most cost?
8. What's the fixed/variable cost split and what's the operating leverage?

If you can answer all eight confidently in 48 hours, you're oriented. You can now ask smart questions, avoid obvious mistakes, and contribute meaningfully.
