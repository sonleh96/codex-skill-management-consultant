# Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

## The Core Reality

You will never have perfect information. Your job isn't to wait for certainty — certainty isn't coming. Your job is to make the best possible decision given what you know right now, and to be honest about what you don't. That's what clients pay for. They're paying for your ability to navigate ambiguity, not to freeze in it.

The consultant who pushes back and says "we need more data" is often just slow. The consultant who makes a defensible call based on incomplete information is the one who moves things forward.

## Expected Value: The Mental Model That Matters

This is the foundation. For any decision, think through four things:

1. **What are the possible outcomes?** List them.
2. **What's the probability of each?** Be honest. If you can't quantify it, estimate.
3. **What's the value (payoff) of each outcome?** What does it mean if this happens?
4. **What's the expected value?** Probability × payoff, summed across scenarios.

Real example: Your client is deciding whether to enter a new market. Entry costs $5M. If demand is high (60% probability), they make $50M. If demand is low (40%), they lose $3M and exit.

Expected value of entering: (0.6 × $50M) + (0.4 × -$3M) = $30M - $1.2M = **$28.8M**

Expected value of staying out: $0

The answer is clear: enter. But the discipline matters more than the exact numbers. This framework stops you from overthinking one scenario at the expense of all others.

When you can't put numbers on probability, use the same logic qualitatively. What outcome is most likely? What would it have to look like for the other outcome to win? How far-fetched is that?

## The Value of Information

Before you commission that market study, ask the hard question: **will it actually change what you decide?**

A $100K study informing a $10M decision is often worth it. A $100K study helping you pick between two options with similar expected values? Probably not.

Ask yourself:
- What's the decision we're making?
- What would we decide *right now*, with current information?
- What would we have to learn to flip that decision?
- How likely is it we'd actually learn that? How fast? At what cost?

If the answer is "we'd need to learn something very unlikely," stop. You have your answer. More information isn't going to help. This rule alone will save you months of analysis time you don't have.

## Scenarios and Stress-Testing

When you can't reach a single-point estimate, scenarios are your tool. Build three:

- **Base case:** Most likely outcome given your assumptions
- **Upside case:** What happens if key assumptions go right
- **Downside case:** What happens if they go wrong

The discipline: identify the 2-3 assumptions that drive your answer. Build scenarios around those. If you're building seven scenarios, you're creating the illusion of rigor without the reality. Stop.

Then stress-test: **Does your recommendation still hold in the downside scenario?** If yes, you have a robust recommendation. If your recommendation only works in the upside, name it as such. You're making a bet, not a certainty.

## Making the Call

You've done the analysis. The data isn't conclusive. Now what?

Ask yourself:

1. **What does the preponderance of evidence suggest?** Don't fixate on one data point. What's the weight of it all?

2. **What does your gut say?** Your gut is pattern recognition from experience. If something feels wrong, that's real data. Take it seriously.

3. **What's the cost of being wrong vs. the cost of waiting?** Usually, waiting costs more than deciding.

4. **Is this reversible?** This is crucial. A wrong reversible decision (which you can fix in six months) is worth much more risk than a wrong irreversible one (an acquisition that destroys value and can't be undone).

5. **Would you bet your own money on this?** That's the test. If you wouldn't, don't recommend it.

Then decide. The uncertainty doesn't go away. But the decision gets made.

## Presenting Uncertainty With Conviction

There's a difference between being uncertain and being wishy-washy.

**Do this:**

"Our recommendation is X. We're highly confident in the direction. The key risk is Y — we'd watch that closely. If we see Z in the first six months, we revisit."

**Don't do this:**

"There are a number of factors to consider, and it's difficult to say definitively, but we might consider exploring X as one of several possible options depending on certain conditions..."

The first tells the client what to do and what to watch. The second tells them nothing. You can be clear and decisive while being completely honest about what you're uncertain about. Frame uncertainty as a risk to manage, not a reason to avoid deciding.

## Two-Speed Decision Making

Not all decisions get the same investment.

**High-stakes, hard to reverse** (entering a market, major acquisition, organization restructuring): Full analysis. Be careful. Spend the time.

**Low-stakes, reversible** (new process, team structure, marketing approach): Decide fast. You can change it in three months.

**Time-sensitive**: Know when speed beats precision. A mediocre decision you execute now often beats a perfect decision you execute six months from now.

Use the **70% rule**: If you'd make the same decision with 70% of the information you could theoretically gather, gather 70% and decide. The last 30% rarely changes the answer. And it always costs time.

## When Consensus Is Wrong

Sometimes the client's leadership team reaches consensus on something you think is a bad call. Group dynamics, sunk cost, political pressure — it happens. Your job is to speak up.

How to do it:

- **Lead with the evidence.** Not "I think" but "here's what the data shows."
- **Disagree without being disagreeable.** You can be direct without being hostile.
- **Distinguish between changing your view because of new evidence (good) and changing it because of social pressure (bad).** If the room is pushing you toward a decision and there's no new information, you need to say so.
- **Make the case once clearly, then let them decide.** You're not there to win. You're there to make sure they've thought it through.
- **If you're asked to recommend something you don't believe in, you can say no.** You won't do this often. But you can. It matters.

The client pays for your independent judgment. Use it.

## The Bottom Line

Uncertainty is the consultant's natural environment. You don't eliminate it. You navigate it. You build a defensible recommendation despite it. And you move forward.

Perfect information never comes. So you decide with what you have, you watch what happens, and you adjust. That's the work.
