# Client and Stakeholder Management

The best analysis in the world fails if you can't get it accepted and acted on. Managing clients and stakeholders is not soft — it's core consulting work.

---

## Reading the Room

Before you present anything, understand who's in the room and what they need:

**The sponsor:** The senior executive who owns the engagement. Needs the recommendation to be defensible to *their* leadership. Cares about strategic coherence, risk, and whether you're making them look smart. Give them a preview before the big room — no surprises.

**The economic buyer:** Who controls the budget for the recommendation? May or may not be the same as the sponsor. Needs to understand the financial case and the ROI clearly.

**The skeptic:** There's almost always one. Usually a functional leader whose territory is being questioned, or someone who's been burned by a previous consulting engagement. Don't try to win them over in the meeting — build the relationship before the meeting.

**The internal champion:** Someone who believes in the change and will carry it forward after the consultants leave. Find them and invest in them. Consulting that doesn't leave behind an internal champion often doesn't last.

**The passive:** People who don't have a strong view but will follow the consensus. Read whether they're leaning toward or away from your recommendation — they often decide outcomes in a close room.

---

## Pre-wiring

In consulting, surprises in the main presentation are almost always bad. By the time you're standing in front of the full stakeholder group, the recommendation should not be news.

**The practice:**
- Walk key stakeholders through your thinking 1:1 before the group session
- Gauge their reactions, surface objections, understand their priorities
- Adjust your framing (not necessarily your conclusions) based on what you learn
- Build coalitions — if you know the CFO and COO are aligned, the CEO meeting is easier

**Why this matters:**
You're not just selling an analysis — you're helping an organization make a decision. Decisions happen through relationships and conversations, not just presentations. The pre-wiring is where real alignment gets built.

## Delivering Hard Messages

At some point you'll have to tell a client something they don't want to hear — their hypothesis is wrong, their pet project doesn't have a business case, the problem is in a function they're protecting, or the answer requires more change than they're comfortable with.

**The principle: be direct, be respectful, be early.**

Early bad news is a gift. Late bad news is a crisis. If you see the analysis heading toward an uncomfortable conclusion, don't wait until the final presentation to surface it. Have the conversation with the sponsor when you first see the signal.

**The framing:**
- Lead with the facts, not the judgment: "The data shows X" before "which means your current approach has a problem"
- Acknowledge why it's difficult: "I know this isn't what we were hoping to find..."
- Connect it to their interests: What's the cost of not knowing this? What's the upside of addressing it?
- Come with a path forward: Bad news delivered without a "here's what we can do" is just pain. Always have a perspective on the next step.

**What not to do:**
- Bury the bad news in the appendix or mention it briefly at the end
- Soften it to the point where it doesn't register
- Present it as more uncertain than it is to avoid the discomfort
- Let the engagement run on assumptions you know are wrong because correcting them is awkward

## The Difficult Stakeholder

There are a few archetypes you'll encounter repeatedly:

**The "I already know the answer" client:**
They've made up their mind before you started and want you to validate it. The risk: you become a justification exercise, not a diagnostic one. The approach: be explicit early that your job is to follow the evidence wherever it leads. If the evidence confirms their hypothesis, great. If it doesn't, you'll tell them. Build trust by surfacing evidence that supports their view where it exists — this makes it credible when you push back on the view where it doesn't.

**The "scope creep" client:**
Every week there's a new thing they want you to add. The approach: be clear and direct about trade-offs. "We can do that — it'll add two weeks and we'll need to drop X from the work plan. Is that the right call?" This is not being difficult — it's being honest about capacity. If you let scope creep go unchecked, the work quality suffers and nobody wins.

**The internal competitor:**
A senior person whose function or budget is being questioned and who is actively undermining the engagement. The approach: get them in the room early. Give them the chance to provide data and context. If they feel heard, they're less likely to undermine. Don't try to go around them — try to bring them along.

**The "analysis paralysis" client:**
They're never quite ready to decide. There's always one more analysis needed. The approach: be clear about what it will take to make a decision. "If we do X analysis, will you have what you need to decide?" If the answer is yes, do it. If the answer is "probably, but..." then you have a different problem — they're not ready to decide and you need to understand why.

## Managing the Engagement

**Work plan discipline:**
The work plan isn't just an internal project management tool — it's a communication tool with the client. Review it with the sponsor at the start of each week. Make explicit trade-offs visible. If something is slipping, surface it early with a proposed adjustment.

**Weekly steering:**
Most engagements have a weekly check-in with the sponsor. Use it to: share emerging findings (not just process updates), identify blockers, preview where the work is heading, and flag any surprises before they become problems.

**The "day before the presentation" rule:**
The day before any major client presentation, you should not be working on the content. You should be reviewing it, stress-testing the logic, and pre-wiring with key stakeholders. If you're still building slides the night before, something went wrong with the work plan.

## Managing Up (to Partners/Principals)

If you're working within a consulting team, managing up is as important as managing the client.

**What partners need from you:**
- Confidence that the work is on track — proactive updates, not just responses to questions
- Early warning when something is going sideways — they'd rather know early and have time to help
- Your point of view — partners don't want to do the thinking for you. Bring a recommendation, not just a status update.
- No surprises in the client meeting

**The update format that works:**
"We're tracking to deliver [X] by [date]. The main finding so far is [Y], which is [consistent with / a change from] our hypothesis. The main risk I'm watching is [Z]. Here's what I need from you: [specific ask]."

**When to escalate:**
When the engagement is at risk — relationship deteriorating with the client, major scope change, finding that fundamentally changes the engagement direction, or a timeline you can't hit. Escalate early, come with a proposed solution, and be honest about what happened.

## Cross-Industry Pattern Recognition

After working across multiple industries, you start to see the same dynamics playing out in different contexts. Use this actively:

- "This looks like the cost structure problem I saw in [financial services] — the root cause there was vendor fragmentation in back-office tech. Let me test whether that's true here."
- "The channel conflict dynamics in this distribution business are similar to what we saw in [industrial goods]. The solution that worked there was [X], but we need to check whether the customer concentration is different here."

Pattern recognition isn't a shortcut to skip diagnosis — it's a tool to generate better hypotheses faster. Always validate the pattern against the specific context. What worked in healthcare may not work in retail. But having seen it before gives you a meaningful head start.
