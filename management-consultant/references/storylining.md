# Storylining: From Analysis to Argument

You've run the analysis. You have 200 data points, three workstreams of findings, and a pile of supporting evidence. Now you need to turn that into something a client can *act on*. That's storylining.

This isn't about making things sound nice. It's about organizing your thinking so the path from problem to solution is inevitable.

## What Storylining Actually Is

Reporting is listing what you found: "Revenue declined 12%. Customer churn increased 8%. Average order value fell 15%."

Storylining is making an *argument* with that data: "Your revenue dropped because your best customers left—not because they stopped buying, but because you stopped serving them. Here's how to win them back."

Most first decks are data dumps because the analyst hasn't spent time asking *why it matters*. You've got findings. You don't yet have insights. And insights are what drive decisions.

A storyline is a causal chain. Situation → Complication → Why That's True (the evidence) → Resolution → Impact. You're building a case.

## The Synthesis Step: From 200 Facts to 5 Key Messages

You cannot present everything. The discipline of storylining is ruthlessness about what matters.

**Affinity mapping on a whiteboard.** Write each finding on a sticky note. Physically group them by theme. You'll see clusters: market shifts, operational broken spots, capability gaps. That clustering is your first draft of structure.

**Ask: surprising or expected?**
- Expected findings are hygiene. They're true, but they're not news. Don't bury your deck in them.
- Surprising findings are where the story lives. "We thought we had a retention problem; we actually have a product problem."

**Find the "so what."**
- For each cluster, ask: "If the client does nothing about this, what happens?" That's your leverage point.
- One cluster might be: "Our salesforce is undersized relative to market opportunity." So what? "We're leaving $40M on the table." That's a storyline.
- Another: "Our pricing isn't optimized." So what? "Our margin is 200bps below peers." There's the story.

**The discipline of reduction.**
- Ask yourself: "If I could only tell the client one thing, what would it be?" That's your headline. Everything else should support it or get cut.
- If you can't identify that one thing, you haven't synthesized yet. Go back to your findings and keep squeezing.

## The SCR Structure: Situation, Complication, Resolution

This is the skeleton of every good consulting story.

**Situation.** Shared context without stating the obvious.
- Bad: "Your company makes software."
- Better: "You're the #2 player in an $8B market with 60% market share controlled by one player."
- That's specific. It positions the client and frames why they exist.

**Complication.** The tension that makes your recommendation necessary.
- Not just "something is wrong." But: "While the market is growing 12% annually, your revenue growth has slowed to 4%. Your churn rate has doubled."
- The Complication is the *gap* between where the client is and where they should be. It's why they hired you.

**Resolution.** How to close the gap.
- Not vague ("improve operations"). Specific and sequenced ("Consolidate your three regional centers into one hub, reallocate $8M in savings to digital marketing, hire 20 field reps in growth markets").
- Specific enough that the client can hand it to someone Monday morning.

Real example:
- Situation: "You're a $200M B2B SaaS company growing 15% YoY, but your net revenue retention has declined from 115% to 104% in two years."
- Complication: "That decline costs you $8M in forward revenue. At this rate, your growth will plateau at $250M in 18 months."
- Resolution: "Restructure your customer success team from reactive support to proactive expansion selling. We project NRR improves to 112% within 12 months, unlocking $35M in incremental revenue over three years."

That's a story. It's also a business case.

## Slide Logic: One Message Per Slide

Most bad decks fail because they don't have *discipline* about information density.

**One message per slide.** Not one metric. One *point*.
- Bad slide: Title "Sales Metrics," body has 8 KPIs in a table. Reader doesn't know what to think.
- Good slide: Title "Sales productivity has declined despite higher headcount" with one chart showing revenue per rep trending down.

**Write the title as the message, not the label.**
- Bad: "Customer Churn Analysis"
- Good: "Our churn accelerated in Q3 as customers migrated to lower-cost competitors"
- The reader should absorb the main point from the title alone. If they read nothing else, they still get it.

**Structure the body to *prove* the title.**
- The chart supports the claim. The data backs it up. You're building a case slide by slide.

**The "ghost deck" technique.** This is how you know your story works:
1. Write titles only. No charts, no body text. Just titles in sequence.
2. Read them top to bottom. Does the argument flow? Does each slide lead to the next?
3. If you're confused, your slide sequence is broken. Fix it before you waste time on pretty charts.
4. Once the story works, fill in the evidence.

This saves you from deck hell. You're not fixing the narrative after you've built 80 slides. You're fixing it when it's 80 title lines in a document.

## Building the Narrative Arc: Sequencing Slides for Inevitability

You're not just arranging data. You're building momentum.

**Use "therefore" and "but" not "and then."**
- "The market is growing 10% YoY. The market is growing 10% YoY *and* we're growing 4%. We're losing share."
- Better: "The market is growing 10% YoY. *But* we're only growing 4%. *Therefore* we're losing share."
- Those words create causality. They make the argument tight.

**Create the "aha moment."**
- There should be one insight in the deck where the client's thinking shifts.
- Usually that's when you separate the *symptom* from the *cause*. "Everyone thought we had a cost problem. It's actually a revenue problem."
- Once you've flipped that, the recommendation becomes obvious. The client sees it before you say it.

**Build toward a recommendation that feels inevitable.**
- By the time you get to your recommendation slide, the client should think "of course." Not "wait, where did that come from?"
- You've built the case through evidence, insight, and logic. The recommendation is the natural conclusion.

**End on action, not reflection.**
- The last slide isn't "implications" or "conclusions." It's "here's what you do on Monday."
- Specific. Sequenced. Accountable. Measurable.

## Common Storylining Failures and How to Fix Them

**The insight that's true but not useful.**
- You found that "customers in the Southwest have higher churn than the Northeast." True. So what?
- If you can't connect it to a *decision*, it's not a storyline. It's trivia.
- Fix: Ask "if this is true, what do we *do differently*?" If the answer is "nothing," cut it.

**The finding that's interesting but not decision-relevant.**
- "Our supply chain is 20% longer than our competitors'." Interesting. Does the client care? Only if it explains why they're losing.
- Fix: Connect it back to business impact. "Long lead times mean we can't respond to demand spikes, costing us 8% in lost sales."

**The recommendation that doesn't follow from the analysis.**
- Your deck shows the client has a talent retention problem. Then you recommend they acquire a competitor. You've made a jump.
- Fix: Every recommendation should feel like the inevitable answer to the Complication you've laid out.

**The story that ends before the implementation.**
- You've diagnosed the problem. You've proposed the solution. But you haven't said *how* to get there.
- Fix: Include the first 90 days. Who owns it? What's the sequencing? What's the first win you need to prove the concept?

**Missing the "red thread."**
- You have good analysis. Individual slides are solid. But they don't connect to the headline recommendation.
- Fix: Every slide should answer the question "why does this matter for the decision we're making?" If it doesn't, it's decoration.

## Testing the Story: Before You Build the Full Deck

You've got your structure. Test it before you invest in full production.

**The elevator test.**
- Pitch the entire story in 2 minutes.
- If you can't, it's not tight enough. Too many moving parts.
- You should be able to say: "Here's what they thought was true. Here's what's actually happening. Here's what it means. Here's what they should do."

**The "so what" test.**
- Read every slide title aloud. For each one, ask "so what?"
- If the answer doesn't advance the story, the slide is padding.
- "Sales productivity declined." So what? "Costs have risen." So what? "Margins are compressed, earnings will miss." Now we're moving.

**The "red thread" test.**
- Draw a line from each slide back to your headline recommendation.
- Does it connect? Does the slide explain *why* you're recommending what you're recommending?
- If a slide doesn't connect to that red thread, it's a tangent. Cut it or move it to an appendix.

**The "client reads the deck alone" test.**
- You won't be in the room for the pre-read. The deck stands alone.
- Read it cold, as if you've never heard this story. Does it make sense? Does it move you?
- If you find yourself re-reading a slide or guessing at what it means, so will they. Fix it.

## The Craft

Storylining is a learnable skill. It's not magic. It's discipline:
- Synthesis: ruthless reduction to what matters
- Structure: situation, complication, resolution
- Logic: one message per slide, message in the title
- Arc: sequencing toward an inevitable conclusion
- Testing: before you build, before you present

Most consultants get good at analysis. Fewer get good at synthesis. Better at synthesis? You're the person they want on difficult problems.

The best storylines feel effortless when you read them. That's because they're not effortless to build. You've made a hundred cuts, tested the logic a dozen times, and asked "so what" until you found the real story buried in the data.

That's the work. Do it.
