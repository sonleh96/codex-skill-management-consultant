# Implementation: A Practitioner's Reference

## The Strategy-to-Execution Gap

The gap between a brilliant strategy deck and actual change is where most transformation work dies. After 3-4 years, you learn the pattern: the sponsor loved the strategy in June, the PowerPoint was elegant, the ROI was compelling. By September, nothing fundamental has changed. The organization is back to its baseline behaviors.

The failure modes are predictable:

- **Unclear ownership.** Everyone owns it, so no one does. The strategy lives in a program office while the business runs itself.
- **Too many priorities.** The implementation becomes the 6th strategic initiative happening simultaneously. Competing for the same resources, same people's time.
- **Capability underestimation.** The plan assumes the team can execute at 100% velocity while running the business. They can't. You need 20-30% more capacity than you think.
- **Organizational overestimation.** The plan assumes steady change appetite. Reality: change fatigue, leadership turnover, market events that shift focus overnight.
- **No tracking mechanism.** Six weeks in, no one can articulate whether it's working. Red flags (missed milestones, quiet scope shrinking) go unnoticed until they're critical.

**Your role after the deck:** The strategy is the starting line, not the finish line. Your job is to make implementation physically possible. That means ruthless prioritization, clear ownership, and a governance structure that surfaces problems early instead of hiding them.

## Setting Up an Implementation

The first 30 days matter disproportionately. This is when you build the machine that runs the change.

**Governance structure:**
- **Steering committee.** Sponsor + C-level stakeholders. Monthly. Real decisions, not reviews. If steering is purely informational, the program has no air cover.
- **Workstream leads.** Typically 3-5 streams (technology, operations, commercial, organization, finance—vary by strategy). The leads own outcomes, not just activities.
- **PMO.** One person early on. Their job: track dependencies, flag risks, keep the calendar. Not to enforce compliance, but to make coordination happen.

**Decision rights—write them down.** Who decides scope changes? Who resolves workstream conflicts? At what value threshold do decisions escalate? The lack of clarity creates delays and rework. By day 30, people should know exactly whom to ask.

**Working rhythm:**
- **Weekly workstream reviews** (1.5 hours each). Status, blockers, dependencies. Led by workstream lead. Consultant observes, helps remove blockers.
- **Bi-weekly steering.** 90 minutes. Escalated decisions, cross-stream issues, progress toward phase gates. Not a deep dive on every workstream.

**Implementation charter.** Not the glossy document—the operating manual. What are we trying to achieve? What's in scope, what's out? How do we make decisions? What does done look like in 30, 60, 100 days? What are the non-negotiables vs. the adjustables? A good charter is 4-5 pages and gets referred to constantly. A bad one sits on SharePoint.

**Project plan vs. implementation plan.** A project plan is granular: Task X, dependent on Y, assigned to Z, done by date D. That's useful for month 1-2. An implementation plan is different: It shows phases (mobilize, build, scale), the logic of sequencing, how you're learning and adapting, where the biggest risks are. It's forgiving of the uncertainty that's actually there.

## The 100-Day Plan

Most implementations either move too slowly (paralyzed by planning) or too fast (chasing activity, not outcomes). The 100-day frame creates a rhythm.

**Days 1-30: Mobilize**
- Secure resources. Get the people who actually make things happen full-time or close to it. If workstream leads are 20% allocated, you've already lost.
- Design in detail. The strategy was correct at 30,000 feet. Translate it into concrete work. How exactly does your commercial model change? What system changes are required? Who needs to behave differently?
- Build the team. Hire the program lead (if not you). Confirm the workstream leads. Identify the 2-3 people in each stream who will actually execute.

**Days 30-60: Build and Pilot**
- Test key assumptions. You made bets in the strategy. Which ones are riskiest? Run small pilots. You'll learn faster than the plan anticipated.
- Generate early wins. These matter psychologically and operationally. They should be: visible (people see the change happening), meaningful (not trivial), achievable (no heroic effort), and confidence-building (this strategy might actually work). A quick win is not a substitution for real change—it's proof of concept.
- Iterate the design. Pilots will surface flaws. Better to find them now than at 100% rollout.

**Days 60-100: Scale and Stabilize**
- Full rollout begins. By now, you have a machine that works at smaller scale. Scaling is harder than it looks—training, system capacity, change fatigue. Expect friction.
- Governance operates. The weekly rhythms, steering, escalation mechanisms should be humming. If they're not, surface it now.
- Make adjustments. The market has changed, a key person left, an assumption didn't hold. Modify the plan. Document what changed and why.

## Value Tracking and Realization

You'll be asked, "Is it working?" at month 2, month 4, month 6. Without good measurement, you'll have no honest answer.

**Before you start:** Baseline. Know the current state of every metric that matters. This is harder than you think. Finance might not have clean cost data. Operations might not track cycle time accurately. Baseline anyway, even if the data is imperfect.

**Leading indicators vs. lagging indicators.** Lagging indicators (revenue, cost, margin) tell you the truth but slowly. Leading indicators (adoption rate, training completion, system uptime, process adherence) tell you whether the change is taking hold. You need both. A good dashboard has 6-8 metrics: 2-3 leading, 2-3 lagging, plus 1-2 that tell you about the change itself (resistance, momentum, capability).

**Simple value dashboard.** Not a 47-tab Excel file. One page per workstream. Current state, target state, 30/60/90/120-day milestones. Color-code it. Update it weekly. It becomes the conversation.

**At month 6, value isn't materializing.** This is where honesty matters. Ask:
- Was the assumption wrong? (The strategy itself was flawed.)
- Is execution delayed? (You're 8 weeks behind but on track.)
- Is adoption blocked? (People can do it but aren't. Change management issue.)
- Is capacity insufficient? (The organization simply can't do it at this pace.)

Each diagnosis has a different remedy. A flawed assumption requires a reset. Delayed execution requires either more resources or an extended timeline. Adoption issues require communication and incentives. Capacity issues require ruthless de-scoping.

## Change Management Reality

The textbooks talk about awareness, desire, knowledge, ability, reinforcement. That's useful framework. But here's the reality:

**Why capable people resist.** It's not usually stupidity. It's role ambiguity (what does this mean for me?), skill concern (can I do the new way?), loss aversion (I'm good at the old way), status concern (do I matter in the new structure?). These are real. Acknowledge them.

**Real resistance vs. noise.** Real resistance: "This won't work because the customer won't accept it" or "We tried this in 2015 and failed because X." Noise: "This is too much change" or "I don't like the look of the new system." Listen to real resistance. Learn from it. Noise is anxiety and usually passes once people see it working.

**Build a coalition.** Find the 20% of the organization who genuinely believe in the change. Make them visible. Give them influence. The 60% in the middle are watching to see if this is real or a fad. The 20% opposed will oppose no matter what.

**The manager in the middle.** She nods in steering committees. Then she tells her team the change is temporary, manages around it, protects the old ways. She's often the single biggest blocker. Call this out early. Either she's genuinely uncertain (education might help), or she feels threatened (you may need to reset her role). Ignoring this problem is a bet on the change failing.

**Communication.** The default is insufficient. You'll over-communicate and still people won't get it. Tailor the message: For leaders, it's business case. For managers, it's their role and trade-offs. For individual contributors, it's "what does this mean for my job next Monday?" Repeat it. Different channels, different messengers. After you think everyone understands, repeat it again.

## Running a PMO That Doesn't Suck

Most PMOs become bureaucratic overhead. They track things that don't matter and miss things that do. Here's how to avoid that:

**Weekly review structure:**
- Status (red/yellow/green). Skip this if everyone actually reads the dashboard.
- Blockers (what's preventing progress). This is the only part that matters.
- Decisions needed (what decision is blocking the next step). Escalate immediately.
- Dependencies (what depends on what). Surface conflicts early.
- Do not spend 30 minutes debating why something is yellow.

**Escalation path.** If workstream lead can't remove a blocker in 5 business days, escalate. Escalation means steering committee decides or sponsor removes impediments. Fast escalation prevents slow-motion failure.

**Governance cadence discipline.** The calendar is important, but the work is more important. If a weekly sync doesn't have a clear agenda and the output isn't a decision or unblocked work, cancel it. Death by a thousand meetings is real.

**PMO as service.** The PMO works for the workstream leads, not the other way around. Useful functions: dependency mapping, capacity planning, risk tracking, communication calendaring. Not useful: compliance checking, permission granting.

## When Implementation Is Going Off Track

The warning signs are subtle if you're not watching:

- **Teams reporting green while missing milestones.** This is the canary in the coal mine. Scope is shrinking quietly, or the team is over-confident. Ask harder questions.
- **Scope quietly shrinking.** "We'll do Phase 2 in the next wave" becomes the standard answer. By month 6, you've cut 40% of the plan without a steering decision.
- **Sponsor disengaging.** He's busy with other things. He stops asking questions. This is fatal. A sponsor who's checked out is a program with no air cover.
- **Organizational capacity genuinely insufficient.** Not as an excuse, but as a fact. You can't create capacity out of nothing. If the number is real, you have to choose: add more people, extend the timeline, or reduce scope.

**The honest conversation.** Have it early, not at month 8 when it's a crisis. "We're 6 weeks behind. This is a problem. Here are three options: add 3 people, extend by 8 weeks, or cut this workstream. Which is it?" Clear, directional, gives the sponsor a choice.

**Reset vs. course-correct.** A reset is: We were wrong about something fundamental. We need 4-6 weeks to replan. A course-correct is: We're behind or things have shifted slightly, but we're still on the trajectory. Resets are expensive. Don't call a reset just because month 2 is hard. But don't hide from a reset if it's needed.

**State of the implementation.** Write it. Monthly, to steering. Honest, specific, no sugarcoating. "We're on track for delivery but adoption is lower than expected. Here's what we're doing about it." This builds trust. Vague optimism until crisis hits destroys trust.

## The Consultant's Exit

At some point, you leave. The change either sustains or unravels. Set it up to sustain.

**Knowledge transfer.** What needs to be documented vs. internalized? Processes should be documented (so a replacement can step in). Judgment calls and decision-making should be internalized by the sponsor and program lead (through doing, not binders). Don't overweight documentation.

**Internal capability.** Build the program lead and workstream leads to run the show without you. This means they learn through decision-making, not through observation. Gradually shift decisions to them. By month 4, you're advising, not directing.

**Dependency warning.** If the implementation success depends on you staying, the design has a problem. If key decisions wait for your input, capability wasn't built. If the sponsor calls you for reassurance on every issue, she doesn't own it. These are exit risks.

**The last 30 days.** Transition all decisions to the internal team. Document assumptions and trade-offs (not just activities). Run a retrospective on what worked, what didn't, what would you do differently. Build the program lead's confidence.

**After you leave.** Check in at 30 days (usually things are fine). Check in at 90 days (this is when momentum flags or inertia locks in the change). After that, it's theirs.

---

**The meta-lesson:** Implementation is 20% strategy, 80% will and capability and clarity. You're not mostly giving advice; you're mostly removing obstacles, clarifying decisions, and building trust that this change is real and doable. The best implementations feel simple in hindsight. That simplicity comes from relentless focus and hard choices, not from better planning.
