# Scenario Planning & War-Gaming

## When to Use Scenarios vs. Forecasts

Forecasting asks: what's most likely to happen? Scenario planning asks: what's the range of futures we need to be prepared for? Use scenarios when:
- The future is genuinely uncertain (not just imprecisely measured)
- A single planning assumption could be catastrophically wrong
- Multiple plausible futures require fundamentally different strategies
- You need to stress-test a strategy before committing capital

Don't use scenarios as a substitute for a forecast when the future is actually predictable. "Scenario planning" for quarterly revenue is just bad forecasting.

---

## The Three Types of Scenarios

**Explorative:** What could plausibly happen? You're not predicting — you're building a set of distinct, credible futures that span the range of uncertainty. Used for long-horizon strategy work (5-10 years), market entry, capital allocation.

**Normative:** What future do we want to create, and what would have to be true to get there? Used for strategy articulation and vision-setting. Works backwards from aspiration to required conditions.

**Predictive:** What's most likely to happen under different assumptions? Closer to modeling — vary 2-3 key drivers and show the distribution of outcomes. Used for financial planning, investment cases, and sensitivity analysis.

Most consulting work uses explorative and predictive scenarios. Know which you're doing before you start.

## Building Explorative Scenarios: The 2x2 Method

The most practical approach for strategy work.

**Step 1: Identify the critical uncertainties**

Not all uncertainties matter equally. You're looking for:
- High impact on the business
- Genuinely uncertain (not just hard to measure, but truly unknown)
- Independent of each other

Start with 8-10 candidate uncertainties. Force-rank by impact × uncertainty. Keep the top 2 — these become your axes.

*Example: A media company planning for 2030 might identify:*
- Will streaming consolidate around 2-3 players or fragment into 20+?
- Will AI-generated content become competitive with human-produced content?

**Step 2: Build the 2x2**

Each axis runs from one extreme to the other. This creates 4 quadrants = 4 scenarios. Name them evocatively (names you'll remember matter).

| | **AI content competitive** | **AI content limited** |
|---|---|---|
| **Streaming consolidates** | "Platform Arms Race" | "Winner Takes All" |
| **Streaming fragments** | "Creative Disruption" | "Niche Renaissance" |

**Step 3: Develop each scenario**

For each quadrant, build a narrative: What happened? Who won? Who lost? What does the industry look like? What are consumers doing? This isn't a spreadsheet — it's a story. Assign a name and a vivid description. Make it real enough that people can argue about it.

**Step 4: Test your strategy against each scenario**

The strategy question: how does our proposed plan perform in each scenario? Plot it on a grid — if the strategy is strong in Scenarios A and B but catastrophic in C and D, you need to either hedge against C and D or be very confident they won't happen.

**Step 5: Identify signposts**

What would tell you which scenario is materializing? These are the early indicators to track — leading signals that a particular future is becoming more or less likely. Build a monitoring cadence into the engagement.

## War-Gaming: Testing Strategy Against Competitors

Scenario planning covers macro uncertainty. War-gaming covers competitive response. The question: if we execute this strategy, how will competitors respond, and does our strategy survive that?

**The Setup**

Divide participants into teams:
- **Blue Team** — your company, executing the strategy
- **Red Team(s)** — one team per major competitor (or one team representing all competitors)
- **Market Team** — customers, regulators, media (optional but powerful for realistic runs)
- **Observer/Judge Team** — scores outcomes, keeps the game honest

Critical: Red Team should be staffed by your smartest people, not your most junior. The most common war-gaming failure is a weak Red Team that validates your strategy by being bad at attacking it.

**The Game Structure**

*Round 0 (Preparation):* Each team briefs itself. Blue Team develops the strategy. Red Teams develop their own independent strategies — they're playing to win for their company, not to counter Blue.

*Round 1 (Year 1):* Blue announces its move. Red Teams respond. Market Team reacts. Judge Team scores market share, economics, customer response.

*Round 2 (Year 2-3):* Second-order responses. What did Blue do after Round 1? What did competitors do? This is where most strategies break — the first move looked fine, but the counter-counter-move creates the real problem.

*Debrief:* What assumptions did Blue make that Red invalidated? Where was the strategy most fragile? What modifications would make it more robust?

**What War-Gaming Actually Tests**

- Whether your competitive assumptions are realistic
- How fast competitors can respond (often faster than assumed)
- Whether your capability advantages are durable or easily replicated
- Which parts of your strategy depend on competitors not noticing

**Red Team Instructions**

Tell your Red Team: you're not here to be difficult. You're here to think like Competitor X as well as Competitor X would think. Read their public filings, investor day materials, and press releases. Understand their incentives. What would their CEO actually do?

## Signposts and Scenario Monitoring

Scenarios are only useful if you update your view as new information arrives. Build a signpost dashboard:

| Uncertainty | Indicator | "Consolidation" signal | "Fragmentation" signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Streaming market structure | # of platforms with >5% share | Declining over time | Stable or growing |
| Streaming market structure | M&A activity | Multiple mergers | Organic growth dominant |
| AI content competitiveness | % of content from AI studios | >10% of top 100 titles | <2% |

Review quarterly. When signposts shift, update your scenario probability weights. When a scenario starts clearly materializing, trigger the associated contingency plan.

## Common Scenario Planning Failures

**Scenarios that aren't actually different.** Four "scenarios" where each is basically "good/moderate/bad economic conditions" — these aren't scenarios, they're a sensitivity analysis.

**Scenarios built around single variables.** Real futures are combinations of multiple shifting forces. Single-variable scenarios (high growth vs. low growth) miss the complexity.

**No clear strategic implication.** If all four scenarios lead to the same recommendation, you didn't need the scenarios. The point is to reveal where your strategy is fragile.

**Scenarios as decorative.** Built once, presented once, forgotten. The value of scenarios comes from using them as a persistent planning lens — not a slide in a strategy deck.

**Red Teams that agree.** War-gaming with a Red Team that holds back because they don't want to embarrass the Blue Team produces false confidence. Make it competitive. Reward the Red Team for finding real vulnerabilities.
