# Managing Analysts: A Reference for First-Time Senior Associates

## The Mindset Shift: From Doing to Enabling

When you move from analyst to senior associate, your job changes from "produce the best analysis" to "help someone else produce good analysis." This sounds simple. It's not.

Your instinct will be to redo work you're not happy with. Don't. That teaches them nothing. At this stage, your job is to develop people, not to optimize this one slide. You'll do less perfect work in the short term to build better consultants in the long term. This is the trade-off you're making, and it's the right one.

## How to Brief an Analyst Well

Bad briefings cause bad work. Before you blame the analyst, ask: did I explain what I needed?

**A strong brief includes:**

- **Context**: Why does this analysis exist? What decision does it support? (Not "we need to understand customer segments" but "the partner is deciding whether to enter this market, and we need to show if the addressable market is large enough")
- **The specific question**: Not the analysis to run—the question it answers. ("What percentage of our target customers are in mid-market?" not "run a segmentation analysis")
- **What good looks like**: Describe the ideal output. ("A one-slide summary showing breakdown by segment, with revenue and growth for each, and a clear recommendation on where to focus")
- **Constraints**: Time, data available, format, audience. ("You have access to the CRM and public data only—not the financial model. Client expects this in 2 days. Format: one slide, max.")
- **Check-in point**: When do you want to see a first draft? Always before the deadline. ("Send me something Friday morning so we can iterate before Monday's presentation")

**Bad brief**: "Can you pull together some analysis on our customer segments?"

**Good brief**: "I need to show the partner that mid-market customers are our highest-growth segment. Pull revenue and growth rate by segment from the CRM for the past 3 years. One slide, simple bar chart. I want to see a draft Friday morning so we have time to refine it. If you can't get the data, flag it by Thursday afternoon."

## The First-Cut Review

This is the single most important habit you'll build. Review a first draft when there's still time to change direction—not when the output is nearly final.

At the first cut, ask:
- **Is the framing right?** Are they answering the right question, or did they miss what you actually needed?
- **Is the logic sound?** Does the analysis actually support the point they're making?
- **Is there a "so what"?** Or just data that means nothing to the client?
- **What needs to change before this goes anywhere?**

Give specific, actionable feedback. "This needs to be better" is useless. "The slide title says 'Revenue Trends'—but your data shows revenue is *slowing*. Change the title to 'Revenue Growth Is Decelerating in Mid-Market Despite Overall Growth' so the client sees the insight, not just the chart" is useful.

**The mistake I made**: Reviewing work the day before it was due, then redoing the whole thing myself. This sent the message that I didn't trust them, and they stopped taking ownership. Now I review early drafts when I can actually guide them through the revision.

## Giving Feedback That Develops People

The goal isn't just fixing this deliverable—it's building their judgment for the next one.

**What works:**
- Be specific: "This chart is hard to read" is vague. "The legend is separated from the chart by a full page of text—move it next to the chart" is clear.
- Explain the why: Not "this is wrong" but "this is wrong because the partner will see this framing and think we didn't understand the customer base."
- Make it about the work, not the person: Never "you missed this." Always "this analysis could be stronger if we included..."
- Give them a path to fix it: "Here's how I'd approach this differently—start with what decision this needs to support, then pick data that speaks to that directly."
- Be timely: Feedback the next day loses half its power. Feedback in the moment, when they remember why they made the choice, actually teaches.

**What doesn't work:**
- Redoing their work without explaining what changed: They learn nothing, and they resent you.
- Vague praise: "Pretty good" doesn't tell them what to keep doing. "This framing is really clear—I especially liked how you led with the decision" does.
- Only critical feedback: If every interaction is criticism, they stop caring about your feedback.
- Waiting until the end-of-engagement review: If something was wrong in week 2, say it then, not three weeks later.

## Protecting Your Team from Scope Creep

When the partner adds work, someone has to say "yes, but here's what we'd need to deprioritize." That's you. Analysts can't push back. You can.

When you absorb scope creep without resizing the team or timeline, you're setting your analyst up to fail—or requiring them to work a 70-hour week. Both are bad.

**The move**: "This is a great question. If we do this analysis, we'd need to either extend the timeline by 5 days or deprioritize the customer journey mapping. What's most important to the partner?" Make the trade-off explicit. Don't silently expect your analyst to absorb it.

## The Stretch Assignment Balance

You develop analysts by giving them hard work. You also have a deliverable to land. How to navigate:

- **Give stretch assignments when time allows**: Not on the deliverable that has to be right the first time.
- **Set expectations**: "I'm giving you something harder than you've done before. We'll iterate together and I'll check in more frequently."
- **Don't assign something you're not willing to teach**: If you're asking them to build a model they've never built, you need to spend time teaching it.
- **Have a backup plan**: If this goes sideways, can you reallocate work without missing the deadline?

**Bad move**: Assigning a complex financial analysis to an analyst who's never built a model, then being frustrated when it takes longer. **Good move**: "This is a stretch—here's a template from the last project, I'll walk you through how to set it up, and I want to see something on Thursday before we present to the partner on Friday."

## Reading Analyst Performance Honestly

Not all analysts develop at the same pace. Learn the difference:

- **Needs development but has potential**: Working hard, making progress, asking questions, responsive to feedback. Invest in them.
- **Working hard but struggling**: Genuinely can't do the work at the required level. This is harder—they need honest feedback about what role fits them, not more opportunities to fail.
- **Capable but checked out**: Smart enough but unmotivated. Different conversation. Find out what's driving disengagement.

Your job isn't to grade them. It's to be honest about where they are and what they need to do next.

## Building Team Culture in Week One

Engagements are short and intense. The culture you build in the first few days sticks for the whole project. What sets the tone:

- **How you handle the first mistake**: With composure and learning focus, not blame. An analyst who sees you blame them will stop taking risks.
- **Whether you model good thinking**: If you talk in pyramids and "so whats," they'll do the same. If you give vague direction and reward the first idea, they will too.
- **How you protect time**: Saying no to unnecessary requests that would consume the analyst's weekend is a statement about what you value.
- **Celebrating good work explicitly**: "I saw your analysis on customer retention—really strong logic and the charts tell a clear story" takes 30 seconds and changes how they show up the next week.

The team culture you build is your deliverable too.

---

**Remember**: This is hard. You'll redo work anyway sometimes. You'll give bad feedback. You'll miss scope creep. The difference between being a good manager and a bad one isn't that you never make these mistakes—it's that you notice when you do and adjust. Your team will follow your lead.
