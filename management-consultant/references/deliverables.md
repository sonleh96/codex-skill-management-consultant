## The Pyramid Principle in Practice

Every communication follows this structure, whether it's a 50-page deck or a 2-sentence email:

### Level 1: Governing Thought
One sentence that captures the entire message. If the reader/listener remembers nothing else, this is it.

**Bad**: "We analyzed the European market"
**Good**: "We should enter Germany first because it offers the largest addressable market with the lowest competitive intensity"

### Level 2: Key Line Arguments
2-4 reasons that support the governing thought. Each must be:
- A complete thought (sentence, not topic word)
- Independently defensible
- MECE as a group

Example supporting the Germany entry:
1. "Germany's addressable market is 2x larger than France or UK at €4.2B"
2. "The competitive landscape has only 2 national players, both with declining NPS"
3. "Our existing partnership with Deutsche Logistik gives us immediate distribution access"

### Level 3: Evidence
Each key line argument is backed by specific data, analysis, or logic.

### Ordering Within Groups
Arguments within any group must be logically ordered:
- **Time**: Past → Present → Future
- **Structure**: Revenue → Cost → Profit (follows a logical structure)
- **Importance**: Most critical first (for action-oriented audiences)

## Slide Design

### The Action Title Rule
Every slide has one — and only one — action title: a complete sentence that states the insight.

**Bad titles (topics, not insights)**:
- "Market Overview"
- "Financial Analysis"
- "Customer Segmentation"

**Good titles (insights)**:
- "The UK market is growing at 8% annually, driven by regulatory changes favoring digital adoption"
- "Switching to supplier B would save $12M annually while maintaining quality standards"
- "The top 3 customer segments account for 78% of profit, suggesting focused investment"

### Slide Body
- **One message per slide**: If you need a second message, make a second slide
- **Assertion-Evidence**: The title asserts, the body proves
- **Visual hierarchy**: The most important information is most prominent
- **Less is more**: Remove everything that doesn't support the title's message

### Chart Titles
Charts also get action titles, not descriptive labels.

**Bad**: "Revenue by segment, 2020-2024"
**Good**: "Enterprise segment drove 85% of revenue growth over 2020-2024"

### Source and Footnotes
- Every data point has a source
- State all assumptions in footnotes
- Include the date of the analysis

## The Consulting Deck Structure

### Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR) Structure

**Opening (2-3 slides)**:
1. **Situation**: Context everyone agrees on
2. **Complication**: What changed, what's the problem
3. **Resolution preview**: Our recommendation (yes, reveal the answer upfront)

**Body (the bulk of the deck)**:
- Organized by key line arguments
- Each section: argument → evidence → so what
- Transitions between sections link back to the overall narrative

**Closing (2-3 slides)**:
1. **Recommendations summary**: Specific actions with owners and timelines
2. **Impact**: Expected financial/operational impact
3. **Next steps**: Immediate actions and decision points

### The Executive Summary Slide
The most important slide in any deck. It must stand alone — if someone only reads this one slide, they understand the entire message.

Structure:
- Governing thought (bolded, at the top)
- 3-4 bullet key findings/recommendations
- Impact quantification
- Timeline/next steps

### The Ghost Deck Method
Before running any analysis, create the skeleton:
1. Write all slide titles as action titles (your hypotheses)
2. Sketch the chart/analysis that would prove each title
3. Identify what data you need
4. Prioritize: which slides are must-have vs. nice-to-have

Then fill in the evidence. If the analysis doesn't support the title, change the title — don't hide the data.

## Executive Memos

### The One-Page Memo
For decisions that need to be made. Structure:

**Header**: Decision to be made (question form)
**Recommendation**: What we recommend and why (2-3 sentences)
**Context**: Background the reader needs (brief)
**Analysis**: Key supporting evidence (3-4 points, with data)
**Risks**: What could go wrong and how we'd mitigate
**Ask**: Specific decision/action requested, with deadline

### The Situation Update
For keeping stakeholders informed. Structure:

**Bottom line up front (BLUF)**: Status in one sentence
**Progress**: What was accomplished since last update
**Issues**: What's blocking progress (with proposed solutions)
**Plan**: What happens next, with dates
**Decisions needed**: Any approvals or input required

## Data Visualization

### Chart Selection

| What you're showing | Chart type |
|---------------------|------------|
| Comparison across categories | Bar chart (horizontal for >5 categories) |
| Change over time | Line chart |
| Part of a whole | Stacked bar or waterfall (NOT pie charts — consultants don't use pie charts) |
| Distribution | Histogram |
| Correlation | Scatter plot |
| Flow / bridge | Waterfall chart |
| Process | Flowchart / swim lane |
| Geography | Map / heat map |
| Ranking | Horizontal bar chart, sorted |
| Two-dimensional comparison | 2x2 matrix or bubble chart |

### The Waterfall Chart
The signature consulting chart. Shows how you get from A to B by showing each contributor:

```
Starting Revenue: $100M
+ Volume growth: +$12M
+ Price increase: +$5M
- Customer churn: -$8M
- Mix shift: -$3M
= Ending Revenue: $106M
```

Use for: Revenue bridges, profit bridges, margin walks, any "what changed" analysis.

### Chart Design Rules
- **Title is the insight**, not the description
- **Annotate the key data points** — don't make the reader hunt
- **Gray the unimportant**, highlight the important (use color strategically)
- **Label directly** on the chart, not in a legend
- **Start axes at zero** for bar charts (line charts can break this rule if needed)
- **Simplify**: Remove gridlines, reduce decimal places, abbreviate large numbers ($12.3M not $12,347,892)

## Common Deliverable Types

### Strategic Options Assessment
When presenting the client with 2-4 strategic options:
- Describe each option clearly (1-2 slides each)
- Evaluate on consistent criteria (3-5 dimensions)
- Show a summary comparison table/matrix
- Make a recommendation (never present options without a recommendation)

### Business Case
When justifying an investment:
- Problem statement and opportunity
- Proposed solution
- Financial model (investment, returns, payback)
- Implementation plan
- Risks and mitigations
- Decision and next steps

### Benchmarking Study
When comparing performance:
- Define the peer set and justify why they're appropriate
- Show performance on key metrics
- Identify gaps vs. best-in-class
- Translate gaps into improvement opportunities with value at stake
- Prioritize actions

### Due Diligence Report
When evaluating a target:
- Executive summary with overall assessment
- Commercial due diligence (market, customers, competition)
- Financial due diligence (quality of earnings, working capital)
- Operational assessment
- Risk register with severity ratings
- Valuation and deal terms assessment

## The CEO Communication Standard

Apply these principles to everything you produce:

**Lead with the answer**: CEOs don't have time for suspense. Tell them the answer first, then prove it.

**Be specific**: "Improve efficiency" is meaningless. "Reduce order processing time from 5 days to 2 days by automating the approval workflow" is actionable.

**Quantify everything**: "Significant improvement" → "12% increase." "Many customers" → "37% of enterprise accounts."

**Use active voice**: "We recommend" not "It is recommended." "The team should" not "There should be consideration of."

**Eliminate weasel words**: Remove "somewhat," "fairly," "relatively," "potentially," "might," "could possibly." Take a position.

**One page per message**: If it takes more than one page to convey, you haven't distilled the message enough. Exceptions: detailed appendices.

**Always include next steps**: Every communication ends with specific actions, owners, and deadlines. Never leave the audience wondering "so what do we do now?"
