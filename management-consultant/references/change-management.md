# Change Management: The Practitioner's Guide

The consulting recommendation is usually the easy part. Getting an organization to actually change is where most transformations fail. This is the craft.

---

## Why Change Programs Fail

Before frameworks, understand the failure modes. Most transformation failures trace to one of five root causes:

1. **Leaders underestimate the human dimension.** They treat change as a project management problem (workstreams, milestones, PMO) and miss the harder work of shifting beliefs and behaviors.

2. **No burning platform.** People don't change unless they feel the cost of not changing. If the message is "we should change because it'll be better," the instinct is to wait. Change requires urgency that feels real.

3. **Weak coalition at the top.** The CEO is behind it but the CFO is passive and two division heads are actively skeptical. The leadership team needs to be genuinely unified — not aligned in the room and resistant in the hallway.

4. **Short-term wins not designed in.** Transformation takes 18-36 months. Without visible progress in the first 6 months, cynicism wins. Early wins aren't luck — they need to be engineered.

5. **Change declared over too early.** As soon as pressure eases, organizations revert. New behaviors aren't embedded; they're performed until the consultant leaves. Then the old way comes back.

---

## The Two Models You Need: ADKAR and Kotter

These aren't competing frameworks. They operate at different levels.

**ADKAR** (Prosci) operates at the **individual** level. It explains why a specific person resists or embraces change, and what they need to move forward.

**Kotter's 8 Steps** operates at the **organizational** level. It describes the sequence of moves the organization needs to execute.

Use both simultaneously: Kotter tells you what the org needs to do; ADKAR tells you why the people in it aren't doing it yet.

---

## ADKAR: Diagnosing Individual Resistance

Every person navigating a change needs to achieve five outcomes in sequence. If they're stuck at any one, they can't move forward regardless of what you do downstream.

**A — Awareness:** Do they understand why the change is happening? Not "I've been told." Do they genuinely understand the business context and the cost of not changing?

**D — Desire:** Do they want to participate? Awareness without desire produces compliance at best. Desire requires that the individual sees some personal benefit or alignment with their own values. Mandating change without building desire produces passive resistance.

**K — Knowledge:** Do they know how to change? Training, coaching, and tools. This is the one organizations over-invest in — they run workshops and assume knowledge will produce change. It doesn't, if desire isn't there first.

**A — Ability:** Can they actually do it? Knowledge ≠ ability. A person may know what to do but lack the time, resources, or confidence to do it. Ability barriers are often structural (their job isn't redesigned to support the new behavior) or psychological (they don't believe they can).

**R — Reinforcement:** Is the change sticking? New behaviors under stress revert to old habits unless reinforced by measurement, incentives, recognition, and accountability. Reinforcement is the most under-resourced stage.

**Using ADKAR diagnostically:**
When someone is resisting or failing to change, identify which stage they're stuck at. Don't assume they need training (Knowledge) when their actual problem is Desire or Ability. Misdiagnosed barrier = misallocated intervention.

## Kotter's 8 Steps: The Organizational Sequence

**1. Create a sense of urgency**
Not fear — urgency. The burning platform needs to be vivid, specific, and connected to things people already care about. Data helps ("we're losing market share at 3% per year"), but stories are more powerful ("here's what happened to our biggest competitor when they didn't adapt"). The CEO needs to own this message and repeat it constantly.

**2. Build a guiding coalition**
A transformation owned by a single executive or the consulting team dies when that person moves on or the engagement ends. The coalition must include: the formal sponsor (budget and authority), credible internal champions (respected peers who model the change), and functional leaders whose domains are affected. The coalition needs to actually believe in the change — not just say they do.

**3. Form a strategic vision**
The vision answers: what will be different, specifically, in 3 years if this succeeds? Not "we'll be more competitive" — "we'll have a single ERP system, halved decision cycle time, and a digital-first customer interface." Concrete. Memorable. Challenging but credible.

**4. Enlist a volunteer army**
Transformation doesn't cascade down org charts. It spreads peer to peer. Find the early adopters — the people excited about the new direction — and give them platform, tools, and permission to influence their peers. This is the "informal leader" strategy: the person others look to for cues about whether the change is real.

**5. Enable action by removing barriers**
The most common barrier: the system punishes the new behavior. If you're asking a sales team to sell the new product but compensation is still based on the old product, you haven't enabled change — you've created conflict. Structural barriers (systems, incentives, processes, org design) must be addressed, not just announced away.

**6. Generate short-term wins**
Engineer visible wins in the first 90 days. Not everything — a meaningful, visible, credible demonstration that the change is working. This serves two purposes: it gives supporters evidence to defend the transformation against critics, and it demonstrates to skeptics that progress is real.

**7. Sustain acceleration**
Early wins create credibility. Use it to tackle harder problems. This is where most programs stall — they declare victory after the quick wins and stop before embedding the change. The change is not complete when the first wave is done. Use momentum to extend scope and deepen commitment.

**8. Institute change**
The new behaviors become the default. They're embedded in systems, hiring criteria, performance management, and culture. "This is how we do things here" is the test. Not "this is what we were asked to do." Sustaining change requires connecting new behaviors to business results — people need to see the link between how they work and why the business is succeeding.

## The Resistance Typology

Not all resistance is the same. Diagnose before intervening.

**Skeptic:** "I'll believe it when I see it." Usually a rational response to past failed initiatives. Give them early evidence. Let them be right that this time is different.

**Pragmatist:** "I don't disagree, but I don't see how I'll get this done with my current workload." Legitimate constraint. Address resourcing or sequencing.

**Political resister:** "This threatens my power, budget, or team." Usually a high-power stakeholder. Engage them directly on their specific concern. Consider whether you can redesign to reduce the threat, or whether you need sponsorship to override them.

**Values-based resister:** "This is wrong and I won't do it." Rare, but real. The only interventions are leadership engagement and, ultimately, replacement if the person can't align with the direction.

**Passive aggressive:** Says yes in meetings, does nothing or the opposite outside. The most damaging archetype because they create false alignment. Surface the resistance explicitly: "When we've talked about this before you've expressed some concerns — help me understand what would need to change for you to move forward."

## Change Communication Principles

**Communicate the why before the what.** People who understand the reason for change are more likely to accept it, even when they don't like it. "We're doing this because our cost structure is uncompetitive and we'll lose market share in 18 months if we don't act" is more powerful than "we've decided to restructure."

**Leaders must communicate in person, repeatedly, personally.** Cascading slide decks don't create belief. The CEO saying the same thing 20 times in town halls, skip-level conversations, and 1:1s does. The "say it 7 times" rule: by the time a leader is tired of saying the message, the front line has heard it once.

**Address the personal questions first.** "Will I lose my job? Will my role change? Will my pay change?" People can't engage with organizational arguments until their personal questions are answered. Answer what you can. Be honest about what you can't answer yet.

**Two-way over broadcast.** Change communication that's one-directional breeds suspicion. Build in genuine listening: town halls with real Q&A, pulse surveys, manager-level feedback loops. When you hear concerns, respond to them visibly.

## Measuring Change Readiness

Track leading indicators, not just lagging ones.

**Leading indicators (during the change):**
- Awareness survey scores ("Do you understand why this change is happening?")
- Resistance rate by ADKAR stage ("What % of employees have moved to Desire?")
- Coalition member engagement (Are your champions actually championing?)
- Early adoption rate of new behaviors/tools

**Lagging indicators (outcomes):**
- Business performance metrics tied to the change
- Employee engagement vs. baseline
- Process adherence (are people using the new system/process?)
- Relapse rate (are people reverting to old behaviors?)

Pulse it monthly during the first 12 months. Don't wait for the annual engagement survey to find out the change is failing.
