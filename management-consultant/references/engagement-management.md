# Engagement Management

The quality of the work isn't enough. Engagements fail because of poor scoping, misaligned expectations, scope creep, or a team that's working hard on the wrong things. This is the operational discipline that makes great work actually land.

---

## Scoping: The Most Important 48 Hours

Bad scoping is the most common source of engagement failure. Too narrow and you deliver technically but miss the real question. Too broad and you're chasing everything and delivering nothing. Wrong framing and the work is irrelevant.

**The scoping conversation:**

Before writing a single slide or proposal, have a 60-minute conversation with the key client stakeholders that answers:

1. **What is the actual decision this engagement should enable?** Not "understand our cost structure" — what decision gets made differently because of this work?

2. **What is the cost of inaction?** This tells you how much the client cares. "We're curious" is different from "we need this to make a $200M capital allocation decision by Q3."

3. **What have you already tried?** Most clients have relevant history. Previous initiatives, failed programs, analyses they ran internally. Not knowing this leads to delivering a recommendation they already considered and rejected.

4. **Who will use this work, and how?** The CEO using it to make a board presentation has different needs than an operational team using it to redesign a process. Know the end user.

5. **What does success look like, and how will it be measured?** If you can't answer this, you're not scoped.

6. **What's in and out of scope?** Be explicit. Unspoken scope assumptions kill engagements. If you're analyzing the North America business and not the international operations, write that down.

**The scope document:**
Before work begins, produce a 1-2 page scope memo:
- Problem statement (in the client's terms)
- Objectives (specific, testable)
- Scope (what's included and what's explicitly excluded)
- Deliverables (formats, audiences, dates)
- Key assumptions (what you're taking as given)
- Dependencies (what you need from the client and when)

This isn't bureaucracy. It's the document you return to when scope starts creeping.

## Workplan Design

A good workplan is a hypothesis about how to answer the client's question. It's built backward from the deliverable.

**Step 1: Define the outputs**
Start with the final deliverable — usually a set of slides or a recommendation memo. What are the 5-8 key findings you expect to need? This becomes the "ghost deck" (see storylining module).

**Step 2: Define the analyses**
For each finding, what analysis produces it? For each analysis, what data do you need?

**Step 3: Map the timeline**
Work backward from the final presentation date. Milestones:
- Client check-in / interim readout (usually ~60% through the engagement)
- Draft recommendation complete (usually ~80%)
- Internal review / partner review (usually ~90%)
- Final delivery

**Step 4: Assign ownership**
Every workstream has one owner. Not "the team" — one named person who is accountable for the output, the timeline, and the quality.

**Step 5: Identify dependencies and risks**
What could delay the work? Data access, client availability for interviews, third-party information. Flag these on Day 1. The engagement manager's job is to eliminate blockers before they become delays.

**The workplan format:**
A simple table works:
| Workstream | Owner | Key analyses | Data needed | Client dependency | Week 1 output | Final output |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Update it weekly. It's a living document, not a launch artifact.

## Managing the Team

**The brief:**
Every analyst and associate should receive a written brief before starting any workstream:
- The question you're answering
- Why it matters (how it fits into the engagement)
- The specific analysis to run
- The data to use (with access instructions)
- The format of the output (slide, model, memo)
- The timeline
- What a first cut looks like and when you want to see it

Verbal briefs get interpreted. Written briefs get executed.

**The first-cut review:**
Ask for a first cut early — not because you expect it to be final, but because it reveals misunderstanding of the brief before it compounds. "Show me where you're headed" after Day 1 of analysis saves you from reviewing a week of misdirected work.

**Managing up the escalation ladder:**
When something is wrong with the engagement — scope changing, client expectations shifting, team running behind — escalate early. Partners would rather hear a problem on Day 5 than on Day 25. The instinct to "handle it yourself" protects your reputation in the short term and damages it in the long term.

## Client Relationship Management During Engagements

**The weekly status meeting:**
Keep it tight. Standard agenda:
- What we completed this week (specific outputs, not activities)
- What we found (early emerging findings — not just "we're analyzing")
- What we're working on next week
- What we need from the client (specific asks, with owners and dates)
- Open issues

The common failure: spending 90% of the status on process and 10% on content. Flip it.

**The pre-read:**
Before any client meeting with substantive content, send a pre-read 24-48 hours in advance. This:
- Respects the client's time (they can engage with the content, not just absorb it)
- Reveals misunderstandings before the meeting (if the pre-read gets a reaction, you can adjust)
- Creates a written record of where the work is

Pre-reads should be tighter than the meeting materials. The rule: if someone reads only the pre-read, they should understand the main message and be ready to engage.

**The check-in call:**
In addition to formal status meetings, maintain informal contact with the day-to-day client sponsor — a quick call or message to stay connected to what's happening in the client's world. Engagements that become "the consultants are doing their thing" and lose daily connection to client priorities produce irrelevant recommendations.

## Scope Creep: Prevention and Management

Scope creep is universal. The question is whether it's managed or not.

**Prevention:**
- Explicit scope document reviewed at kickoff
- Clear statement of what's out of scope
- "That's a great question — let me check if it's within our current scope" as a standard response to new requests

**When scope expands:**
Not all scope expansion is bad. Sometimes you discover the original question was the wrong question. The issue is when scope expands without corresponding adjustment to timeline, resources, or quality expectations.

When a client asks for something outside scope, the response:
1. Acknowledge the value of the request
2. Clarify what's involved: "That would require [X analysis / Y weeks of additional work]"
3. Offer a choice: "We can add this to the current scope if we extend the timeline by two weeks, or we can scope a second phase"
4. Never just absorb it silently — the team will pay for it in quality or burnout

**The "one more slide" trap:**
Clients often request additions to the final deck that seem small but cumulatively expand scope significantly. Apply the same logic: "Happy to add that. To keep the deck tight, what should we remove, or should we move that to the appendix?"

## Engagement Closure

**The final readout:**
Not a presentation of analysis — a recommendation with a clear decision being sought. Structure:
- Situation (brief — the client knows their situation)
- Key findings (the 3-5 things we learned that matter)
- Recommendation (specific, actionable)
- Implementation plan (who does what, when)
- Expected impact (financial and operational)
- Risks and mitigations

**Knowledge transfer:**
Before the engagement ends, identify what needs to transfer to the client team:
- Models and tools they'll maintain
- Analytical frameworks they can reapply
- Key contacts (experts, data sources) you leveraged

An engagement that leaves no lasting capability is a missed opportunity to build a long-term relationship.

**The debrief:**
Internal team debrief within one week of engagement close: What went well? What would we do differently? What surprised us? What would we do first if we were starting again?

This institutionalizes learning. Without it, the same mistakes repeat across engagements.
