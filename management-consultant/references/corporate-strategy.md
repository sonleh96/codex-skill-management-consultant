# Corporate Strategy & Portfolio Management

Corporate strategy answers a different question than business unit strategy. Business unit strategy asks: how do we compete in this market? Corporate strategy asks: which markets should we be in, and does it make sense to own them together?

---

## The Corporate Parenting Question

The fundamental justification for a diversified corporation is that the corporate parent creates more value for each business unit than those businesses could create independently. If this isn't true — if the businesses would be worth more as standalone companies — the corporation is destroying value and should either restructure or divest.

This is called **parenting advantage**: the distinctive ways the corporate center adds value to its business units that justify the cost, complexity, and constraints of operating within a larger organization.

**Four mechanisms of parenting value:**

1. **Standalone improvement:** The corporate center actively improves the performance of individual BUs through better management practices, talent allocation, capital, and operational discipline. Think PE firms driving portfolio company performance.

2. **Cross-unit linkages:** Shared customers, shared R&D, shared procurement, shared platforms. The BUs together create more value than separately because they share assets, capabilities, or access.

3. **Centralized services:** Shared legal, finance, HR, IT — functions where scale creates efficiency. Only adds value if the centralized service is genuinely better or cheaper than each BU could achieve independently.

4. **Portfolio composition:** The right mix of businesses (diversification benefits, counter-cyclical balance) or the discipline to add/remove businesses through M&A and divestitures.

**The Parenting Fit Matrix (Ashridge):**
Map each business against two dimensions: how well does the parent understand the business (parenting fit), and how much value can the parent add (parenting opportunity)?

- **Heartland:** High fit, high opportunity. These are core businesses the parent should own and invest in.
- **Edge of Heartland:** Potential fit if parent develops new capabilities. Judgment call — invest in fit or exit.
- **Ballast:** Parent understands these businesses but doesn't add much. They do no harm but no good. Consider divesting to a better parent.
- **Value Trap:** Parent adds value in some ways but has low fit overall — often leads to mismanagement.
- **Alien Territory:** Poor fit and limited value creation. Divest.

## Total Shareholder Return (TSR) Analysis

TSR = share price appreciation + dividends, expressed as an annualized %. It's the ultimate measure of corporate value creation.

**What drives TSR:** BCG's research identifies five paths to TSR outperformance:
1. Revenue growth (drives earnings growth)
2. Margin expansion (drives earnings growth independently)
3. Multiple expansion (the market re-rates the business upward)
4. Dividends and share buybacks (direct return to shareholders)
5. Deleveraging (reducing financial risk improves equity value)

The art of corporate strategy is choosing the right TSR path given competitive position, capital structure, and market expectations. A company trading at a discount multiple should focus on multiple expansion (re-rating the story). A company with excess cash in a declining market should return capital. A growth company should prioritize revenue growth and margin expansion.

**TSR decomposition:**
To understand what actually drove TSR over a period:
- Earnings contribution: Change in EPS × starting P/E multiple
- Multiple contribution: Starting EPS × change in P/E
- Dividend contribution: Dividend yield over period

This tells you whether TSR came from actual business performance (earnings) or market sentiment change (multiple) — fundamentally different stories.

## Capital Allocation

The CEO's most important job is deciding where capital goes. Poor capital allocation compounds over time — every dollar deployed in a low-return business is a dollar not invested in a high-return one.

**The capital allocation hierarchy:**

1. **Maintain the core** — Sustaining capex to maintain existing competitive position (non-negotiable)
2. **Grow the core** — Organic growth investment in existing businesses with positive NPV
3. **Adjacencies and M&A** — Enter new markets or acquire capabilities
4. **Return capital** — Buybacks and dividends when internal investment opportunities don't clear the hurdle rate

The discipline: define the hurdle rate clearly (WACC + risk premium appropriate to the investment) and apply it consistently. "Strategic investments" that don't clear the hurdle but are funded anyway are a symptom of weak capital allocation governance.

**Common capital allocation failures:**
- **The gravity trap:** Capital flows to legacy businesses by default (they have the most staff, history, and political weight), even when those businesses have the lowest return potential.
- **Average ROIC thinking:** Looking at corporate average ROIC instead of incremental ROIC on new investments. The average includes historical sunk investments; what matters is what the next dollar earns.
- **Synergy overestimation:** M&A capital deployed against synergies that don't materialize — a chronic destroyer of corporate value.

## Portfolio Management

**The portfolio role framework:**

Assign each business unit an explicit role in the portfolio. The role determines capital allocation, performance expectations, and exit criteria.

- **Scale for growth:** Invest aggressively to win market share. Accept lower short-term margins for longer-term position. Measure on growth, share, and future positioning.
- **Harvest/optimize:** Maximize cash generation from a mature, competitive position. Limit reinvestment. Measure on margin and cash conversion.
- **Turn around:** Restore competitive position in a business that has fallen behind. Accept investment and losses in the short term. Set a clear time horizon — if the turnaround doesn't materialize, exit.
- **Exit/divest:** The business has no path to competitive advantage, or a better parent exists. Maximize value in the sale process.

The critical discipline: be explicit about the role. Many corporate failures come from treating a "harvest" business as a "scale for growth" business (over-investing in a declining franchise) or treating a "scale for growth" business as a "harvest" business (under-funding a competitive opportunity).

**Portfolio rebalancing:**
Active portfolio managers treat the portfolio as dynamic, not static. Standard triggers for rebalancing:
- A business unit underperforms against its role for 2+ consecutive years
- A strategic shift changes the parenting advantage thesis
- A divestiture opportunity arises at an attractive multiple
- An acquisition opportunity arises that improves portfolio positioning

## Corporate Center Design

The corporate center creates value and costs. As the portfolio grows, the center tends to grow — adding staff, creating processes, and building bureaucracy that constrains BU agility without adding proportionate value.

**The center's appropriate scope:**
- Strategy and portfolio management (the CEO and strategy function)
- Capital allocation (CFO and finance)
- Risk management and compliance (legal, audit, risk)
- Talent pipeline for key roles (CHRO)
- Shared services where scale genuinely creates efficiency

**What the center should NOT do:**
- Approve operating decisions BUs can make more quickly and with better information
- Create processes that don't serve the BUs
- Build redundant capabilities the BUs already have

The test: for every corporate center activity, ask — does this add more value than it costs (direct cost + BU time + lost agility)? If not, eliminate it or push it to the BUs.

## M&A as Corporate Strategy

M&A creates value in theory. In practice, over half of large acquisitions destroy shareholder value. The failures cluster around a few patterns:

**Strategic rationale:**
Valid reasons to acquire:
- Capabilities you need and can't build fast enough
- Market access or geographic entry
- Scale economics that genuinely exist (not assumed)
- Competitive preemption (blocking a competitor from a key asset)

Invalid reasons: "Diversification" (shareholders can diversify themselves), "Synergies" as the primary rationale without specifying mechanisms, strategic FOMO ("everyone else is doing it").

**Synergy credibility:**
Cost synergies (procurement, headcount, facilities) are more achievable and faster. Revenue synergies (cross-sell, market access) are often right in theory but take 2-3x longer and cost more than modeled. In an acquisition case:
- Mark revenue synergies at 50% of the thesis value until proven
- Cost synergies: 80% if they're head-count driven, 60% if procurement/process dependent
- Discount all synergies for integration cost — typically 10-20% of annual synergy value as one-time cost

**Integration design:**
Decide on the integration model before you close:
- **Absorption:** Acquire and fully integrate — one operating model, one system, one culture. Maximum synergy, maximum disruption risk.
- **Preservation:** Acquire and leave largely independent — protect the culture and capability you bought. Minimum synergy, minimum disruption.
- **Symbiosis:** Selectively integrate where synergies are clear, preserve where uniqueness matters. Complex to execute, but the right model for capability acquisitions.
