# Board Communication and Decision Support

Board decks are not management decks. The audience, purpose, and success criteria are different — and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes consultants and management teams make.

This file provides the practical standard for what board-level communication looks and feels like, how it differs from management reporting, and how to structure materials that actually get directors to the decision you need them to make.

This file works alongside `references/board-governance-future.md` (which covers board composition, skills matrices, and governance structure) and `references/pyramid-principle-and-scqa.md` (which covers the SCQA opening). This file is about *how you communicate* in the boardroom.

---

## The Fundamental Difference: Oversight vs. Management

Management decks and board decks serve opposite purposes.

| Dimension | Management Deck | Board Deck |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Align the team on execution | Inform, challenge, and get decisions from oversight body |
| Audience mindset | "How do we do this?" | "Should we do this? Are the risks managed? Do we have confidence in management?" |
| Information depth | Operational detail, process steps, workstream status | Strategic context, key risks, options and tradeoffs, decision request |
| Assumptions | Shared — the team knows the business | Explicit — directors may not know the detail |
| Length | As long as needed | Shorter is almost always better; directors read in advance |
| Tone | Collaborative, working | Formal, authoritative, decision-ready |
| What triggers success | Team aligned and moving | Board confident in management and decision made |

**The most common mistake:** Taking a management deck and "presenting it to the board." The result is a 40-slide operational briefing that buries the decision request in slide 32, exhausts the board's attention, and leaves directors unsure what they're being asked to do.

---

## What the Board Actually Needs

Directors are time-poor, often come from different functional backgrounds, and are responsible for oversight — not execution. They need to answer three questions in every board meeting:

1. **Are we on track strategically?** (Are we executing the right strategy? Has anything changed that requires a strategic pivot?)
2. **Are the major risks being managed?** (What are the top risks to the business, and do we have confidence that management is on top of them?)
3. **What decisions do we need to make?** (What requires board-level approval, direction, or endorsement today?)

Every element of board communication should serve one of these three needs. If a slide or section doesn't serve any of them, it probably belongs in a management pack, not a board pack.

## Structuring a Board Pack

### Opening: Context and Governing Thought

Use SCQA for the executive summary or opening remarks. See `references/pyramid-principle-and-scqa.md`.

The executive summary — ideally one page — should state:
- The current strategic position (Situation)
- What has changed or what tension exists (Complication)
- The question the board needs to engage with (Question)
- Management's recommendation or request (Answer)

Directors should be able to read the executive summary alone and understand exactly what they're being asked to do.

### Strategic Performance Section

**What to include:**
- 3–5 strategic KPIs with clear trend (green/amber/red RAG status against target)
- Brief narrative on what's driving variance — not what happened, but what it means
- Any change to strategic outlook since the last meeting

**What to exclude:**
- Every operational metric tracked by the business
- Process updates ("we have been working on...")
- Detailed financial tables without a narrative "so what"

**Principle:** Board members are not auditors. They do not need to see all the data — they need to see the data that would change their view of the strategic position.

### Risk Section

This is often the most underdeveloped section in board packs, and the one boards care most about.

**Structure:**
1. Top 5 risks: description, likelihood, potential impact, current mitigation, risk owner
2. Changes since last meeting: which risks escalated, which de-escalated, any new risks emerged
3. One deep-dive risk: at each meeting, one risk gets a full treatment — root causes, scenario analysis, mitigation roadmap

**The risk heatmap:** A 2×2 (likelihood × impact) visual of the top risks is the single most efficient way to communicate risk posture. Directors can absorb it in 10 seconds. Update it every meeting. Changes signal that management is actively managing the portfolio, not just reporting it.

**Common mistake:** Listing risks without likelihood and impact ratings. A list of risks with no calibration gives directors no way to prioritize their attention.

### Decision Items

Every board meeting should have explicit decision items — matters that require board-level approval, direction, or endorsement.

**Format for each decision item:**
```
DECISION REQUIRED: [one-sentence statement of what the board is being asked to approve]

Context: [2–3 sentences of background — no more]

Options considered: [typically 2–3 options, briefly characterized]

Management's recommendation: [what management believes is the right path and the top 2 reasons]

Key risks: [the 1–2 risks most relevant to the decision]

If approved: [what happens next, who does what, by when]

If not approved: [what are the consequences of deferring]
```

Directors should be able to make the decision from this one-page summary. Supporting detail belongs in an appendix.

**Principle:** Never let a board meeting end without clarity on what was decided. Every agenda item should have either a decision outcome or an explicit deferral with a timeline.

### Appendix

The appendix is not a dumping ground — it is a deliberate archive of supporting evidence.

Good appendix material:
- Detailed financial tables referenced in the board pack
- Full risk registry (beyond the top 5)
- Supporting analysis for any key claim in the pack
- Minutes and action items from the previous meeting

Bad appendix material:
- Slides cut from a management deck that have no decision relevance
- Data that management finds interesting but that doesn't inform any board-level question

## The Board Deck Length Principle

Target: 10–15 slides for a regular board meeting. 20–25 slides for a strategy review. Never more than 30 slides for any board meeting.

If you cannot make the case in that space, the problem is in the clarity of thinking, not in the page limit.

**Pre-reading vs. presentation:** Boards read packs before the meeting. The meeting is for discussion and decision, not for the management team to read slides to directors. Design the pack for pre-reading. Design the verbal presentation as a 5–10 minute summary and discussion facilitation, not a slide walk-through.

## Verbal Communication in the Boardroom

### Presenting to the Board

**Opening:** "Good morning. Three things today: a strategic performance update, a deep-dive on [risk/opportunity], and two decisions we need your direction on. The pack is in front of you — I'll take 8 minutes to highlight the key points and then we have 40 minutes for discussion."

**The 30-second rule:** Every major point you make verbally should be expressible in 30 seconds. If you are still explaining after 60 seconds without a question, you have lost the room.

**When a director asks a question:**
1. Answer the question directly — lead with the answer, then the reasoning.
2. If you don't know: "I don't have that number in front of me. I'll get it to you by [specific time]." Never guess in a board meeting.
3. If the question is outside the meeting agenda: "That's an important question — let me make a note and either respond in the pack or schedule a separate briefing."

**Reading the room:** Directors signal engagement through body language and questions. If the chair is summarizing and moving on, you've answered the question. If directors are leaning in and asking follow-ups, you have their attention — go deeper. If phones are out, you have lost them — shorten and get to the decision.

### Delivering Hard Messages

Boards need to hear hard truths. The consultant or management team that softens bad news or buries it in slide 28 loses credibility faster than the one that delivers it directly.

**The structure for bad news:**
1. State the problem directly in the first sentence. ("We missed our Q3 revenue target by 18% due to an unexpected customer churn event.")
2. Explain the root cause — briefly and honestly. ("Three large enterprise customers did not renew, driven by competitive pricing pressure in our core segment.")
3. State what you're doing about it. ("We have initiated a pricing review and retained two of the three customers on revised terms. One remains at risk.")
4. State what the board needs to know or do. ("We are managing this within current-year guidance, but if the third customer churns, we will need to revise full-year forecasts. We will update you within two weeks with confirmed Q4 guidance.")

**What not to do:** Describe the situation at length before acknowledging the bad outcome. Directors will sense the build-up and lose confidence. State the bad news first.

## Consulting Deliverables Designed for Boards

When building consulting deliverables specifically for board consumption, apply these standards:

### Board-Ready Recommendation Deck

**Structure:**
1. Executive summary (1 page, SCQA structure) — the full recommendation in one page
2. Strategic context (1–2 slides) — why this matters and why now
3. Key findings (3–4 slides) — the evidence that drives the recommendation
4. Recommendation (1–2 slides) — what we're recommending and why
5. Risk and mitigation (1 slide) — top 3 risks and how we've addressed them
6. Decision request (1 slide) — explicit: "We are asking the board to approve [X] today"
7. Implementation overview (1 slide) — who does what by when; governance model
8. Appendix — detailed analysis, alternative options, supporting data

**Principles:**
- Every slide title is an action title (complete sentence, states the insight)
- Risk and uncertainty are stated explicitly — not buried
- The recommendation is in the first 2 minutes of any verbal presentation

### Board Risk Register Update

**Format per risk:**
```
Risk: [One-line description]
Category: [Strategic / Operational / Financial / Regulatory / Reputational]
Likelihood: [High / Medium / Low with brief rationale]
Impact if realized: [$X, or qualitative severity]
Current mitigation: [What is in place today]
Residual risk: [Risk level after mitigation]
Owner: [Named executive]
Change since last review: [Escalated / Stable / De-escalated / New]
```

### Board Education Session (for capability building)

When running a board education session (AI governance, ESG, cyber, geopolitical risk):
- Format: Workshop, not lecture. Directors engage when they can challenge and question.
- Length: 2–4 hours maximum
- Structure: Context → case studies (real examples from peer companies) → implication for this board → discussion
- Outcome: Directors leave with 3–5 questions they should be asking management going forward
- Follow-up: Reference materials distributed post-session; one-page "Board Questions" guide per topic

## Common Board Communication Mistakes — and How to Fix Them

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Too much detail | Management wants to demonstrate effort | Boards evaluate judgment, not effort. Cut to what changes the director's view. |
| Decision request buried | Management nervous to ask | State the decision request on slide 1. Repeat it on the final slide. |
| No "so what" on risks | Risk section is a compliance exercise | For every risk, state: "Here is what this means for our strategy and what we are doing about it." |
| Recommendations that hedge | Fear of being wrong | State a clear recommendation. Acknowledge the key risk. Boards want conviction, not optionality. |
| Presenting at the board instead of to it | Default to slide walk-through | Send the pack 5 days early. Spend the meeting on discussion and decisions, not reading. |
| Avoiding the bad news | Fear of director reaction | Bad news delivered early and with a mitigation plan builds credibility. Buried bad news destroys it. |
| No clear decision trail | Minutes are vague | Every item should end: "Approved / Not approved / Deferred to [date] pending [condition]." |
