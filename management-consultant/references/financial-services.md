# Financial Services Consulting Reference

## 1. Banking Sector Economics

### Core Performance Metrics

**Net Interest Margin (NIM)** represents the spread between what a bank earns on assets versus what it pays on liabilities. It's the most fundamental driver of profitability in traditional banking. A healthy NIM typically ranges from 2.5% to 4.0%, depending on geography, market rates, and competitive intensity. The WHY: NIM compresses in low-rate environments and under intense competition, forcing banks to seek fee income or cut costs. Strategic pricing discipline and deposit franchise strength directly determine NIM sustainability.

**Return on Equity (ROE)** and **Return on Assets (ROA)** measure capital efficiency. Industry targets: 12%+ ROE, 0.8-1.2% ROA. ROE is particularly sensitive to leverage levels (Tier 1 capital ratios), which are now constrained by Basel III/IV regulations. A bank with 11% ROA but 12x leverage will show ~9-10% ROE before regulatory and operational deductions.

**Cost-to-Income Ratio** benchmarks operational efficiency. Best-in-class universal banks operate at 50-55%, while retail specialists achieve 45-50%. Investment banking divisions run 55-70% due to variable compensation. This metric explains why digital banks initially promised 20-30% ratios—they underestimated fraud, compliance, and customer acquisition costs.

**Non-Performing Loan (NPL) Ratios** typically range 0.5-2.0% depending on economic cycle and underwriting standards. When NPLs exceed 3%, capital is consumed by provisions, reducing lending capacity. During crises (2008-2009), NPLs hit 5-10%, triggering forced deleveraging.

**Loan-to-Deposit Ratio (LTD)** measures liquidity and funding stability. Conservative: 75-80%; aggressive: 95-105%. Above 100% means the bank funds loans through wholesale markets—a red flag during stress periods (2008, 2011, 2020).

### Regulatory Capital Framework: Basel III & Basel IV

**Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) Capital** is the highest quality capital. Basel III mandates 4.5% CET1 against risk-weighted assets (RWA). With additional buffers (Capital Conservation Buffer 2.5%, Countercyclical Buffer 0-2.5%), minimum CET1 reaches 7-9.5%. G-SIBs (Globally Systemically Important Banks) add another 1.0-3.5% based on systemic importance.

**Basel IV**, finalized in December 2017, introduces output floors (minimum 72.5% of standardized approach capital) and more granular risk-weighting for corporate and real estate exposures. This increased RWA by 10-15% for major banks, forcing either capital raises or loan derisking.

**Stress Capital Buffer (SCB)** replaces the Basel III buffer for U.S. banks, set individually based on CCAR stress test results. A bank failing to generate enough capital under stress must raise equity or reduce dividends—a powerful disciplinary mechanism.

### Liquidity Regulation

**Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)** ensures banks hold sufficient high-quality liquid assets to survive 30 days of stress. Target: ≥100%. Most G-SIBs operate 120-150%. The WHY: 2008 showed that even solvent banks face death from liquidity drought.

**Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)** forces funding stability over one year. Non-stable funding (short-term wholesale, deposits above insurance limits) must be minimized. NSFR ≥100% became a Basel III requirement in 2018.

## 2. Insurance Sector Economics

### Underwriting Metrics

**Combined Ratio** = (Incurred Losses + Operating Expenses) / Earned Premium. Below 100% = underwriting profit; above 100% = underwriting loss. Best-in-class insurers target 92-96%, with disciplined pricing and claims management. Property & Casualty insurers' Combined Ratio is THE key metric; Life insurers monitor persistency and lapse rates instead.

**Loss Ratio** (Incurred Losses / Earned Premium) segregates claim economics from expense management. Inflation in claims (medical, auto repair, construction) directly pressures Loss Ratios. Catastrophe-heavy years (hurricanes, wildfires) can spike Loss Ratios to 120%+.

**Expense Ratio** (Operating Expenses / Earned Premium) includes acquisition costs, underwriting expenses, and administrative overhead. Digital-native insurers promised 15-20% Expense Ratios; reality is typically 25-35% due to claims handling, regulatory compliance, and customer service.

**Investment Income Yield** is the second profit lever. Insurers hold large float (customer premiums awaiting claims) invested in bonds, equities, and alternatives. A 3.5% yield on $10B float generates $350M annual income. In 2022's rate spike, revaluations inflicted massive unrealized losses, temporarily suppressing equity valuations.

### Reserve Risk

**Loss Development and IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported)** reserves represent management's estimate of future claims on past underwriting. Underestimation forces charge-offs; overestimation inflates earnings. This is where actuaries earn their credibility. A 5-10% reserve error on a $50B premium base = $2.5-5B unexpected charge-off.

## 3. Asset Management Sector

### Scale Economics

**Assets Under Management (AUM)** is the revenue engine. Annual fees typically 0.50-0.75% of AUM for active equity strategies, 0.15-0.30% for fixed income, 0.05-0.15% for passive/ETFs, and 1.0-2.0% for alternative (hedge funds, private equity, real estate). The WHY: passive assets generate 1/10th the revenue of active assets but capture 1/5th of new flows—a structural headwind.

**Fee Compression** is the industry's existential challenge. Passive ETF inflows ($2.5T+ annually) cannibalize active strategy revenue. Managers must either grow AUM (organic growth rates of 5-8% or M&A) or cut costs. Organic growth = (investment performance + net flows) × (1 + market appreciation).

**Alpha Generation** justifies active management's higher fees. Risk-adjusted alpha of 0.5-1.5% per year (net of fees) is credible and sustainable. Below 0.25% alpha is indefensible; sophisticated institutional clients will migrate to lower-cost passive + smart beta.

### Business Model Economics

**Passive vs. Active Economics**: A passive manager with $500B AUM at 0.10% fee collects $500M revenue with minimal incremental cost, generating 70%+ margins. An active manager with $50B AUM at 0.75% fee collects $375M revenue but incurs $250M+ in research, trading, and alpha capture costs—leaving 30-40% margins. The math explains consolidation in asset management.

## 4. Fintech & Payments Economics

### Unit Economics Framework

**Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)** for neobanks typically $50-150 per customer. For payments, CAC = marketing spend / new customers acquired. The WHY: digital channels are cheaper than branches but saturated with competitors, inflating CAC.

**Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)** = (annual revenue per customer × gross margin) / churn rate. For a neobank earning $150/year per customer with 40% gross margin and 8% annual churn:
LTV = ($150 × 0.40) / 0.08 = $750. At $100 CAC, payback is 8 months—acceptable. At $150 CAC, payback is 12 months—marginal.

**Interchange Fees** represent the revenue model for many payments players. In the U.S., card interchange is ~1.55% for credit, ~0.05% for debit (capped by Durbin Amendment). In Europe, MiFID II and PSD2 cap interchange at 0.3% credit, 0.1% debit. The fee differential explains why U.S. payments are more profitable than European equivalents.

**Merchant Discount Rate (MDR)** and **Take Rate** are fintech payment processors' core metrics. Stripe, Square, and Block typically retain 1.5-2.5% of transaction value while passing the remainder to card networks, banks, and customers. At 2.0% take rate on $100B annual processed volume, that's $2B revenue—sufficient for profitability at scale.

### BNPL (Buy Now, Pay Later) Unit Economics

BNPL merchants pay 2-8% transaction fees; consumers pay 0% (often loss-making). Default rates of 3-5% are embedded in pricing. Early players (Affirm, Klarna) prioritized growth over profitability, burning $200M+ annually. The WHY: lack of credit bureaus access meant higher defaults; regulatory arbitrage (less stringent than bank lending) proved temporary as regulators tightened scrutiny post-2022.

## 5. Regulatory Framework & Compliance

### Capital & Liquidity (Basel III/IV)

- **CET1 Ratio**: 4.5% minimum + 2.5% conservation buffer + 0-2.5% countercyclical = 7-9.5%
- **Leverage Ratio**: 3% non-risk-weighted backstop (prevents excessive leverage regardless of risk models)
- **CCAR/DFAST**: Annual stress tests simulating severe recession, stress capitalization ratios must remain above 4.5% CET1

### Consumer Protection & Market Conduct

- **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)**: Created CFPB, mandated Volcker Rule (proprietary trading ban for banks), imposed higher capital for systemically important institutions
- **Volcker Rule**: Prohibits federally insured banks from proprietary trading in stocks, bonds, derivatives (except hedging and market-making). Compliance costs $1B+ annually for major banks
- **MiFID II (Europe, 2018)**: Investment Services Directive II mandates best execution, product governance, inducement disclosures. Compliance reduced profitability of retail brokerage by 20-30%
- **PSD2 (Europe, 2018) / PSD3 (proposed 2024)**: Payment Services Directive mandates open banking, Strong Customer Authentication, reduced interchange. Enables fintech disruption but commoditizes payment clearing
- **DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act, EU, 2024)**: Requires operational resilience frameworks, mandatory incident reporting. Increases compliance spend by 15-20%

### Insurance Regulation

- **Solvency II (Europe)**: Requires 99.5% Value-at-Risk capital (1-in-200 year loss threshold). Solo insurance companies typically hold 200-250% of minimum capital—far above minimum due to volatility of mortality, catastrophe, and market risks
- **Own Risk & Solvency Assessment (ORSA)**: Insurers model 3-year solvency under stress, informing dividend and M&A decisions

### AML/KYC/Sanctions Compliance

Financial institutions must identify customers, screen against sanctions lists, and report suspicious activity. Compliance budgets: $500M-2B annually for major banks. False positive rates in sanctions screening average 98%—meaning 98 innocent transactions blocked per 1 actual violation detected.

## 6. Competitive Dynamics

### Market Structure

**Universal Banking** (Citi, Deutsche Bank, HSBC) spans retail, commercial, investment banking, asset management, and insurance. Competitive advantage: client relationships, capital scale, global reach. Disadvantage: complexity, legacy systems, regulatory burden.

**Specialist Banking** (Barclays exiting retail; Goldman Sachs expanding) focuses on high-margin segments (investment banking, private wealth, payments). Lower regulatory burden but revenue concentration risk.

**Neobanks** (Revolut, N26, Chime) offer zero-fee checking, app-only access, no branches. Unit economics: LTV of $600-900, CAC of $100-150, payback periods of 6-9 months. Profitability remains elusive; most lose $0.50-1.00 per customer annually due to fraud, compliance costs, and inability to retain deposits in high-rate environments.

### Disruption Vectors

**Open Banking** (PSD2-mandated in Europe) allows third-party developers to access customer account data via APIs. This enables neobanks to rent payment infrastructure and disrupt traditional banks' customer relationships. Competitive response: incumbent banks build app ecosystems and offer premium features (wealth management, insurance bundling).

**Banking-as-a-Service (BaaS)** providers (Galileo, Marqeta, Treasury Prime) provide core banking infrastructure to fintechs, eliminating the need for bank charters. This commoditizes banking operations but concentrates systemic risk in BaaS providers (e.g., Silvergate Bank's 2023 collapse dragged crypto clients into bank run dynamics).

**Embedded Finance** (insurance in e-commerce, lending at point of sale) captures customers in their moments of need. Profitability depends on volume and cross-selling—a single embedded auto loan at 8% APR needs 5-8 cross-sells of other products to justify customer acquisition.

## 7. Common Consulting Engagements

### Category 1: Digital Transformation

**Challenge**: Legacy systems consuming 40-60% of IT budgets, unable to support mobile/API-first customer experience.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Assess current architecture (systems, data, workflows)
- Benchmark competitors' digital maturity
- Define future state (cloud, microservices, composable architecture)
- Build business case (NPV of cost reduction + revenue uplift from new channels)
- Execute implementation roadmap (12-36 months, $100M-500M+ for large banks)

**Value Driver**: A bank reducing legacy system maintenance from $300M to $100M while launching open banking APIs nets $200M benefit in years 2-3, justifying a $50M consulting spend.

### Category 2: Cost Transformation

**Challenge**: Cost-to-Income ratio at 65% vs. peer benchmark of 52%.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Detailed cost-driver analysis (identify 100+ cost pockets)
- Best practice benchmarking (internal, peer, frontier players)
- Root cause analysis (organizational bloat, inefficient processes, legacy systems, excess real estate)
- Develop detailed 2-3 year reduction roadmap
- Manage implementation (organizational change, technology, process redesign)

**Typical Opportunity**: 15-25% cost reduction ($200-400M for a $2B expense base), phased over 2-3 years. Risk: revenue loss from customer attrition if cuts are too aggressive.

### Category 3: M&A Integration

**Challenge**: Bank A acquiring Bank B faces $50M+ integration costs, 12-18 month timeline, 20-30% key talent attrition.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Pre-close: synergy validation (revenue synergies rarely materialize; cost synergies are real but 70% smaller than expected)
- Post-close: 100-day plan (decide on systems, locations, customers, products)
- Integration delivery (systems migration, customer communication, staff consolidation)
- Performance tracking (actual synergies vs. plan)

**Value Driver**: Overstated synergies are the #1 post-M&A failure mode. A rigorous synergy validation saves acquirers from overpaying by 10-15%.

### Category 4: Capital Optimization

**Challenge**: Bank holding excess capital (e.g., 12.5% CET1 when regulatory minimum is 8.5%) due to overly conservative stress testing or capital planning.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Stress test validation (challenge modeling assumptions, historical loss rates, correlation assumptions)
- Capital planning refinement (match capital to business risk, not regulatory pessimism)
- Capital allocation decision (what businesses earn their cost of capital; which should be exited or de-risked)
- Deployment roadmap (buybacks, dividends, M&A, capital reduction)

**Value Driver**: Releasing $5B excess capital, redeployed at 2.0% dividend yield, = $100M annual shareholder return.

### Category 5: Product Pricing & Revenue Optimization

**Challenge**: Loan pricing at risk-adjusted returns below cost of capital; deposit pricing insufficient to fund loan growth.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Product profitability analysis (allocate capital, funding costs, operational costs to each product)
- Competitive benchmarking (what do peers charge for similar risk?)
- Pricing recommendations (by segment, tenure, risk bucket)
- Implementation (sales team training, system setup, monitoring)

**Value Driver**: 25-50 basis points of pricing improvement on a $100B loan book = $25-50M incremental annual revenue.

### Category 6: Regulatory Compliance & Risk

**Challenge**: Post-enforcement action (e.g., CFPB fine), bank must overhaul AML/KYC systems and governance.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Process & control assessment (identify gaps vs. regulatory expectations)
- Root cause analysis (why did control failures occur? leadership accountability? technology? resources?)
- Remediation roadmap (technology investment, process redesign, governance changes, skill uplift)
- Monitoring framework (ensure sustained compliance)

**Value Driver**: Avoiding a second enforcement action, which could trigger $500M+ fine and loss of business licenses.

### Category 7: Fintech Partnerships & Platform Strategy

**Challenge**: Incumbent bank must decide whether to build, buy, or partner for open banking/embedded finance capability.
**Engagement Structure**:
- Competitive landscape scan (what have peers done? what's the market moving toward?)
- Build vs. buy vs. partner analysis (cost, time-to-market, control, talent)
- Technology stack assessment (how does fintech's tech integrate with legacy bank systems?)
- Post-deal value capture (how does the bank monetize new capabilities?)

**Value Driver**: Correct partnership (e.g., with a well-capitalized fintech) can accelerate market entry by 2 years vs. building in-house, while de-risking technology and market risk.

## Key Takeaways for Consulting Practice

1. **Regulatory Constraints Drive Strategy**: Basel III/IV, MiFID II, PSD2/3, Solvency II, DORA, and AML/KYC regulations determine which businesses are profitable. Capital-intensive strategies must generate 500-600 basis points of risk-adjusted returns to justify Basel III capital costs.

2. **Disruption is Incremental, Not Explosive**: Neobanks have not killed incumbent banks because neobanks lack deposit franchises, underwriting discipline, and profitability. Embedded finance and open banking are real threats but evolve over 5-10 years, not 2-3.

3. **Consolidation is Inevitable**: Fee compression, scale requirements (AUM >$100B to be truly competitive in asset management; branch networks $10B+ in deposits to support in some regions), and regulatory burden favor large, diversified players. Specialist players must dominate niches or sell.

4. **Cost Transformation Beats Revenue Growth**: In a low-growth, high-regulation environment, 15-25% cost reduction is more achievable than 10-15% revenue growth. M&A integration and legacy system sunset drive 70%+ of consulting value.

5. **Fintech is Middleware, Not a Threat**: Fintech excels at customer experience (UX), speed (build in weeks, not quarters), and data (behavioral signals, real-time risk). But fintech struggles with profitability, capital, scale, and trust. Partnerships with incumbents unlock value; standalone fintech fails unless it finds a durable competitive advantage (e.g., Stripe's developer platform, Block's hardware + software integration).

## Investment Banking: Deal Economics and Advisory

Investment banking advisory is a high-margin, capital-light business generating fees from advising on M&A, capital markets transactions, and restructuring. Understanding IB economics is essential for consulting clients who are investment banks, or for understanding how the deal origination and execution process works.

**M&A advisory fees** follow a Lehman-derived formula: approximately 1% of deal value for transactions up to $100M, declining on a sliding scale to 0.1-0.3% for mega-deals ($10B+). Buyside and sellside fees are both paid by the client commissioning the advice (sellside: the seller pays; buyside: the buyer pays). A $5B transaction typically generates $15-30M in advisory fees split between lead and co-advisors. The economics are highly skewed toward deal completion — most advisory contracts include a "success fee" structure where 80-90% of the fee is contingent on transaction close. This creates well-understood incentive misalignment: advisors have strong incentive to close deals even when stopping a transaction would be in the client's best interest.

**Fairness opinions** are a discrete product often attached to major M&A transactions: an IB provides a formal opinion that the deal consideration is "fair, from a financial point of view" to shareholders. Fairness opinions are required by boards to protect against shareholder litigation and fulfill fiduciary duties. Fee: $1-10M depending on deal size. Fairness opinions are prepared using DCF analysis, comparable company analysis, and precedent transaction analysis — the same tools used in broader M&A advisory.

**ECM (Equity Capital Markets)**: IPOs carry underwriting discounts of 5-7% for US transactions ($100-500M deals) and 3-4% for very large IPOs ($1B+). This fee is divided among lead underwriters, co-managers, and the selling group. Follow-on offerings (additional equity raises post-IPO) are discounted at 2-4%. Block trades (large secondary share sales) are executed at 1-3% discount to market with immediate settlement. The trend toward direct listings (Spotify, Palantir) and SPACs (2020-2021 peak; now largely discredited) represent attempts to circumvent traditional IPO fees.

**DCM (Debt Capital Markets)**: underwriting investment-grade bonds carries fees of 0.25-0.75% of issuance size; high-yield bonds earn 1.5-3.5%. Leveraged loans (syndicated term loans for LBOs) generate arrangement fees of 1-3% plus annual administration fees. Bridge loans (short-term financing committed by banks pending longer-term capital markets execution) earn commitment fees of 0.5-1.5% per year plus a margin. Bridge financing is a meaningful IB revenue line because failed or delayed capital markets transactions leave the bridge loan on bank balance sheets, creating both fee income and balance sheet risk.

**IB revenue volatility** is extreme. M&A volumes can decline 40-60% in a single year (2022 M&A volumes fell 35% vs. 2021 peaks); IPO markets can essentially close for quarters at a time. Banks manage this volatility by diversifying across advisory, ECM, DCM, and trading businesses, but even diversified IBs see 20-30% revenue swings across market cycles.

## Private Credit and Direct Lending

Private credit has grown from a niche asset class to a $1.7 trillion market as of 2026, driven by banks' retreat from leveraged lending (due to capital requirements) and private equity sponsors' preference for flexible, certainty-of-close financing from non-bank lenders. The three largest direct lenders — Ares Capital, Blue Owl, and Golub Capital — each manage $50-100B+ in credit assets.

**Direct lending economics**: middle-market companies (EBITDA $10-100M) borrowing from direct lenders pay base rate (SOFR, which was ~5.3% in mid-2024) plus spread of 500-700 basis points, for all-in rates of 10-12%. This compares to broadly syndicated loans for large-cap borrowers at SOFR+175-300bps. The spread premium compensates lenders for illiquidity (private credit loans are not traded), lower documentation quality relative to syndicated loans, and the complexity of covenant monitoring and restructuring if performance deteriorates.

**Fee structures**: origination fee (1-2% upfront), management fee (1.0-1.5% of committed capital), incentive fee/carried interest (15-20% of returns above an 8% preferred return). Direct lending BDCs (Business Development Companies) are publicly listed and add another layer of economics. BDC structures provide favorable tax treatment (pass-through) in exchange for distributing 90%+ of income.

**PIK (Payment-in-Kind) interest** allows borrowers to add interest to the outstanding principal rather than paying cash, common in stressed situations or for sponsor-friendly deals. PIK compounds — $100M of debt at 12% with 3% PIK grows to $109.3M in year one — and creates lender risk that leverage increases as the company struggles. PIK elections are often a signal of deteriorating credit quality.

**Covenant-lite (cov-lite) loans**: since 2012, the leveraged loan market has shifted dramatically toward covenant-lite structures — loans with maintenance covenants (tested quarterly regardless of borrower action) replaced by incurrence covenants (triggered only when the borrower takes specific actions, like additional debt issuance). Cov-lite loans now represent 90%+ of syndicated leveraged loan volume and a growing share of direct lending. Cov-lite reduces lender ability to detect and respond to deterioration early, increasing recovery risk in distress scenarios. The 2022-2023 rate cycle revealed the risk: hundreds of cov-lite borrowers experienced covenant violations that would have triggered earlier intervention under traditional structures.

**Direct lending underwriting**: EBITDA addback scrutiny is the critical due diligence area. Sponsors present "Adjusted EBITDA" that adds back management compensation, one-time restructuring charges, run-rate synergies from acquisitions, and projected cost savings from initiatives not yet implemented. Aggressive addbacks can inflate stated EBITDA by 15-30%, making leverage appear lower than it is. Experienced direct lenders apply their own "quality of earnings" analysis, often narrowing reported Adjusted EBITDA by 10-20% to reach an internally defensible figure.

## UHNW, Family Office, and Private Wealth

Ultra-high-net-worth (UHNW) clients — typically defined as individuals with $30M+ in investable assets — represent one of the highest-margin client segments in financial services. The global UHNW population is approximately 300,000 individuals controlling $40-50T in assets, of which roughly half sits in family offices.

**Family office structures**: single-family offices (SFOs) serve a single family and are established when investable assets exceed $100-150M (the threshold at which the annual cost of running an SFO, typically $1-3M, is justified by fee savings relative to paying external managers). Multi-family offices (MFOs) aggregate assets from multiple families to achieve scale; they serve clients with $10M-100M and charge 0.5-1.0% of AUM annually. Family offices have become significant allocators to alternative assets: a typical SFO allocates 30-40% to private equity, 10-20% to hedge funds, 10-20% to real assets (real estate, infrastructure), and the remainder to liquid public markets.

**Private banking economics**: private banks charge management fees (0.5-1.5% of AUM, tiered with larger relationships paying lower rates), transaction fees (if they execute trades for clients), and product fees (upfront and trailer fees on structured products, funds). True all-in annual yield to the private bank on a UHNW relationship: 0.8-1.5% of AUM. On a $100M relationship, that's $800K-1.5M in annual revenue — explaining the extreme competition for large private banking clients.

**Succession and estate planning** is the anchor advisory service for UHNW clients. An HNWI dying without a trust and estate plan can face estate taxes of 40% (US) on assets above $13M exemption (2026 levels; scheduled to revert to ~$7M in 2026 under TCJA sunset). Family wealth councils, dynasty trusts, irrevocable life insurance trusts (ILITs), grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs), and qualified opportunity zone investments are the primary planning tools. The consulting implication: private banks that provide integrated estate planning, investment management, and family governance services retain clients for generations; those providing only investment management face perpetual threat from fee compression and competitive switching.

**Governance of family-owned businesses** is a growing consulting practice adjacent to private wealth. Family business governance involves: establishing a family council (forum for family members to discuss business and wealth issues), a family board (formal governance with independent directors), and a family charter (documented values, roles, and expectations). Family businesses that fail to establish governance typically experience conflict as ownership fragments across generations — the "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations" pattern documented across cultures worldwide.

## RegTech: The Compliance Technology Opportunity

Regulatory technology (RegTech) has emerged as a distinct investment and consulting category as financial services firms face escalating compliance costs. Global financial services compliance spending exceeds $270B annually, with compliance headcount at major banks growing 10x since 2008.

The major RegTech use cases by regulatory function: **AML/KYC** (anti-money laundering / know your customer) — identity verification, transaction monitoring, PEP/sanctions screening. Technology vendors include ComplyAdvantage, NICE Actimize, Refinitiv World-Check. AI-based transaction monitoring reduces false positive rates by 30-60%, cutting the analyst time spent on non-suspicious alerts (the dominant cost driver in AML). **Regulatory reporting** — EMIR (European Markets Infrastructure Regulation), MiFID II transaction reporting, Basel Pillar III disclosures require structured data submission to regulators. Technology vendors automate the data extraction, transformation, and submission, reducing both the cost and the error rate. **Model risk management** — SR 11-7 (Federal Reserve guidance on model risk) requires banks to independently validate all models used in credit decisions, capital calculations, and stress testing. Model risk management platforms automate documentation, validation workflows, and ongoing monitoring. **Fraud detection** — real-time transaction scoring using ML models reduces fraud losses by 15-25% while reducing false positives that friction legitimate transactions.

The consulting opportunity in RegTech sits at the intersection of regulatory change, technology assessment, and operating model design. When a new regulation takes effect (DORA's ICT risk management requirements effective January 2025; CSRD's financial services provisions; Basel IV's output floor implementation), banks need external help to interpret requirements, assess gaps in current systems and controls, select and implement technology, and validate compliance. These engagements typically run $500K-3M and require deep regulatory expertise combined with technology assessment capability.
