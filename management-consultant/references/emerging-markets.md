# Emerging Markets & Global Strategy

Emerging markets represent the majority of future global growth. This reference covers how to think about cross-border strategy, entry sequencing, operating model adaptation, currency and political risk, and the specific dynamics of the world's most important high-growth markets.

## The Global Strategy Framework

### The Integration-Responsiveness Grid (Prahalad & Doz)

The fundamental tension in global strategy: standardize for efficiency vs. adapt for local relevance.

|  | Low Local Responsiveness | High Local Responsiveness |
|--|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **High Global Integration** | **Global Strategy**: One product, one brand, one value chain (e.g., Intel chips, Boeing planes) | **Transnational**: Globally integrated but locally adapted (e.g., Nestlé — global brand, local flavors) |
| **Low Global Integration** | **International Export**: Sell standard product globally from home base | **Multi-domestic**: Each market operates independently (e.g., newspapers, local retail) |

Most companies under-invest in local adaptation. Most multinationals over-index on "global standards" at the cost of market share to local competitors who understand the customer better.

### Why Emerging Markets Are Different

**Not just smaller versions of developed markets.** Key structural differences:

1. **Price sensitivity**: Income levels may be 5-20× lower; price points must be radically different
2. **Infrastructure gaps**: Logistics, power, payments, internet may be unreliable or absent
3. **Institutional voids**: Legal systems, contract enforcement, credit bureaus, regulatory agencies may be weak or absent
4. **Distribution complexity**: Modern retail may be <30% of sales; need to reach millions of small trade outlets
5. **Competitive dynamics**: Local champions have deep customer relationships, lower cost structures, and government relationships
6. **Speed of change**: Market structures, regulations, and competitive positions can shift dramatically in 2-3 years

### The CAGE Framework (Ghemawat)
Measure distance between home market and target market across four dimensions:

**Cultural Distance**: Language, religion, social norms, values
- High cultural distance → harder to transfer brand positioning, management practices, and product design

**Administrative Distance**: Colonial history, trade agreements, regulatory similarity, political alignment
- High admin distance → more regulatory adaptation required; may face protectionism

**Geographic Distance**: Physical distance, landlocked, time zones, climate
- High geographic distance → higher logistics cost; harder to manage; need local management autonomy

**Economic Distance**: Income levels, distribution infrastructure, consumer behavior
- High economic distance → need to fundamentally rethink product design, pricing, and GTM

Use CAGE to prioritize which markets to enter first (lowest distance → fastest path to profitability) and to anticipate adaptation requirements.

## Emerging Market Entry Assessment

### Market Attractiveness Factors

**Market size and growth:**
- Total addressable market (current and 5-year)
- GDP growth rate and per capita income trajectory
- Urbanization rate (accelerates consumption)
- Demographic dividend (young, growing workforce → consumption growth)

**Market accessibility:**
- Quality of infrastructure (logistics, telecom, power)
- Trade policy (tariffs, import restrictions, local content requirements)
- Regulatory environment (ease of doing business, speed of approvals)
- Financial system (banking penetration, payment infrastructure, FX convertibility)

**Competitive landscape:**
- Are global players already present? How entrenched?
- Who are the local champions? What's their moat?
- Where are the whitespace opportunities?

### Entry Mode Selection

| Mode | Investment | Control | Speed | Risk | Best For |
|------|-----------|---------|-------|------|----------|
| Export | Low | Low | Fast | Low | Testing demand; high-value B2B |
| Licensing/Franchising | Low | Low-Med | Fast | Med | Brand-intensive; limited capital |
| Joint Venture | Med | Shared | Med | Shared | Regulated markets; need local knowledge |
| Acquisition | High | High | Med | High | Speed to scale; acquire local talent/brand |
| Greenfield | High | Full | Slow | High | Custom capability build; long-term commitment |
| Agent/Distributor | Low | Low | Fast | Low-Med | Initial market test; limited resources |

**JV-specific guidance:**
JVs are the required entry mode in many EM markets (China, India sectors, Saudi Arabia). They routinely fail because:
- Partner selection is wrong (different values, different time horizons)
- Governance is unclear (who decides what?)
- Knowledge transfer creates a future competitor
- Exit is complicated

Success factors: Clear strategic rationale for the JV (not just regulatory necessity), well-defined governance and decision rights, explicit milestones for transition to full ownership if intended, strong relationship management at senior level.

### The Sequencing Decision

Not all markets at once. Sequence entry based on:

1. **Strategic priority** (which market is biggest long-term?)
2. **Learning value** (which market teaches you skills needed for harder markets?)
3. **Distance** (closest markets build confidence and capability)
4. **Competition** (enter before the market consolidates)
5. **Resource requirements** (can you actually staff and fund multiple entries simultaneously?)

Classic sequence: Start with one or two markets that are large, relatively accessible, and have high learning value → use those learnings to enter harder markets.

## Operating Model Localization

### What Must Be Localized (vs. What Must Stay Global)

**Typically localized:**
- Pricing (radically different from developed markets — often needs 50-80% price reduction)
- Product formulation / packaging (size, flavors, functionality adapted to local preferences and income)
- Sales & distribution model (often traditional trade-dominant in EM)
- Marketing messaging and channels (local language, cultural references, media mix)
- HR practices (compensation benchmarks, management style, hierarchy expectations)
- Regulatory and compliance (local legal entity, tax, employment law)

**Typically kept global:**
- Brand identity and values (consistency is the brand's equity)
- Quality standards (non-negotiable; local compromise kills the brand)
- Financial reporting and governance
- Core technology platform (though interfaces may be localized)
- Ethics and compliance standards

### The "Good Enough" Product Strategy

In EM, your global premium product is often unaffordable. Options:

1. **Price-pack architecture**: Smaller pack sizes at lower absolute price (same per-unit economics, lower entry barrier). Example: Shampoo sachets in India.

2. **Tiered product line**: A stripped-down version of the core product at a lower price point. Risk: cannibalization and brand dilution.

3. **Separate value brand**: Acquire or build a separate brand for the mass market. Example: Unilever's Wheel detergent in India vs. Surf Excel.

4. **Reverse innovation**: Design a product specifically for EM constraints (unreliable power, poor infrastructure, different use cases) that may then be sold globally. Example: GE's portable ultrasound machine designed for rural India.

### Distribution in Emerging Markets

Modern retail (supermarkets, hypermarkets) penetration in EM:
- India: ~12-15% of FMCG sales
- Southeast Asia: ~30-40%
- Sub-Saharan Africa: ~5-10%
- Latin America: ~40-60%

This means most sales go through **traditional trade** (small independent kiosks, mom-and-pop stores, wet markets). Reaching this channel requires:
- A network of local distributors
- A large field sales force visiting outlets regularly
- Route-to-market optimization (which outlets to visit, how frequently, how to sell in)
- Trade credit management (small retailers often need credit terms)

Technology disruption: B2B e-commerce platforms (like Udaan in India, Wasoko in Africa) are beginning to disrupt traditional distribution — reducing distributor margins and improving manufacturer reach.

## Currency & Financial Risk

### The Currency Risk Toolkit

**Transaction Risk**: A specific deal or contract is exposed to FX moves between signing and settlement.
- Hedge with: Forward contracts, FX options, natural hedging (match revenue and cost currencies)

**Translation Risk**: EM subsidiary's financials translate to different amounts in home currency as FX moves.
- Mitigation: Diversification across currencies; accept translation volatility as a cost of geographic diversification

**Economic Risk**: Long-term competitiveness is affected by sustained currency moves.
- This is the hard one — a prolonged 30% depreciation in the EM currency fundamentally changes the economics
- Mitigation: Local sourcing (costs also fall with currency), USD-denominated pricing where possible

### Common EM Currency Pitfalls

**Dollarizing revenue in soft-currency markets**: Pricing in USD while costs are local seems protective, but if the local currency collapses, customers can't afford USD-priced goods. Either revenue falls with the currency, or you lose market share.

**Ignoring inflation in financial models**: EM inflation can run 5-30%+ annually. A financial model that ignores inflation will dramatically overstate real profitability. Always model in real (inflation-adjusted) terms alongside nominal.

**Capital controls**: Some EM countries restrict repatriation of profits (Argentina, Nigeria, Ethiopia at various times). Structure investments to anticipate this risk — local reinvestment plans, netting agreements, careful entity structure.

### Key Metrics for EM Financial Health

- **Current account balance**: Persistent current account deficits signal currency vulnerability
- **Foreign exchange reserves**: <3 months of import cover = danger zone
- **External debt as % of GDP**: >60% raises sustainability questions
- **Inflation rate**: >10% signals macroeconomic instability
- **Political risk score**: Use indices like Political Risk Services (PRS), Economist EIU, or Moody's country risk

## Political & Regulatory Risk

### The Political Risk Framework

**Stability risks**: Government change, elections, civil unrest, coup risk
- Monitor: Political risk indices, election calendars, civil society indicators

**Policy risks**: Sudden regulatory changes, nationalization, expropriation, taxation changes
- Monitor: Policy pipeline, industry lobbying landscape, government-business relations

**Compliance risks**: Corruption, sanctions, regulatory enforcement
- Monitor: Transparency International Corruption Perception Index, FCPA/UK Bribery Act exposure

### Managing Political Risk

**Before entry:**
- Political risk insurance (MIGA, OPIC/DFC, commercial insurers)
- Structuring investments through favorable bilateral investment treaty (BIT) jurisdictions
- Building government relationships before they're needed

**During operations:**
- Local partnership (shared political risk, local advocate)
- Corporate social responsibility investment (social license to operate)
- Broad stakeholder engagement (government, community, media)
- Scenario planning for key political events (elections, regulatory reviews)

**FCPA/Anti-bribery compliance:**
US companies operating internationally are subject to the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act. UK companies to the UK Bribery Act. These prohibit bribery of foreign officials and can result in massive fines and criminal prosecution. EM operations need robust compliance programs — especially in high-risk markets.

## Market Deep Dives

### India
**Why it matters**: ~1.4B people, 3rd-largest economy by PPP, growing at 6-7% annually, demographic dividend (median age 28), rapidly expanding middle class (~300M today, projected 500M+ by 2030)

**Key dynamics:**
- Incredibly diverse market — 28 states with different languages, cultures, and purchasing behaviors; can't treat as a single market
- Digital adoption is leapfrogging — UPI payment system, e-commerce penetration growing rapidly
- Regulatory complexity: GST, FDI restrictions by sector, data localization (DPDP Act), "Make in India" policy push
- Competitive landscape: Strong local conglomerates (Tata, Reliance, Adani) + domestic startups (Flipkart, Zomato, Paytm)
- Infrastructure rapidly improving: NHAI highway buildout, dedicated freight corridors, logistics parks
- **Key challenge**: Distribution reach (600,000+ villages), regulatory navigation, talent retention as opportunities multiply

### China
**Why it matters**: ~1.4B people, 2nd-largest economy, manufacturing powerhouse, massive consumer market; but also increasingly challenging for Western companies

**Key dynamics:**
- State-owned enterprises (SOEs) in strategic sectors; FDI restrictions in many industries
- Data localization and cybersecurity laws require local data storage; significant compliance burden
- Tech ecosystem is parallel and independent: WeChat (not WhatsApp), Baidu (not Google), Alipay (not Visa)
- Local competitors have deep customer knowledge, government relationships, and often better products for local needs
- US-China decoupling: Geopolitical risk is high and rising; supply chain dependencies being stressed
- **Key challenge**: IP protection, regulatory unpredictability, talent competition, geopolitical risk

### Southeast Asia (ASEAN)
**Why it matters**: 680M people across 10 diverse markets, $3.6T+ GDP, fastest-growing internet economy in the world, young demographic profile

**Key dynamics:**
- Not one market — Singapore, Indonesia, Vietnam, Thailand, Philippines, Malaysia are all very different
- Indonesia is the anchor (280M people, 4th most populous country)
- Digital adoption driven by mobile-first consumers; e-commerce and super-apps (Grab, GoTo, Sea) dominating
- Manufacturing shift: Vietnam, Thailand, and Indonesia attracting China+1 manufacturing
- **Key entry point**: Singapore as regional hub; then expand to Indonesia, Vietnam, Thailand
- **Key challenge**: Market fragmentation (must localize for each country), regulatory differences, infrastructure gaps outside major cities

### Africa
**Why it matters**: 1.4B people (will be 2.5B by 2050), youngest population in the world, resource-rich, fastest urbanization rate globally

**Key dynamics:**
- 54 countries — enormous diversity; not "Africa strategy" but "Nigeria strategy," "Kenya strategy," etc.
- Nigeria (220M), Ethiopia (120M), Egypt (105M), DRC (100M) are the biggest population centers
- Mobile money revolution (M-Pesa model) has enabled financial inclusion ahead of banking infrastructure
- Commodity exposure: Many African economies are heavily dependent on oil, gas, or minerals — cyclical and volatile
- AfCFTA (African Continental Free Trade Area) — creating a single market of 1.4B people; still early but significant long-term
- **Key challenge**: Infrastructure gaps, currency instability, regulatory inconsistency, political risk, talent availability outside South Africa and Nigeria

### Latin America
**Why it matters**: 650M people, $5T+ GDP, strong consumer culture, significant natural resources, proximity to US market

**Key dynamics:**
- Brazil and Mexico together = ~60% of regional GDP
- Brazil: Large domestic market, resource-rich, but regulatory complexity (the "Custo Brasil") and political volatility are major challenges
- Mexico: USMCA integration with North America is a structural advantage; nearshoring trend benefits Mexico enormously
- Commodity cycle sensitivity: Many LatAm economies swing with commodity prices (copper in Chile, oil in Colombia, soy in Argentina)
- Currency volatility: Argentina (peso), Brazil (real), Colombia (peso) have all seen significant devaluations
- Digital: Rapid e-commerce growth; Mercado Libre is the regional champion

## Global Portfolio Management

### The Multi-Market Operating Model

As you expand globally, the operating model question becomes critical: How much autonomy do local markets get?

**Centralized model**: HQ makes all key decisions; markets execute. Good for: brand consistency, scale economies, capital efficiency. Bad for: speed, local relevance, talent retention in markets.

**Federated model**: Markets operate autonomously. Good for: local agility, talent empowerment. Bad for: inconsistency, duplication, inability to leverage scale.

**Hub-and-spoke model**: Global centers of excellence for strategy, brand, technology, finance; regional hubs for adaptation and coordination; local countries for execution. This is the MBB recommendation for most large multinationals.

### The Portfolio Rebalancing Question

Periodically ask: Is our geographic portfolio right?
- Are we over-indexed in slow-growth developed markets?
- Under-invested in high-growth EM with real ability to win?
- Do we have too many markets to manage well (common trap — better to be #1 in 10 markets than #7 in 30)?
- Where is our ROIC highest? Where are we value-destroying?

**Portfolio simplification**: Exiting markets is painful but often right. Criteria: Can we realistically reach top-3 position? Is the investment required proportionate to the prize? Are there structural reasons we can't win (local competitor moat, regulatory disadvantage)?
