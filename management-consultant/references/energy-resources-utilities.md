# Energy, Resources & Utilities Sector

The energy, resources, and utilities sector spans the value chain from resource extraction through transformation to end-user delivery. Understanding this sector requires grasp of commodity market dynamics, regulatory frameworks, capital intensity, and the structural transition reshaping the industry.

---

## Industry Economics

**The economics of energy are fundamentally different from other sectors.** Most participants operate in long-value-chain businesses with high capital intensity, long project payback periods, and exposure to commodity price cycles.

**Three distinct segments with different economics:**

1. **Upstream (Exploration & Production)**
   - Capital intensity: Highest ($10B+ per major project)
   - Payback horizons: 10-20+ years
   - Economics driven by: Commodity price, reserve replacement cost, production cost, exploration success rate
   - Key metric: Production cost per barrel (or unit), reserve replacement ratio
   - Competitive advantage: Access to low-cost reserves, operational excellence, geopolitical positioning

2. **Midstream (Transportation & Processing)**
   - Capital intensity: High but lower than upstream ($1-5B per major project)
   - Payback horizons: 15-20 years
   - Economics driven by: Volume throughput, transportation fees/tariffs, utilization rates
   - Key metric: Cost per unit transported, utilization, pipeline capacity
   - Competitive advantage: Strategic asset positioning, long-term contracts, cost management
   - Structural advantage: Many midstream assets have natural monopoly characteristics; regulated rates provide stable cash flow

3. **Downstream (Refining, Distribution, Retail)**
   - Capital intensity: Moderate to high (varies by segment)
   - Payback horizons: 7-15 years
   - Economics driven by: Crude cost, product pricing power, operational efficiency, asset utilization
   - Key metric: Refining margin (crack spread), throughput, return on assets
   - Competitive advantage: Scale, refining complexity (ability to process cheaper heavier crude), distribution networks, brand (retail)

**Utilities (Electric, Gas, Water Distribution)**
   - Capital intensity: High (replacement cycle of 40-50 years)
   - Payback horizons: 15-30 years (long regulatory approval cycles)
   - Economics driven by: Regulated rate of return, operating cost efficiency, capex discipline
   - Key metric: Return on regulatory asset base (RAB), operating margin, capex efficiency
   - Structural advantage: Essential service with inelastic demand, regulated monopoly in most markets, long-term contracts

**Cross-sector economics principles:**
- **Commodity exposure:** Price volatility in oil, gas, metals, coal directly impacts profitability. Most companies cannot fully hedge and must manage through the cycle.
- **Capital discipline:** Given long payback periods, a single bad capital allocation decision (overpaying for an asset, poor project execution) can destroy shareholder value for years.
- **Operating leverage:** Fixed costs (depreciation, maintenance) are high relative to marginal costs. Small changes in volume or price create large earnings swings.
- **Cycle management:** Success requires managing through commodity price cycles — maintaining financial discipline in boom times, preserving capability and position in downturns.

## Key Metrics & Financial Analysis

**Production and operational metrics (E&P):**
- Reserve replacement ratio: New reserves added ÷ production in the year. Below 100% means resource depletion.
- Finding and development cost: Total capex ÷ new reserves. Competitiveness requires sub-$10/boe (barrel of oil equivalent) for major players.
- Production cost per barrel: Total operating cost ÷ production volume. Ranges from $5-15/bbl in best-in-class to $50+/bbl in marginal fields.
- Net present value (NPV) of reserves: Discounted value of future cash flows from proved reserves. Heavily influenced by assumed commodity prices.

**Midstream metrics:**
- Throughput volume: Primary volume metric; stability of contracts indicates cash flow predictability.
- Cost per unit transported: Operating cost ÷ throughput. Scale drives efficiency.
- EBITDA per unit of capacity: Indicates asset productivity.
- Contract coverage and duration: Percentage of capacity under long-term take-or-pay contracts; duration of contract. Higher and longer = more stable cash flows.

**Downstream metrics:**
- Crude oil throughput: Barrels processed per day (bpd); utilization rate as % of capacity.
- Refining margin or "crack spread": Price of refined products minus crude cost minus operating cost. Negative margins are catastrophic; strong margins signal competitive advantage.
- Product yield mix: % of gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, fuel oil, other products. Flexibility to shift mix as market demand changes is valuable.
- Return on invested capital (ROIC): Net profit ÷ invested capital. For refiners, ROIC is often compressed (5-8%) due to commodity exposure.

**Utility metrics:**
- Regulated asset base (RAB) or rate base: Assets on which utility earns regulated return. Growth in RAB drives earnings growth.
- Return on equity (ROE): Allowed rate of return set by regulator, typically 8-12%. Actual ROE should track allowed ROE; outperformance through operational efficiency creates upside.
- Operating cost as % of revenue: Benchmark against peers; efficiency improves cost ratio.
- Capex-to-revenue ratio: Reflects modernization investment and maintenance intensity. Higher capex in utility infrastructure is typical (10-15% of revenue common).
- Load growth: Growth in electricity demand or gas demand served. Flat or declining load constrains earnings growth.

**Corporate-level metrics (all segments):**
- Return on invested capital (ROIC): Must exceed cost of capital over full cycle to create value.
- Free cash flow: Operating cash flow minus capex. Critical given capital intensity; FCF generation ability determines dividend sustainability.
- Leverage (net debt ÷ EBITDA): Must be managed carefully; over-leverage during downturns destroys value.
- Earnings per share (EPS) and dividends: Often more volatile than other sectors due to commodity exposure; dividend sustainability is key focus.

## Regulatory & Market Structure

**Upstream regulation:**
- Exploration rights: Governments grant exploration/production licenses (often through auctions). Terms vary by country; some jurisdictions have aggressive local content requirements.
- Royalties and taxes: Upstream cash is heavily taxed — typical effective tax rates 30-50% before corporate tax. Fiscal terms vary by country and can change with political shifts.
- Environmental permitting: Increasingly stringent; impact assessments, methane regulations, flaring restrictions. Permitting delay is a major project risk.
- Geopolitical risk: Production in conflict zones or countries with unstable regimes carries additional risk. Nationalization is a tail risk in some jurisdictions.

**Midstream regulation:**
- Pipeline regulation: Most pipelines are regulated utilities (US, Canada) with cost-of-service rate-making. FERC (US) regulates interstate pipelines; state regulators handle intrastate.
- Tariff setting: Rates are set through regulatory proceedings; utilities must justify capex and operating costs. Long-term contracts reduce regulatory risk.
- Environmental and safety: Strict operating and maintenance standards; pipeline integrity management is heavily regulated.
- New infrastructure permitting: Siting new pipelines requires environmental review and often state/local approval; regulatory timelines can delay projects 2-5 years.

**Downstream regulation:**
- Environmental (clean air/water): Refiners must meet strict emission standards, fuel specifications, and waste management. Compliance is capital-intensive.
- Import tariffs and trade: Subject to tariffs and trade agreements; affects feedstock cost and product competitiveness.
- Product price controls: In some countries, fuel prices are controlled or subsidized, destroying refiner margins.
- Blending mandates: Biofuel mandates (US Renewable Fuel Standard, EU renewable energy directive) add cost and create complexity.

**Utility regulation:**
- Rate structure: Regulated utilities earn allowed rate of return on rate base. Rates are set through regulatory proceedings; utilities propose capex and cost plans.
- Cost recovery: Some costs are passed through to customers; others are not. Utilities lobby regulators for favorable cost treatment.
- Performance incentives: Modern regulation increasingly includes incentives and penalties for efficiency, reliability, renewable energy adoption.
- Franchise and service area: Most utilities have monopoly service areas granted by government. Prevents destructive competition but also limits growth.
- Renewable energy and decarbonization mandates: Require utilities to source increasing % of electricity from renewables; creates capex needs and potentially stranded asset risks.

**Market structure dynamics:**
- Commodity market structure: Varies by commodity. Oil is globally traded; natural gas more regional due to transportation constraints; electricity is local/regional.
- Market concentration: Varies by segment; some upstream regions are concentrated (few players), others commoditized. Consolidation is an ongoing trend.
- Oligopolistic pricing: Large players have some pricing power in some markets, but commodity exposure limits pricing control.
- Vertical integration: Common across sectors; integrated oil companies (upstream + downstream + trading) benefit from internal flexibility but also add complexity.

## Energy Transition & Renewables Economics

**The structural shift reshaping the sector:**

The energy transition from fossil fuels to renewables and low-carbon sources is not a 5-year disruption — it's a 20-30 year fundamental restructuring. The pace and magnitude vary by region and by fuel type, but the direction is clear.

**Renewables economics:**
- **Capital intensity:** Very high upfront ($1-3M per MW for solar/wind), but near-zero operating costs. Contrast to natural gas plants ($500-1000/kW capex, high ongoing fuel costs).
- **Payback horizon:** 7-10 years for solar, 8-12 years for onshore wind, longer for offshore wind. Depends on location, capacity factor, and cost of capital.
- **Capacity factor:** Actual output ÷ theoretical maximum. Solar: 15-25%, onshore wind: 25-40%, offshore wind: 35-45%, hydroelectric: 35-50%. Low capacity factor requires more installed capacity for same output.
- **Intermittency:** Renewables don't produce on-demand; requires either storage, backup dispatchable capacity, or demand management.
- **Cost trajectory:** Solar and wind costs have fallen 80-90% over 15 years. Cost reductions continue but at slower pace; other costs (grid connection, storage, balance-of-plant) are becoming larger fraction of total.

**Energy storage and grid:**
- Battery storage: Costs have fallen 90% in 10 years. Becoming economic for daily cycling in some markets. Longer-duration storage (4+ hours) still expensive.
- Grid modernization: Aging grid must be upgraded to handle distributed generation, higher reliability requirements, electrification of transport and heating. Massive capex required over 20 years.
- Demand management: Variable pricing, smart grids, and demand response become increasingly important as renewables penetration grows.

**Hydrogen economy:**
- Gray hydrogen (from natural gas) already exists and is cheap ($2-4/kg). Demand is mostly industrial (refining, fertilizer).
- Green hydrogen (from electrolysis with renewable power) is currently expensive ($5-10/kg) but cost declining. Timescale to competitiveness: 5-10 years in favorable markets.
- Use cases under development: Heavy transport, industrial heat, long-duration storage, feedstock for chemicals. Uncertainty remains on which applications will scale.

**Stranded assets and transition risks:**
- Coal plants and mines: Structural decline; many will not earn back remaining capex. Early closure likely without support.
- Oil and gas reserves: If carbon pricing rises sharply or demand transitions faster than expected, significant reserves may become uneconomic. Reserve base impairments have already begun.
- Long-life assets: Pipelines, refineries, and utilities with 30-40 year economic lives face uncertainty if demand for their products declines faster than expected.

**Corporate positioning:**
- Oil majors strategy: All major IOCs have announced energy transition strategies; most are targeting net-zero by 2050. Actual capex allocation still heavily weighted to fossil fuels, but trajectory is clear.
- Utilities strategy: Transitioning to renewable generation, managing declining fossil fuel generation. Capex redirected to renewables and grid modernization.
- Stranded capital risk: Companies that don't manage transition may face write-downs of reserves, assets, or long-term contracts valued on outdated assumptions.

## Common Consulting Engagements

**Upstream (E&P):**

1. **Portfolio optimization:** Which assets to keep/divest? Company has 5 producing fields + 10 exploration prospects. How to allocate capex? Requires reserve analysis, NPV modeling under different price scenarios, and strategic fit assessment.

2. **Cost reduction:** Many E&P companies have not fundamentally restructured operations since the 2015-16 downturn. Identify opportunities to reduce production costs by 20-30% through operational efficiency, supply chain optimization, and organizational design.

3. **Major project evaluation:** Evaluating a $5-10B development project. Is the return sufficient? Have key risks been captured? Peer benchmarking on capex intensity, cycle time, cost overruns.

4. **M&A target assessment:** Evaluating acquisition of competitor's production assets. Financial analysis, reserve replacement cost, operational synergies, integration planning.

5. **Energy transition strategy:** How should our E&P company position itself as oil and gas demand mature? Diversify into renewables? Invest in hydrogen? Increase natural gas (lower carbon than oil)? If divesting assets, maximize proceeds.

**Midstream:**

1. **Asset utilization and volume risk:** Pipeline is 60% utilized due to weak commodity prices. How to increase throughput? What is the value proposition to shippers? Should we expand capacity or optimize existing?

2. **New project business case:** Proposal to build new natural gas pipeline connecting to LNG export facility. Requires demand forecasting for LNG export, shipper contract discussions, regulatory timeline assessment, financial modeling.

3. **M&A integration:** We acquired competitor's pipeline network. How to integrate systems, eliminate redundancies, achieve cost synergies? Timeline and execution risk assessment.

4. **Tariff optimization:** Regulated rates are set by formula; company has opportunity to propose new rate structure. How to present capex program and cost structure to maximize allowed return?

**Downstream (Refining & Marketing):**

1. **Refinery optimization:** Refinery has run at low margins for 3 years. Options: upgrade the complex (invest capex to process cheaper feedstock), cut costs, or exit? Financial analysis of each path.

2. **Product portfolio and market positioning:** Company sells commodity gasoline/diesel. Market is shifting to renewable fuels, EV charging. What products and services to develop? Where to invest?

3. **Supply chain restructuring:** Evaluate consolidating or closing distribution centers. Logistics cost analysis, customer impact, capital recovery.

4. **Renewable fuels integration:** Evaluate opportunity to co-process biofuels at existing refinery. Technical feasibility, capex requirements, margin profile, risk of stranded existing assets.

**Utilities:**

1. **Regulatory strategy and rate case:** Preparing rate case (every 3-5 years). How to present capex program to regulators? What capital projects to prioritize? How to achieve favorable rate base growth and ROE?

2. **Grid modernization strategy:** Aging grid requires $10B+ of capex over 10 years. Which investments should be prioritized? How to justify to regulators and ratepayers?

3. **Renewable energy portfolio:** Utility must meet 50% renewable target by 2035. Build vs. buy? Mix of solar/wind/hydro? Optimal contract structures. Integration challenges.

4. **Cost efficiency and margin expansion:** Utility ROE lags allowed ROE by 100bps. Identify operational efficiency opportunities, organizational redesign, and procurement optimization.

5. **Electrification and demand growth:** Electrification of transport and heating could increase electricity demand 20-30% over next 10 years. Evaluate capex and rate trajectory needed to support growth.

6. **Organizational design and talent:** Energy transition requires different skill sets. How to restructure organization, redeploy talent, and build new capabilities?

## Benchmarking & Performance Standards

**E&P benchmarks:**
- Finding and development cost: Top quartile <$8/boe, industry average $15-20/boe
- Production cost per barrel: Top quartile <$20/bbl, industry average $30-45/bbl
- Reserve replacement ratio: Minimum 100% to avoid depletion; best-in-class achieve 150%+
- Return on average capital employed (ROACE): Target >15% over commodity cycle for major IOCs

**Refining benchmarks:**
- Refining margin: Highly volatile; strong margin >$8/bbl, weak margin <$2/bbl
- Utilization rate: Target 90%+; below 85% indicates structural overcapacity or market share issues
- Operating cost per barrel: Best-in-class <$3-4/bbl, industry average $4-6/bbl
- Return on invested capital: 5-8% typical due to commodity exposure; >10% indicates competitive advantage

**Utility benchmarks:**
- Operating margin: Typically 15-20%; benchmark against peers to identify efficiency gap
- Capex-to-revenue ratio: 10-15% typical; higher suggests asset base modernization needs
- Customer satisfaction (J.D. Power): Benchmark against peer group; impacts regulatory relationships
- System reliability (SAIDI/SAIFI): Minutes of outage per customer per year; regulatory incentives tied to reliability metrics

**Corporate portfolio metrics:**
- Revenue per employee: Energy sector typically $500k-$1M per employee; benchmark against peer group and by function
- ROIC: Target >10% over commodity cycle
- Net debt to EBITDA: Varies by company strategy and credit rating; typically 2-3x
- Dividend yield: Energy companies prized for dividends; typical 3-5% yield for majors
