# Stakeholder Interviewing & Workshop Facilitation

The ability to extract insight from people — through interviews, workshops, and facilitated sessions — is one of the most underrated consulting skills. Analysis tells you what happened; people tell you why. This reference covers how to design, run, and synthesize qualitative research at a professional level.

## Stakeholder Interview Fundamentals

### Why Interviews Matter in Consulting

Quantitative analysis tells you what is happening. Interviews tell you:
- Why it's happening (context that doesn't show up in data)
- What people believe is happening (even if wrong — perception shapes behavior)
- What's been tried before and why it failed
- Where the political landmines are
- What the organization is capable of actually doing
- The informal power structure and culture

Consultants who skip qualitative work produce technically correct analyses that organizations can't act on.

### The Interview Principles

**1. Listen, don't pitch**: Your job is to understand their reality, not to validate your hypotheses. Resist the urge to share your views.

**2. Ask about specifics, not generalities**: "Can you walk me through the last time X happened?" is more powerful than "How does X work in general?" People rationalize and generalize; specific examples reveal truth.

**3. Follow curiosity**: The most valuable insights often come from an unexpected comment that you follow. Build space for tangents — sometimes the tangent is the real story.

**4. Build rapport before content**: 3-5 minutes of genuine small talk changes the quality of the entire interview. People share more with people they feel comfortable with.

**5. Manage confidentiality carefully**: Be clear about what you'll share and with whom. "I'll share themes from interviews but not attribute specific comments" enables honesty. If someone asks whether their name will appear — be truthful and specific.

**6. Never waste a goodbye**: The last 2 minutes of an interview are often the richest. "Is there anything you think I should know that I didn't ask about?" and "Who else should I be talking to?" are always worth asking.

### Stakeholder Interview Sequence

**Internal interviews: The cascade**
Start at the top (CEO, C-suite) and cascade down. Reasons:
- Senior leaders set the context — understand their framing before you test it lower
- Lower-level interviews are enriched by knowing the strategic intent you're testing
- It signals organizational seriousness — if the CEO sat for an interview, others take it seriously

**External interviews: Triangulate**
- Customers (current and churned)
- Competitors (former employees, analysts, industry events)
- Industry experts (academia, consultants, trade associations)
- Suppliers (they see your operations from the outside)
- Regulators (for regulated industries)

Always triangulate: if multiple independent sources say the same thing, it's likely true. If there's contradiction, understand why.

## Interview Design & Question Crafting

### The Interview Guide Structure

An interview guide is not a script — it's a map. You'll deviate from it; that's fine. It ensures you cover the essentials.

**Template:**
```
Interview: [Name, Title, Company]
Duration: [Planned time — typically 45-60 min]
Objective: [2-3 sentence description of what you're trying to learn]

Opening (5 min):
- Introduction and purpose of the project
- Confidentiality and usage of information
- Permission to take notes

Background (5-10 min):
- Their role and tenure
- How they interact with [subject of the study]

Core questions (25-35 min):
[See below for question design principles]

Closing (5 min):
- Any other perspectives not captured?
- Who else should we speak with?
- Thank you and next steps
```

### Question Design Principles

**Open-ended, not closed**: "Tell me about your experience with X" not "Is X working well?"

**Specific, not general**: "Walk me through the last time you had to deal with Y" not "How do you handle Y generally?"

**Neutral, not leading**: "How do you think about the trade-off between A and B?" not "Don't you think A is more important than B?"

**The "why" funnel**: Start broad, then go deeper with why questions:
- "What do you see as the biggest challenge?"
- "Why do you think that's happening?"
- "What have you tried to address it?"
- "Why hasn't that worked?"

**Hypothetical probes** (use carefully): "If you could change one thing about how X works, what would it be?" surfaces frustrations people are too polite to volunteer.

**Competitive / external benchmarks**: "Have you seen this done better anywhere else? What made it work there?"

### Sample Question Banks by Interview Type

**Diagnosis / Problem Discovery:**
- "In your view, what's the single biggest reason we're not hitting [target]?"
- "Where in the process does value get lost or destroyed?"
- "What would a new CEO be surprised to learn in their first 30 days?"
- "Where are the things the organization talks about but never actually changes?"

**Strategy / Future State:**
- "What would you do if you were in my shoes and had no constraints?"
- "Where do you see the market going in 5 years that most people in the company don't yet appreciate?"
- "What are competitors doing that we should be watching closely?"
- "What would have to be true for [proposed strategy] to succeed?"

**Implementation / Change:**
- "What's the biggest risk to actually getting this done?"
- "Who in the organization most needs to be on board — and aren't yet?"
- "What's been tried before? Why didn't it work?"
- "What would make you personally feel confident this is going to happen?"

**Customer / User:**
- "Walk me through the last time you used [product/service] for [job]."
- "Where did it work well? Where did it frustrate you?"
- "What would have to change for you to use it twice as often / pay twice as much?"
- "What do you use instead when [product] doesn't work for you?"

## Expert Network Interviews

Expert networks (GLG, Guidepoint, Third Bridge, AlphaSights) connect consultants with former industry executives for paid interviews. They're invaluable for rapid industry knowledge acquisition.

### When to Use Expert Networks
- Entering a new industry where the team has limited experience
- Getting the view of a former executive at a target company (M&A due diligence)
- Understanding competitive dynamics from someone who lived inside a competitor
- Testing hypotheses with someone who has seen this specific situation before

### Getting Maximum Value from Expert Calls

**Prepare intensively**: These calls are typically 30-60 minutes and expensive (~$300-1,000/hour). Every minute counts. Read everything available before the call; know what specific questions only an expert can answer.

**Prioritize the questions you can only ask an expert**: You don't need an expert to tell you what's publicly known. You need them for: informal dynamics, why strategies failed, what the data doesn't capture, and their personal hypothesis.

**The opening setup**: "I want to respect your time, so I'll jump right in. We're working on [topic] and specifically trying to understand [question]. I've read [X and Y] and have a hypothesis that [Z]. I'd love to know where you'd push back on that."

**Probe for specifics**: Experts sometimes give safe, general answers. Push for specifics: "Can you give me an example of what you mean? When you were at [Company], how did that play out?"

**The non-answer answer**: Sometimes how an expert responds tells you as much as what they say. Reluctance to criticize a company, unusual emphasis on a specific risk, or a strong reaction to a hypothesis — note these reactions.

**The 80/20 question at the end**: "If you were doing this analysis, what would you spend 80% of your time on?"

### Expert Interview Note-Taking

Capture:
- Direct quotes (in quotation marks) — these are gold; don't paraphrase unless you're certain
- Your inference vs. their words (label clearly)
- Contradictions with other interviews or data (flag for follow-up)
- Follow-up questions the call raised

## Workshop Facilitation

### When Workshops Beat Analysis

Use workshops when:
- You need to build alignment, not just answer a question
- The answer requires knowledge distributed across many people
- You want the organization to own the output (not just consume it)
- The problem involves trade-offs that need to be made together
- You're launching a change initiative and need people committed from the start

Use analysis when:
- The answer is empirical and doesn't require debate
- The group is too large or too political to have a productive conversation
- Speed matters more than buy-in

### Workshop Design Principles

**Design backward from the outcome**: What decision or commitment do you need by the end? Work backward to the agenda. Every session should move toward that outcome.

**Prepare the key tensions**: Identify the 2-3 difficult trade-offs the group needs to make. Design activities that surface and resolve them — don't avoid the hard conversations.

**The right room**: Enough people to be credible; few enough to have a real conversation. 8-15 is typically optimal. Above 20, workshops become presentations.

**Pre-work**: Send participants a short briefing document (3-5 pages max) on key data, context, and questions to reflect on. This shifts the conversation from "let me learn" to "I've processed the information, let me engage."

**80/20 the agenda**: 80% of workshop time should be on the hardest 20% of the problem. The easy stuff can be communicated asynchronously.

### The Standard Workshop Toolkit

**Opening Frame**
Start with: Context (why are we here?), Stakes (why does this matter?), Process (how will we work today?), and Norms (how will we behave? e.g., "Yes, and" vs. "Yes, but"; every voice counts; phones away).

**Icebreaker / Warm-Up**
A 5-minute activity to shift people from task mode to thinking mode. Can be as simple as: "In one word, what do you need from today's session?"

**Data Presentation + Reaction**
Present key findings (concisely — 10-15 min max). Then structured reaction: "What surprised you? What confirmed what you already believed? What's missing?" This builds shared understanding without steamrolling.

**Diverge → Converge**
Classic creative problem-solving rhythm:
- **Diverge**: Generate many options, ideas, or perspectives without judgment (brainstorm, small group work, "how might we?")
- **Converge**: Prioritize, cluster, and select — not everything is equally valid

**Small Groups, Large Group**
Use breakout groups (3-4 people) for discussions that benefit from psychological safety. Bring back to large group for integration. People say more in small groups.

**Parking Lot**
A visible space (flip chart, virtual board) for important but off-agenda questions. Acknowledges them without derailing the session.

**Commitment Close**
End every workshop with explicit commitments: Who will do what by when? This turns conversation into action. Write it down publicly. Send it within 24 hours.

### Virtual Workshop Facilitation

The same principles apply but require more deliberate effort:
- **Engagement tools**: Miro, Mural, Mentimeter for interactive participation — passive watching kills virtual workshops
- **Shorter sessions**: 90 minutes max without a break; 2-hour blocks with a mid-session break
- **Cameras on**: Non-negotiable for meaningful interaction
- **Explicit transitions**: "We're moving from X to Y now" needs to be said; it happens naturally in physical rooms
- **Chat facilitation**: Have a second facilitator monitoring and surfacing chat comments
- **Breakouts**: Use consistently — they're even more valuable in virtual settings

## Design Thinking & Co-creation

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation. It's particularly useful for product/service design, customer experience, and organizational design challenges.

### The 5-Stage Design Thinking Process

**1. Empathize**
Understand the human experience through observation, interviews, and immersion. The goal is to see the world through the user's eyes — not to confirm what you think you know.
- Tools: User interviews, journey mapping, shadowing ("day in the life"), ethnography

**2. Define**
Synthesize your observations into a clear problem statement — the "How Might We" (HMW) question.
- "How might we help [user] to [need] in a way that [constraint]?"
- Example: "How might we help busy parents to provide healthy school lunches in a way that doesn't require significant preparation time?"

**3. Ideate**
Generate a wide range of possible solutions. Quantity over quality in this phase.
- Tools: Brainstorming, "Crazy Eights" (8 ideas in 8 minutes), analogical thinking ("How would Amazon solve this?"), SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse)

**4. Prototype**
Build rough, cheap versions of your top ideas. Prototypes are for learning, not for presenting. A sketch, a role-play, or a cardboard mock-up can be enough.
- Principle: "Build to think, not think to build"

**5. Test**
Get your prototype in front of real users. Observe their reactions — what they do is more honest than what they say.
- Ask: "Can you show me how you'd use this?" not "Do you like it?"

### Journey Mapping

A journey map visualizes the end-to-end experience of a customer or employee across a process. It shows:
- **Stages**: The major phases of the experience
- **Actions**: What the person does at each stage
- **Thoughts**: What they're thinking
- **Emotions**: What they're feeling (often shown as an emotional curve)
- **Pain points**: Where the experience breaks down
- **Opportunities**: Where intervention could create the most value

Journey maps are most powerful when built collaboratively with actual customers/users in a workshop setting, not constructed by consultants based on assumptions.

## Synthesis — Turning Conversations Into Insight

### The Note-Taking Protocol

During every interview:
- Verbatim quotes in quotation marks
- Your observations in square brackets [seemed uncomfortable discussing X]
- Hypotheses or questions the interview raised with a ★ symbol
- Contradictions with other sources flagged with a ⚠ symbol

### The Synthesis Process

After completing all interviews:

**Step 1: Individual interview summaries**
For each interview, write a 5-10 sentence summary: who this person is, their key perspective, the 2-3 most important things they said, and any unique insight only they provided.

**Step 2: Theme extraction**
Read across all summaries. What themes appear across multiple interviews? What's the distribution of views? Where is there consensus? Where is there contradiction?

**Step 3: Frequency vs. importance**
Not all themes are equally important. A point made by 1 person might be more important than one made by 10. Weight by:
- Proximity to the problem (who is closest to it?)
- Access to information (who knows most?)
- Track record (who has been right before?)
- Independence (are multiple people saying this independently, or are they echoing each other?)

**Step 4: The "collective wisdom" synthesis**
Write 3-5 findings statements that represent what the interviews told you as a whole. Each should be:
- A complete sentence (insight, not topic)
- Supported by 2-3 quotes or examples
- Honest about where there was contradictory evidence

**Step 5: Integrate with quantitative findings**
Qualitative findings + quantitative analysis together are more powerful than either alone. Use interviews to explain what the data shows; use data to validate or challenge what interviews suggest.

### Handling Contradictory Interviews

When two senior people tell you opposite things:
1. Don't average them — understand why they disagree
2. Is it a factual disagreement (someone has wrong information) or a values/priorities disagreement?
3. The contradiction itself is often the finding: "There is no consensus on this — which is itself the problem"
4. Present both views honestly: "We heard two distinct perspectives: [A view] and [B view]. We believe [A] is better supported by the evidence because [X], but [B view] represents a real alternative held by [part of the organization]."
