# Problem-Type Playbooks

Each playbook reflects how an experienced consultant actually approaches these problems — not the textbook version, but the one that works under time pressure with real clients.

---

## 1. Cost Transformation

**The real question:** Where is the money going and what's actually cuttable without breaking the business?

**How to orient fast:**
- Get the P&L broken out by function or business unit. Where are the biggest absolute dollar pools?
- Do a quick benchmarking sanity check — headcount ratios, cost-to-revenue ratios, SG&A as % of revenue vs. peers. This tells you within 2 days where you're structurally out of line.
- Ask what's already been tried. Clients rarely come to this problem fresh — there's usually a history of failed cost programs. Understanding why they failed is as important as finding new opportunities.

**The core analysis toolkit:**
- **Benchmarking:** Compare your cost structure to best-in-class peers. This is your most powerful tool because it creates external legitimacy. "You're spending 2x the industry average on [function]" is harder to argue with than "we think you're overstaffed."
- **Zero-based analysis:** For key cost pools, build up from zero — what does this function actually need to do its job? Not "what is it spending today."
- **Should-cost modeling:** For procurement or manufacturing, model what the cost *should* be given inputs, volumes, and efficient operations. Gap to actual = opportunity.
- **Spans and layers (for headcount):** Map management layers and span of control. Industry norm is 6-8 direct reports. Anything under 4 is a red flag.

**Where costs actually hide:**
- Procurement: tail spend, fragmented supplier base, no volume leverage, auto-renewing contracts
- Headcount: management layers, duplicated functions across BUs, support:revenue staff ratio drift
- Real estate: underutilized space, above-market leases, too many locations for actual footprint needs
- Technology: legacy systems with high maintenance costs, redundant tools, shelfware
- Outside services: professional services and consulting spend that's become structural, not episodic

**The hard part:**
Not finding the opportunities — every cost program finds plenty of opportunities. The hard part is building a credible implementation plan that accounts for sequencing, organizational capacity to absorb change, and the difference between cuts that are real (contracts cancelled, headcount reduced) vs. "savings" that evaporate in 6 months.

**Watch out for:**
- False savings (reclassifying costs rather than eliminating them)
- Cutting costs that enable revenue (marketing, sales capacity, product development)
- The client who wants the number before the plan — push back on this
- Not accounting for one-time implementation costs (severance, system costs, etc.)

## 2. Growth Strategy

**The real question:** Where should we invest to grow, and what does winning actually require?

**How to orient fast:**
- Understand the current growth profile first: what's growing, what's declining, what's flat? Break revenue by segment, product, channel, geography. Pattern recognition kicks in here — are you losing share or is the market shrinking? One is a you problem, the other is a market problem.
- Ask where leadership *thinks* the growth is coming from vs. where the data says it is. This gap is often the most interesting thing in the room.

**The core question structure:**
- **Where to play:** Which markets, segments, geographies? (Attractiveness × ability to win)
- **How to win:** What's your right to win in each space? Capability, cost position, brand, relationships?
- **What it takes:** What would you need to build, buy, or partner to capture the opportunity?

**Key analyses:**
- **Market sizing and growth rates:** Don't just use industry reports. Triangulate — bottom-up from customer data, top-down from market sizing, cross-check against analogs
- **Segment profitability:** Who are your most profitable customers? What do they have in common? Too often growth conversations happen without anyone asking "which growth is good growth?"
- **Share analysis:** Are you growing with the market or taking share? Losing share in a growing market is a strategic warning sign even if revenues look fine
- **Adjacency mapping:** What capabilities and assets do you have that could be deployed in adjacent markets? What would it take to extend? Who else is competing there?
- **Customer whitespace:** For existing customers — what are they buying elsewhere that you could be selling them?

**The strategic options:**
- Core: More penetration in existing markets with existing offer — usually the highest ROI but limited upside
- Adjacent: New segment, new geography, or expanded offer to existing customers — medium risk, medium upside
- New: New market or business model — highest risk, highest upside, needs strong strategic rationale

**Watch out for:**
- Growth strategies that are really just "do more of what we're already doing"
- Adjacencies that sound logical but require capabilities you don't actually have
- Market sizing that's so large it's not useful — TAM of "$200B" doesn't tell you what you can capture

## 3. Market Entry

**The real question:** Should we enter this market, and if so, how?

**The two-part structure:** First, is the market attractive? Second, can we win in it? Both have to be true. A great market you can't win in is a trap. A winnable market that's shrinking isn't worth the investment.

**Market attractiveness:**
- Size and growth rate (absolute and relative)
- Profitability structure — use Porter's Five Forces as a quick diagnostic, but focus on what actually drives margins in this specific market, not all five forces equally
- Competitive intensity — how many players, how differentiated, what's the basis of competition (price, capability, relationships, speed)?
- Customer dynamics — are customers locked in, price-sensitive, relationship-driven? How do they buy?
- Regulatory and structural barriers — what's the cost of entry from a compliance/certification standpoint?

**Ability to win:**
- What's your right to win? Genuine competitive advantage — not "we're good at this." Be specific and honest.
- Who are you displacing? Incumbents don't sit still. Model their likely response.
- What capabilities do you have that transfer? What gaps do you have?
- Build, buy, or partner — which is the fastest credible path to competitive parity?
- Time to scale — how long before you're at a competitive cost position and market presence?

**Entry mode decision:**
- Organic: Full control, highest capability-building, slowest
- Acquisition: Speed + capabilities, highest cost, integration risk
- Joint venture / partnership: Shared risk, shared control, faster than organic
- Licensing / distribution: Asset-light, limited upside, lowest commitment

**The go/no-go test:**
You need a genuine answer to: "Why us, why now, why here?" If you can't answer all three convincingly, the market entry case is weak.

## 4. Organizational Design

**The real question:** Is the organization structured in a way that enables the strategy, and if not, what needs to change?

**The critical framing:** Org design is not an end in itself. It's a means to executing a strategy. The question isn't "what's the right org chart" — it's "what structure gives us the best chance of executing our strategy given our talent and culture?"

**How to orient:**
- Get the current org chart and understand the rationale for how it's structured
- Ask: what's the strategic priority that isn't being served well today? That's usually where the structural problem shows up
- Understand the informal structure too — where do decisions actually get made? Who has influence regardless of title?

**The design dimensions:**
- **Structure:** Functional, divisional, matrix, network — each has tradeoffs. Functional = efficiency and expertise. Divisional = accountability and customer focus. Matrix = flexibility and cost, with coordination overhead.
- **Decision rights:** Who decides what? Where are decisions getting stuck? Where are they being made too low (slow, inconsistent) or too high (bottleneck, disengagement)?
- **Spans and layers:** As noted in cost — typical norm is 6-8 direct reports. More layers = slower, more expensive, more diluted accountability.
- **Role clarity:** Are people clear on what they own vs. what they influence? RACI is a useful forcing function here — who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed?

**The talent dimension:**
Org design and talent strategy are inseparable. A great org design with the wrong people in key roles fails. Get clear on: what does the new structure require in terms of capability? Who among the current team fits the new model? Where are the gaps?

**What consultants often miss:**
The cultural reality. An org design that's theoretically correct but requires behaviors the culture doesn't support will fail on implementation. You have to design for the organization you have, while building toward the organization you want.

**Watch out for:**
- Redesigning the org to solve a talent problem (you need a different person in the role, not a different role)
- Matrix structures that create the appearance of accountability without the reality
- "Reorganizations" that are really just shuffling boxes to signal change without changing how work gets done

## 5. M&A and Due Diligence

**The real question:** Is this target worth acquiring at this price, and can we actually create value from it?

**Strategic rationale first:**
Before any financial analysis, be clear on *why* this acquisition makes sense strategically. There are really only a few valid reasons:
- Capability acquisition (you need something they have and it's faster/cheaper to buy than build)
- Market access (entering a new geography or segment quickly)
- Scale economics (combining creates meaningful cost or revenue synergies)
- Competitive defense (blocking a competitor from acquiring this asset)

If the strategic rationale is weak, no amount of financial engineering makes it a good deal.

**The due diligence workstreams:**
- **Commercial:** Is the revenue story real? Verify customer concentration, churn, contract terms, pipeline quality. Talk to customers if possible.
- **Financial:** Are the reported numbers a true picture? Revenue recognition, working capital management, non-recurring items, off-balance-sheet items.
- **Operational:** How well-run is the business? What's the cost to maintain vs. the cost to improve?
- **Synergies:** Revenue synergies are almost always overestimated, cost synergies are more reliable. Model both conservatively. Assign clear ownership and timeline.
- **People:** Who are the key people and will they stay? What's the cultural fit?
- **Legal/regulatory:** Any skeletons? IP issues, pending litigation, regulatory exposure?

**Synergy modeling:**
- Cost synergies: Procurement leverage, duplicated functions (finance, HR, IT, legal), facility consolidation. These are more predictable.
- Revenue synergies: Cross-sell, new markets, channel access. These are real but harder to time and often take 2-3x longer to materialize than modeled.
- Dis-synergies: Don't forget the costs of integration and the revenue disruption during it.

**The valuation reality:**
Delegate detailed modeling to `finance:strategic-finance` or `finance:comps`. Your job is to frame the strategic rationale, identify the key value drivers, stress-test the assumptions, and form a view on whether the price is justified under realistic scenarios.

**Watch out for:**
- Synergies that depend on behaviors the combined organization has never demonstrated
- "Strategic" deals that are really financial acquisitions dressed up as strategy
- Underestimating integration cost and complexity (this kills more deals than bad strategy)

## 6. Pricing

**The real question:** Are we leaving money on the table, and if so, where?

**The pricing diagnostic:**
Start with the price waterfall — what does the customer actually pay vs. list price? Where are discounts applied (upfront, contractual, distributor, off-invoice)? In most B2B businesses, the realized price is significantly below the published price and nobody has mapped the full waterfall.

**Three types of pricing opportunity:**
- **Price level:** Are you underpriced relative to the value you deliver? This requires understanding customer willingness to pay, competitive positioning, and your ability to capture value through pricing vs. needing to pass it through to win volume.
- **Price architecture:** Do you have the right pricing structure? Good/better/best, usage-based, outcome-based, subscription. The architecture often matters more than the absolute price.
- **Price execution:** Are your salespeople giving discounts when they don't have to? Do you have a discount approval process with teeth? What's the correlation between discount depth and win rate?

**Value-based pricing:**
The right anchor for pricing is the economic value you create for the customer, not your cost-plus or competitive benchmarking. This requires understanding:
- What does the customer save or earn by using your product?
- How does that compare to alternatives (including doing nothing)?
- What share of that value can you reasonably capture?

**Watch out for:**
- Race-to-the-bottom pricing to win volume that isn't profitable
- Pricing strategies that work in theory but can't be implemented by the sales force
- Failing to account for customer response — price increases often have volume consequences

## 7. Digital Transformation

**The real question:** What technology and process changes will have the most impact on business performance, and what's the realistic path to getting there?

**The grounding question:**
"Digital transformation" gets used to mean everything from "we need better software" to "we need to rebuild our entire business model." Get specific: what business outcome is the transformation supposed to deliver? Cost reduction? Revenue growth? Customer experience? Speed to market? The technology choices flow from the business objective, not the other way around.

**The common patterns:**
- **Core modernization:** Replacing legacy systems that are slowing you down. Usually the right thing to do but rarely delivers dramatic business value on its own.
- **Data and analytics:** Turning operational data into decisions — better customer understanding, demand forecasting, predictive maintenance, etc. Often high ROI when focused on specific decisions.
- **Customer experience:** Digital channels, self-service, personalization. High customer impact but requires both technology and business process change.
- **Operations automation:** RPA, workflow automation, AI-assisted work. Usually a cost play.
- **New business models:** Platforms, digital products, data monetization. High potential, high risk, requires genuine strategic intent.

**The capability assessment:**
Technology is the easy part. The hard part is whether the organization has the capability to use it. What's the data quality? Do you have the talent to build and run digital products? Is the culture willing to work differently? Assess honestly — many digital transformations fail not because of technology but because of organizational readiness.

**Prioritization:**
Which initiatives have the clearest value case and the most achievable path? Build a 2x2: value vs. difficulty. Start with high-value, lower-difficulty initiatives to build momentum and fund harder longer-term work.

## 8. Turnaround

**The real question:** What's actually broken, how bad is it, and what do we need to do in the next 90 days to stabilize before we can fix it for the long term?

**The liquidity question comes first:**
In a true turnaround, cash is the only thing that matters in the first phase. How much runway do you have? What are the cash burn drivers? What can be done in the next 30-60 days to extend runway? Restructuring, financing, asset sales — what's on the table?

**The diagnostic:**
- Is this a revenue problem (market declined, competitive loss), a cost problem (cost structure grew beyond what revenue supports), or an execution problem (bad decisions, bad management)?
- Usually it's all three, but understanding the primary driver determines the sequence of interventions.

**The 30-60-90 day frame:**
- **30 days:** Stop the bleeding. Identify immediate cash preservation actions. Assess the leadership team honestly. Communicate to key stakeholders (employees, creditors, major customers/suppliers) — silence breeds panic.
- **60 days:** Stabilize. Implement quick-win cost actions. Begin developing the restructuring plan. Identify the core of the business that's worth saving.
- **90 days:** A credible plan for the path forward, with enough stakeholder alignment to execute it.

**What makes turnarounds fail:**
- Moving too slowly to preserve liquidity
- Not making hard people decisions early enough
- Trying to save everything instead of identifying the viable core
- Underestimating how long it takes to restore creditor and customer confidence
