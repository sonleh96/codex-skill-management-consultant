# The Structured Case Approach

You probably learned case structure for interviews. It turns out it's not just for interviews — it's how you actually work as a consultant. When a client drops a question in a meeting, when a partner asks for your view cold, when you're asked to "think through" something on the spot, you're running the same playbook. The only difference is pace: 30 minutes in an interview, days or weeks in the real world. But the moves are identical.

## The Five Moves

### Move 1: Clarify the Question

Before you structure anything, make sure you're answering the right question. Ask 2-3 clarifying questions — not to stall, but to scope.

**Example:**
Partner: "The CEO is concerned about margin compression."

You: "When you say margin compression — is that gross margin, EBIT, or both? Across all product lines or specific ones? And over what period are we looking — year-over-year, or trend over three years?"

This takes 60 seconds. It saves you from structuring a beautiful answer to the wrong question.

### Move 2: Frame the Problem

State your understanding in one or two clear sentences, then explain your approach. This signals thinking and lets someone correct you early.

**Example:**
"So the company is a mid-market software vendor with declining gross margins despite flat or growing revenue. I'd like to understand whether this is driven by product mix (selling more lower-margin SKUs), pricing pressure, or cost of goods sold. I'd start by looking at the revenue breakdown by product and compare margins by product over time."

### Move 3: State Your Initial Hypothesis

Say what you think is likely true before diving into analysis. You might be wrong. That's fine. A hypothesis orients your work.

**Example:**
"My initial hypothesis is this is a mix problem, not a pricing problem. Revenue has grown, customer counts are up, so they're not losing pricing power — but they've been doing more business with large customers who buy the lower-margin enterprise product. That would show up as revenue growth alongside margin compression."

### Move 4: Structured Analysis

Work through your framework systematically. Surface important findings immediately — don't hold them for the end. Think out loud so others can follow and redirect you.

When a branch isn't productive, say so and move on.

**Example:**
You pull the revenue by product. Enterprise product is up 40% while standard product is flat. That supports the mix hypothesis.

Then you check customer counts. Enterprise customer count is up, standard customer count is down slightly.

Then pricing: Enterprise price per customer is actually up, not down. So it's not price erosion on the enterprise side.

**Your voice:** "Mix is definitely part of it — we're selling more enterprise at lower margins. But enterprise pricing is actually up, so there's something else. Let me look at the standard product side... Ah, there it is. Standard product per-customer margin is down 30% year-over-year. That's where the compression is coming from."

### Move 5: Clear Recommendation

Land with a specific, actionable answer.

**Example:**
"The margin compression is driven by two distinct problems: one is product mix — more enterprise, which is lower margin, that's strategic. The second is deteriorating margins in the standard product — looks like they've been discounting heavily to keep volume. I'd recommend: (1) Accept the mix shift as intended strategy, (2) Reset pricing in the standard product — you're overcompensating with discounts, and (3) Segment the customer base so you're not offering enterprise-level discounts to standard-product customers."

## Handling Spontaneous Questions in Meetings

### Pattern A: Short Answer Needed

Client or partner asks something straightforward.

- Pause briefly: "Good question — let me give you my thinking."
- Answer first with your best view
- Give 2-3 supporting points
- Invite pushback: "Does that align with what you're seeing?"

### Pattern B: Complex Question Needing Structure

Something multifaceted that actually needs decomposition.

- Pause briefly
- "I'd think about this across two/three dimensions..."
- Work through each concisely
- Synthesize to a point of view

**Example:**
Partner: "Should we expand this product into Europe?"

You: "I'd look at this in three parts. First, market attractiveness — how big is the opportunity relative to the US and how competitive? Second, our right to win — do we have capabilities to operate there, or would we need to build? Third, financial return — given the investment, what's the payback and upside?"

[Then walk through each in 2-3 minutes total, end with a working view.]

### Pattern C: You Don't Know

Don't bluff.

"I don't have enough context to give you a confident answer — let me come back by end of day" or "That's a gap in our current analysis — worth filling before we move forward."

## Common Problem Types: Instinctive Structures

When you see a problem type, the decomposition often follows a pattern:

**Profitability problem:**
Revenue drivers (volume × price × mix) vs. Cost drivers (fixed vs. variable, by category)

**Market entry:**
Market attractiveness (size, growth, margin, competition) × Ability to win (right to win, capabilities, competitive response)

**Growth question:**
Core growth (market share in existing markets) vs. Adjacencies (new segments, geographies, products) vs. New business models

**Acquisition/investment:**
Strategic rationale → Financial return → Execution risk → Alternative uses of capital

**Organizational/capability problem:**
Strategy clarity → Structure → Capabilities → Incentives → Culture
(Most org problems that feel like culture are actually structure or incentive problems.)

## The 2-Minute Case Structure

When asked to "think through" something in a meeting, people expect a working view in roughly 2 minutes.

- **20 seconds:** Clarify and frame
- **40 seconds:** State your decomposition ("I'd think about this across three dimensions...")
- **40 seconds:** Size or prioritize which dimension likely matters most
- **20 seconds:** State your hypothesis and what you'd need to validate it

You're not solving the problem in 2 minutes. You're demonstrating you have a structured mind and a working hypothesis. Practice until it's automatic.

## When Structure Gets in the Way

Don't over-structure. If a client asks a straightforward question and you respond with "I'd like to frame this across three dimensions," they'll lose patience.

Match structure to complexity. Simple questions deserve direct answers. Save the framework for genuinely complex situations.

The goal is always clarity. Structure is just the tool.
