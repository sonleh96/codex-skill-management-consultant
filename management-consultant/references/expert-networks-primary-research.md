## Expert Network Ecosystem

### Market Overview & Dynamics

The expert network industry has matured into a $2.5–$3.5B global market, with annual growth rates of 12–18% driven by institutional investor demand, consulting firm reliance, and corporate research requirements. The ecosystem serves three primary constituencies: buy-side investors (hedge funds, private equity) seeking proprietary intelligence; consulting firms (MBB and others) augmenting primary research; and corporations conducting competitive intelligence, customer research, and market validation.

The market structure reflects two distinct economic models: **direct expert networks** (GLG, AlphaSights, Third Bridge, Guidepoint) operate two-sided marketplaces connecting vetted experts with research buyers, charging 30–50% of expert compensation as commission; **expert aggregators** (Inex One, ExpertConnect, Atheneum) layer technology and curation over multiple networks, adding additional fees for sourcing and matching.

A critical shift has occurred in expert call positioning: previously dominated by buy-side information advantages (now heavily restricted by compliance frameworks), expert networks have repositioned toward **primary market research for strategy consulting**. This reframing allows consulting firms to access on-demand subject matter expertise, customer research, and market validation without maintaining permanent research teams. For consulting projects, expert networks provide rapid market sizing validation, customer perception data, competitive positioning intelligence, technology trend validation, and regulatory/policy interpretation—all complementing quantitative secondary research.

### Expert Network Segmentation

**Tier 1 Global Platforms** (GLG, AlphaSights, Third Bridge, Guidepoint):
- GLG (Gerson Lehrman Group): Founded 2001, ~650,000 vetted experts across 200+ verticals. Positioned as broadest horizontal network. Strengths: speed (48–72 hour turnaround), depth across industry/function/geography, strong compliance infrastructure. Typical consulting use: market sizing, competitive intelligence, customer research. Fee structure: $400–$3,000+ per expert hour depending on seniority/specialization.
- AlphaSights: Founded 2011, ~500,000 experts with particular strength in financial services, technology, healthcare. Differentiation: higher-quality expert vetting, dedicated relationship managers, integration with major consulting firms. Typical consulting use: technology trend validation, healthcare market research, financial services benchmarking. Fee structure: $350–$2,500+ per hour with retainer models for ongoing relationships.
- Third Bridge: Founded 2010, ~400,000 experts with strong positioning in private equity/corporate strategy. Differentiation: investor-grade research quality, focus on decision-grade intelligence. Typical consulting use: market validation, investor thesis testing, management team assessment (post-identification). Fee structure: $500–$3,000+ per hour with project bundles ($15K–$50K+ per engagement).
- Guidepoint: Founded 2004, ~350,000 experts with particular strength in legal, accounting, and specialized industries. Differentiation: compliance-first approach, strong regulatory expertise. Typical consulting use: regulatory intelligence, technical due diligence, complex policy interpretation. Fee structure: $400–$2,500+ per hour.

**Tier 2 Specialized Networks**:
- Dialectica: Founded 2015, positioned in healthcare/life sciences. Differentiation: high-caliber scientific experts, strong academic relationships. Typical consulting use: clinical validation, reimbursement intelligence, technology assessment. Fee structure: $600–$4,000+ per hour for senior scientists.
- Stratos: Focus on aerospace/defense/engineering. Strength: deep technical expertise, security clearance-appropriate sourcing. Typical consulting use: technical due diligence, market validation in highly specialized categories.

**Aggregators & Meta-Networks**:
- Inex One: Layers optimization algorithms over 40+ expert networks, reducing sourcing friction. Value proposition: access to broader networks without managing multiple relationships. Typical consulting use: rapid multi-network sourcing for complex questions requiring diverse expertise.
- ExpertConnect: Similar aggregation model with emphasis on enterprise customers. Useful for large projects requiring dozens of expert interviews.

### Expert Sourcing Criteria & Quality Assurance

For consulting applications, expert networks employ multi-layered vetting:

1. **Credentials Verification**: Academic degrees, professional certifications, published work, board positions, consulting experience verified through third-party sources and background checks.
2. **Experience Depth**: Minimum 7–10 years in relevant domain for C-level experts; 5–7 years for subject matter experts. Role specificity (e.g., "VP Product at Series B SaaS" vs. generic "product executive").
3. **Industry Positioning**: Current or recent operational role preferred over consultants; geographic diversity within markets; current employment verification (reduces likelihood of dated knowledge).
4. **Availability & Communication**: Trial calls assess responsiveness, communication clarity, and willingness to engage directly without scripting.
5. **Conflict Management**: Formal conflict-of-interest screening prevents experts with direct financial stakes from speaking (e.g., short sellers discussing a company, direct competitors discussing market strategies).

Quality degradation occurs when networks prioritize volume over vetting. Red flags: high accept-rates (>70%), rapid expert matching (<4 hours), limited expert preview information, or networks showing inflated expert counts without clear specialization.

## Expert Call Methodology

### Pre-Call: Expert Identification & Screening

**Expert Targeting**:
Define the ideal expert profile with precision. Vague requests ("VP of Sales at a B2B SaaS company") yield poor matches. Precise targets: "VP of Sales or Sales Director at B2B SaaS companies with $50M–$200M ARR, 5+ years tenure, prior experience at later-stage company (Series B or beyond), customer base in EMEA."

Network platforms provide expert profiles including current role, company (if disclosed), background, past interactions, and availability. Screen 3–5 candidate profiles, reviewing for:
- **Current role alignment** with target expertise
- **Tenure in role** (minimum 2–3 years preferred; >6 months minimum acceptable if recent elevation)
- **Company relevance** (size, stage, business model, geography match)
- **Geographic/timezone feasibility**
- **Conflict-of-interest red flags** (direct competitor, financial interest in outcome, recent company departure under contested circumstances)

**Conflict & Confidentiality Screening**:
Before expert confirmation, conduct compliance screening:
1. **Material Non-Public Information (MNPI)**: Does the expert have access to inside information about securities you're analyzing? (Applies primarily to investor-facing research; lower concern for corporate strategy consulting.)
2. **Competing Financial Interests**: Does the expert have shorts, longs, or material financial interests in the company/topic under discussion?
3. **Advisor/Consultant Conflicts**: Is the expert already advising a competitor or potential client conflict?
4. **Confidentiality Commitments**: Non-disclosure agreements with current employers, past severance agreements restricting commentary.

Most consulting applications trigger **low MNPI risk** since the research targets market dynamics, technology trends, and customer sentiment rather than corporate financial information. Networks manage this through standard compliance templates.

**Expert Matching & Confirmation**:
Most networks require expert confirmation within 24–48 hours. High-quality networks conduct initial match validation (confirming expert has relevant experience and is willing to speak). Expert Networks typically charge for confirmed expert interviews regardless of completion (kill fees 50–100% of confirmed rate apply to cancellations within 24 hours).

### Call Preparation & Interview Design

**Research Phase (48–72 hours before call)**:
1. Gather expert's professional background, recent published work, current company, industry positioning.
2. Identify knowledge gaps from secondary research that expert can validate: market sizing assumptions, customer perception points, competitive positioning claims, technology capability assessments.
3. Develop hypothesis-driven questions, not open-ended exploration. Example: instead of "What's changing in the market?", ask "We've heard from three customers that vendor switching costs in this category have declined from 15–20 months to 6–8 months due to cloud migration. Is this consistent with your customer base?"
4. Identify 4–6 core questions that drive the analysis; allow 5–7 minutes per question for a 45–60 minute call.

**Interview Guide Development**:
Structure calls with:
- **Opening (2–3 minutes)**: Expert introduction, research context, confidentiality reminder, agenda overview. Set clear time bounds.
- **Background Validation (5 minutes)**: Confirm expert's current role, customer base, geographic coverage, tenure. Establishes credibility frame for subsequent commentary.
- **Core Topic Questions (35–45 minutes)**: Hypothesis-driven questions arranged from broad to specific. Example structure for market validation: "What percentage of your customers are evaluating new solutions in this category?" → "What factors are driving evaluation?" → "What's your win rate against competitive set X?" → "How has pricing evolved?"
- **Competitive Validation (5–10 minutes)**: Specific competitive positioning questions validated against market intelligence.
- **Closing (2–3 minutes)**: Check if expert has additional perspectives; clarify any ambiguous responses; thank expert.

**Tone & Facilitation**:
- **Never leading questions**: Ask "How would you characterize customer satisfaction?" not "We think satisfaction is low, right?"
- **Acknowledge expertise**: "Given your background, you're probably seeing this from X angle—walk me through your perspective."
- **Validate understanding**: "If I'm hearing you correctly, you're saying X is true because Y. Is that fair?"
- **Probe for specificity**: "Can you give me a specific example?" "How do you measure that?" "What's your confidence level?"

### Compliance & Ethical Guardrails

**MNPI Sensitivity**:
Consulting projects generally have lower MNPI risk than investor research, but remain vigilant:
- Do NOT ask experts about confidential company information, roadmaps, financial projections, or strategic decisions that experts have access to through their current employers.
- DO ask about market dynamics, customer behavior, competitive positioning, technology trends that draw on operational knowledge without breaching confidentiality.
- Red flag questions: "What's your revenue trajectory?" (if discussing their company), "When will you launch feature X?", "What's your gross margin?" (unless the expert is discussing industry benchmarks).

**Documentation & Record-Keeping**:
- Record calls only with expert consent. Document expertise/credentials at call outset.
- Maintain call notes capturing specific facts (quotations), dates, expert role/company, and any conflicts disclosed.
- Flag uncertain or speculative commentary: "Expert noted this is speculation on their part; they haven't verified this."
- Separate facts from opinions in call notes for downstream synthesis.

**Use Restrictions**:
- Expert networks prohibit re-identifying experts without consent (except to client/project team).
- Do not attribute commentary to company without explicit expert approval.
- Standard research use is permitted; insider trading, front-running, or regulatory violation use is prohibited.

## Survey Research for Consulting

### Survey Applications in Strategy Consulting

Surveys complement expert interviews by providing **quantified customer perception, market-wide benchmarking, and statistical validation** of qualitative findings. Typical consulting applications include:

1. **Market Sizing & Adoption Validation**: Quantify TAM expansion, feature awareness, willingness-to-pay, and adoption barriers across customer segments.
2. **Customer Perception Benchmarking**: Competitive positioning (brand perception, feature preference, consideration sets), satisfaction/NPS trends, switching intent.
3. **Pricing & Economic Sensitivity**: Conjoint analysis to quantify feature value drivers, price sensitivity, and optimal feature/price combinations.
4. **Segmentation Validation**: Identify distinct customer archetypes, their feature priorities, budget constraints, and decision drivers.
5. **Go-to-Market Testing**: Test messaging resonance, positioning concepts, and channel preference before launch.
6. **Regulatory/Policy Perception**: Validate customer reaction to proposed regulations, estimate compliance cost pass-through tolerance.

### Survey Methodologies for Consulting

**Conjoint Analysis (Discrete Choice Modeling)**:
Conjoint analysis quantifies how customers trade off features, price, and brand. Methodology:
- **Design**: Create factorial design with 5–8 attributes (product features, price, brand, support model). Each respondent evaluates 10–20 choice scenarios selecting preferred option.
- **Sample Size**: 300–500 respondents minimum; 800–1,200 for segment-level analysis.
- **Output**: Utility scores for each feature/price level; willingness-to-pay (WTP) estimates; price elasticity; feature importance rankings.
- **Consulting Use**: Replace speculation about feature value with quantified customer preferences. Example output: "Customers value 24/7 support at +$400/year; mobile app at +$250/year; integration with Salesforce at +$600/year."
- **Typical Cost**: $25K–$50K including design, fielding, and analysis.

**MaxDiff (Maximum Difference Scaling)**:
Simplified alternative to conjoint for feature prioritization:
- **Design**: Present respondents with subsets of features; ask "Which feature is most important? Least important?"
- **Sample Size**: 200–400 respondents adequate for broad market.
- **Output**: Relative importance scores across features; segment-level variation.
- **Consulting Use**: Rapid prioritization of feature roadmaps, go-to-market positioning.
- **Typical Cost**: $15K–$25K.

**Quantitative Perception Surveys**:
Standardized questionnaires assessing brand/competitive positioning:
- **Design**: Awareness → consideration → perception (NPS, satisfaction, trust, feature perception) → intent → demographics/firmographics.
- **Sample Size**: 400–800 respondents for market-wide; 200–300 for specific segments.
- **Output**: Competitive positioning maps, perception gaps, segment-level insights.
- **Consulting Use**: Validate qualitative positioning findings from expert interviews; quantify market perception shifts.
- **Typical Cost**: $10K–$20K.

**Pricing Studies & Price Sensitivity Analysis**:
- **Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter**: Respondents identify ideal price, acceptable price range, too-cheap/too-expensive thresholds. Outputs demand curve estimating price elasticity.
- **Gabor-Granger**: Sequential price-point testing where respondents indicate willingness to purchase at varying prices. Outputs precise demand curve.
- **Consulting Use**: Validate pricing strategy, identify price optimization opportunities, estimate revenue impact of price increases.
- **Typical Cost**: $20K–$40K.

### Survey Panel Providers & Data Quality

**Major Platforms for Consulting**:
- **Dynata**: Largest panel provider (~2M respondents), strong B2B capabilities. Quality: good to excellent. Cost: standard market rates. Typical use: large-scale consumer perception surveys.
- **Cint**: Global network aggregating 100+ partner panels. Quality: varies by partner panel. Cost: competitive. Typical use: rapid international deployment, niche segment targeting.
- **Lucid**: Boutique quality focus, strong consumer brand data. Quality: excellent. Cost: premium (+15–20% vs. standard). Typical use: high-stakes brand/positioning research.
- **Qualtrics**: Self-service platform allowing direct panel access plus consulting integration. Quality: excellent with proper panel selection. Cost: moderate to high. Typical use: ongoing customer research, integrated with client systems.
- **SurveyMonkey Enterprise**: Self-service platform with panel access. Quality: adequate for consumer research, weaker for B2B. Cost: low to moderate. Typical use: rapid ad-hoc surveys, small budgets.

**B2B Survey Challenges**:
B2B surveys (surveying business decision-makers) differ materially from consumer surveys:
- **Respondent Quality**: Decision-makers (CFOs, VPs) less available in panels; incentives required are higher ($100–$250 per 30-minute interview). Fraud risk higher (respondents mis-representing role).
- **Sample Size**: Targeting specific roles + company size + industry filters yields smaller viable samples (80–150 respondents often adequate for B2B; <50 if targeting specific C-level role).
- **Panel Fit**: Dynata, Cint strongest B2B providers; consumer-focused panels (SurveyMonkey, Qualtrics default) often unsuitable.

**Data Quality Red Flags**:
- Completion times significantly faster than median (often indicates low-engagement, rushed responses).
- High disqualification rates (>30% screened out for not meeting criteria) suggesting panel quality issues.
- Open-end response quality declining (generic, copy-paste answers).

## Triangulation & Evidence Integration

### Three-Legged Evidence Stool

Consulting recommendations gain credibility through **triangulation**: converging evidence from independent sources yielding consistent conclusions. The triangulation framework combines:

1. **Expert Interviews**: Qualitative depth, operational perspective, market nuance, recent trends, contextual color. Limits: individual perspectives, potential confirmation bias in expert selection, limited sample size.
2. **Survey Data**: Quantified customer sentiment, market-wide perspective, statistical confidence, competitive benchmarking. Limits: survey-specific response bias, insufficient depth on "why", limited to predetermined questions.
3. **Secondary Research**: Published market data, analyst reports, company disclosures, public databases. Limits: often dated, limited to public companies, analyst bias.
4. **Internal Client Data**: Customer base analysis, sales pipeline, win/loss analysis, customer satisfaction metrics. Limits: internal-specific, potential bias toward client worldview.

### Integration Methodology

**Phase 1: Hypothesis Development (Secondary Research)**
- Develop 4–6 core hypotheses from secondary research, competitive intelligence, and internal client data.
- Example hypotheses: "Market is consolidating toward 3–4 dominant vendors"; "Customer switching costs have declined due to cloud migration"; "Pricing pressure is emerging from non-traditional competitors."
- Document assumption foundations; identify high-uncertainty areas requiring primary validation.

**Phase 2: Qualitative Validation (Expert Interviews)**
- Structure expert calls to specifically validate/refute hypotheses.
- Document expert agreement/disagreement; probe reasons for divergence.
- Identify patterns across 6–10 expert interviews; flag outlier perspectives.
- Refine hypotheses based on qualitative findings; identify quantification opportunities.

**Phase 3: Quantitative Confirmation (Surveys)**
- Design surveys capturing the quantitative dimensions of validated hypotheses.
- Include both perception questions (aligning with expert interviews) and behavioral data (actual adoption, satisfaction, switching).
- Analyze segment-level variation identifying where hypotheses hold vs. break down.

**Phase 4: Synthesis & Reconciliation**
- Compare findings across sources. Expected overlaps: expert consensus on market trends should correlate with survey data; internal client data should align with market-wide survey benchmarks.
- Identify divergences and investigate. Example: "Experts said switching costs have declined, but customer satisfaction remains high—this suggests other factors beyond switching costs are retaining customers."
- Segment findings by customer type/geography/use case, acknowledging that market-wide trends may not apply uniformly.

### Conflict Resolution & Weighting

When evidence conflicts, apply **conflict resolution hierarchy**:

1. **Operational Data > Expert Opinion > Survey Data > Analyst Reports**: Actual customer behavior (NPS trends, churn, expansion revenue) trumps expert prediction or survey intent.
2. **Recent Data > Historical Data**: 2025 expert interviews supersede 2023 analyst reports.
3. **Large Sample Size > Small Sample**: 500-respondent survey carries more weight than 8 expert interviews, all else equal.
4. **Specific Data > Aggregate Data**: "Customers in enterprise segment are churning; SMB segment stable" more credible than "overall churn is 15%."
5. **Consistent > Outlier**: Consensus across multiple experts more reliable than one contrarian perspective.

**Example Conflict**: Experts say "customers are increasingly price-sensitive" (7 of 8 experts agree), but customer survey shows 80% willing to pay 5–10% premium for added features. Resolution: Likely reflects different customer segments or interpretation of "price sensitivity" (willingness to switch for 10% savings ≠ unwillingness to pay premium for features). Recommend: "While price competition is intensifying in SMB segment, enterprise segment remains feature-driven and willing to pay premium. Recommend tiered pricing strategy to serve both segments."

### Synthesizing Evidence into Client Deliverables

**Outputs & Presentation**:
- **Market Sizing**: Synthesize survey sample sizes, expert estimates, and analyst data into ranges with confidence intervals. Present as: "TAM estimated at $3.2B–$3.8B (90% confidence) based on survey penetration rates (n=450), expert benchmarking (8 interviews), and analyst estimates."
- **Competitive Positioning**: Create perception matrices comparing brand/feature positioning from expert interviews and survey data. Identify positioning gaps where client perception diverges from market reality.
- **Customer Segmentation**: Segment survey respondents by behavior/needs; validate segments with expert perspective on segment growth/profitability.
- **Pricing & Value**: Present conjoint-derived WTP estimates alongside expert commentary on pricing trends and internal margin data.

**Confidence & Caveats**:
- Flag confidence levels for each finding (high: converging evidence; medium: majority evidence with outliers; low: limited/conflicting evidence).
- Acknowledge sample limitations: "Expert interviews reflect operators in $50M–$500M revenue range; early-stage or enterprise perspectives underrepresented."
- Note temporal sensitivity: "Survey conducted Q4 2025; economic conditions may have shifted customer preferences."

## Commercial Dynamics & Engagement Economics

### Expert Network Spending & Pricing

**Typical Consulting Project Spend**:
- **Rapid Market Validation Engagements** (2–4 weeks): $15K–$35K (typically 15–20 expert interviews at $600–$1,500/hour including network fees, plus survey if included).
- **Comprehensive Primary Research** (4–8 weeks): $50K–$150K (30–40 expert interviews, 400–600 respondent survey, competitive analysis).
- **Deep Market/Customer Research** (8–12 weeks): $150K–$300K+ (60+ interviews, 1,000+ respondent surveys, multiple methodologies including conjoint analysis and segmentation).

**Expert Hour Rates** (inclusive of network fee):
- **Individual Contributors / Senior Specialists**: $400–$800/hour (industry experts, functional leads)
- **Director / VP Level**: $800–$1,500/hour
- **C-Level / Highly Specialized**: $1,500–$3,000+/hour

Network fees represent 30–50% of expert compensation; thus effective expert costs are ~$600–$2,200/hour depending on seniority and specialization.

**Survey Costs** (including panel, fielding, analysis):
- **Basic quantitative survey** (400 respondents, 10-minute questionnaire): $8K–$15K
- **Segmentation survey** (600 respondents, segment analysis): $15K–$25K
- **Conjoint analysis** (400 respondents, 12–15 attributes): $25K–$50K
- **Integrated perception + behavioral** (800 respondents, multi-question): $20K–$40K

### Staffing Models & Engagement Structure

**Small Engagements ($15K–$35K)**:
- Senior Associate + 1 Project Manager / Analyst
- 2–3 weeks execution
- 15–20 expert interviews; limited or no survey component

**Mid-Size Engagements ($50K–$150K)**:
- Manager + 2–3 Associates / Analysts
- 4–8 weeks execution
- 30–40 expert interviews, 400–600 respondent survey, synthesis

**Large Engagements ($150K–$300K+)**:
- Principal/Senior Manager + 1 Manager + 3–5 Associates
- 8–12 weeks execution
- 60+ interviews, 1,000+ respondent surveys, multiple methodologies, expert workshops

### Measuring ROI on Primary Research

Expert network and survey spending delivers ROI through:

1. **De-risking Strategic Decisions**: Validation of market opportunity prevents investment in zero-TAM or hyper-competitive markets. Example: "Market sizing research prevents client from expanding into segment with <5% growth rate, saving $2M+ in misallocated resources."
2. **Enabling Profitable Pricing**: Conjoint analysis identifies feature combinations capturing willingness-to-pay, improving margins 5–15%. Example: "$40K pricing research identifies $5–10K pricing optimization opportunity for 2,000-customer base, yielding $10–$20M annual revenue uplift."
3. **Accelerating GTM Execution**: Customer perception validation shortens go-to-market cycle by 2–4 months, enabling faster revenue capture.

Consulting firms typically justify primary research spend as 5–15% of project value (delivering $2–$5M client impact per $50K primary research investment).

## Best Practices & Common Pitfalls

### Expert Interview Best Practices

1. **Prepare thoroughly**: Spend 2–3 hours on secondary research per expert call. Preparedness signals respect for expert's time and yields higher-quality responses.
2. **Maintain objectivity**: Avoid leading questions; probe expert's confidence in estimates. "On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you in that estimate?" surfaces uncertainty.
3. **Triangulate across networks**: No single expert network guarantees quality. Source experts from 2–3 platforms to reduce platform-specific bias.
4. **Document comprehensively**: Record specific facts, quotations, dates, and expert role. "Expert noted this is speculation" prevents misuse of uncertain commentary.
5. **Conduct timely synthesis**: Schedule retrospective interviews within 48 hours of calls while details are fresh. Flag contradictions immediately for follow-up.

### Survey Research Best Practices

1. **Pilot before launch**: Test survey with 50 respondents; assess completion rates, response quality, time to complete. Adjust clarity issues.
2. **Specify quality filters**: Screen respondents for decision-making role, company size, geography. Weaker filters yield unreliable data.
3. **Benchmark against internal data**: Validate survey results against client NPS, retention, win rates. Material divergence suggests survey quality issues.
4. **Build skip logic carefully**: Complex branching can confuse respondents. Prefer straightforward questionnaires with <20 questions.
5. **Analyze at segment level**: Aggregate results often obscure material differences across customer segments. Require segment-level analysis.

### Integration Best Practices

1. **Document confidence levels**: Clearly indicate which conclusions rest on converging evidence vs. single sources.
2. **Acknowledge limitations**: Note sample sizes, temporal constraints, and potential biases in expert/survey selection.
3. **Present ranges not point estimates**: "Market is $2.8B–$3.4B" more credible than "market is $3.1B."
4. **Use data to challenge assumptions**: Explicitly test client's pre-existing beliefs against primary research findings. Identify areas of agreement/divergence.

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Overweighting expert consensus**: Agreement among 8 experts carries less weight than 500-respondent survey showing opposite sentiment. Experts may share systematic biases.
2. **Underfunding survey research**: Cutting survey budgets to save $10K often yields poor data quality; prefer smaller samples with high-quality respondents to large samples with fraud/low-engagement.
3. **Neglecting B2B-specific challenges**: Applying consumer survey methodology to B2B yields poor respondent quality. Budget 2x+ for B2B respondent incentives and quality filtering.
4. **Failing to pre-specify hypotheses**: Post-hoc pattern-finding in survey data yields spurious correlations. Define hypotheses before fielding; test hypotheses in survey design.
5. **Using only single evidence source**: Relying solely on expert interviews risks expert bias; relying solely on surveys misses nuance and "why" context. Triangulate across sources.
