# Private Equity Consulting: Reference Guide for MBB Consultants

## The PE Operating Model vs. Corporate

**Speed and Pressure**: PE work moves faster—diligence cycles in weeks, not months. Decisions are binary (buy/pass, fix/sell). There's less tolerance for "further analysis" and more comfort with probabilistic thinking. You're not building consensus; you're validating assumptions.

**Financial Primacy**: Every question circles back to return. A problem isn't "important for the business"—it's important if it moves the needle on IRR. This reframes what matters. Customer satisfaction trends are interesting only if they predict churn. Market share is a proxy for pricing power, which is a proxy for margin, which is a proxy for value. Learn to think in EBITDA and cash flow, not revenue.

**Exit Mindset**: PE owns assets to exit them, typically in 3–7 years. This changes your time horizon and your definition of success. A sustainable competitive advantage matters less than an achievable 25%+ IRR. Strategic positioning matters less than whether you can tell a compelling story to the next buyer.

**Client Sophistication and Impatience**: PE sponsors are financially literate; you won't win credibility explaining EBITDA. They're skeptical of long-tail assumptions, prefer conservative estimates, and respect crisp downside cases. They're also time-constrained partners—their capital is deployed; every hour you work is opportunity cost. Get to the point. Bad news delivered quickly beats good news delivered slowly. They'd rather know the deal is broken than wait six weeks while you write a 200-page report.

**Sponsor-Side vs. Portfolio Company**: When you work for the fund (pre-deal or portfolio oversight), you're validating their thesis and protecting their capital. When you work for the portfolio company (post-acquisition), you're executing the thesis and proving the value creation case. The same PE firm is both your client and your client's client—manage that tension carefully.

## Commercial Due Diligence

**The Structure**: CDD typically runs 4–8 weeks in parallel with financial due diligence. You'll own three or four workstreams: market/competitive landscape, customer health and stickiness, pricing and go-to-market, and operational benchmarking. You're not writing strategy; you're validating the assumptions embedded in the model.

**The Core Questions**:
- Is the market attractive? (Size, growth, durability, cyclicality)
- Is the target well-positioned to win? (Market share, brand, competitive moat, go-to-market)
- Are the revenue assumptions achievable? (Customer concentration, pricing power, churn, NRR)
- Are the margin assumptions achievable? (Cost of goods, operating leverage, headcount, procurement)
- What could break the thesis? (Concentration, regulation, competition, customer concentration)

**Customer Conversations**: You'll interview 10–15 customers and 5–10 competitors under NDA. Don't ask "are you satisfied?"—you'll get diplomatic answers. Ask specific questions: Why do they buy? When did they last evaluate competitors? How sticky is the contract? What would it take to switch? How do they benchmark pricing? What keeps the CEO up at night about their vendor relationships? Listen for emotional commitment vs. economic incumbency.

**Competitive Benchmarking**: Find 3–5 comparable companies (often private or smaller public peers). Compare unit economics, CAC, LTV, churn, NRR, gross margin. Visit their websites, talk to their customers and former employees. Understand who the real competitors are—often they're not the ones the target names. A target might fear a large competitor that's not actually focused on their market; the real threat is often a smaller, hungrier player or a category-adjacent entrant.

**Sponsor-Ready CDD**: Looks different from corporate strategy work. It's not comprehensive; it's credible and pressure-tested. You've stress-tested the model assumptions against ground truth. You've identified the biggest risks and characterized their magnitude. You've found the one or two assumptions that move the IRR needle most. You've war-gamed downside cases. You've brought bad news early and clearly. You've made no promises you can't keep at day 100. A CDD that gets "picked apart" in sponsor meetings usually means you over-promised, didn't test your assumptions with enough rigor, or missed a key risk.

## Investment Thesis Development

**The Thesis as Scaffolding**: The investment thesis isn't a strategy document. It's a structured argument for why the sponsor will make money. It typically has 3–5 value creation levers and a handful of key assumptions that, if wrong, sink the deal. Your job is to help sharpen it by identifying which levers are real, which assumptions need validation, and which are brittle.

**Testing the Thesis**: Work backward from the return. If they need 25% IRR on a 4x leverage, 7-year hold, what does the exit look like? What EBITDA multiple? What revenue? What margin? Now work forward from current state: What would have to change to get there? Are those changes realistic? Are they mutually exclusive? Have other companies in the sector pulled them off?

**Stress-Testing the Levers**: A typical thesis might rest on (1) 15% annual revenue growth, (2) margin improvement from 20% to 25%, and (3) exit at 10x EBITDA instead of 8x. Run the math separately: If growth is 8% but margin hits 26%, what's the return? If growth is 15% but you exit at 8x, what's the return? Which assumptions are load-bearing? Which ones have implicit dependencies (e.g., growth requires new management; new management is expensive)?

**Credibility vs. Hope**: A credible thesis is one where the value creation levers have been proven in the sector or within the same company at scale. It's one where management can execute and the market can absorb the change. A hopeful thesis assumes the market will behave differently or that this team will outperform historical benchmarks. Hopeful theses aren't bad—but you need to know which part is hope and size the risk accordingly.

## The 100-Day Plan in PE

**Why It Matters**: PE sponsors measure 100-day plans ruthlessly. It's a forcing function. You prove the thesis early or you raise questions about whether the thesis was sound. By day 30, they expect to see traction on quick wins (cost cuts, rapid revenue actions, operational fixes). By day 60, they're watching whether the strategic initiatives are credible. By day 100, they're asking: Did we miss anything? Is the management team capable? Will we hit our year-one targets?

**The Workstreams**:
1. **Cash and Quick Wins** (weeks 1–4): Find $5–15M of annual run-rate improvements in cost. These are negotiated vendor rebates, headcount optimization, SG&A cuts, and low-risk operational improvements. Lock them in. Early wins build credibility and buy time for harder initiatives.
2. **Financial Governance** (weeks 1–4): Install a monthly close, rolling cash flow forecast, and weekly executive reporting. PE sponsors live in their model; they need to know actuals vs. plan daily. This is unsexy but critical.
3. **Strategic Initiatives** (weeks 1–8): Define 3–4 meaningful value creation workstreams (new product line, go-to-market redesign, margin improvement program, M&A target). Assign owners, set milestones, build detailed 90-day plans for each.
4. **Leadership Assessment** (weeks 1–8): Assess the existing management team rigorously. Who stays, who goes, who needs coaching? Who do you need to hire? This is political and it's urgent.
5. **Narrative Building** (weeks 8–12): Start building the "exit ready" story. What's the narrative for a buyer? What are the key risks to solve between now and exit?

**Sequencing Matters**: Cash first, then governance, then strategy. Don't spend weeks designing a new go-to-market motion before you've cut $10M in cost and stabilized the financial close. Sponsors respect the discipline of this sequence.

## Value Creation Levers: The PE Playbook

**Revenue Growth**:
- Pricing: Identify price elasticity. Often targets are underpriced by 5–15%. Run through customer concentration risk first.
- Go-to-market: Hire a head of sales or reposition the sales team. Invest in pipeline. Often the bottleneck is salesforce effectiveness, not market demand.
- Product/adjacent offerings: Launch a new module, expand into adjacent customer segments, build a platform play. Usually a 2–3 year initiative.
- M&A: Acquire a competitor or a bolt-on product. Rarely the core lever, but sometimes critical for scale or defensibility.

**Margin Improvement**:
- Procurement: Consolidate vendor relationships. Often worth 1–3 points of EBITDA margin. Requires ruthlessness about switching and leverage.
- Headcount: Right-size the organization to revenue and operating model. Usually 10–20% reduction, sometimes more. Never an easy win, but often a necessary one.
- Operational efficiency: Automation, asset-light models, shared services. Lower impact per dollar invested but often sustainable.
- Mix shift: Emphasize higher-margin products or customers. Requires discipline about deprioritizing lower-margin business.

**Working Capital**:
- Days sales outstanding (DSO): Accelerate collections. Often worth $10–30M of cash. Run rate improvements are modest, but the one-time benefit is material.
- Inventory: Reduce inventory days without creating stockout risk. Usually 5–15% reduction.
- Days payable outstanding (DPO): Extend payment terms. Works once. Requires vendor management.

**Multiple Expansion**:
- Story: Build a narrative of consistent growth, margin improvement, and de-risked business model.
- Scale: Revenue growth supports a higher multiple. $50M revenue might sell at 6x EBITDA; $150M might sell at 9x.
- Quality of earnings: Make sure historical earnings are clean and repeatable. Restate if you have to.
- Sustainability: Remove key-person dependencies, customer concentration, and supply chain risks. Buyers pay for predictability.

**Sizing the Levers Quickly**: Model each lever independently, then conservatively together (25% benefit when you stack two levers that should add 30%). Ask: Have we seen this in comparable companies? Have we seen it in this company at scale before? If not, discount by 30–50%. This is how you move from "ambitious" to "credible."

## Working with Portfolio Company Management

**The Political Reality**: The CEO and CFO who were running the company pre-acquisition are often not the ones who will take it to exit. They're insecure about their future, uncertain whether the new owners trust them, and sometimes actively hostile to consultants (who they see as threats). The sponsor is watching whether they can execute a new agenda.

**Building Credibility with the CEO**:
- Show them early that you're not here to replace them. You're here to help them prove the thesis and buy equity or retention bonuses.
- Give them credit for the wins. Financial governance improvements, cost cuts, revenue actions—frame these as "you were waiting for capital and permission to do this."
- Respect their operational knowledge. They know the business better than you do. You know what's worked in other companies. Combine the two.
- Listen more than you talk in the first 30 days. You'll learn where the real problems are and where the team is already aligned with change.

**Navigating Ownership**: The sponsor wants pace and rigor. The CEO wants autonomy and stability. Your job is to be the translator. "Sponsor needs to see X by day 30" is more credible than "I think you should do X." Let them own the plan; you own the rigor.

**When the CEO Is a Liability**: If the CEO can't execute the thesis or won't adapt, the sponsor will move faster than you think. Have a plan for transition leadership, loss of knowledge, and business continuity. Be clear about which levers require the existing CEO vs. a new one.

## The Exit Thesis: Building an Exit-Ready Narrative

**From Day One**: PE sponsors are asking "Who will buy this and why?" Start thinking about exit narrative in week one, not year six. What's the story? Bolt-on acquisition? Roll-up platform? Strategic buyer in adjacent market? IPO?

**What Buyers Look For**:
- **Recurring revenue**: SaaS or contracted businesses are worth 2–3x multiples higher than transactional ones. Show contract duration, renewal rates, and NRR.
- **Scalability**: Demonstrate that the business can double revenue without doubling headcount. Show unit economics and operating leverage.
- **Defensibility**: What prevents a competitor from taking share? Is it product, brand, switching costs, or just inertia? Inertia doesn't hold up in an exit.
- **Management**: A team that can run without the founder. A track record of hitting targets. Ideally, a team that's credible to the buyer's stakeholders.
- **Clean financials**: No restatements, no surprises. Clear definition of revenue recognition and EBITDA. Normalized for one-time items or founder spending.
- **Customer quality**: Diversification, stickiness, no concentration in a customer that's at risk. Show churn and NRR trends over time.

**Common Hair in an Exit Process**:
- Customer concentration (>30% from top 3 customers): Diversify or be transparent about why concentration is stable.
- Key-person dependency: Formalize the CEO role, build the team, show redundancy.
- Revenue quality: Ensure the largest contracts are renewed and growing. Show actual churn, not assumed churn.
- Margin sustainability: Prove that margin improvements were structural, not one-time. Normalize for any post-acquisition investments.
- Regulatory or compliance risk: Clean up any ambiguity about licensing, data handling, or industry regulation before exit.
- Integration synergy dependency: If part of the thesis was synergies with parent, show how you'll hit targets without them.

**Building the Narrative**: Work with the sponsor and CFO to build a clear "exit ready" story by year two. It should be true (not fiction), but crafted. "We're a niche, profitable, recurring-revenue business with strong margins, a proven go-to-market, and a management team that's scaled before" is stronger than "We're a $100M revenue business."

## Key PE Metrics: The Language You Need

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return)**: The annualized return on the equity investment. A 20% IRR is solid. 25%+ is the fund's target. Run the math: If you invest $100M in 2026 and sell for $400M in 2032, what's your IRR? It's the discount rate that makes the NPV of those cash flows zero. (Answer: ~25%.)

**MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital)**: The ratio of exit value to entry value. 3x MOIC = you tripled your money. MOIC is easier to calculate than IRR (exit ÷ entry) but it ignores timing. A 3x return in 3 years is better than a 3x return in 7 years (higher IRR).

**Cash-on-Cash Return**: The ratio of cumulative cash distributions to cash invested. Useful when there are interim distributions (dividends paid from FCF).

**EBITDA Bridge**: The quarterly or annual progression of EBITDA. It should show: starting EBITDA, add revenue growth (gross profit improvement), add margin expansion, add working capital benefit, equals ending EBITDA. This is how you track whether the thesis is progressing.

**Debt Paydown**: How much debt have you paid down since close? Sponsors measure this rigorously. It's a proxy for free cash flow and for the business improving. A strong EBITDA with flat debt is red flag (where's the cash going?). Strong EBITDA with steady debt paydown is proof of concept.

**Leverage Covenant Math**: If you borrowed 4x EBITDA, you need to hit EBITDA targets to stay under covenants. Miss EBITDA by 10%, miss your leverage covenant, and you have a waiver conversation (expensive). This is why sponsors obsess over near-term EBITDA. It's not just a KPI; it's a constraint on your flexibility.

**Dealing with Leverage**: More leverage amplifies return (if you hit your targets) and risk (if you miss them). A 4x leveraged deal that generates 25% IRR is better than a 2x deal that generates 16% IRR (same equity outlay, different returns). But miss your targets and 4x leverage forces recapitalization or a down-round exit. Always stress-test the model at -10% EBITDA to see if you violate covenants or create tension with the lender.

**Multiple Expansion vs. EBITDA Growth**: If you buy a business at 8x EBITDA for $100M and exit it at 10x EBITDA with flat earnings, you've made 25% (10/8). If you grow EBITDA 20% and stay at 8x, you've made 44%. Sponsors usually build their thesis on some combination. "We'll grow EBITDA 15% and expand multiple from 8x to 9x" is credible. "We'll grow EBITDA 5% and expand to 12x" is hopeful (you're betting the market re-rates the business).

## Practical Reminders

**Know Your Boundaries**: You're validating and executing the thesis, not setting strategy or replacing management. When the sponsor asks you to do both, be clear about the tension. It limits your credibility with the CEO if you're also the one evaluating them for replacement.

**Default to Conservatism**: In the model, in your assumptions, in your communication. Sponsor will appreciate it. It buys you credibility for the moments when you need to raise a genuine concern.

**Speed Over Perfection**: 80% of the insight in 20% of the time. You don't need to solve every problem; you need to identify the material ones and validate or invalidate the thesis. After week eight, scope creep is your enemy.

**Covenants and Lenders**: The lender isn't your ally, but they're part of the landscape. Understand the covenant package and flex clauses. Some sponsors are ruthlessly optimistic; the lender is the adult in the room. If the lender is nervous, you should be too.
