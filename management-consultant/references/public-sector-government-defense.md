## Contracting Fundamentals

### Three Layers of U.S. Government Contracting

**Federal Level**
- Governed by Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) and Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS)
- Largest addressable market; most rigid compliance requirements; longest sales cycles
- Primary buyers: DoD, intelligence community (CIA, NSA, DIA), civilian agencies (HHS, VA, DHS, EPA, GSA, etc.)
- Typical engagement cycle: 6–24 months from proposal to contract award

**State & Local**
- Less heavily regulated than federal; often follows Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200) for federal passthrough funds
- Smaller contract values but faster cycles (3–6 months typical)
- Key buyers: state education agencies, state health departments, city/county governments, public authorities (transit, water, housing)
- Often more receptive to innovation pilots and capability-building engagements

**International Development**
- Governed by bilateral donor rules (e.g., USAID ADS, World Bank procurement framework)
- Longer timelines; emphasis on local capacity building and sustainability
- Key buyers: USAID, State Department, World Bank, regional development banks, UN agencies

## Federal Procurement Models

### IDIQ (Indefinite Delivery, Indefinite Quantity) Contracts

**Structure:**
- GSA (General Services Administration) or agency awards a multi-year IDIQ vehicle; contractors compete for task orders under it
- Indefinite timeline and quantity; contractor has no guaranteed minimum; government has no cap on total spend
- Typical term: 5 years base + 5 years option (10 years total potential)

**Consultant Role:**
- Help clients (often mid-market contractors) win IDIQ vehicles by building strong past performance case studies
- Advise on pricing strategy: must be competitive on task order bids but sustainable long-term
- Support proposal writing for IDIQ wins; help set up indirect cost structures and labor categories
- Track indirect cost rates with government Contracting Officer, which affect future bid competitiveness

**Financial Model:**
- Revenue is lumpy; may have 6–12 month gaps between task order awards
- Working capital planning essential; many smaller firms underestimate cash flow risk
- Overhead absorption: must carefully project labor utilization to avoid overhead burn

---

### GSA Schedule (IDIQ Light)

**Structure:**
- Pre-competed, pre-priced indefinite-quantity contract with GSA
- Contractor submits pricing schedule (labor categories + rates, materials, indirect costs); GSA approves; contract is awarded
- Vendors compete to win customers through proposal responses; GSA plays matchmaker role

**Consultant Role:**
- Help mid-market firms prepare GSA Schedule proposal and pricing schedule
- Advise on labor category selection and rate-setting: must be competitive but protect margin
- Build marketing collateral post-award to win task order bids
- Monitor compliance (e.g., annual sales reporting, price adjustments, subcontracting rules)

**Advantages vs. IDIQ:**
- Faster approval (3–6 months vs. 12+ months for IDIQ)
- Lower proposal cost
- Access to GSA's small contractor universe and federal agency buyers
- Transparent pricing encourages higher order volume

**Common Pitfall:** Pricing too aggressively at award to win the contract, then margin starvation on subsequent task orders.

---

### Task Order Competitions

**Single-Award Task Orders**
- Government issues RFQ/RFP; one contractor wins; delivers services
- Typical ceiling: $2.5M for small business set-asides; up to $10M+ for unrestricted
- Timeline: 30–90 days from RFQ to award
- Consultant role: proposal strategy, past performance narrative, pricing optimization

**Multiple-Award Task Orders**
- Government holds multiple IDIQ vehicles (often 3–5); each task order competitively awarded
- Encourages lower pricing; contractor must bid hard to win
- Consultant role: competitive intelligence (who else is on the vehicle?), win strategy, bid pricing calibration

**Best-Value vs. LPTA Competitions**
- **Best-Value:** Technical merit + past performance + price weighted together; highest-scoring proposal typically wins
- **LPTA (Lowest Price, Technically Acceptable):** Lowest-priced proposal that meets minimum technical requirements wins
- LPTA dominates DoD and civilian agencies in cost-focused procurements; best-value reserved for complex, high-risk work
- Consultant implication: In LPTA, proposal writing becomes commoditized; win strategy shifts to cost control and delivery excellence

## Socioeconomic Set-Asides & Programs

### 8(a) Business Development Program

**Eligibility:**
- Firm must be at least 51% owned and controlled by socially and economically disadvantaged individual(s)
- Average annual revenue <$7.5M (for most sectors; higher threshold for certain industries)
- Requires SBA certification; can take 2–4 months to process

**Benefit:**
- Access to 8(a) set-aside competitions (federal contracts reserved for 8(a) firms)
- 8(a) set-asides typically 15–20% higher win rates than open competitions due to reduced competitor pool
- Joint ventures with non-8(a) primes allowed (limited to 49% of work)
- Ability to transition to 9(j) small business status after graduation

**Consultant Role:**
- Help eligible entrepreneurs apply for 8(a) certification and structure business to maintain eligibility
- Advise on pipeline: which 8(a) set-aside opportunities align with firm capability
- Build past performance record during 8(a) phase to enable larger unrestricted competitions post-graduation
- Model economic impact of set-asides vs. open competitions for growth planning

**Lifecycle:** Typical 8-year term in program; can apply for graduation after 5 years if exceeding size standards.

---

### HUBZone (Historically Underutilized Business Zone)

**Eligibility:**
- At least 51% owned and controlled by U.S. citizen(s)
- Principal office located in federally designated HUBZone (economically distressed areas)
- At least 35% of workforce must be HUBZone residents
- Average annual revenue varies by industry

**Benefit:**
- Preferential scoring in competitive procurements (typically 10% price preference)
- Access to HUBZone set-aside competitions
- Contracts reserved for HUBZone firms in specified dollar ranges

**Consultant Role:**
- Verify eligibility; HUBZone boundaries change; must confirm location status annually
- Advise on workforce planning to maintain 35% residency threshold
- Help quantify cumulative benefit of HUBZone preference in specific procurement landscape

**Typical Profile:** Regional contractors, construction firms, IT services firms based in Rust Belt or rural areas.

---

### SDVOSB (Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business)

**Eligibility:**
- At least 51% owned and controlled by service-disabled veteran(s) (with disability rating from VA)
- Veteran must be active in management

**Benefit:**
- Access to SDVOSB set-asides across federal and DoD procurements
- DoD heavily favors SDVOSB on cybersecurity, IT, and professional services
- No size standard cap; can compete for large contracts post-graduation from small business status

**Consultant Role:**
- Help veteran entrepreneurs structure ownership and management roles
- Advise on DoD contracting landscape where SDVOSB preference is strongest
- Build narrative around veteran leadership and resilience in proposals

---

### Women-Owned Small Business (WOSB) & Economically Disadvantaged Women-Owned Small Business (EDWOSB)

**WOSB:** At least 51% owned and controlled by women; must meet size standard.

**EDWOSB:** WOSB + members must be economically disadvantaged (net worth <$750k).

**Benefit:** Access to WOSB and EDWOSB set-aside competitions; EDWOSB has more set-asides.

**Consultant Role:** Similar to 8(a); help structure ownership, build pipeline, model set-aside benefit in contracting strategy.

## Key Government Sectors

### Defense & National Security

**Primary Buyers:** DoD (Army, Navy, Air Force, Space Force, Marines, DLA), intelligence community (CIA, NSA, DIA, NGA), defense agencies (DTRA, DARPA, STRATCOM)

**Consulting Themes:**
- **Digital Modernization:** Migrating legacy systems to cloud, AI/ML for predictive maintenance and supply chain, edge computing for tactical environments
- **Acquisition Reform:** Streamlining procurement processes, reducing time-to-capability, adopting agile for software development
- **Workforce Transformation:** Reskilling workforce for cyber operations, data science, autonomous systems
- **Supply Chain Resilience:** Reshoring critical capabilities, qualifying alternative suppliers, reducing single-source dependencies
- **Cybersecurity & Resilience:** Zero-trust architecture, CMMC compliance, incident response planning

**Key Constraints:**
- Security clearances required for many roles (minimum 6-month delay to obtain Secret; 12–18 months for Top Secret)
- DFARS compliance (cybersecurity, counterfeit parts, supply chain risk management) adds significant overhead
- Contract type: Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee (CPFF) or Time-and-Materials (T&M) most common; fixed-price rare due to scope uncertainty
- Decision cycles: Military decision-making in budget year (B-year) vs. execution year; long lag between decision and spend authorization

**Typical Engagement Size:** $500k–$5M per year; often multi-year (3–5 years)

**Go-to-Market:** Nearly impossible without prime contractor relationship; often enter as subcontractor to large primes (Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman) and build credibility over 3–5 years

---

### Civilian Agencies

**Key Buyers:** HHS (CMS, NIH, CDC), VA, DHS, DOJ, EPA, Treasury, State Department, Education, Interior, Agriculture, Commerce, Labor

**Consulting Themes:**
- **IT Modernization:** Legacy system retirement, cloud migration, API-first architecture, open source adoption
- **Shared Services Consolidation:** Finance shared service centers, HR transformation, IT service consolidation
- **Program Evaluation & Analytics:** RCT-based program evaluation, predictive analytics for program targeting, cost-effectiveness analysis
- **Healthcare Transformation:** CMS payment reform (value-based care), interoperability, behavioral health integration
- **Workforce Analytics:** Skills gap analysis, recruitment pipeline optimization, retention modeling
- **Data Governance:** Master data management, data quality frameworks, governance structures post-FITARA

**Key Constraints:**
- Budget cycles rigid (fiscal year Oct–Sep); multiyear contracts must align with annual appropriation
- Political risk: Change in administration can halt programs or shift priorities
- FOIA transparency: Consultant deliverables, timelines, costs often public record; must build defensible methodologies
- Union dynamics: Federal workforce often unionized (AFGE); change management must account for union consultation requirements

**Typical Engagement Size:** $250k–$3M per year

**Go-to-Market:** Often direct RFQ response; less hierarchical than defense prime landscape

---

### State & Local Government

**Key Buyers:** State education agencies, state health departments, city/county governments, public utilities, transit authorities, housing authorities

**Consulting Themes:**
- **Education Reform:** District turnaround (low-performing schools), charter school growth, teacher evaluation and development, STEM pipeline
- **Medicaid Transformation:** State-specific payment models, behavioral health integration, long-term care optimization, eligibility simplification
- **Smart City / Civic Tech:** Permitting modernization, data-driven budgeting, police reform, traffic optimization
- **Workforce Development:** Skills training alignment with labor market, apprenticeship programs, incumbent worker upskilling
- **Public Financial Management:** GASB accounting reform, pension sustainability, enterprise risk management

**Key Constraints:**
- Smaller budgets than federal; longer cycle due to local political approval (city council, school board)
- Federally-funded programs (e.g., ARPA funding) come with specific rules and audit requirements
- Change fatigue common; consulting must emphasize quick wins + sustainable change, not transformation theater
- Public employee unions often politically powerful; consulting must navigate labor relations carefully

**Typical Engagement Size:** $100k–$1M per engagement

**Go-to-Market:** Often through state/local government procurement office; bid preparation essential

---

### International Development

**Primary Buyers:** USAID, State Department, World Bank, Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, UN agencies (UNHCR, WFP, WHO), bilateral donors (UK's FCDO, Germany's GIZ, Canada's IDRC)

**Consulting Themes:**
- **Fragile States:** Governance strengthening, conflict sensitivity, institution-building in post-conflict environments
- **Development Finance:** Blended finance models, impact investing, public-private partnerships for development
- **Health Systems:** Primary care strengthening, epidemic preparedness, maternal health, HIV/AIDS programs
- **Economic Growth & Competitiveness:** Value chain development, SME access to finance, trade policy reform, Special Economic Zone development
- **Climate & Environment:** Climate adaptation, renewable energy transition, natural resource governance, water resource management
- **Education Systems:** Teacher training, curriculum reform, girls' education, STEM in low-income countries

**Key Constraints:**
- Sustainability imperative: Consulting must build local capacity, not create dependency on external consultants
- Local context complexity: Must navigate political economy, informal institutions, cultural norms
- Donor compliance: USAID ADS Manual, World Bank fiduciary standards, anti-corruption rules (Foreign Corrupt Practices Act) add overhead
- Procurement cycles: 3–6 month proposal cycles; 6–12 month contract negotiation; payment delays common

**Typical Engagement Size:** $500k–$3M per year

**Go-to-Market:** Through USAID BPA (Blanket Purchase Agreement) vehicles or World Bank framework contracts; relationship-based

## Common Consulting Engagements

### Agency Modernization

**IT Modernization Roadmap**
- Assessment of legacy systems: technical debt, vendor lock-in, security vulnerabilities
- Multi-year cloud migration strategy: sequencing, infrastructure-as-code, cost modeling
- Skills gap and upskilling plan
- Governance model for cloud adoption
- Budget estimate and ROI modeling

**Typical Timeline:** 3–4 months
**Typical Cost:** $300k–$800k
**Key Success Factors:** Strong sponsorship from CIO/Deputy CIO; alignment with strategic plan; realistic cost estimates

**AI/ML Capability Roadmap**
- Use case discovery: scan agency operations for AI applications
- Pilot business case: cost-benefit, timeline, technical feasibility for top 1–2 use cases
- Data readiness assessment: data quality, governance, access controls
- Skills and vendor strategy
- Governance and ethics framework

**Typical Timeline:** 3–4 months
**Typical Cost:** $400k–$1M
**Key Success Factors:** Executive sponsorship; realistic expectations on timelines and data quality; vendor-agnostic approach

---

### Shared Services Consolidation

**Finance Shared Service Center (SSC)**
- Process mapping: invoice-to-pay, order-to-cash, GL and close processes across agencies
- Benchmark: processing costs, cycle times vs. best-in-class organizations
- Target operating model: centralized vs. distributed, in-house vs. outsourced, geography (domestic vs. offshore)
- Business case: headcount reduction, cycle time improvement, cost savings; phasing and transformation cost
- Governance model: steering committee, change management, SLA framework

**Typical Timeline:** 4–6 months
**Typical Cost:** $500k–$1.5M
**Key Success Factors:** Executive alignment on vision; realistic headcount reduction assumptions; union notification plan; detailed change plan

**HR Service Center Transformation**
- Current state: process mapping, channel analysis (phone, email, portal), transaction costs
- Target state: self-service, automation, shared service center architecture
- Technology selection: HRIS, chat bots, RPA for transactions
- Organizational redesign: FTE requirement, skill re-mapping, training plan
- Business case and phasing

**Typical Timeline:** 4–6 months
**Typical Cost:** $400k–$1.2M

---

### Workforce Transformation

**Reskilling Program Design**
- Skills gap analysis: compare current workforce skills to future-state requirements (e.g., cybersecurity, data science, cloud engineering)
- Learning path design: competency frameworks, curriculum development, training modalities (classroom, online, apprenticeship)
- Incentive design: retention bonuses, promotion pathways, sabbaticals for retraining
- Measurement framework: completion rates, competency assessments, retention, time-to-productivity

**Typical Timeline:** 3–4 months (for design; implementation 12–24 months)
**Typical Cost:** $250k–$600k
**Key Success Factors:** Union engagement; realistic timelines; clear incentives for participation; executive commitment to hiring those who complete training

**Recruitment Pipeline Transformation**
- Source mapping: where are target talent pools (colleges, bootcamps, career switchers)?
- Experience and education requirements: are current KSAs too restrictive? Can bootcamp graduates qualify for cybersecurity roles?
- Brand and outreach: federal employment brand often weak vs. private sector; must invest in recruitment marketing
- Hiring process: can streamline to 60–90 days vs. current 6–12 months?
- Internship and apprenticeship programs: pipeline for early-career talent

**Typical Timeline:** 3–4 months
**Typical Cost:** $150k–$400k

---

### Program Evaluation

**Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) Design**
- Intervention design: What is the program testing? (e.g., Does text-based reminder increase preventive care utilization?)
- Randomization strategy: individual-level, cluster-level (e.g., clinics), stepped wedge (rollout over time)
- Sample size calculation: statistical power, effect size assumptions, dropout rates
- Data collection plan: which outcomes, how measured, data sources
- Analysis plan: intent-to-treat, heterogeneous effects, cost-effectiveness

**Typical Timeline:** 3–6 months (for design; study 12–36 months)
**Typical Cost:** $200k–$600k (design only)
**Key Success Factors:** Clear theory of change; feasible randomization; executive commitment to use findings; sufficient sample size

**Quasi-Experimental Evaluation**
- When RCT infeasible (program already rolled out, ethical concerns, etc.), use quasi-experimental design
- Approaches: regression discontinuity (if there's a cutoff), difference-in-differences (compare treatment vs. control areas over time), propensity score matching (match similar units)
- Validity threats: selection bias, time trends, measurement error
- Mitigation strategy: sensitivity analyses, robustness checks, triangulation with qualitative data

**Typical Timeline:** 3–4 months (design); 6–12 months (execution)
**Typical Cost:** $150k–$500k

---

### Policy Analysis

**Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)**
- Follows OMB Circular A-94 (guidance on regulatory analysis)
- Identify all costs and benefits to society (not just government budget)
- Monetize benefits: willingness-to-pay, revealed preference, hedonic pricing, value of statistical life
- Discount future benefits/costs using OMB-prescribed rates (typically 3% and 7% real discount rates)
- Sensitivity analysis: vary discount rate, benefit assumptions, cost assumptions
- Report NPV, benefit-cost ratio, internal rate of return

**Typical Use:** Regulatory impact analysis, major program expansion, technology investment decisions

**Typical Timeline:** 4–8 weeks
**Typical Cost:** $50k–$200k

**Evidence Synthesis / Evidence Gap Mapping**
- When policy question is complex and evidence is dispersed, systematically map existing research
- Rapid evidence review: literature search, screening criteria, data extraction
- Identify gaps: what is well-studied? What is unstudied?
- Synthesize findings: systematic review or meta-analysis if sufficient studies
- Recommend research priorities and piloting

**Typical Timeline:** 4–12 weeks
**Typical Cost:** $75k–$300k

## Consulting Frameworks for Government

### Logic Model / Theory of Change

**Logic Model Structure:**
- **Inputs:** Resources required (budget, staff, partner capacity, data)
- **Activities:** What the program does (e.g., train teachers, provide subsidized childcare)
- **Outputs:** Direct products of activities (# trained, # children served)
- **Outcomes (Short/Medium/Long-Term):** Changes in knowledge, behavior, condition; ultimate impact
- **Assumptions:** Things that must be true for the model to work

**Use Case:** Clarify program logic before RCT or evaluation; align stakeholders on theory; identify key leverage points

**Example (Education):** Logic model for teacher professional development program
- Input: $5M, 50 PD specialists, partnership with universities
- Activity: Provide 3-day workshops + coaching to 500 teachers on literacy instruction
- Output: 500 teachers trained, 15,000 students reached
- Short-term outcome: Teachers adopt new practices (measured by classroom observation)
- Medium-term outcome: Student literacy gains (measured by reading assessments)
- Long-term outcome: 5-year graduation rate increases
- Assumptions: Teachers have autonomy to change practice; literacy instruction is a bottleneck; coaching reinforces classroom practice

---

### Balanced Scorecard (Government Context)

**Four Perspectives (adapted for public sector):**
- **Mission Achievement:** Outcomes aligned with agency mission; include leading indicators (e.g., % of eligible population reached)
- **Operational Excellence:** Efficiency (cost per unit served, cycle time), quality (defect rates, customer satisfaction)
- **People & Culture:** Workforce engagement, retention, diversity, skill level
- **Learning & Innovation:** R&D investment, innovation pipeline, organizational learning metrics

**Difference from Private Sector:** Less emphasis on financial returns; more emphasis on equity (are outcomes equitable across populations?) and sustainability (can outcomes be sustained with current resources?)

**Implementation:** Typical 3-month engagement to develop scorecard; pilot with one business unit before rollout.

---

### GPRA (Government Performance and Results Act)

**Requirement:** All federal agencies must establish performance goals (annual), performance measures, and report on achievement

**Consultant Role:** Help agencies define SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), select meaningful measures, establish baselines, and develop data systems to track results

**Key Pitfalls:** Goals too ambitious or too conservative; measures misaligned with actual mission; data not collected reliably

---

### FITARA (Federal IT Acquisition Reform Act)

**Core Requirement:** CIO must have budget authority and governance over all agency IT spending (was: agency heads could buy IT without CIO approval)

**Consultant Role:** Help agencies restructure IT governance, establish IT investment review boards, align IT strategy with mission, implement PMO discipline

**Key Challenges:** Organizational resistance (agency heads accustomed to autonomy); weak CIO authority in some agencies; legacy IT spending hard to govern retroactively

## Government vs. Private Sector Differences

| **Dimension** | **Government** | **Private Sector** |
|---|---|---|
| **Decision Cycle** | Budget cycle-driven (1-year); political election cycles (4 years) | Market-driven; quarterly earnings cycles; CEO tenure average 5-7 years |
| **Budget Flexibility** | Annually appropriated; often cannot carry forward unspent funds; supplement ("rescission") rare | Annual plan; can reallocate mid-year; more flexibility on investment timing |
| **Stakeholder Complexity** | Elected officials, unions, interagency partners, public interest groups, media, IG office; every decision scrutinized | Board, executive team, shareholders, customers; limited external scrutiny |
| **Procurement Timelines** | 6-24 months from RFQ to contract award (FAR compliance, competition) | 3-6 months typical; some fast-track (agile RFPs) |
| **Labor Market** | Unionized; rigid grade/pay scales; hard to hire/fire; tenure-protected | Competitive labor market; flexible compensation; at-will employment (most states) |
| **Transparency** | FOIA, public records, congressional hearings; all deliverables/costs public record | Confidential; competitive sensitivity allows information hiding |
| **Risk Tolerance** | Low; public failure high-consequence (media, IG investigation); innovation limited | Higher; failure manageable; rapid iteration cultural norm |
| **Compliance Burden** | High (FAR, DFARS, Circular A-94, OMB guidance, ethics rules, FOIA, union contracts) | Lower; industry-specific (healthcare, financial services) but not government-level |
| **Performance Measurement** | Output & outcome metrics required; attribution difficult (political factors dominate); leading indicators less trusted | P&L; ROI; market share; attribution via financial reporting |
| **Consulting Engagement Model** | Often body-shop (time-and-materials); long-term resident relationships common | Project-based; fixed deliverables; shorter timelines |

## Benchmarks & Market Intelligence

### Federal IT Spending
- **Total Federal IT Budget:** ~$100B annually (2024 estimate across all agencies)
- **Cloud Adoption:** 30-40% of federal IT spending now cloud-based (growing 15-20% annually)
- **Breakdown by agency:** DoD ~$40B, Civilian ~$30B, Veterans ~$10B, other ~$20B
- **Growth Drivers:** Cloud migration, cybersecurity post-breach, AI/ML pilots, legacy system retirement

### Defense Budget & Procurement
- **Total Defense Budget:** ~$820B (2024)
- **Acquisition Budget:** ~$140B (17% of total)
- **Top Contractors by Revenue:** Lockheed Martin ($66B), Boeing ($34B), Northrop Grumman ($36B), Raytheon ($28B), General Dynamics ($44B)
- **Growth Areas:** Hypersonics, autonomous systems, space, AI, cyber

### GovCon Market Growth
- **Total Federal Contracting Spend:** ~$650B annually (2023)
- **Annual Growth Rate:** 3-5% (compared to GDP growth ~2%)
- **Small Business Set-Asides:** ~20-25% of federal spend (declining % but growing absolute $ due to overall growth)
- **Fastest Growing Sectors:** Cybersecurity, cloud services, AI/ML, healthcare IT

### Consulting Day Rates (Government)
- **Entry-Level Consultant (2-4 years):** $200-$350/day
- **Senior Consultant (4-8 years):** $350-$600/day
- **Manager (8-12 years):** $600-$1,000/day
- **Principal/Partner (12+ years):** $1,000-$2,000+/day

**Note:** Government rates 20-30% lower than private sector due to budget constraints and longer cycles; offset by larger engagement sizes and relationship stability.

### Typical Engagement Sizes
- **Small Engagement (pilot/assessment):** $50k-$200k
- **Mid-Sized Engagement (implementation):** $250k-$1.5M
- **Large Engagement (multi-year transformation):** $2M-$10M+

**Typical Duration:**
- Assessment/scoping: 2-3 months
- Design: 2-4 months
- Implementation: 6-24 months
- Optimization/continuous improvement: 12-36 months

- **Global Big 3:** McKinsey (strong DoD, strong civilian, weak state/local), BCG (strong DoD, moderate civilian, weak international dev), Bain (moderate government presence; strong private sector focus)
- **Government-Specialist Firms:** Booz Allen Hamilton, Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC), Deloitte (government consulting division), EY, PwC, Accenture, IBM

### Win Rate Benchmarks
- **Open Competition:** 10-15% win rate (very competitive)
- **Set-Aside Competition (8(a), HUBZone, SDVOSB):** 30-50% win rate (smaller competitor pool)
- **Follow-On from GSA Schedule / IDIQ:** 40-60% win rate (incumbency advantage)
- **RFI Response to RFQ:** 20-30% conversion rate (if well-structured)

### Common Budget Allocation (Government Organization)
- **Personnel Costs:** 55-65% of operating budget
- **Contractors/Consultants:** 10-20% (growing due to IT modernization, compliance, and specialized expertise)
- **Technology/Tools:** 10-15% (cloud, software licenses, hardware)
- **Travel/Training:** 3-5%
- **Other:** 10-15%

---

### Red Flags / High-Risk Engagements
- **Engagement with unstable political leadership:** Administration change risk can halt programs
- **Lack of union engagement in transformation:** Will face implementation resistance; may trigger grievances
- **Vague scope or undefined success criteria:** Government stakeholder multiplicity often leads to scope creep
- **Insufficient budget for proper change management:** Consulting can design change; execution requires sustained investment
- **No clear owner for accountability:** Consulting deliverables often sit on shelf if no one responsible for implementation

---

## Summary

Government and defense consulting requires deep understanding of procurement mechanics, political economy, and stakeholder complexity. Success depends on mastery of compliance frameworks (FAR, DFARS, OMB guidance), realistic timelines, and relationship-building with risk-averse, budget-constrained buyers. The addressable market is large ($650B+ federal + state/local) but entry barriers are high (security clearances, past performance, compliance overhead). Differentiation comes from sector expertise (e.g., healthcare IT transformation, defense supply chain, USAID programs) rather than generic consulting. Engagement sizes are typically larger and longer than private sector, but day rates are 20-30% lower and decision cycles are slower.
