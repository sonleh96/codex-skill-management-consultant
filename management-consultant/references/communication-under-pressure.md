# Communication Under Pressure: MBB Consultant Reference

## 1. When a Client Challenges Your Analysis

A partner says: "I don't buy that number." Your instinct is to defend. Don't.

**First: Listen for what they're actually saying.** Are they:
- Pointing out a methodological flaw you missed? (This happens. Own it immediately.)
- Uncomfortable with the implication and pushing back? (Different problem.)
- Testing you to see if you're brittle? (Actually common.)

**If it's a real flaw:** Don't hedge. "You're right—I missed that. Let me recalculate and come back to you by EOD." Credibility lives in swift acknowledgment and follow-through, not in pretending you're infallible.

**If it's pushback on conclusion:** This is where your backbone matters. Don't backpedal because someone is senior. The right response is something like: "I understand why that feels like a stretch. Here's why I'm confident: the data shows X, Y, Z, and when I sense-check it against [benchmark/precedent/logic], it holds." Then shut up. Let them respond.

**The dangerous middle ground:** Collapsing into defensive explanation ("Well, actually, we used a rigorous methodology...") or over-accommodating ("Let me revisit this completely"). Both destroy credibility. You can be respectfully confident. Your job is to think clearly, not to be liked.

**Physical reality:** Lower your voice slightly when under pressure. Don't rush to fill silence. A 3-second pause before responding makes you sound like you're thinking, not scrambling. It also gives you time to actually think.

## 2. Hostile Q&A

The presentation is fine until it isn't. Someone leans forward. The tone shifts. You get questions like: "How did you even come up with this number?" or "Have you actually talked to anyone in operations?"

**What's really happening:** They're either threatened by the finding, doubt your credibility, or both.

**Listen beneath the words.** "How did you come up with this?" might mean "I think this is bull" or "I genuinely want to understand your work." Treat it as the latter until proven otherwise. Answer the professional question, not the emotional accusation.

**For aggressive framing of a real question:** "That's an important question. Let me make sure I answer it properly—are you asking about our sample selection, or about the representativeness of our findings?" This buys you time, shows you're thinking, and often reveals they're asking something narrower than their tone suggested.

**For questions you don't know:** This is a strength, not a weakness, if you handle it right. "I don't have that specific data in front of me, but here's my instinct based on X and Y. Let me confirm that by end of day, and if I'm wrong, I'll flag where it changes our recommendation." This is vastly better than guessing or rambling. Clients respect precision and intellectual honesty.

**For loaded questions:** Someone asks, "Given that your team doesn't understand our business, how can you possibly recommend this?" The loaded assumption is embedded in the framing. Call it out gently: "I hear the skepticism. I think it's fair to say we're learning your business in real time, and I'd push back on the idea that we don't understand it—here's what we've gotten right so far. That said, if there's something we're missing, tell me now." Then listen.

**End hostile Q&A how you started it:** Calmly, clearly, with no performative frustration.

## 3. When You Get Something Wrong

You're 10 slides in. You reference a number and instantly know it's off. Or worse: a client catches it.

**In the moment—move fast but not frantically:** Interrupt yourself if it's yours to catch. "Actually, let me correct that—that number is X, not Y. The implication for this finding is..." and then tell them what it means. Don't apologize excessively. Don't overthink it. Correct, explain impact, move forward.

**When a client points it out:** "You're right to catch that. Let me confirm we're looking at the same data, but yes, if it's Z instead of Y, that changes this conclusion to [revised conclusion]." Again: acknowledge, don't minimize, don't spiral.

**Why this works:** You're treating errors as what they are—course corrections, not character indictments. Experienced leaders respect consultants who correct fast and keep moving. What kills you is appearing defensive or rattled.

**The follow-up:** Send a corrected analysis the same day if possible. One sentence: "Per our discussion in the meeting, I've corrected the analysis. Here's the updated finding. It doesn't change our primary recommendation, but I wanted to flag that [implication]." You've solved the problem and shown diligence.

## 4. Delivering Bad News Executives Don't Want to Hear

Your finding is solid. It's also not what they want to hear. Their flagship product underperforms. Their reorganization created silos it was supposed to eliminate. The market has moved and they're behind.

**Never lead with methodology.** Executives don't care why you're right; they care what it means and what they do about it.

**Lead with implication, then evidence:** "This initiative isn't generating the ROI we planned. Here's why that matters for next year's budget allocation. And here's what the data shows." Implication first anchors them. Then data makes it undeniable.

**Frame around their goals, not your analysis.** Instead of "Our survey shows customer satisfaction is down," try: "If we want to hit next year's revenue target, we need to address what's driving the satisfaction gap in [segment]."

**Pre-wire ruthlessly.** Never let bad news surface in a main meeting for the first time. Your sponsor should have heard it in a one-on-one. Her boss should have heard it from her. By the time it hits the room, it's confirmed, accepted, and the conversation is about what to do.

**Give them language to take it up the chain.** They need to tell their boss. Help them: "When you present this, leading with the fact that we caught it early gives you credibility. And I'd emphasize that the option to course-correct is still open—here's what that looks like." Executives in difficult conversations are grateful for air cover.

**Separate the diagnosis from the blame.** "The strategy isn't working because the market shifted faster than we anticipated" is different from "You missed market signals." Be honest about what happened without making it personal.

## 5. The Room That's Going Sideways

Energy is off. Two senior people are debating something tangential and getting heated. Someone's checking their phone. The sponsor looks frustrated. You can feel it.

**Read early, intervene early.** By the time tension is obvious, you've missed the moment. Watch for the micro-signals: posture shifts, the tone of "clarifying" questions, eyes moving away from the slides.

**Pause before you're forced to.** "I'm sensing we need to zoom out here and make sure we're aligned on the fundamental assumption. Should we pause on the presentation for a minute?" This isn't weakness. It's reading the room and respecting it.

**Convert to conversation when the energy dies.** A one-way presentation into a room that's checked out is wasting everyone's time. Shift: "This matters because of X. What am I missing about your reality here?" Suddenly people re-engage because you're asking, not telling.

**If two people are debating:** Don't referee it. Acknowledge it: "You two clearly see this differently. Let me ask: what's the core disagreement?" Often it's not about your analysis at all; it's about a prior belief. Once you see the actual disagreement, your data might resolve it, or you might realize this specific question is outside your scope.

**Know when to cut.** If the room is fractured and the sponsor isn't repairing it, you're not going to save it by pushing harder. "I think we've got a lot to unpack here. Let me give you a chance to talk through some of this, and we can reconvene when we're clearer on priorities." You're not failing; you're being adult about it.

## 6. Managing Your Own Composure

You're running on adrenaline, the room is hostile, and you can feel your voice rising.

**Slow down deliberately.** Not noticeably. But take your presentations at 60% of your natural speed when you're nervous. You'll sound measured instead of rushed. You'll also think clearer.

**Use the pause.** When someone asks something difficult, don't answer for 2-3 seconds. Blink. Think. Respond. This feels eternal to you; to the room, you're obviously thinking, not stalling.

**Don't fill silence.** The urge to elaborate after you answer is almost irresistible. Resist it. Answer the question. Stop. Let them ask the next one.

**Recover from a fumble immediately.** You mispeak, stammer, or get a number wrong mid-sentence. Don't pretend it didn't happen. "Sorry, let me be clearer. What I meant to say is..." and then say it. You look human, not dishonest.

**Ground yourself physically.** Feet flat. Hands visible or at rest. Don't rock, sway, or gesture frantically. Stability on the outside projects confidence on the inside.

**The 3-second rule:** When nervous, you rush. You interrupt your own breathing. Consciously breathe. In for 3 counts, out for 4. This isn't meditation; it's physiology. It genuinely works.

**Remember: they can't see your heart rate.** You feel panicked. You sound fine. You look fine. The internal experience and external appearance are not the same. Assume they're seeing the external version.

## 7. Saying "I Don't Know" Well

This is where junior consultants lose credibility and experienced ones build it.

**"I don't know" alone is weak.** It sounds like you're unprepared.

**"I don't have that data in front of me" is stronger.** It implies you could have it; you're just being honest about this moment.

**"I don't have that data in front of me, but here's my instinct and I'll confirm by EOD" is powerful.** You've admitted the gap, given them something useful now, and committed to precision later.

**Example:** Client asks about attrition in a specific region. You don't have it memorized. "I don't have the regional breakdown right here, but I know our overall attrition is X%. Given what we've learned about the regional differences, I'd estimate that region is running 2-3 points higher. Let me pull the exact number and confirm by end of business." You've given them useful information, shown you understand the business, and made a credible commitment.

**What you never do:** Guess. Hand-wave. Pretend to remember. These destroy credibility permanently.

**The difference between confidence and brittleness:** A confident consultant admits what they don't know. A brittle one pretends to know everything and sounds defensive when questioned. Be the former.

---

## Final Note

Every experienced consultant has been in rooms where things went sideways. It's not about being perfect; it's about staying clear, humble, and honest under pressure. The best clients remember not that you were always right, but that you thought carefully, owned your mistakes, and didn't crumble when things got hard.

That's what they actually hire you for.
