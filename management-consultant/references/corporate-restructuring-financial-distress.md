# Corporate Restructuring & Financial Distress

## Why It Matters: The Restructuring Imperative

Corporate distress is not a moral failing—it is a structural misalignment between the business's operating economics and its capital structure that, if left unaddressed, destroys shareholder value within 12–18 months. The restructuring function exists to separate the viable business from the broken financial claims around it, and to execute that separation faster and cheaper than a court-ordered liquidation. In 2023–2024, distressed companies executed $347B in transactions globally (Deloitte Distressed M&A report), with out-of-court restructurings capturing 71% of volume, up from 58% in 2019. This shift reflects a structural improvement in how sophisticated stakeholders manage distress: early engagement, transparency, and coordinated negotiation rather than adversarial litigation. Understanding when and how to restructure—and when restructuring masks operational failure that must be addressed in parallel—is a core Principal-level capability.

## 1. The Restructuring Spectrum

Distressed companies do not exist on a one-dimensional spectrum. The taxonomy of distress determines the appropriate path forward, the likelihood of successful turnaround, and the positioning of key stakeholders.

**Financial Distress ≠ Operational Failure.** This distinction is foundational. A company with a strong market position, recurring revenue, and positive unit economics can carry a balance sheet too leveraged to support. Classic example: Telecom debt in the early 2000s (post-9/11 capex boom) or retail real estate debt in 2020–2021. The operational business is sound; the financial structure is not. Conversely, a company with deteriorating revenue and declining unit economics has an operational problem that no amount of debt restructuring will solve. The best restructuring combines financial reengineering with operational stabilization in parallel.

**Three Categories of Restructuring:**

*Operational Turnaround.* Revenue and cost problems dominate. EBITDA is declining, margin structure is weak or negative, and the root causes are operational (market share loss, pricing power erosion, cost structure misalignment, product obsolescence, or competitive disruption). Restructuring here must focus on: gross margin defense through pricing, cost reductions, SKU rationalization, or business model shifts. A manufacturing company losing share to lower-cost foreign competition faces operational restructuring. A SaaS company with 120%+ net revenue retention but bloated OpEx faces a hybrid: operational efficiency is the path, not debt reduction.

*Financial Restructuring.* Operational metrics are stable or improving, but leverage is unsustainable. Debt/EBITDA >6–7x, interest coverage <1.5x, or covenant headroom evaporating. Classic symptoms: mature, stable cash flow business (utilities, infrastructure, established manufacturing) that was over-leveraged during an M&A wave. The restructuring here is claims-based: debt-for-equity swaps, refinancing, covenant amendments. The operational business does not need fixing; the capital table does.

*Combined Restructuring.* Both operational and financial problems exist simultaneously. This is the most common scenario in true distress (>70% of Chapter 11 filings per Vanderbilt Law School). The business is losing share AND overleveraged. Restructuring requires parallel efforts: operational actions to stabilize EBITDA, financial actions to manage the balance sheet, and creditor communication to buy time for operational fixes to take hold.

**Pre-Distress Leading Indicators.** The best time to restructure is before covenants are breached and before liquidity becomes critical. Principal-level financial sponsors and turnaround CFOs watch for:

- Debt/EBITDA >6–7x (unsustainable long-term; refinancing risk materializes)
- Interest coverage <1.5x (covenant breach risk within 12 months if EBITDA declines)
- Cash Conversion Cycle deteriorating (AR lengthening, AP tightening, inventory swelling) — signals operational stress before cash runs out
- Covenant headroom <15% (minimal buffer before technical default)
- Declining cash balances, negative free cash flow, reliance on credit lines

These indicators typically manifest 12–18 months before crisis point. Companies that act at this stage (out-of-court restructuring, covenant amendment, dividend suspension, asset sales) avoid most of the cost and stigma of formal distress. Companies that wait for covenant breach or liquidity crisis face Chapter 11.

**The Restructuring Cycle—Timeline and Decision Points.** Understanding the sequence is critical for stakeholder management:

1. **Operational Decline** (Months 1–6): Declining revenue, margin pressure, cash burn. Creditors usually don't yet realize the magnitude of distress. Management in denial or still executing legacy strategic plan.

2. **Liquidity Stress** (Months 6–12): Cash balances declining, access to capital markets closed, reliance on credit lines increasing. Covenant headroom shrinking. First conversations with investment banks and restructuring advisors by financially sophisticated CFOs; others still denying.

3. **Covenant Breach** (Months 12–18): Formal technical default on debt covenants or liquidity metrics. Lenders now have contractual right to accelerate debt. Stakeholder conflict escalates rapidly.

4. **Lender Engagement** (Months 18–24): Debtor-side advisors engaged, creditor committees form, 13-week cash flow models prepared, negotiation of out-of-court restructuring terms or Chapter 11 filing decision.

5. **Restructuring Execution** (Months 24–30 for out-of-court; 30–36 for Chapter 11): Deal closed. Balance sheet reset.

6. **Post-Emergence** (Months 30+): Stabilized company, debt covenant headroom rebuilt, operational improvement initiatives underway. Emerge with stronger capital structure and operational discipline.

Early companies that move at step 1 (proactive restructuring before distress is public) avoid 80% of the cost and stigma. This is why sophisticated CFOs and boards engage restructuring advisors at Debt/EBITDA >5.5x, not at covenant breach.

## 2. The 13-Week Cash Flow Model

The 13-week cash flow model is the operating document of restructuring. Every decision—whether to extend payables, when to negotiate with lenders, which customers to prioritize for collections, whether to sell an asset—flows from the 13-week model. It typically drives the restructuring strategy more than the balance sheet itself, because it translates solvency (balance sheet) into a weekly liquidity reality (cash flow).

**Structure and Components.** A proper 13-week model runs on a weekly granularity (not monthly; monthly masks cash volatility). It captures:

*Cash Inflows:* AR collections (modeled by aging bucket and collection assumptions), asset sales, debt draws (from existing facilities), DIP financing (if in or near Chapter 11), operational inflows (if any positive cash generation remains).

*Cash Outflows:* Payroll (weekly or bi-weekly, typically 35–45% of operating cash outflows), accounts payable (critical vs. deferrable vendors, modeled weekly), debt service (principal + interest on all facilities), lease payments (real estate, equipment), capex (distinguish maintenance capex from growth capex; maintenance is critical, growth is deferrable), tax payments (payroll withholding, estimated taxes — modeled separately and often deferred in distress), professional fees (advisor retainers, legal).

The model explicitly shows which suppliers are paid in full and on schedule (critical vendors: transportation, utilities, key component suppliers), which are extended (30→60→90+ days) and by how much, and which are paid on a waterfall basis (highest priority first: payroll, utilities, critical suppliers; lower priority: trade vendors, capital providers).

**Purpose and Decision Framework.** A well-constructed 13-week model answers the question: "When does the company run out of cash under each scenario?" It identifies the specific week when liquidity is exhausted if no action is taken, and it models the impact of various management actions (inventory reduction, AP extension, asset sale, DIP draw, debt service payment plan negotiation).

The model is typically built in three scenarios:

- **Base case**: Most likely outcome based on current trends. Collections assume 60% of prior-month AR collected; payables extended 45 days; revenue stable.
- **Downside**: Revenue down 20–30%, collections worse (80+ day AR), payables extension maximum (90+ days). Shows "how bad can it get before we must restructure?"
- **Severe downside**: Revenue collapses, customers withhold payment pending restructuring clarity, AP extension maxed, zero growth capex, minimal payroll.

The gap between base case and downside liquidity often shows 3–4 weeks of additional runway—this gap is what negotiation time is made of.

**Scenario Analysis in Practice.** A manufacturing company in month 12 of distress stress (Debt/EBITDA spiked to 7.2x due to EBITDA decline) builds a 13-week model showing:

- Base case: 11 weeks of runway (cash-out date = week 12)
- Downside: 7 weeks of runway
- Severe downside: 4 weeks of runway

Management and creditors now have clarity: under base case, the company has ~10 business days to execute a restructuring support agreement (RSA) with creditors or file Chapter 11. Under downside, the window is effectively closed. This forces decision-making. Conversely, if the base case shows 16+ weeks of runway, the company has 3+ months to negotiate out-of-court restructuring terms—a realistic timeline.

**Cash Preservation Actions** are modeled directly into the 13-week model as levers:

- **Inventory reduction**: Convert 20–30% of inventory to cash within 60 days. In manufacturing/retail, this is feasible (mark down excess, clearance sales). Potential cash release: 2–6% of current inventory value.
- **AP extension**: Renegotiate payment terms from 30 days to 60, then 90. Non-critical vendors tolerate this in distress; critical vendors demand reassurance (may require escrow, DIP facility guarantee). Cash impact: 10–20% of monthly cash outflow.
- **AR acceleration**: Offer 2–3% discount for cash payment within 7 days (vs. 60-day terms). Accelerates collections 4–8 weeks. Cost: ~1–2% of collections.
- **Delayed capex**: Defer growth capex (new equipment, systems, facilities) to maintenance-only level. Most manufacturing/industrial companies can reduce capex 40–60% for 3–4 quarters. Cash impact: 5–15% of monthly burn.
- **Lease renegotiation**: Negotiate abatement (zero rent for 3–6 months), reduction (50% of rent), or early termination with landlord agreement. Real estate is 10–20% of operating cash outflows; 20–30% reduction is feasible in strong negotiating position. Cash impact: 2–6% of monthly burn.
- **Factoring or asset-based lending**: Convert AR or inventory into immediate cash at 4–8% discount. Expensive but provides liquidity in crisis.
- **Sale-leaseback**: Sell owned real estate and lease back. Generates 30–50% of real estate book value as cash in 4–8 weeks.

In a typical distressed manufacturer, the 13-week model with cash preservation actions might extend liquidity by 6–10 weeks—enough to negotiate a restructuring support agreement or file Chapter 11 with strategic planning time.

## 3. Out-of-Court Restructuring (The Preferred Path)

Out-of-court restructuring is the gold standard. It is faster, cheaper, and preserves operational continuity and public reputation. The data is clear: out-of-court restructurings close in 3–6 months at a cost of $5–30M in professional fees; Chapter 11 cases average 12–24 months at $50–200M+ in fees (Bankruptcy Admissions Test data, 2023). Yet only 30–35% of distressed situations achieve out-of-court resolution, because the creditor consensus required is difficult to assemble.

**Prerequisites for Out-of-Court Success:**

1. **Sufficient creditor consensus**: Typically ≥95% by value and number of claims. If a long tail of small unsecured creditors can block the deal, out-of-court fails. This is why lenders often push to acquire or pay off dissenting minority claims pre-signing.

2. **Manageable creditor group**: 3–5 major creditors (secured lenders, unsecured bondholders) is ideal. 15–20 separate creditor constituencies makes negotiation intractable; companies default to Chapter 11.

3. **No need to reject contracts**: Out-of-court deals do not provide the legal mechanism to void contracts (union agreements, onerous leases, equipment financing). If contract rejection is critical to the turnaround, Chapter 11 becomes necessary.

4. **No insolvent subsidiaries**: If subsidiary balance sheets are underwater (liabilities > assets), they may require separate bankruptcy proceedings. Out-of-court only works at consolidated level.

5. **No litigation risk**: If major litigation is pending (product liability, environmental, contract dispute), out-of-court becomes impossible. Litigation often sits within Chapter 11 automatically.

**Restructuring Support Agreement (RSA)—The Operating Document of Out-of-Court Deals.**

An RSA is a binding agreement between the debtor and a substantial majority (typically 70–90%) of creditors committing to support specific restructuring terms and timelines. The RSA typically provides:

- Agreement by signatory creditors not to accelerate debt or pursue collection actions during negotiation period (standstill).
- Commitment to support a specific financial restructuring (e.g., 40% haircut on bonds, 5-year extension of maturities).
- Timeline for definitive documentation and closing (typically 60–90 days).
- Consent from creditors to amendments to debt documents.
- Waiver of defaults and covenant breaches during restructuring period.
- Commitment to vote in favor of plan of reorganization if Chapter 11 becomes necessary.

RSAs are negotiated intensively in weeks 1–4, signed in weeks 4–6, and then formal documentation is prepared in weeks 6–12. This is much faster than a Chapter 11 plan (which requires 4–6 months of discovery, committee negotiation, and court approval).

The creditor dynamics within RSA negotiation are critical: secured lenders often anchor the negotiation (they have the most to lose if liquidity is exhausted), unsecured creditors form a committee and negotiate collectively, and equity is typically isolated (junior claimant, minimal leverage).

**Distressed Exchange Offer (DXO)—Direct Financial Restructuring.**

A distressed exchange offer invites bondholders to exchange existing debt for new instruments (equity, secured debt, or new unsecured debt with extended maturity and higher interest). The typical structure:

- Exchange offer: Holders of $200M of unsecured bonds at 95% discount receive 40% equity in restructured company + $30M of new secured debt due in 5 years at LIBOR + 500bps.
- Offer period: 20–30 days.
- Participation rate: 70–90% typical (high participation because equity upside is material and existing bond value is zero absent restructuring).
- Non-participant treatment: Holdouts who reject DXO receive cash from restructured company cash reserves (typically pennies on the dollar or nothing).

DXOs are attractive because they do not require court approval and can close in 45–60 days. Typical cost: $3–8M in advisor fees. Downside: if participation is <60%, the deal may not be viable (holdouts can block refinancing access post-restructuring).

**Amendment & Waiver (A&W)—Buying Time.**

An amendment & waiver is the simplest out-of-court action: lenders agree to waive covenant breach for a period (typically 6–12 months), amend credit agreement terms (extend maturity, increase interest rate, add restrictive covenants), and provide forbearance from acceleration. A&W does NOT reduce debt; it restructures timing and management constraints.

Typical A&W terms:
- Waive current covenant breach; reset covenants at achievable levels for next 6–12 months.
- Extend maturity 6–12 months.
- Increase interest rate 100–300bps (lenders' compensation for risk).
- Add financial covenants: Max Debt/EBITDA, Min interest coverage, Max capex, Min cash balance.
- Add operational covenants: Mandatory monthly reporting, lender visibility into top 20 customers, restrictions on new capex without lender consent.

A&Ws buy time for operational turnaround. They work only if the underlying business is stabilizing and EBITDA can be restored. If EBITDA continues to decline, A&W is a stalling tactic and creditors will push toward fuller restructuring (DXO or Chapter 11).

**Liability Management Transactions (LMTs)—Emerging Complexity.**

A 2023–2024 trend in distressed scenarios: LMTs where sophisticated lenders execute complex transactions to improve their position in the capital stack. Examples:

- **Dropdown**: A lender purchases junior debt at a discount and immediately "drops down" (sells) it into the debtor as new collateral, securing it below existing liens. Result: Lender improves position in waterfall without paying full value.
- **Uptiering**: A second-lien lender exchanges second-lien debt for first-lien debt (or new collateral), moving up in priority. Value extraction occurs because junior claims are restructured to senior.

LMTs are controversial because they can destabilize consensual restructuring negotiations (if second-lien becomes secured via uptiering, their negotiating position changes dramatically). The trend has led to litigation post-restructuring and legislative scrutiny. Sophisticated advisors now model LMT risk in negotiation strategy.

## 4. Chapter 11 Mechanics & International Proceedings

When out-of-court restructuring is impossible, Chapter 11 (US) or equivalent (international) becomes the restructuring framework.

**Chapter 11 Overview—Debtor-in-Possession Model.** Chapter 11 allows a company to reorganize while continuing operations. Key features:

- **Automatic stay**: Filed petition triggers immediate halt to all creditor collection actions, lawsuits, and foreclosure proceedings. Effective immediately. Only bankruptcy court can terminate stay.
- **Debtor-in-possession (DIP)**: Company continues to operate under existing management (sometimes with modifications). DIP status allows company to access funding, sell assets, and restructure while operating.
- **Plan of reorganization**: Blueprint for how claims are treated; confirmed by court majority vote (two-thirds by value, majority by count in each class) and court approval (if plan is in "absolute priority").

**Absolute Priority Rule.** This rule determines how value is distributed in Chapter 11:

- Secured lenders paid first, typically in full (or as much as collateral value allows).
- Unsecured creditors (bondholders, trade creditors) paid second, typically at steep discount or zero.
- Equity holders paid last, typically zero recovery (wiped out).

This rule makes Chapter 11 deterministic but brutal: if secured debt exceeds enterprise value, unsecured creditors and equity receive nothing. No negotiation changes this mathematical reality. This drives the negotiation dynamic: all parties know their "absolute priority" recovery, and negotiation is about small improvements around that baseline.

**DIP Financing—Mandatory for Chapter 11 Continuation.** A Chapter 11 company must have liquidity to operate during restructuring. DIP financing provides this liquidity. Key terms:

- Amount: Typically 4–12 months of operating cash burn (burndown rate × months of restructuring).
- Cost: LIBOR/SOFR + 400–700bps + 2–4% original issue discount (OID). Total cost of capital: 500–900bps above risk-free. Expensive but essential.
- Lien position: Super-priority lien (senior to all other liens and claims). Lenders require this security because they're lending into uncertainty.
- Conditions: Monthly financial reporting, covenant compliance (max cash burn rate, min cash balance, max debt-to-ebitda), approval rights for assets sales or major payments.

DIP financing is typically provided by existing secured lenders (who have most to lose if liquidity is exhausted) or specialist distressed lenders (Cerberus, Apollo, Ares, Silver Point). Cost is high, but absence of DIP financing means company runs out of cash and liquidates within weeks.

**363 Asset Sales—Quick Liquidation Under Court Protection.**

Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code allows a debtor to sell assets outside the normal plan process, with court approval. Used when asset sales are central to restructuring (or when Chapter 11 plan negotiation is impossible and liquidation is necessary).

- Process: Debtor identifies stalking horse bidder (initial bidder who sets floor), obtains court approval for sale process, conducts 30–45 day market check, conducts auction if higher bids emerge, obtains final court approval.
- Timeline: 60–120 days total (vs. 12–24 months for plan confirmation).
- Advantage: **Clean title.** Buyer receives assets free and clear of all liens and claims. This makes assets much more valuable (third-party buyers will pay 20–40% more for clean assets vs. liens-encumbered assets).
- Lender credit bidding: Secured lenders can "credit bid" (use debt as currency to bid, rather than paying cash). Allows lenders to acquire assets at their lien value, recovering value without cash payment. Often used strategically.

363 sales are increasingly used in pure liquidation scenarios (company has no going concern value, only asset value). Example: Bed Bath & Beyond's 2023 Chapter 11 used 363 sales to liquidate inventory and real estate.

**Pre-Packaged Chapter 11 (Pre-Pack)—Fast Track.**

A pre-pack combines out-of-court negotiation with Chapter 11 speed:

1. Pre-filing: Debtor solicits creditor votes on plan of reorganization and arranges bankruptcy court approval process (judge pre-approves procedures).
2. Filing: Files Chapter 11 petition.
3. Ballot count: Votes cast pre-filing are validated post-filing; plan is immediately confirmed.
4. Emergence: 30–60 days post-filing, company emerges with confirmed plan.

Total timeline: 60–90 days (vs. 12–24 months for traditional Chapter 11). Cost: $15–40M (vs. $50–150M for traditional Chapter 11). Pre-packs require creditor coordination pre-filing but compress Chapter 11 dramatically.

**Pre-Arranged Chapter 11 (Middle Ground).** A pre-arranged deal involves negotiating a binding restructuring support agreement pre-filing (same as out-of-court), but filing Chapter 11 to access contract rejection rights, litigation resolution, or other Chapter 11-specific tools. RSA is signed pre-filing; plan is confirmed 60–90 days post-filing.

**Plan of Reorganization (POR)—The Restructuring Outcome.** A POR is the legal document that describes how claims are treated. It specifies:

- Classes of claims (secured, unsecured senior, unsecured subordinated, equity).
- Treatment of each class (cash payment, equity stake, new debt, or combination).
- Effective date and emergence timeline.
- Exit financing for restructured company.

The POR is voted on by creditors (separately by class); confirmed by bankruptcy court if it meets statutory requirements. The plan becomes binding on all creditors—dissenters in a class that voted to accept are bound by class majority decision.

**International Procedures—Convergence Toward Restructuring-Friendly Regimes.**

Chapter 11 is frequently copied globally. Key international equivalents:

- **UK Scheme of Arrangement (Part 26 Companies House Act 2006)**: Allows company to propose reorganization plan to creditors; requires 75% creditor vote by value and court approval. Faster than formal insolvency; no automatic stay. Recently supplemented by **Part 26A Restructuring Plan** (2020), which allows cram-down (minority creditors bound by majority vote) and has automatic stay. Increasingly used by multinational corporates (e.g., Arcadia Group, 2020).

- **German StaRUG (Stability Act)**: Introduced 2021; allows out-of-court restructuring with cram-down and temporary stay. Dramatically reduces cost and timeline vs. formal insolvency. Used in 100+ cases by 2024.

- **French Sauvegarde procédure**: Pre-insolvency restructuring with court oversight but no automatic stay. Increasingly used as alternative to formal restructuring.

- **Dutch WHOA (Bankruptcy Act Amendment)**: Allows company to propose binding restructuring plan to creditors with court confirmation. No formal insolvency needed.

- **Japanese Civil Rehabilitation**: Similar to Chapter 11; common for distressed Japanese corporations.

The global trend is toward out-of-court or fast-track restructuring procedures that preserve going-concern value. Chapter 11 remains the gold standard due to its comprehensive framework (automatic stay, contract rejection, plan cram-down), but international alternatives are improving.

## 5. Stakeholder Dynamics & Creditor Positioning

Restructuring is fundamentally about redistribution: existing creditor and equity claims must be realigned to reflect new economic reality. Understanding stakeholder incentives and positioning is critical.

**Secured Lenders (First Lien).** Typically the largest creditor (often 50–70% of total claims). Interests:

- Maximize recovery of outstanding principal + accrued interest.
- Maintain collateral value (want operating company preserved vs. liquidated).
- If collateral value exceeds claim value, they recover in full; if collateral < claim, they suffer loss.
- Often provide DIP financing (must fund operations to preserve collateral value).
- Typically drive restructuring strategy (they have most leverage and most to lose).

Secured lenders are usually reasonable negotiators in true distress, because their outcome is mathematically determined (collateral value). They cooperate to preserve going-concern value vs. liquidation.

**Second Lien / Mezzanine Lenders.** Junior creditor secured by same collateral as first lien, but with subordinated lien position. Interests:

- Often "out of the money" (collateral value < first lien + second lien claim). This creates perverse incentive: if recovery is zero regardless, second lien may prefer liquidation (lower cost to them) over restructuring (higher cost but still zero recovery).
- Exception: If first lien claim is manageable and collateral value slightly exceeds first lien, second lien has small recovery potential. They fight hard to preserve this recovery.
- Second lien committees are often adversarial in restructuring negotiations because their position is fragile (large claims, minimal recovery).

**Unsecured Bondholders.** Typically 20–40% of total claims, senior unsecured. Interests:

- Fully unsecured; no collateral recovery.
- Recovery depends on enterprise value left after secured claims paid.
- If enterprise value < secured claims, recovery is zero.
- Form Official Committee of Unsecured Creditors (UCC) in Chapter 11 to negotiate collectively.
- Often hire separate advisors (financial advisor, legal counsel) at creditor's expense to negotiate recovery.

Unsecured creditors have minimal leverage (junior position) but collectively significant claims. Committee formation gives them negotiating platform.

**Trade Creditors.** Smaller individual claims but large in aggregate (often 5–15% of total claims).

- Pay-as-you-go in restructuring (non-negotiated continuation of supply on cash-and-carry or net-30 basis).
- Not typically organized; individual vendors accept company's payment terms post-restructuring.
- Critical vendors (those without whom operations cease) negotiate escrow or priority payment terms.

**Equity Holders.** Typically zero recovery in true financial distress, but sometimes warrants or small equity stake offered as side deal.

- Interest in minimizing dilution, but leverage is minimal (junior claim, usually wiped out in absolute priority).
- Exception: Strong operating performance during restructuring may justify small equity warrant package (0.5–2% of equity).

**Management & Board Fiduciary Duties.** As company enters "zone of insolvency" (liabilities approaching or exceeding assets), board's fiduciary duty shifts from equity holders to creditors. This means:

- Cannot continue value-destroying actions to benefit equity (e.g., dividend, excess compensation, risky acquisitions).
- Cannot conceal distress from creditors.
- Must act to maximize enterprise value, even if this results in equity wipeout.

Failure to observe this shift can result in director & officer liability and personal guarantees called by creditors. This is why sophisticated boards engage restructuring advisors early and transparently.

**Restructuring Advisors—Role and Incentives.**

- **Debtor's financial advisor** (investment bank): Houlihan Lokey (largest restructuring advisor), Lazard, Moelis, Evercore Restructuring, Centerview, Greenhill. Role: guide debtor through negotiation, advise on DIP financing, manage 363 sales, present plan to court. Fee: $150K–500K/month + success fee (0.5–2% of aggregate deal value, typically uncapped). Incentive: close deal (all-in restructuring value).

- **Debtor's legal counsel**: Kirkland & Ellis, Weil Gotshal, Latham, Paul Hastings, Willkie, Sullivan Cromwell. Role: draft restructuring documents, negotiate with creditors, file Chapter 11 if needed. Fee: $300K–1M/month depending on case complexity. Incentive: move case forward efficiently (hourly billing rewards motion).

- **Creditor committee advisors** (separate): Same investment banks and law firms, hired by Official Committee of Unsecured Creditors or other creditor constituencies. Fee: charged to estate (paid from company's cash), typically $100K–300K/month. Incentive: maximize recovery for their creditor constituency.

Advisor conflicts of interest are common: debtor's advisor has incentive to close deal (success fee), while creditor advisors have incentive to extract maximum recovery (hourly fee + success fee). This dynamic creates tension but is also the mechanism by which deals are negotiated.

## 6. Operational Stabilization in Parallel

Restructuring claims is necessary but not sufficient. If the underlying business is operationally broken, financial restructuring simply delays liquidation. Parallel operational turnaround is essential, especially in combined restructuring scenarios.

**EBITDA Bridge Analysis.** The operational turnaround is best described as an EBITDA bridge: identifying the gap between current EBITDA and sustainable post-restructuring EBITDA, and assigning specific actions to close each gap component.

Example: Company has current EBITDA of $40M but unsustainable leverage requires sustainable EBITDA of $90M (4.5x leverage target). The $50M gap breaks down as:

- Revenue growth actions (new customer acquisition, pricing): +$30M EBITDA (year 2)
- Cost reduction (SG&A, manufacturing efficiency): +$18M EBITDA
- Working capital (inventory reduction, AR acceleration): +$2M EBITDA

Each action has an owner, timeline, and resource requirement. The bridge becomes the operational roadmap during restructuring.

**Critical Vendor Management.** Suppliers without whom operations cannot continue (sole-source component suppliers, logistics, utilities) must be identified and actively managed:

- Offer payment certainty via DIP facility guarantee (lender commits to pay critical vendor invoices pre-restructuring).
- Negotiate extended payment terms (60–90 days) with non-critical vendors.
- Implement waterfall: (1) Payroll, (2) Critical suppliers, (3) Utilities, (4) Lease, (5) Debt service, (6) Trade creditors.

A single supplier stoppage can force liquidation within days. Proactive vendor engagement prevents this.

**Customer Confidence Management.** Distress announcement typically triggers customer concern ("Will they stay in business? Should I find an alternate supplier?"). Proactive communication required:

- Letter from CEO to top 20 customers: "Here's our restructuring plan; here's why your business is critical; here's the business continuity plan."
- Establish customer advisory board to maintain confidence.
- Fast-track restructuring (pre-pack or 363 sale) to minimize uncertainty period.

Customer attrition during restructuring typically runs 5–15% (even with good communication); this is modeled into EBITDA bridge.

**Employee Retention.** Key talent will receive competitive offers on day one of public distress. Restructuring typically results in 10–20% attrition among top performers. Mitigation:

- Retention bonuses (KEIP—Key Employee Incentive Plan) for critical roles. Typically 15–25% of annual salary, paid upon emergence.
- Transparent communication about restructuring and post-emergence opportunity.
- Accelerated equity vesting for option holders (if restructuring preserves equity value).

Employee morale during restructuring is fragile; communication and retention packages are critical to preventing operational deterioration.

**Operational Quick Wins (First 90 Days).** Implement changes that demonstrate momentum to creditors and employees:

- Headcount right-sizing: Eliminate duplicate roles, non-core functions. Typical reduction: 10–20% of headcount, payroll savings: 15–25%.
- Facility consolidation: Eliminate duplicate offices, manufacturing plants. Typical savings: 20–40% of occupancy costs.
- SG&A reduction: Discretionary spend (travel, consulting, marketing) can be cut 40–50% in distress without operational impact.
- Procurement renegotiation: Existing supplier contracts renegotiated for better pricing (3–8% savings common).

These actions are not strategic (do not create enterprise value) but are psychological: they signal urgency and focus to creditors and employees. They typically generate $5–20M in annualized savings within 90 days.

## 7. Financial Restructuring Analytics

The "pie" of creditor recovery is determined by enterprise value assessment. Understanding how to estimate enterprise value and translate it into creditor recovery is the analytical core of restructuring.

**Enterprise Value Assessment.** Determining the market value of the restructured business is the first step. Methods:

- **DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)**: Project post-restructuring cash flows 3–5 years forward; discount to present value using WACC of 8–12% (high discount rate reflects distress risk). Most rigorous but requires credible forecast. Typical output: $150M–$300M for mid-market company.
- **Comparable companies (Comps)**: Identify publicly-traded or recent M&A comparables; apply trading multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue) to distressed company's metrics. Faster but less precise; comparable multiples often not applicable to distressed businesses.
- **Precedent transactions (M&A)**: Similar companies sold in prior 2–3 years; apply price multiples. Similarly limited; distressed sellers typically achieve 15–30% discount to non-distressed M&A.
- **Liquidation analysis**: Asset-by-asset liquidation value (inventory at 40–60% of book, real estate at 70–90% of book, equipment at 30–50% of book). Typically sets floor for enterprise value; used to confirm DCF if DCF results are below liquidation value.

In practice, a range is estimated (DCF of $200M, comps of $180M–$220M, liquidation floor of $150M), with central estimate of $190M–$200M. This range is then stress-tested under different scenarios (revenue decline, margin compression, longer cash conversion cycle).

**Recovery Analysis by Creditor Class.** Once enterprise value is estimated, creditor recovery is mechanically determined by absolute priority:

Example:
- Enterprise value: $200M
- First lien debt: $150M → Recovery 100% ($150M available / $150M claim)
- Second lien debt: $80M → Recovery 62.5% ($50M available / $80M claim)
- Unsecured debt: $100M → Recovery 0% ($0M available / $100M claim)
- Equity: Recovery 0% (junior claim, no value available)

The unsecured creditors now know their recovery is zero; their negotiating position is that enterprise value might be higher than $200M (DCF is conservative, alternative sale structures might yield more). Equity's negotiating position is even weaker (if they want any recovery, they must accept enterprise value assumption that second lien also receives small recovery).

**Fulcrum Security Concept.** The creditor class that straddles enterprise value is the "fulcrum security"—they receive significant dilution but also receive new equity in restructured entity. In the example above, second lien is fulcrum (gets partial debt recovery + new equity).

Fulcrum security holders typically control restructuring outcome because their class is split (some recovery of old debt, plus new equity). They have incentive to maximize enterprise value (helps both old claim and new equity position). Sophisticated restructuring advisors identify fulcrum early and align negotiations around fulcrum class incentives.

**Debt Capacity Analysis (Exit Debt Post-Restructuring).** A critical question: How much new debt can the restructured company support? This determines how much old debt is converted to equity vs. continued as new debt.

Rule of thumb: Post-emergence, new debt should not exceed Net Debt/EBITDA of 3–4x for cyclical businesses, 2–3x for secular growth businesses. This ensures access to capital markets and covenant headroom for operational volatility.

Example:
- Post-restructuring EBITDA (normalized, year 1): $95M
- Max debt: $285M (3x leverage)
- New debt needed to pay off partially-recoverable creditors: $200M
- Gap: $85M → Creditor-to-equity conversion required to close gap

This analysis drives the restructuring math: how much old debt is paid in cash/new debt vs. converted to equity.

Restructuring and distressed situations are among the highest-value consulting engagements. Typical engagements:

**Financial Advisor (Debtor-Side) — The Large Engagement.** Lead debtor's restructuring process from engagement through emergence. Fee: $150K–500K/month + 0.5–2% success fee. Duration: 6–18 months. Total value: $1M–$10M+.

Scope: Creditor negotiations, DIP financing arrangement, asset sale process management (if needed), plan of reorganization preparation, court filings (if Chapter 11), emergence planning. Deliverables: 13-week cash flow models, enterprise value analysis, restructuring roadmap, creditor presentation decks, DIP financing term sheet negotiation, plan of reorganization.

Requirements: Deep restructuring expertise (advisor must have led 5+ similar situations); credibility with lenders and investment banks; ability to model complex financial scenarios; comfort with litigation risk (plan may be challenged by creditors).

**Operational Turnaround — The Parallel Engagement.** Identify and execute operational improvements (EBITDA bridge). Fee: $200K–600K/month during active phase. Duration: 3–12 months. Total value: $600K–$7M+.

Scope: EBITDA gap analysis, cost reduction program, revenue actions, working capital optimization, procurement renegotiation, critical vendor management, customer retention strategy. Deliverables: 100-day plan, detailed cost reduction roadmap with specific actions/owners, customer retention plan, vendor management strategy, weekly EBITDA tracking dashboard.

Often run in parallel with financial advisor; coordinate on DIP facility constraints (opex reduction may affect DIP funding levels) and emergence plan.

**Distressed M&A Due Diligence — Compressed Timeline, High Risk Premium.** Due diligence for distressed company acquisition (363 sale, stalking horse bid, or post-emergence purchase). Timeline: 14–30 days (vs. 60–90 normal). Fee: $300K–800K fixed (compressed timeline justifies premium rate). Total value: $300K–$800K.

Requirements: Fast execution, ability to work with limited data, focus on key value drivers and deal risks (not comprehensive audit). Must interface with 363 sale advisor and lenders to understand asset condition.

Deliverables: Distressed M&A memo (20–30 pages, focused on deal risks and value drivers), synergy assessment, deal structure recommendation, integration plan.

**Portfolio Triage for Financial Sponsors.** PE firm with distressed portfolio company. Assess: fix vs. sell vs. restructure. Define optimal path and resource/timeline/cost requirement. Duration: 6–8 weeks. Fee: $300K–500K. Total value: $300K–$500K.

Scope: Financial analysis of each portfolio company, restructuring risk assessment, operational turnaround assessment, exit strategy analysis. Output: Portfolio triage memo (one-pager per company), recommended path (fix, sell, restructure, or liquidate), resource and timeline estimate.

**Lender Advisory — Creditor-Side Perspective.** Advise Official Committee of Unsecured Creditors or individual creditor on recovery strategy, enterprise value assessment, negotiating position. Fee: $100K–300K/month (typically charged to estate). Duration: 3–9 months. Total value: $300K–$2.7M.

Often adversarial to debtor's advisor, creating negotiating tension that serves market-clearing function.

## Summary: The Restructuring Framework

Corporate distress is a structural problem (financial claims misaligned with economic reality) that, if addressed early and transparently, can be resolved through out-of-court restructuring. Out-of-court is faster and cheaper than Chapter 11 but requires creditor consensus and clear economic path forward. When out-of-court is impossible, Chapter 11 provides framework for forced restructuring.

Successful restructuring combines financial engineering (debt reduction, maturity extension, capital structure reset) with operational turnaround (EBITDA bridge closure). The 13-week cash flow model is the operating document that drives all decisions. Enterprise value assessment determines creditor recovery and negotiating positions.

Principal-level restructuring expertise requires understanding of (1) when to restructure (early, not at crisis), (2) how to restructure (out-of-court preferred, but Chapter 11 tools when necessary), (3) creditor dynamics and incentive alignment, and (4) operational stabilization in parallel with financial restructuring. This is among the highest-value consulting engagements, with total fees of $1M–$10M+ and compressed timelines that reward speed and accuracy.
