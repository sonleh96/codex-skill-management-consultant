## Corporate Strategy & Portfolio Design

### The Corporate Parent Value Question
The fundamental question of corporate strategy: Does the corporate parent add more value to the business units than an alternative owner would? If not, the conglomerate discount applies and shareholders are better off with breakup.

**Value-add tests:**
- Does the parent provide capital more cheaply than markets would?
- Does the parent provide capabilities/knowledge unavailable in the market?
- Does the parent create synergies between units that exceed the cost of complexity?
- Does the parent provide governance and talent superior to what stand-alone would attract?

### Portfolio Strategy Tools

**GE-McKinsey 9-Box**: Plot each business unit on Industry Attractiveness × Competitive Strength. Prescribes invest, hold, or divest.

**Growth-Profitability Map**: Plot each BU on Revenue Growth (x) × ROIC (y). Quadrants: Stars (high growth, high return → invest), Cash Engines (low growth, high return → harvest), Turnarounds (low growth, low return → fix or exit), Bets (high growth, low return → watch carefully).

**Synergy Map**: Identify and quantify synergies between business units:
- Revenue synergies: Cross-sell, shared customers, distribution
- Cost synergies: Shared procurement, shared services, scale
- Knowledge synergies: Technology, talent, best practice transfer
- Financial synergies: Tax efficiency, lower cost of capital

**Portfolio Simplification Logic**: Complexity costs are almost always underestimated. Each additional business unit adds management distraction, corporate overhead, and complexity. The case for simplification is usually stronger than it looks.

### Corporate Center Design
The corporate center should be as small as possible while still enabling:
1. Capital allocation
2. Talent management at senior levels
3. Risk management and compliance
4. Synergy capture between units
5. Strategic planning and M&A

Every corporate center function should answer: "Why can't the business unit do this itself, or buy it in the market?" If there's no good answer, it shouldn't exist at the center.

## Capital Allocation Framework

Capital allocation is the CEO's most important job. How a company deploys its cash determines long-term value creation more than almost any other decision.

### The Capital Allocation Hierarchy

```
Operating Cash Flow (after maintenance capex)
    ↓
First: Maintain competitive position (required reinvestment in core business)
    ↓
Second: Organic growth investments (R&D, capex, working capital for growth)
    ↓
Third: M&A (acquisitions that clear the hurdle rate)
    ↓
Fourth: Return capital to shareholders
    ├── Dividends (signals confidence in sustained earnings)
    └── Share buybacks (signals undervaluation; more flexible than dividends)
```

### ROIC vs. WACC: The Value Creation Test
- **ROIC > WACC**: Growth creates value — invest more
- **ROIC = WACC**: Growth is value-neutral
- **ROIC < WACC**: Growth destroys value — do not grow, return capital to shareholders

This is the single most important capital allocation insight. Companies that grow with ROIC < WACC are destroying shareholder value even as revenues rise.

### The Capital Allocation Scorecard
Assess management's capital allocation track record:
- Historical ROIC vs. WACC over 5-10 years
- M&A track record (did acquisitions create value?)
- R&D productivity (revenue per dollar of R&D)
- Capex intensity vs. peers (over- or under-investing?)
- Dividend/buyback timing (did they buy back at highs? Cut dividends at lows?)

### Portfolio Reshaping
Levers for reshaping the portfolio:
- **Divestitures**: Sell non-core, low-synergy, or capital-inefficient units. Typical value unlock: 10-30% premium to current carrying value.
- **Spin-offs**: Tax-efficient separation that unlocks "conglomerate discount" (typically 15-30% value creation)
- **Carve-outs**: IPO a minority stake while retaining control; brings market discipline and currency
- **Bolt-on acquisitions**: Buy small, synergistic companies in core markets
- **Transformational M&A**: Large deals that reshape the portfolio; higher risk, higher reward

## Activist Investor Defense

Activists take a significant stake (typically 5-15%) and push for value-creating changes. The best defense is a well-communicated, credible strategy that pre-empts activist arguments.

### The Activist Playbook (Know Their Moves)
Common activist demands:
1. **Break up the conglomerate**: Argue the sum of parts > whole
2. **Return capital**: Demand buybacks, special dividends, or reduced capex
3. **Replace management**: Argue underperformance is a leadership problem
4. **Cost cuts**: Identify excess SGA, redundant corporate costs
5. **Strategic sale**: Push for the company to sell itself
6. **Board seats**: Get representation to push agenda from inside

### Activist Vulnerability Assessment
Assess your client's vulnerability on these dimensions:
- **Valuation gap**: Is the stock trading below sum-of-parts? Below peers on EV/EBITDA?
- **Capital allocation**: Is ROIC < WACC? Is excess cash accumulating?
- **Portfolio complexity**: Is there a conglomerate discount?
- **Operational gaps**: Are margins below peers?
- **Corporate governance**: Is the board independent? Staggered? Pay-for-performance?
- **Shareholder base**: Are there large, patient institutional holders or short-term funds?

### Defense Strategy
1. **Offense is the best defense**: Articulate a credible value creation plan before the activist does
2. **Proactive shareholder engagement**: Know your top 20 shareholders' views; meet them regularly
3. **Rapid response**: Have a response ready before the activist goes public
4. **Engage, don't dismiss**: Activists are often right about the problem, just wrong about the solution
5. **Board preparation**: Brief the board on activist dynamics; ensure board is cohesive and aligned

## ESG & Sustainability Strategy

ESG has moved from reporting exercise to core strategic imperative. The best companies integrate ESG into strategy, not just compliance.

### The Business Case for ESG
- **Risk mitigation**: Physical climate risk, transition risk, social license to operate, regulatory risk
- **Value creation**: Cost reduction (energy efficiency), revenue growth (sustainable products), talent attraction
- **Cost of capital**: ESG ratings affect access to capital and borrowing costs (green bonds, ESG-linked loans)
- **Customer demand**: B2B customers increasingly require suppliers to meet ESG standards
- **Regulatory compliance**: EU taxonomy, CSRD, SEC climate disclosure rules — non-compliance has real costs

### ESG Strategy Framework

**Environmental:**
- Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions baseline and reduction targets
- Net zero pathway (what year? What abatement levers? What offsets?)
- Physical climate risk assessment (which assets are exposed?)
- Circular economy / waste reduction
- Water and biodiversity

**Social:**
- Labor practices and human rights (own operations + supply chain)
- Diversity, equity & inclusion (targets, metrics, accountability)
- Community impact
- Customer data privacy and security
- Product safety

**Governance:**
- Board composition, independence, diversity
- Executive compensation alignment to long-term value
- Anti-corruption and ethics
- Tax transparency
- Shareholder rights

### The Net Zero Pathway
For any large industrial client, the net zero question requires:
1. **Baseline emissions** by Scope (1/2/3) and source
2. **Abatement cost curve**: List all available levers, cost per tonne CO2, and potential volume
3. **Pathway options**: Different speeds / costs / approaches (e.g., electrify operations vs. offset vs. CCS)
4. **Capital requirement**: How much investment does net zero require?
5. **Business case**: What's the NPV considering carbon price, regulatory risk, brand value, energy cost savings?

## Geopolitical Risk Management

Geopolitical risk has become a board-level concern, especially post-2020 (COVID supply chain disruption, Russia-Ukraine war, US-China decoupling).

### The Geopolitical Risk Framework

**Supply Chain Exposure**:
- Single-source dependencies in high-risk regions
- Concentration of production in politically sensitive locations
- Critical raw materials sourced from geopolitically risky countries

**Market Exposure**:
- Revenue concentration in high-risk geographies
- Regulatory risk (tariffs, sanctions, data localization)
- Currency and capital controls risk

**Asset Exposure**:
- Physical assets in politically unstable regions
- Intellectual property at risk of theft or forced transfer
- Expropriation risk

### Resilience Strategies
- **Supply chain diversification**: "China +1" or "China +2" strategy; nearshoring/friendshoring
- **Revenue diversification**: Reduce dependence on any single geopolitically sensitive market
- **Scenario planning**: Develop strategies for multiple geopolitical scenarios (e.g., Taiwan Strait escalation; US-China full decoupling)
- **Political risk insurance**: Available through MIGA (World Bank), commercial insurers
- **Stakeholder management**: Build relationships with government officials in key markets; understand regulatory dynamics

## Board Governance & Advisory

### What Boards Actually Do
- **Oversight**: Monitor strategy execution, financial performance, risk
- **CEO succession**: The board's most consequential decision
- **Compensation**: Set executive pay aligned to long-term value creation
- **Risk governance**: Oversee enterprise risk management
- **Capital allocation**: Approve major investments, M&A, dividends, buybacks
- **Stakeholder relations**: Represent shareholder interests; manage ESG expectations

### Board Effectiveness Assessment
Strong boards have:
- **Independent majority**: >2/3 independent directors
- **Relevant expertise**: Skills matrix aligned to company's strategy and risks
- **Diverse composition**: Gender, ethnicity, background, perspective
- **Active engagement**: Genuine challenge to management, not rubber-stamping
- **CEO succession depth**: Clear pipeline; tested in real situations
- **Appropriate committees**: Audit, Compensation, Nominating/Governance; Risk where appropriate

### Common Board Failure Modes
- **Captured board**: Too close to management to provide genuine oversight
- **Missing skills**: No one understands the technology/markets the company competes in
- **Stale directors**: Long-tenured directors with accumulated conflicts
- **Poor information flow**: Management controls the information board receives
- **Weak CEO succession**: Over-reliance on a single leader with no depth behind

## Investor Relations & Equity Story

### The Equity Story
The equity story answers: Why should a rational investor own this stock vs. an alternative?

**Components:**
1. **Value proposition**: What unique value do we create and for whom?
2. **Competitive moat**: Why is our advantage durable and hard to replicate?
3. **Growth algorithm**: What are the specific drivers of revenue and earnings growth?
4. **Capital efficiency**: How do we generate superior returns on capital?
5. **Financial outlook**: What does the P&L look like in 3-5 years?
6. **Capital returns**: How will we return cash to shareholders?
7. **Management credibility**: What's the team's track record of delivering?

### The Valuation Conversation
Understand what drives your client's multiple:
- **EV/EBITDA**: Standard for most industrials, consumer, healthcare
- **P/E**: Consumer, financial services
- **EV/Revenue or Price/ARR**: High-growth SaaS / tech
- **P/NAV**: Real estate, financials
- **EV/DACF**: Energy

Multiple drivers: Growth rate, return on capital, free cash flow conversion, balance sheet quality, management credibility, ESG profile, and market sentiment.

## CEO Transition & Leadership Agenda

### The First 100 Days Framework

**Days 1-30: Listen and Learn**
- Meet every direct report and key stakeholders
- Understand what the organization believes is true and what you're not being told
- Identify the 2-3 things that must be fixed urgently
- Make no major structural changes yet (except true crises)

**Days 30-60: Diagnose and Form a View**
- Build your own view of the strategy, portfolio, and culture
- Test hypotheses with data and external perspective
- Identify your leadership team assessment (who stays, who goes, who needs development)
- Communicate your values and ways of working clearly

**Days 60-100: Set the Agenda**
- Articulate a clear strategic direction
- Make structural/personnel changes you've decided on
- Launch a few symbolic quick wins that demonstrate your priorities
- Set clear targets and accountability

### CEO Agenda Prioritization
The typical CEO has 10-15 priorities but can move the organization on 3-4 simultaneously. Force rank ruthlessly:
1. What is existential / urgent? (Do immediately, regardless of difficulty)
2. What is high-value and high-readiness? (Quick wins that build momentum)
3. What is high-value but requires capability building? (Plan carefully, sequence well)
4. What is noise? (Delegate or deprioritize — most things are here)
