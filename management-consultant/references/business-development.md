## The Consulting Sales Cycle

Consulting is a trust-intensive sale with a long cycle. Unlike product sales, you're selling an intangible — the confidence that a specific team of people will solve your problem. The cycle typically runs:

```
Awareness → Relationship → Opportunity → Proposal → Negotiation → Engagement → Expansion
```

**Key truths about consulting BD:**
- 80% of revenue at top firms comes from existing clients. New logo acquisition is expensive; organic growth within accounts is the core growth engine.
- Clients buy people, not firms. The partner relationship is what drives retention.
- Trust is built in the small moments — being responsive, keeping commitments, being honest when something goes wrong.
- The best business development is great work. Nothing wins the next project like delivering the current one exceptionally.

## Relationship Building & Client Management

### The Trusted Advisor Model (Maister, Green, Galford)
The trusted advisor is the goal — someone the client calls before they've even defined the problem. Four dimensions:

**Credibility**: Do they believe you know what you're talking about?
- Demonstrate through: Insight-rich interactions, relevant expertise, accurate predictions, candid assessments

**Reliability**: Do they trust you to do what you say?
- Demonstrate through: Meeting every deadline (especially small ones), flagging problems early, following up on commitments without being asked

**Intimacy**: Do they feel comfortable being honest with you?
- Demonstrate through: Showing genuine curiosity about their problems, handling sensitive information well, being discreet, showing personal care

**Self-orientation**: Do they believe you're working in their interest?
- The most important and most overlooked dimension. Clients immediately sense when a consultant is selling rather than helping.
- Demonstrate through: Recommending against a larger engagement when it's not needed, sharing inconvenient truths, explicitly saying "I don't think you need us for that"

**Trust formula**: Trust = (Credibility × Reliability × Intimacy) / Self-Orientation

A partner who scores perfectly on the first three but is seen as primarily interested in fees will never be truly trusted.

### The Relationship Map

For every major client, map the key stakeholders:
- **Economic Buyer**: Has budget authority and makes the final call
- **Technical Buyer**: Evaluates the solution on its merits (often a CTO, CFO, or functional head)
- **User Buyer**: Will use the output day-to-day
- **Coach/Champion**: Inside the organization, wants you to win, gives you intelligence

You need coverage at each layer. If you only know the day-to-day project manager, you're one reorganization away from losing the account.

### Touchpoint Cadence
Keep relationships warm between engagements:
- **Monthly**: Forward a relevant article, insight, or data point ("Thought of your challenge with X when I saw this")
- **Quarterly**: Coffee or lunch; catch up on their priorities, share what you're seeing in the market
- **Annually**: Invite to firm events, thought leadership roundtables, or provide a complimentary perspective on something they're working on
- **Immediately**: When their industry has a big development (earnings miss, regulatory change, competitor move), reach out with a point of view

## Spotting Opportunities

Great partners see opportunities in conversations that others miss. Train yourself to hear opportunity signals:

### The Problem-to-Project Translation

| What the client says | What you hear |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "We're struggling to hit our growth targets" | Growth strategy / market entry / pricing |
| "Our margins have been under pressure" | Profitability diagnostic / cost transformation |
| "We're thinking about [acquisition]" | M&A due diligence / integration planning |
| "Our IT systems are a mess" | Digital transformation / tech strategy |
| "We have too many priorities, nothing gets done" | Org design / operating model |
| "We're worried about [competitor]" | Competitive response / strategic positioning |
| "We just announced a restructuring" | Implementation support / change management |
| "The board is asking questions about [topic]" | Board-ready analysis on that topic |

### The Annual Planning Season
Q3 is when most clients finalize next year's priorities and budget. This is the single most important time to be in conversation with clients — decisions about what to work on (and who to hire) are made during this window.

### Trigger Events
Watch for events that create consulting demand:
- New CEO/CFO appointment (new leaders audit everything)
- Earnings miss (pressure to fix something fast)
- Regulatory change (compliance + strategy implications)
- Major acquisition or divestiture (integration / separation)
- Technology disruption hitting their industry
- Competitor makes a major move
- IPO preparation or going-private transaction

## Writing Winning Proposals

### What Clients Are Really Evaluating
Clients don't read proposals to understand your methodology. They read them to answer three questions:
1. Do they understand my problem?
2. Do they have a credible approach to solving it?
3. Can I trust this team?

Most losing proposals fail on question 1 — they're too generic, too much about the firm and not enough about the client's specific situation.

### The Winning Proposal Structure

**Executive Summary (1 page)**
This is what the decision-maker reads. It must stand alone.
- Restate the client's problem in your own words (shows you understand)
- Your hypothesis / initial point of view on what's driving the problem
- Your recommended approach
- Expected outcomes and value at stake
- Timeline and investment

**Problem Statement (1-2 pages)**
Demonstrate you've listened. Include:
- Current situation (what's happening now)
- The specific challenge or opportunity
- Why it matters (business impact if unsolved / value if solved)
- What's been tried before and why it hasn't fully worked

This section should make the client nod — "they get it."

**Our Approach (2-4 pages)**
- Workstream structure and phasing
- Key analytical questions to answer in each phase
- Methodology for the hardest parts (the parts where you differentiate)
- Data and access requirements
- Deliverables and milestones

Show thinking, not just process. "We'll conduct interviews and analysis" is generic. "We'll start by testing whether the problem is supply-side or demand-side because our experience suggests clients in this position are usually surprised by the answer" is specific and expert.

**Team (1-2 pages)**
Lead with the partner and engagement manager — the people who will actually be in the room.
- Specific relevant experience (not their whole career)
- 1-2 sentences on why this person is right for this project
- Include photos — it makes it human

**Commercials (1 page)**
- Fee total and structure (T&M, fixed, phased)
- What's included / excluded
- Expenses policy
- Payment terms
- Assumptions and dependencies

**Appendix**
Case studies of similar work (anonymized if needed), firm credentials, extended bios.

### Common Proposal Mistakes

- **Too much firm puffery, not enough client empathy**: Every firm claims to be "global leaders" — clients don't care. Show you understand their specific situation.
- **Generic approach**: Copy-pasted methodology slides signal you didn't think about their problem.
- **No point of view**: Proposals that say "we'll investigate and see what we find" signal no expertise. Have a hypothesis about what you'll find.
- **Death by qualifications**: Endless case studies before you show them your approach. Put credentials in the appendix.
- **Wrong decision-maker**: A brilliant proposal that the wrong person reads is a wasted proposal. Know who decides.

## Scoping & Pricing Engagements

### How to Scope
Start with the decision the client needs to make, work backward to what analysis is needed:
1. What is the deliverable? (Specific output, not "report")
2. What are the key questions to answer?
3. What analyses are needed for each question?
4. What data and access is required?
5. What is the highest-risk/most uncertain part?

Time-box the uncertain parts or treat them as separate phases.

### Pricing Approaches

**Bottom-up (cost-plus)**
Count the days needed per team member × daily rate = fee.
- Use for: Unfamiliar work, complex regulatory requirements, highly uncertain scope
- Risk: Can be high if scope is large; clients compare to their internal cost

**Value-based**
Price as a percentage of the value you're expected to create.
- Rule of thumb: Consulting fees are typically 5-15% of the value at stake for cost/revenue work
- Example: A $50M cost reduction program → consulting fee of $2.5-7.5M is reasonable
- Use for: High-value, well-scoped work where you can quantify the upside

**Comparable/market**
What do similar engagements cost at comparable firms? Use this to sanity-check both methods.

### Daily Rate Benchmarks (US, MBB approximate, 2024)
- Analyst: $2,500-4,000/day
- Associate/Consultant: $4,500-7,000/day
- Engagement Manager/Project Lead: $8,000-12,000/day
- Principal/Associate Partner: $12,000-18,000/day
- Partner/Director: $18,000-35,000+/day

Firms typically bill at blended rates. A 4-person team (1 EM, 2 consultants, 1 analyst) at $8,000 blended = $32,000/day or ~$640,000 for a 4-week engagement.

### Managing Scope Creep
Scope creep is the most common source of engagement margin erosion. Prevent it:
1. Define scope explicitly in the SOW — deliverables, not just work streams
2. Track against scope in every status meeting
3. When a new request comes in: "That's outside current scope — let me think about how we handle it" (never just say yes)
4. Create a "Phase 2 parking lot" — capture good ideas that are out of scope; they become the next engagement

## The Statement of Work (SOW)

The SOW is the contract. Get it right.

### SOW Essential Elements

**Scope of Work**
- Specific deliverables (not "strategy," but "a 30-slide board-ready strategy deck with 3 scenario options")
- What is explicitly OUT of scope
- Assumptions the scope depends on (data availability, client access, etc.)

**Timeline**
- Start date, end date, key milestones
- Dependencies on client actions (data delivery, interview availability, decision dates)
- What happens if dependencies slip

**Fees and Payment**
- Total fee and breakdown by phase
- Billing schedule (milestone-based is better than monthly — aligns payment to progress)
- Expense cap and reimbursement policy
- Change order process (how additional scope is priced and approved)

**Team**
- Named partners and engagement managers (clients want to know who's on their work)
- Commitment levels (% time per week)
- Process for changes to staffing

**Governance**
- Client sponsor and point of contact
- Frequency of steering committee meetings
- Decision-making authority (who can approve changes, access new data, etc.)

**Intellectual Property**
- Client owns all deliverables
- Firm retains right to use frameworks and methodologies developed independently
- Confidentiality obligations (both ways)

## Managing Up — Delivering to Keep Clients

### The Delivery Principles That Win Repeat Business

**No surprises upward.** The partner and client should never hear bad news for the first time in a meeting. Escalate problems early — clients forgive problems handled well; they don't forgive being blindsided.

**Proactive communication.** Don't wait for the client to ask for a status update. Send one before they need to ask. A weekly email (3 bullets: what we did, what we found, what we're doing next) keeps the client confident without requiring meetings.

**Make the client look good.** Understand what success looks like for your client sponsor inside their organization. Structure deliverables so they can present them internally and get credit.

**Treat the client as a partner, not an audience.** Involve them in the analysis, not just the presentation. Clients who feel ownership of the work advocate for the recommendations.

**Disagree directly.** When you think the client is wrong, say so — once, clearly, with evidence. Then respect their decision. Consultants who just tell clients what they want to hear are eventually fired for being useless.

### The Status Report Formula (Weekly)
```
Week X Status Update — [Project Name]

Progress this week:
• [Completed deliverable or key finding — be specific]
• [What analysis was done and what it showed]

Key finding / insight:
• [The most important thing we learned this week — with a "so what"]

Next week:
• [Specific tasks and milestones for next week]

Decisions / input needed from client:
• [Any blockers or decisions you need them to make, with deadline]

Risks:
• [Any emerging risks — be honest even if uncomfortable]
```

## From Project to Relationship

The goal of every engagement is to earn the right to the next one.

### The Expansion Playbook

**Discovery during delivery**: While doing the work, you're learning things the client doesn't know you're learning. Note everything that could become future work — organizational friction, data quality issues, strategic gaps, capability holes.

**The leave-behind**: At every presentation, include 1 slide of "additional opportunities we identified" — not a sales pitch, a service to the client. Two or three interesting questions you couldn't address in this scope but that deserve attention.

**The relationship debrief**: End every engagement with a formal debrief: "What did we do well? What could we have done better? What do you see as the most pressing challenge for the next 12 months?" This shows maturity and often surfaces the next project.

**The warm handshake introduction**: Ask satisfied clients to introduce you to peers — "Is there someone else in your network who's facing similar challenges?" This is the highest-quality lead generation.

**The quarterly check-in**: Three months after the engagement ends, reach out: "I've been thinking about the work we did — have you been able to implement the recommendations? Are there any new developments I should know about?"
