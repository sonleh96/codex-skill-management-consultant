# Structured Verbal Communication

## The Core Pattern: Answer First

In every spoken response—whether you're answering a partner's question, a client asking for your view, or a CEO pushing back on your recommendation—lead with the answer. Then structure your reasoning. This is the pyramid principle applied to speech.

**Weak approach:**
> "Well, we looked at several factors including the competitive dynamics, the cost structure, and customer feedback, and after analyzing all of that we came to the conclusion that..."

**Strong approach:**
> "We recommend Option A. Three reasons: first, it's the fastest path to margin recovery. Second, it requires no new capabilities. Third, our customer research shows it's what the market will reward."

The difference is dramatic. Senior people don't wait for you to finish building to the point—they'll interrupt, assume you don't know the answer, or tune out. State it first. Then earn it.

## The Rule of Three

Structure almost everything in threes. The brain processes three points effortlessly. Two feels thin. Four or more, people lose track.

When you're answering a question: three reasons. When framing a situation: three components. When listing next steps: three actions. If you have five insights, group them into three buckets. This sounds formulaic, but it forces you to prioritize and makes you sound clearer.

> "We see three main barriers to entry: capital intensity, customer switching costs, and regulatory compliance. The first is the highest hurdle."

## The BLUF Principle

**Bottom Line Up Front.** State your conclusion before context. Especially when time is short or stakes are high.

Bad: "So I wanted to update you on how things have been progressing this week. We've been looking at several scenarios, and there are some interesting dynamics emerging..."

Good: "The project is at risk. We need a decision on supplier consolidation by Friday."

In emails, Slack, impromptu updates, formal presentations—lead with the bottom line. Give context after people understand what you're saying.

## Structuring an Impromptu Answer

You're caught off guard. A client CFO asks: *"Do you think we should be looking at acquisitions instead of organic growth?"*

You weren't prepped on that. Here's how to respond:

**Buy yourself time (3-5 seconds):**
> "That's an important question—let me give you my thinking."

Never stop at "I don't know." Never open with "Great question!"—it sounds like you're stalling.

**Anchor immediately:**
> "There are really two dimensions here: the speed to scale, and the cost of integration..."

Naming the structure buys you thinking time and signals confidence.

**Structure as you speak:**
> "First, acquisitions get you to scale faster, but you inherit integration risk and cultural baggage. Organic growth takes longer but gives you optionality. Second, the cost profile is very different—acquisitions need capital upfront; organic spreads costs over time. And the key implication is..."

Land each point. Move to the next one cleanly.

**Close cleanly:**
> "So my take: acquisitions make sense if speed is the constraint and you have strong integration muscle. If you're under-capitalized or new to M&A, organic growth de-risks the strategy. We should run the math both ways."

You've answered the question, acknowledged the tradeoffs, and proposed next steps. No trailing off.

## Calibrating Confidence in Speech

Your language signals your confidence level. Know the difference:

**High confidence** (use when you have data, precedent, or strong conviction):
- "Our view is X."
- "The data clearly shows Y."
- "I'd recommend Z."

**Moderate confidence** (use when you have evidence but not certainty):
- "Our working hypothesis is X."
- "The evidence suggests Y."
- "We'd lean toward Z but want to pressure-test it."

**Low confidence** (use when you're forming a view):
- "One possibility is X."
- "It's too early to say definitively, but we're seeing signals of Y."
- "We're still developing our perspective on Z."

Mismatches kill credibility. Speaking with high confidence when you're unsure gets you called on it—and you look reckless. Speaking with low confidence when you ARE sure makes you sound uncertain and loses influence. Match the language to your actual conviction level.

## Handling Interruptions and Challenges

When someone interrupts mid-point:

**Don't get defensive. Don't speed up.**

Acknowledge: "That's a fair challenge—let me address it directly."

Answer their challenge cleanly and directly. Then either return to your point ("Coming back to what I was saying...") or let it go if it's no longer relevant.

When someone says *"I disagree"* or *"That doesn't match what I'm seeing":*

**Ask first:** "Can you tell me more about what you're seeing?"

Understand their basis. Then either update your view (if their information changes it) or hold your position with additional evidence. Don't capitulate to signal agreeableness. Don't dig in defensively.

## The Executive Conversation

Executives communicate differently:
- They get to the point in 30 seconds, not 5 minutes
- They say "I think" and mean it—they're not hedging, they're acknowledging their perspective
- They ask questions more than they explain—they're testing your thinking, not looking for a lecture
- They interrupt when they've heard enough. It's not rude. It's efficiency.

To communicate well with executives: **match their pace.** If they're moving fast, you move fast. If they interrupt, don't restart your explanation—answer their question and wait to see if they want more.

Read the room constantly. If the CEO is checking her phone, you're taking too long. If she's leaning forward, you have her attention. Adjust.

## Common Verbal Communication Failures

**Burying the lead.**
Explaining for 3 minutes before stating the point. State it first, then explain.

**Over-hedging.**
"It's possible that there may be some indication that..." This signals uncertainty and exhausts listeners.

**Verbal padding.**
"So, um, you know, basically..." Silence is better than filler. Pause, think, speak.

**Starting with process.**
"So what we did was we first looked at X and then we ran Y analysis and then we modeled Z..." Nobody cares about your process. Lead with the insight.

**Not landing.**
Trailing off without a clear conclusion. "...and yeah, so that's kind of where we are." Finish with a statement. A recommendation. A question. Something.

**Talking past the answer.**
Continuing to explain after the person has what they need. When you've answered the question, stop. Offer more if they ask.

---

**The underlying principle:** Treat your spoken communication with the same rigor as your written work. Be direct. Be structured. Respect the listener's time. The best consultants sound like they think clearly because they do.
