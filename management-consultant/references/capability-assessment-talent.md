# Capability Assessment and Talent Strategy

This file covers the consulting methodology for assessing organizational capability gaps — the rigorous process of determining whether an organization has the human capabilities required to execute its strategy, what the gaps are, and what the options are for closing them. It is distinct from `references/org-design-workforce.md` (which covers the structural and workforce planning dimensions of organizational design) and `references/change-management.md` (which covers the behavioral and adoption dimensions of transformation). This file covers the specific methodology for capability assessment — how to define the capabilities required by a strategy, how to assess current capability against those requirements, how to prioritize the gaps, and how to design a capability-building strategy across the build/buy/borrow/bridge framework.

The operating reality: most organizations cannot accurately describe what capabilities they need to execute their strategy, and even fewer have a rigorous view of what capabilities they currently have. The result is a capability investment pattern that reflects historical priorities rather than strategic requirements — the organization continues to build the capabilities it has always invested in, which may or may not be the ones the strategy requires. The capability assessment methodology corrects this by working forward from strategy, not backward from current state.

---

## The Capability Architecture: Defining What Matters

Before assessing capability, define what you are assessing. "Capability" is used loosely in most organizational discussions; a rigorous capability assessment requires a precise definition.

### The Capability Definition Framework

A capability in this framework is an organizational ability — the combination of skills, processes, tools, and practices that enables an organization to consistently perform a specific activity at a defined performance level. Capabilities are distinct from:

- **Skills:** Individual competencies that people possess. Skills are inputs to capabilities.
- **Roles:** Job categories that describe what people do. Roles are how capabilities are organized.
- **Functions:** Organizational units. Functions may contain multiple capabilities, or capabilities may span multiple functions.

**The capability definition template:**

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Capability name** | Specific and functional (e.g., "Customer segmentation and targeting" not "Analytics") |
| **Description** | What the organization can do consistently when it has this capability |
| **Performance definition** | What "good" looks like — the measurable standard of performance |
| **Constituent elements** | The skills, processes, data, tools, and governance that together constitute this capability |
| **Strategic link** | Which strategic priority this capability enables, and how critical it is |

### The Capability Mapping Process

Capability mapping starts from strategy, not from the current organizational structure.

**Step 1: Strategy translation.** For each strategic priority, ask: "To execute this strategy, what must we be able to do consistently and at scale?" The output is a set of capability requirements expressed in active verb form: "segment customers by behavioral drivers," "launch new products in under 12 months," "operate in 10 markets simultaneously with consistent standards."

**Step 2: Capability definition.** Convert each capability requirement into a defined capability using the template above. The discipline is specificity — "commercial capability" is not a capability; "pricing at the customer segment level, adjusting for competitive position and willingness-to-pay" is.

**Step 3: Criticality triage.** Not all capabilities are equally strategic. Triage into:
- **Differentiating capabilities:** The 3–5 capabilities where excellence creates competitive advantage. These are the capabilities in which the organization must be demonstrably better than competitors.
- **Required capabilities:** Capabilities that must be present at a baseline level for the strategy to function but that do not create competitive advantage. These are managed to minimum standard, not to excellence.
- **Legacy capabilities:** Capabilities the organization has historically invested in that are no longer strategically central. These are managed for efficiency or wound down, not invested in.

The criticality triage is often the most strategically important output of the capability mapping process — it forces the organization to make explicit choices about where capability investment will and will not be concentrated.

## The Capability Assessment: Measuring Current State

Once the capability requirements are defined, assess current capability against each requirement using a structured methodology.

### The Three-Dimension Assessment

For each defined capability, assess across three dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Depth (individual skill level)**
- Expert: Can perform at the highest level independently and can teach others
- Proficient: Can perform reliably in standard situations; requires support for complex or novel situations
- Developing: Has foundational knowledge; requires significant guidance for most applications
- Absent: The capability does not exist in the organization

**Dimension 2: Breadth (organizational coverage)**
- Systemic: The capability is embedded in processes, systems, and governance; it does not depend on specific individuals
- Widespread: Multiple people and teams have the capability; it is not dependent on a single team or individual
- Concentrated: The capability exists but is concentrated in one team or a small number of individuals; creates key-person risk
- Isolated: The capability exists in one or two individuals; highly fragile

**Dimension 3: Scalability (can it grow with the strategy?)**
- Scalable: The capability can expand to meet growing strategic requirements without rebuilding from scratch
- Constrained: The capability exists but will require significant investment to scale to meet strategic requirements
- Not scalable: The capability as currently structured cannot scale; fundamental redesign is required

**The assessment grid:** Map each defined capability on all three dimensions. The combination reveals the assessment:
- Deep + Widespread + Scalable = Strong (maintain, not invest)
- Deep + Concentrated + Scalable = Fragile (broaden before scaling)
- Developing + Widespread + Constrained = Priority investment (broadly distributed but insufficient for strategic requirements)
- Absent or developing + Concentrated + Not scalable = Critical gap

### Assessment Methods: The Evidence Base

The most common capability assessment error is relying primarily on self-reported capability data. Managers systematically overrate their own function's capabilities; employees have limited ability to assess themselves against an external performance standard. A credible capability assessment uses multiple evidence sources.

**Structured assessment tools:**
- Skills assessments: validated tests of specific technical or analytical competencies (most reliable for technical capabilities where objective assessment is possible)
- Work sample review: reviewing actual work products (analyses, presentations, client deliverables, operational decisions) for quality against defined standards
- Structured behavioral interviews: using STAR-format interviews (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to assess how capability was applied in specific past situations

**Performance data:**
- Output quality metrics: error rates, customer satisfaction scores, speed, compliance rates — metrics that reflect the quality of capability application
- Comparative performance: how does the organization's performance on capability-intensive activities compare to competitors or benchmarks?
- Outcome tracking: what outcomes does the organization achieve on activities that require this capability?

**Expert assessment:**
- External benchmark: engagement of external practitioners who know what "excellent" looks like and can assess the organization against that standard
- Peer review: structured review of work products by expert practitioners
- Interview with people who have worked at best-in-class organizations: their ability to describe the difference between the current organization and their prior experience is often the most illuminating gap evidence

**The minimum evidence standard:** A capability assessment that is based only on interviews and surveys is insufficient for a strategic decision. At minimum, include: structured interviews or behavioral assessment for critical capabilities; work product review for capabilities where quality is assessable; and external benchmark data for at least the differentiating capabilities.

## The Gap Analysis: Prioritizing Investment

The gap analysis converts the capability assessment into a prioritized investment agenda. Not all gaps are equal — gaps in differentiating capabilities are more strategic than gaps in required capabilities; gaps that are 24 months from the critical strategic decision are more urgent than gaps that are 36 months out.

### The Gap Prioritization Matrix

Plot each capability gap on two axes:
- **Strategic importance:** Differentiating vs. Required vs. Legacy
- **Time to critical need:** Within 12 months / 12–24 months / 24+ months

**Priority 1 (immediate investment):** Differentiating capabilities with critical need within 12 months. These gaps must be addressed through accelerated build or buy — they cannot wait for a standard development cycle.

**Priority 2 (planned investment):** Differentiating capabilities with 12–24 month timeline; Required capabilities with <12 month timeline. These gaps should be addressed in the current annual plan cycle.

**Priority 3 (monitored):** All other gaps. These are on the radar but do not require immediate investment.

**Divest:** Legacy capabilities — the organization should be explicit about where it is choosing to reduce capability investment, not just where it is choosing to invest.

## The Build/Buy/Borrow/Bridge Framework

For each Priority 1 and Priority 2 gap, the strategy question is how to close it. The four options:

### Build (Develop Internally)

Build means investing in developing the capability through training, practice, and experience accumulation in the existing workforce.

**When to build:**
- When the organization has the foundational skills and the gap is in application and practice, not in fundamental knowledge
- When the capability is so organization-specific that external hires or partners cannot immediately apply it effectively
- When 18–36 months is available for development before the capability is critically needed
- When the capability needs to be culturally embedded (deep behavioral change) rather than technically acquired

**Build mechanisms:** Formal training programs (for foundational knowledge); structured development assignments (for practice and experience); coaching and mentoring (for application in context); communities of practice (for peer learning at scale). The most effective builds combine all four; programs that rely exclusively on formal training rarely produce genuine capability improvement.

**Build timeline reality:** Genuine capability development — not awareness or introductory knowledge, but the ability to perform reliably — typically requires 12–24 months of deliberate practice for intermediate-complexity capabilities, and 3–5 years for the most complex capabilities. Any program promising significant capability development in 90 days is developing knowledge, not capability.

### Buy (Hire Externally)

Buy means acquiring capability by hiring people who already have it.

**When to buy:**
- When the time available is insufficient for build
- When the capability is fundamentally different from what currently exists in the organization (not a development of existing skills, but a genuinely new type of thinking or expertise)
- When a small number of excellent people can meaningfully shift the capability level of the broader organization (the "seed and spread" model)

**Buy design principles:**
- Hire for the capability the organization needs, not for the industry experience it is most comfortable with. Organizations often hire from within their industry when the capability they need is best developed in a different sector.
- Hire at a level that can create organizational change. A single expert individual embedded in a large team will typically be absorbed by the existing culture rather than shifting it. Buy at scale — enough critical mass that the new capability is not isolated.
- Be explicit about what is being bought. The hire template should specify the specific capability being acquired, not just the role title and seniority.

**Buy integration risk:** External hires who have the required capability often fail to produce organizational capability improvement because they are not integrated effectively — they apply their capability individually but do not build it in the surrounding team. Mitigation: pair external hires with explicit capability-spreading responsibilities; measure organizational capability improvement, not just individual performance.

### Borrow (Access Externally Without Hiring)

Borrow means accessing capability through partnerships, consulting, outsourcing, or contractor arrangements — without adding to the permanent workforce.

**When to borrow:**
- When the capability is needed for a defined period or project, not on an ongoing basis
- When the market offers high-quality capability providers and the cost of accessing them is lower than building or buying
- When the capability is a commodity (available from multiple equivalent providers) rather than a proprietary advantage
- When the organization is testing whether the capability is genuinely needed before committing to a permanent investment

**Borrow mechanisms:** Consulting engagements (for diagnostic, strategy, and design work); staff augmentation / contractors (for implementation work where volume is needed temporarily); strategic partnerships (for capability access that is ongoing but not core); alliances with academic or research institutions (for early-stage capability development in emerging areas).

**The borrow limitation:** Borrowed capability does not accumulate inside the organization. If the capability is genuinely strategic, the organization's dependence on borrowed capability is a strategic risk. Borrow is most appropriate when it is transitional — used while the build or buy strategy produces results.

### Bridge (Interim Solution While Building)

Bridge means implementing a temporary solution that substitutes for a missing capability until the build or buy strategy produces results.

**Bridge mechanisms:**
- Tool-based bridges: technology that enables non-experts to produce outputs that would otherwise require expert capability (analytics platforms that enable non-data-scientists to run analyses; AI tools that enable non-writers to produce first-draft content)
- Process bridges: standard operating procedures that encode expert judgment into steps that non-experts can follow
- Decision support bridges: decision frameworks, decision trees, or playbooks that structure the decision in ways that compensate for capability gaps

**The bridge design principle:** Design bridges explicitly as temporary. A bridge that is designed as a permanent solution tends to become one — and the organization never develops the underlying capability because the bridge makes the gap invisible. Bridges should have a defined end date at which the underlying capability is expected to be in place.

## The Capability Building Roadmap

The output of the capability strategy is a capability building roadmap — a multi-year plan that sequences the capability investment agenda and makes explicit the investment required, the timeline for results, and the intermediate milestones.

**Roadmap elements:**
- Capability gaps addressed in each year (with priority ranking)
- Method for each gap (build/buy/borrow/bridge)
- Investment required: financial (training budget, hiring cost, partnership cost) and leadership time
- Expected capability milestones: what level of capability is expected by what date
- Measurement mechanism: how will progress be tracked?
- Risk: what might prevent the capability development from progressing as planned?

**The measurement discipline:** Capability roadmaps that lack measurement mechanisms drift. At least annually, reassess the capability gaps using the original assessment methodology and update the roadmap based on actual progress. The most common roadmap failure: the capability investment is made (training programs run, hires are made) but the capability improvement is never measured — so the organization does not know whether the investment produced the intended result.

## Common Failure Modes

**The skills training solution to a capability problem.** The capability gap is addressed by running a training program. The training builds knowledge but not capability — because capability requires practice, feedback, and application in real work, not classroom instruction. The organization declares the capability gap closed because the training was delivered; the capability remains absent. Prevention: always specify the practice and application mechanism alongside the knowledge delivery mechanism.

**The capability map that does not connect to strategy.** The capability assessment produces a comprehensive inventory of current capabilities without a clear link to strategic priorities. Every gap looks equally important; nothing is prioritized. Prevention: lead with the strategy-to-capability linkage; do not map capabilities that are not connected to a specific strategic priority.

**The key-person capability.** The capability assessment identifies "excellent" capability — but the excellent capability resides in one or two specific individuals. When those individuals leave, the capability is gone. Prevention: the assessment must evaluate breadth and scalability explicitly, not just depth. A capability that scores "Expert" on depth but "Concentrated" on breadth is a fragile capability, not a strong one.

**The hire without spread.** The organization hires several excellent people with a new capability, but does not invest in spreading that capability to the broader organization. The excellent people are extremely busy, produce high-quality individual work, but the organization's overall capability level does not improve. Prevention: externally hired capability builders must have explicit capability-spreading objectives and time in their role dedicated to it.

**The borrow dependency.** The organization has borrowed a capability (from consultants or contractors) for an extended period and, because the borrowed capability meets the immediate need, never invests in building it internally. The capability remains externally dependent. This becomes visible — and expensive — when the relationship ends or the external provider's quality changes. Prevention: set a deliberate build timeline for any capability that has been borrowed for more than 18 months.
