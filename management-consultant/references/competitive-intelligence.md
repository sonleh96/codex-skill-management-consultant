# Competitive Intelligence: The Real Game

## What This Actually Is

You land on an engagement. Your client says, "We need to understand our competitive landscape." What they mean is: *I'm nervous about what's happening to my business and I don't know who to be nervous about.*

Competitive intelligence isn't a framework exercise. It's not "here are five competitors, we'll put them on a 2x2." The goal is to understand what competitors are actually doing, why they're doing it, and what it means for your client's strategy.

There's a difference between a competitive landscape slide that says "Competitor A is a large player with broad capabilities" and one that says "Competitor A has hired 35 ML engineers from Google and Meta over the past 18 months, filed 12 patents in predictive logistics, and just spun up a new product team in Singapore. They're 12-18 months from a product that directly threatens your client's core offering."

One is a label. The other is intelligence.

## Building a Landscape from Scratch

When you need to understand the competitive environment fast:

**Step 1: Define the competitive frame.** What problem are customers solving? Write it down in customer terms, not product terms. "Supply chain optimization" is a product category. "How do we reduce logistics costs while maintaining service levels?" is the customer problem. Now — who solves this problem? Include non-obvious substitutes. The competitor might not be another software vendor. It might be "do nothing," it might be internal build, it might be adjacent solutions like TMS systems or 3PL partnerships. The boundary of your competitive set is set by how customers solve the problem, not by industry definitions.

**Step 2: Map the players.** You need multiple sources:
- Industry reports and analyst coverage (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, CB Insights for early-stage)
- Trade press — the actual industry publications your customers read
- LinkedIn job searches by function and company (signals strategic hiring)
- Funding databases (Crunchbase, PitchBook) for VC-backed players
- Customer review sites (G2, Trustpilot, industry-specific review platforms)

You're building a roster of 6-12 significant competitors, not an exhaustive list of 50.

**Step 3: Gather primary signals on each major player.** Job postings, earnings calls, patent filings, pricing, customer reviews, trade presentations. This is where intelligence happens — you're reading signals for strategic intent, not collecting facts.

**Step 4: Build the positioning map.** A 2x2 or multi-dimensional map showing where each player competes. The map should reveal white space (underserved positions), crowded battlegrounds (where everyone competes on the same dimensions), and your client's current position vs. aspirational position.

## Primary Intelligence Sources

**Job postings.** One of the most underused intelligence sources. A competitor's hiring reveals strategic priorities 12-18 months out.

A logistics company that's never had a data science function suddenly posting for 30 data scientists? They're building something new. A competitor opening a sales office in a new geography? Expansion signal. A competitor shifting hiring from software engineers to solutions architects? They're moving toward custom implementation, suggesting their platform is maturing.

Read job descriptions for strategic inference. A "senior ML engineer, supply chain optimization" hire tells you what they're building. Cluster the hires — are they hiring across a function (multiple data scientists) or point-solving (one machine learning engineer for a specific project)? Volume and patterns matter.

**Earnings calls and investor materials.** If your competitor is public, you have direct access to management priorities. Earnings call transcripts are on Seeking Alpha, company IR websites, or SEC Edgar. Read the analyst Q&A section — analysts push for specificity and forward-looking commentary. Annual reports (10-K) contain risk disclosures that reveal what management is actually worried about.

Look for: What capabilities are they building? What markets are they entering? What are they de-emphasizing? How are they talking about price competition?

**Patent filings.** Early indicator of technology direction. Search Google Patents or USPTO for free. Look at filing volume and themes over time, not individual patents. A competitor filing 50 patents in autonomous routing is a signal. A competitor that hasn't filed anything in two years despite claiming technology leadership is a signal.

**Pricing intelligence.** Competitor pricing pages, sales collateral, customer proposals (sometimes clients have these). Mystery shopping — going through their sales process as a prospect to see how they position, price, and sell. Win/loss data from the client's sales team (rarely discussed systematically, usually the most valuable data in the room).

**Customer reviews and feedback.** G2, Trustpilot, app stores, industry forums. Customers tell you what competitors do well and poorly in ways competitors would never admit. Read the negative reviews carefully — they're honest.

**Former employee interviews.** Approached ethically and professionally, ex-employees provide operational and strategic perspective you can't get elsewhere. LinkedIn is your sourcing tool. Be direct about who you are and what you're asking. Don't misrepresent yourself — it's bad practice and burns sources.

**Trade press and conferences.** What topics are competitors speaking about at industry conferences? What do their keynotes emphasize? What are they NOT talking about publicly? Trade publications often have deeper investigative reporting than analyst coverage.

## Market Segmentation: Finding the Real Structure

Most clients think their market is one thing. Usually it's 3-4 fundamentally different businesses wearing the same label.

**The analytical approach:**

Start with customer data if you have it — purchase patterns, product usage, support ticket types, contract sizes, industries, geographies. Identify candidate segmentation variables: customer size, use case/job-to-be-done, buying behavior (price sensitivity, relationship vs. transactional), decision-maker type, deployment model.

Test whether segments actually behave differently. Do they buy differently? Have different retention rates? Respond differently to price changes? Require different go-to-market motions? If not, it's not a real segment.

Size each segment and profile its economics — TAM, growth rate, typical deal size, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, profitability.

**Common mistakes:**

Segmenting by demographics when behavior is what matters (segmenting by company size when what actually varies is buying process). Building segments that are analytically clean but don't map to how you actually sell. Over-segmenting until every segment is too small to act on.

The test of a good segmentation: customers within a segment look similar to each other and different from other segments in ways that matter strategically.

## Win/Loss Analysis

One of the highest-ROI research activities for any commercial strategy. Most clients never do it systematically.

**Methodology:**

Sample recently closed deals — wins AND losses, across different customer types, deal sizes, geographies. Include deals lost to specific competitors and deals lost to "no decision" (customer decided to do nothing or build internally). Aim for 15-20 interviews minimum.

Interview the economic buyer or primary decision-maker — not procurement, not the internal champion who liked you. Get on the phone.

Walk them through their decision process. What were the 2-3 things that mattered most? Where did your client perform well? Less well compared to alternatives? What almost made them go a different direction? What would have changed their decision?

Don't ask leading questions. Don't give them the party line answer option.

**What you'll find:**

Competitive differentiators you didn't know you had. Table-stakes requirements you're missing. Gaps between how your client's sales team talks about the product and how customers actually evaluate it. Pricing perception gaps. Sales process issues that look like product issues.

Pattern across the interviews. What's consistent? What surprises you? What does the client's sales leadership believe that's wrong?

## Competitive Positioning Map: The Output

Most competitive intelligence work ends in a positioning map — a visual showing where each competitor sits relative to axes that matter.

**Good axes:** Things customers actually care about. Speed vs. cost. Depth of customization vs. out-of-the-box capability. Enterprise-grade compliance vs. ease of use. Breadth of solution vs. depth in a specific use case. Not generic axes like "low quality / high quality."

Build the axes from customer decision criteria first. What do customers actually evaluate when they choose? Then place competitors on those axes based on what you've learned.

**The trap:** Picking axes that make your client look best rather than axes that reflect how customers actually evaluate. You'll get a map that's technically correct but strategically useless.

**What to look for:** White space — positions that are underserved, where a competitor could win. Crowded positions — where everyone is fighting on the same dimensions. Your client's current position vs. aspirational position. Are they equidistant from multiple competitors or is one directly adjacent?

## Synthesizing Intelligence into Strategy

Raw intelligence isn't useful without synthesis. For each major competitor, you need:

- **Apparent strategy.** Not what they claim, but what their actions reveal. They say they're focused on enterprise. They're hiring primarily SMB sales reps. Read the hiring, not the press release.
- **Investment vectors.** Where are they hiring? What are they acquiring? What are they building? Signals point to future direction.
- **Customer perception.** What do customers think of them? Strengths and vulnerabilities from customer perspective often diverge from competitor's narrative.
- **Likely competitive responses.** If your client makes move X, what does Competitor A do? How quickly can they respond? What's their pricing move to an aggressive rate cut? What's their feature response if your client launches a new capability?

The "competitive response" section in your strategy recommendation shouldn't just describe the current landscape. It should anticipate moves. If your client takes an action, how do competitors react? This is where intelligence becomes strategy.
