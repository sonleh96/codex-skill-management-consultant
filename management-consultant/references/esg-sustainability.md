# ESG & Sustainability: Integration into Business Strategy

ESG has moved from a reporting exercise to a strategic imperative. Regulators are mandating disclosure. Investors are pricing ESG risk into valuations. Customers and employees increasingly make choices based on ESG credentials. The question is no longer whether ESG matters — it's how to integrate it into strategy without making it separate from strategy.

---

## The Materiality Assessment: Where Strategy Begins

Not all ESG issues are equally relevant to every business. Materiality assessment identifies which ESG topics are significant enough to warrant strategic focus and disclosure.

**Dual materiality:**
- **Financial materiality (outside-in):** Which ESG issues create financial risk or opportunity for the business? Climate physical risks to supply chains, water scarcity affecting manufacturing, carbon pricing affecting cost structures — these affect enterprise value.
- **Impact materiality (inside-out):** Which of the company's activities have significant positive or negative effects on society and environment? Labor practices in the supply chain, GHG emissions, product safety — these represent the company's footprint in the world.

Double materiality (used in EU reporting frameworks) requires disclosing both. Financial materiality is required in the US under SEC rules.

**Running a materiality assessment:**
1. Identify all potentially relevant ESG topics (use GRI's topic list as a starting point — 40+ categories)
2. Survey internal stakeholders (executives, business unit leaders) on relative importance
3. Survey external stakeholders (investors, customers, NGOs) on their priorities
4. Plot results on a 2x2: importance to external stakeholders vs. financial/business impact
5. Identify the topics in the upper right — these are your material issues

The output: a shortlist of 8-12 material ESG topics that will guide disclosure, target-setting, and strategy integration.

## The Three E's: What Actually Matters in Each Domain

**Environmental:**

*Climate/GHG emissions:* Measure scope 1 (direct operations), scope 2 (purchased electricity), and scope 3 (supply chain and product use). Scope 3 typically represents 70%+ of total emissions but is the hardest to measure and control. Carbon reduction targets are increasingly expected — net zero by 2050 is the standard ambition; 2030 interim targets matter more for near-term strategy.

*Physical climate risk:* Map your assets and supply chains against physical climate risk scenarios (flooding, extreme heat, water stress). TCFD provides the standard framework. This is increasingly required for risk disclosure to investors.

*Nature and water:* Biodiversity loss and water scarcity are growing material risks, particularly for food/agriculture, chemicals, and consumer goods. Emerging frameworks (TNFD) are formalizing reporting here.

**Social:**

*Labor practices and human rights:* Labor conditions in direct operations and supply chain. Supply chain due diligence legislation (Germany's Supply Chain Act, EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive) is creating legal obligations to identify and address human rights risks in supply chains.

*Workforce:* Diversity, equity, and inclusion metrics. Employee health and safety. Pay equity. Employee engagement and turnover — increasingly seen as leading indicators of operational risk.

*Community:* Community impact of operations. Particularly important for extractives, utilities, and large employers in small communities.

**Governance:**

*Board composition and independence:* Diversity of background, gender, and expertise. Independent directors as % of board. CEO/Chair separation.

*Executive compensation:* Alignment of incentives with long-term value creation and ESG metrics.

*Transparency and ethics:* Anti-corruption programs, whistleblower protections, tax transparency, political lobbying disclosure.

## ESG Reporting Frameworks

The landscape is complex and converging. Know the major frameworks:

**GRI (Global Reporting Initiative):**
The broadest and most widely used. Covers all three dimensions comprehensively. Audience: broad stakeholder reporting (customers, employees, NGOs, investors). Approach: impact-focused (what effect does the company have on the world?). Required by many supply chain partners and procurement requirements.

**SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board):**
Industry-specific. 77 industry standards identifying the financially material ESG topics for each sector. Audience: investors. Approach: financially material metrics that affect enterprise value. Best for investor-focused disclosure and integration with financial reporting.

**TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures):**
Climate-specific. Four pillars: Governance (board oversight of climate risk), Strategy (how climate affects strategy), Risk Management (how climate risk is identified and managed), Metrics & Targets (KPIs and targets). Now mandatory in many jurisdictions and integrated into IFRS S2.

**CSRD/ESRS (EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive):**
New EU mandatory framework effective 2024+. Requires double materiality assessment. More comprehensive than anything previously required. Affects EU companies and many non-EU companies operating in the EU.

**ISSB/IFRS S1 and S2:**
Global baseline standards for sustainability disclosure. S1 covers general sustainability disclosures; S2 covers climate specifically. Increasingly adopted by countries as the regulatory standard.

**Practical advice:** Use SASB to identify your material issues. Use TCFD structure to report on climate. Use GRI for comprehensive stakeholder disclosure. Report against ISSB/CSRD as regulatory requirements apply.

## Integrating ESG into Business Strategy (Not Just Reporting)

The most common failure: ESG becomes a disclosure function disconnected from strategy and operations. The CFO's team fills in the reporting templates, publishes the ESG report, and the business unit leaders continue making decisions with no ESG lens.

**Integration checkpoint 1: Strategy setting**
When developing business unit strategy, explicitly assess: What are the ESG risks in this market/geography/supply chain? What ESG opportunities exist (green products, sustainable supply chain, low-carbon operations)? How do ESG commitments constrain the strategy?

**Integration checkpoint 2: Capital allocation**
Apply an ESG screen to major capital investments. What's the carbon footprint of this facility? How does this supply chain expansion affect our scope 3 emissions? Does this acquisition create ESG risk (legacy environmental liabilities, labor practices)?

**Integration checkpoint 3: Incentives**
Executive and management compensation tied to ESG targets (GHG reduction, safety metrics, diversity targets) is the most reliable way to make ESG real in decision-making. Without it, ESG remains aspirational.

**Integration checkpoint 4: Supply chain**
Increasingly, ESG risk IS supply chain risk. Physical climate risk, human rights violations, water scarcity — the most material ESG exposures for many companies are in their supply chains. Supplier ESG scorecards, audits, and engagement programs are operational risk management.

## How Consultants Add Value in ESG Engagements

**Materiality assessment:** Stakeholder research, benchmarking against peers, structuring the materiality matrix — all standard consulting skills applied to an ESG context.

**Target-setting:** Science-based targets for GHG emissions require modeling (understanding current emissions, trajectory to target, intervention levers). The consultant provides analytical rigor.

**ESG integration into strategy:** The bridge between the ESG team and the strategy team — helping business unit leaders understand what ESG means for their specific competitive context.

**Investor relations preparation:** Translating ESG performance into investor-friendly language. Anticipating ESG-related investor questions. Positioning the ESG story alongside financial performance.

**Supply chain due diligence:** Mapping supply chain ESG risk, prioritizing high-risk suppliers, designing engagement or audit programs.

**The credibility test:** ESG engagements are increasingly scrutinized for greenwashing — commitments that are aspirational rather than evidence-based. Your job is to help clients make commitments they can actually deliver, backed by credible implementation plans.
